**Semantic shift in translation:** Minimal. Hebrew laqach is an extremely broad "take" verb, and Greek lambano matches this breadth almost exactly. ## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The core semantic range is remarkably stable — both laqach and lambano function as the default "take" verb in their respective languages
- Both cover active taking (seizing objects), receiving (from a giver), and idiomatic constructions (taking counsel, taking a wife)
- Both serve as the unmarked, general-purpose "take" word against which more specific verbs (snatch, seize, grasp) are defined

**What shifts:**
- The NT develops a stronger "receive" sense for lambano, especially in theological contexts: receiving the Spirit (Acts 2:38), receiving grace (Rom 1:5), receiving power (Rev 5:12). In the OT, laqach is more weighted toward active "taking"
- The perfect tense form (eilephas/eilephen) becomes theologically loaded in Revelation, marking completed divine actions — a development without a direct OT parallel since Hebrew tense/aspect works differently
- The give/receive (didomi/lambano) pairing becomes a major NT theological structure for grace and Spirit-reception, more prominent than any analogous OT pairing with laqach
