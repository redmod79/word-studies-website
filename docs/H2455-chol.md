## Lexicon Definition

From H2490; properly, exposed; hence, profane: — common, profane (place), unholy.

The underlying image is of that which is "laid open," i.e., not fenced off or set apart. The word is the conceptual opposite of קֹדֶשׁ (qodesh, H6944, "holy/sacred"). Where qodesh marks what is withdrawn for God's use, chol marks what remains in the ordinary, accessible sphere.

## Semantic Categories

### 1. Priestly Distinction Formula: Holy vs. Profane
The core usage — defining the boundary between the sacred and the ordinary as a priestly teaching responsibility.

- **Lev 10:10** — "And that ye may put difference between holy and **unholy**, and between unclean and clean"
- **Eze 22:26** — "Her priests have violated my law... they have put no difference between the holy and **profane**, neither have they shewed difference between the unclean and the clean"

### 2. Common / Non-Sacred Bread
The only narrative usage — bread that is not sanctified, ordinary rations as opposed to consecrated showbread.

- **1 Sam 21:4** — "There is no **common** bread under mine hand, but there is hallowed bread"

### 3. Profane / Non-Sacred Space

- **Eze 42:20** — "it had a wall round about... to make a separation between the sanctuary and the **profane place**"

The LXX translators overwhelmingly chose βέβηλος for chol. This is significant because the NT uses βέβηλος 5 times (1 Tim 1:9, 4:7, 6:20; 2 Tim 2:16; Heb 12:16) but uses κοινός (G2839) for the "common/profane" concept in Acts 10:14, 28; Rom 14:14. The two Greek words carry different nuances: βέβηλος = "profane, godless" (moral weight); κοινός = "common, shared, not holy" (ritual status).
