**Semantic shift in translation:** The LXX preserves the core "miss the mark" etymology (Greek ἁμαρτάνω also originally meant "miss" in Homer). However, the Piel purification sense of châṭâʼ is NOT carried into the Greek verb hamartanō — the LXX uses other verbs (καθαρίζω, ἁγνίζω) for those contexts. This means the remarkable Hebrew semantic link between sinning and purifying (within the same root) is severed in the Greek translation.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The core meaning "to sin against God" carries through unchanged from OT Hebrew to NT Greek
- The "I have sinned" confessional formula (Qal perfect → aorist) is preserved verbatim
- The universality axiom ("no man that sinneth not") anchors both testamental discussions of human sinfulness
- The personal-relational dimension — sinning "against" God or another person — remains central

**What shifts:**
- The Piel/Hithpael purification meaning of the Hebrew root is completely lost in Greek. The LXX translates the Piel of châṭâʼ with katharizō or hagnizō, not hamartanō. This severing means Greek readers cannot see what Hebrew readers saw: that the same root that means "sin" also means "purify from sin"
- The Hiphil causative "made [Israel] to sin" (30x in Kings) has no NT structural equivalent — the concept of causing corporate sin through leadership is expressed differently
- The literal "miss the mark" sense (Judg 20:16) does not appear in NT usage of hamartanō
