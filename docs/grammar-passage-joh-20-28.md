# Grammar Analysis: John 20:28 — Thomas's Confession

## Text

**Greek (NA/TR):** ἀπεκρίθη Θωμᾶς καὶ εἶπεν αὐτῷ· Ὁ Κύριός μου καὶ ὁ Θεός μου.

**KJV:** And Thomas answered and said unto him, My Lord and my God.

**Context (Jn 20:29, Jesus' reply):** λέγει αὐτῷ ὁ Ἰησοῦς· Ὅτι ἑώρακάς με, πεπίστευκας; μακάριοι οἱ μὴ ἰδόντες καὶ πιστεύσαντες. ("Jesus saith unto him, Thomas, because thou hast seen me, thou hast believed: blessed [are] they that have not seen, and [yet] have believed.")

Parser verification: both content nouns are tagged **N-NSM** (nominative singular masculine), each preceded by the nominative-singular-masculine article Ὁ / ὁ. No vocative form appears in the morphology — this is crucial. The spoken address εἶπεν αὐτῷ ("said **to him**") is not quoted speech *about* Jesus; the dative αὐτῷ locates the address directly on Jesus as recipient.

## Key Grammatical Features

### 1. Ὁ Κύριός μου καὶ ὁ Θεός μου — Articular Nominative for Vocative

**Form:** Two parallel predicate-free noun phrases, each Article (T-NSM) + Noun (N-NSM) + enclitic genitive pronoun μου. Morphologically pure nominative; syntactically a direct address (address of Jesus introduced by εἶπεν **αὐτῷ**).

**Nominative-for-vocative in Koine:** Greek has a dedicated vocative case, but the nominative is regularly used in address when (a) the noun is articular, and (b) solemnity, emphasis, or Semitic-style possessed address is in view. The vocative κύριε (voc. of κύριος) and θεέ (voc. of θεός) were both grammatically available — Thomas could have said κύριέ μου καὶ θεέ μου. He did not. He used the **nominative with article**, which in NT Koine has a distinct pragmatic force: it marks the addressee with the full force of identification-predication rather than mere summons. Wallace (GGBB 56–59) classes this as "nominative of address" (sometimes "pendent nominative" in address); BDF §147 treats articular nominative-for-vocative as a Semitism carried in through the LXX.

**Parallel NT articular-nominative addresses:** Mk 14:36 αββα ὁ πατήρ; Mt 11:26 ναί, ὁ πατήρ; Lk 8:54 ἡ παῖς; Heb 1:8 ὁ θρόνος σου, ὁ θεός (cited of the Son). The articular form carries a Hebrew-tinged solemnity distinguishable from the bare vocative.

**Significance:** The construction is **not** a detached exclamation ("My Lord! And my God!" as surprise or oath), which would normally be anarthrous, have a different word order, or lack the dative addressee αὐτῷ. The syntax is an *address to Jesus* identifying him as Thomas's κύριος and Thomas's θεός. The two articular-nominative noun phrases function as one complex vocative-style predication.

**Not an exclamation:** This reading (sometimes offered in polemical literature as "Thomas was swearing in surprise") fails on three grammatical counts: (i) Koine oaths/exclamations targeting God do not take the first-person possessive μου — they are generic (e.g., θεέ vocative, or μὰ τὸν θεόν accusative); (ii) the verb εἶπεν αὐτῷ explicitly routes the speech to Jesus as dative addressee, not to the air; (iii) Jesus' following reply (20:29) treats Thomas's words as an act of πιστεύειν ("thou hast believed"), not as a profane outburst to be corrected.

### 2. Coordinated Article-Noun-Pronoun Parallelism

Two morphologically identical noun-phrase units bracketed by καί:

| | NP 1 | NP 2 |
|---|---|---|
| Article | Ὁ | ὁ |
| Noun | Κύριός | Θεός |
| Possessive | μου | μου |

This tight parallelism — same article, same case/gender/number, same possessive — is a Semitic-style appositional pairing (cf. Hebrew `אֲדֹנִי וֵאלֹהָי`, "my Lord and my God"). In Greek, the coordination καί + repeated article + repeated possessive makes the two nouns **co-referential to a single addressee** (Granville Sharp's first rule applies to singular, personal, non-proper nouns under one article; here the repeated article yields *parallel* identification rather than strict unification, but still co-referring: Jesus is both the κύριος-referent and the θεός-referent, not one person under the first noun and someone else under the second).

The two μου-possessives are reciprocal and personal: Thomas predicates *his own* Lord-relation and *his own* God-relation of the same Jesus standing in front of him.

### 3. Jesus' Non-Rebuke (Jn 20:29 vs. Rev 19:10; 22:9)

**Grammatical observation:** Jesus' reply (20:29) contains no disavowal verb — no ὅρα μή (as Rev 19:10; 22:9), no σύνδουλός σού εἰμι ("I am thy fellow-servant"), no imperative redirecting worship to God. Instead:

- `ὅτι ἑώρακάς με, πεπίστευκας` — **perfect active indicative** πεπίστευκας of πιστεύω. Jesus categorizes Thomas's utterance as an act of *faith*, not of blasphemy or misdirected worship. The perfect tense marks the completed inception of a continuing state of belief.
- `μακάριοι οἱ μὴ ἰδόντες καὶ πιστεύσαντες` — a **beatitude** extending the same πιστεύειν to future non-witnesses. Thomas's confession becomes the paradigm of the faith Jesus pronounces blessed.

**Contrast with Revelation 19:10:** καὶ ἔπεσα ἔμπροσθεν τῶν ποδῶν αὐτοῦ προσκυνῆσαι αὐτῷ. καὶ λέγει μοι· ὅρα μή· σύνδουλός σού εἰμι … τῷ θεῷ προσκύνησον. ("And I fell at his feet to worship him. And he said unto me, See [thou do it] not: I am thy fellowservant … worship God.")

**Contrast with Revelation 22:9:** καὶ λέγει μοι· ὅρα μή· σύνδουλός σού εἰμι … τῷ θεῷ προσκύνησον.

In both Apocalypse scenes the angel issues an **aorist imperative of prohibition** ὅρα μή ("see [that you do] not"), explicitly disavows divine status (σύνδουλός σού εἰμι), and **redirects worship** via the imperative προσκύνησον to τῷ θεῷ. These are the Johannine corpus's canonical examples of a proper creaturely refusal of worship addressed to oneself.

**Grammatical force of the contrast:** John wrote both this Gospel and the Apocalypse (on traditional authorship) or at minimum comes from the same Johannine circle. The same author/school that documents angels refusing worship documents Jesus *not* refusing the articular-nominative address "the Lord of me and the God of me." The absence of the disavowal formula is not accidental silence; it is deliberate juxtaposition against a pattern the Johannine corpus elsewhere insists on. Jesus' acceptance constitutes an implicit grammatical assent: the syntax of non-rebuke matches the syntax of πιστεύειν-affirmation.

### 4. Formal Parallel with LXX Psalm 34:23 (MT 35:23)

**LXX Psa 34:23 (Rahlfs):** ἐξεγέρθητι, κύριε, καὶ πρόσχες τῇ κρίσει μου, ὁ θεός μου **καὶ ὁ κύριός μου**, εἰς τὴν δίκην μου.

**MT Psa 35:23:** הָעִירָה וְהָקִיצָה לְמִשְׁפָּטִי אֱלֹהַי וַאדֹנָי לְרִיבִי — literally "my God and my Lord" (`אֱלֹהַי וַאדֹנָי`).

**KJV Psa 35:23:** "Stir up thyself, and awake to my judgment, [even] unto my cause, my God and my Lord."

**The lexical-grammatical match:** The LXX phrase ὁ θεός μου καὶ ὁ κύριός μου and the Johannine phrase ὁ κύριός μου καὶ ὁ θεός μου contain the **same four lexical and grammatical elements** — definite article + κύριος + μου and definite article + θεός + μου, coordinated by καί — with the two noun-pairs simply reversed in order. In the LXX Psalm the addressee is YHWH (`אֱלֹהַי וַאדֹנָי`); in John 20:28 the addressee is the risen Jesus. The correspondence is formal, not accidental: the articular-nominative-plus-enclitic-μου address is the LXX's standard template for possessed divine-address, and Thomas's confession lands in it exactly.

**Closest NT-internal parallel:** Rev 4:11 ἄξιος εἶ, ὁ κύριος καὶ ὁ θεὸς ἡμῶν ("thou art worthy, O Lord and our God") — the same articular-nominative-for-vocative template, applied to God the Father on the throne. Jn 20:28 is the only unambiguous NT occurrence where a human addresses Jesus with the full LXX divine-address template.

**Hermeneutical gravity:** By speaking over the risen Jesus the articular form the LXX reserves for addressing YHWH, Thomas applies to Jesus the same grammatical slot the Septuagint reserves for Israel's God. This is not isolated lexical echo (κύριος alone can be polite "Sir"); it is a **template match** — article + κύριος + μου **καί** article + θεός + μου — that the LXX establishes as divine-address.

### 5. Climactic Placement in the Fourth Gospel's Inclusio

**The prologue-to-epilogue inclusio:** John's Gospel opens (1:1) with καὶ θεὸς ἦν ὁ λόγος ("and the Word was God") — the narrator's opening Christological thesis. It closes its main narrative (20:28) with a human confession using the same noun θεός, now in articular-vocative address, spoken directly *to* the incarnate risen Logos by a formerly doubting disciple. Prologue **thesis → disciple confession** bracket the entire Gospel.

**The confession ladder in John's Gospel (partial):**

| Reference | Speaker | Christological title applied to Jesus |
|---|---|---|
| Jn 1:29 | John the Baptist | ὁ ἀμνὸς τοῦ θεοῦ (Lamb of God) |
| Jn 1:41 | Andrew | Μεσσίαν … ὅ ἐστιν μεθερμηνευόμενον Χριστός |
| Jn 1:49 | Nathanael | ὁ υἱὸς τοῦ θεοῦ … βασιλεὺς … τοῦ Ἰσραήλ |
| Jn 4:42 | Samaritans | ὁ σωτὴρ τοῦ κόσμου |
| Jn 6:68–69 | Peter | ὁ ἅγιος τοῦ θεοῦ |
| Jn 9:38 | Healed blind man | κύριε … καὶ προσεκύνησεν αὐτῷ |
| Jn 11:27 | Martha | ὁ Χριστὸς ὁ υἱὸς τοῦ θεοῦ |
| **Jn 20:28** | **Thomas** | **ὁ κύριός μου καὶ ὁ θεός μου** |

Thomas's confession is the **highest rung** of this ladder — the only one that pairs κύριος with θεός under full articular-vocative morphology, and the only one that picks up the prologue's θεός-predicate in first-person possessed address.

**Purpose-statement placement:** Immediately after Thomas's confession John states the Gospel's purpose (20:30–31): ταῦτα δὲ γέγραπται ἵνα πιστεύ[σ]ητε ὅτι Ἰησοῦς ἐστιν ὁ χριστὸς ὁ υἱὸς τοῦ θεοῦ, καὶ ἵνα πιστεύοντες ζωὴν ἔχητε ἐν τῷ ὀνόματι αὐτοῦ. The confession is the narrative goal toward which all the σημεῖα (signs) have been building: "these things are written **so that** you might believe…" The grammar reinforces climax — ἵνα + subjunctive purpose clause naming the exact πιστεύειν verb Jesus has just commended Thomas for (20:29 πεπίστευκας).

**Narrative emphasis:** Thomas is the previously-doubting disciple (20:25: ἐὰν μὴ ἴδω … οὐ μὴ πιστεύσω — double negation intensifying refusal). Placing the Gospel's highest confession in his mouth makes the point that the resurrection evidence turns even committed skepticism into unqualified address of Jesus as θεός. The rhetorical structure is: doubter → sees → confesses the articular-nominative divine formula → is commended by Jesus → Gospel declares its purpose fulfilled.

## Clause Structure

- **Main narrative frame:** ἀπεκρίθη Θωμᾶς καὶ εἶπεν αὐτῷ — two aorist indicatives coordinated (ἀπεκρίθη aorist middle-deponent of ἀποκρίνομαι; εἶπεν 2nd aorist of λέγω, suppletive), standard Johannine speech-introduction doublet. αὐτῷ dative of indirect object fixes the risen Jesus as the speech recipient.
- **Quoted speech (verbless vocative-style address):** Ὁ Κύριός μου καὶ ὁ Θεός μου — two coordinate nominal phrases, no finite verb, no copula. The absence of a copula is normal for Koine vocative-style address; it does not require supply of "You are" (though such a supply is exegetically harmless).
- **Jesus' reply (v. 29):** λέγει αὐτῷ ὁ Ἰησοῦς (historical present λέγει, standard Johannine discourse marker) introduces a bipartite statement — (i) ὅτι-clause with perfect active πεπίστευκας (state of belief), (ii) beatitude with two substantive aorist participles (ἰδόντες "having seen," πιστεύσαντες "having believed").

### Classification

- **Grammar:** articular nominative functioning as vocative-of-address; two-fold Semitic-parallelism coordinated by καί; absence-of-rebuke as grammatical-rhetorical signal; LXX-template divine-address match.
- **Christology:** Gospel-climactic confession; prologue/confession inclusio (Jn 1:1 ↔ 20:28); highest rung of Johannine title-ladder; grammatical precondition of the purpose statement (20:30–31).
- **Verdict on "exclamation" reading:** ruled out by (i) dative addressee αὐτῷ, (ii) first-person possessive μου incompatible with Koine oath-formulae, (iii) Jesus' πιστεύειν-classification of the utterance in v. 29, (iv) the fixed Johannine contrast-pattern where misdirected worship is explicitly refused (Rev 19:10; 22:9) and this is not.
