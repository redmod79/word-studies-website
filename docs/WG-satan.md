# WG: Satan / Adversary / Accuser

## Overview
The adversary/accuser concept spans both Testaments through five related words — one Hebrew noun, its root verb, and three Greek terms. The Hebrew śāṭān (used both as a common noun for earthly opponents and as the title of a specific celestial accuser) is rendered in the LXX primarily as diábolos, and carried into the NT through both that Greek word and the Aramaic loanword Satanâs.

## Relationships

### Shared Roots
- **H7854 and H7853** — noun and root verb from the same stem שׂטן (śṭn): to oppose, accuse
- **G4567** — Aramaic/Chaldee equivalent of H7854; same word-form borrowed into Greek as a proper name
- **G1228** — derived from διαβάλλω (diaballō): to throw across, accuse/slander; conceptually parallel to H7853 (to accuse)

### LXX Bridge: H7854 → G1228
The LXX translates H7854 as διάβολος in 14 of its 23 verses (co-occ 14, PMI 9.82). This is the primary cross-testament bridge. The prosecutorial meaning of the Hebrew (an accuser who brings charges before God) is preserved in the Greek ("one who slanders/traduces"). In the NT, diábolos and Satanâs appear as interchangeable titles (Rev 12:9; 20:2).

## Roles in the Concept

- **Action:** H7853 śāṭan (verb) — the act of opposing, accusing
- **Title/role:** H7854 śāṭân (noun) — the one who performs that role; "the adversary"
- **LXX rendition:** G1228 diábolos — "the slanderer/accuser" (capturing the prosecutorial sense)
- **NT proper name:** G4567 Satanâs — the Aramaic name carried into Greek; used especially in the Gospels and Revelation
- **Legal framing:** G476 antídikos — the formal legal opponent; used to describe Satan's posture toward believers (1 Pet 5:8)
