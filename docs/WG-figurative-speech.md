# WG: Figurative Speech (Parable / Proverb / Riddle / Taunt)

## Overview

The biblical vocabulary for figurative, oblique, wisdom-laden speech clusters around the Hebrew root מָשַׁל (mashal) and its Greek LXX/NT equivalent παραβολή (parabole). The group covers a continuum from short pithy proverbs, through riddles and allegories, to taunt-songs and prophetic oracles — all forms of speech that communicate indirectly and require interpretation. The Septuagint consistently collapses the Hebrew diversity (mashal, chidah, shninah) into the single Greek term παραβολή, establishing the semantic range that Jesus' "parables" inherit in the NT.

- **H4912 mashal (noun) ↔ H4911 mashal (verb)** — cognate pair; the verb is denominative from (or parallel to) the noun. The verb describes the act; the noun the product.

The LXX's uniform use of παραβολή across all four Hebrew words means the NT παραβολή inherits the full Hebrew semantic range — not just "comparison" (classical Greek sense) but proverb, allegory, riddle, taunt, and oracle combined.

## Roles in the Concept

- **The saying (product):** mashal (H4912) — any unit of figurative speech, long or short
- **The act (process):** mashal-vb (H4911) — uttering such speech; "taking up" a mashal
- **The puzzle-mode:** chidah (H2420) — the hidden/riddle aspect needing solution
- **The sharp-mode:** shninah (H8148) — the cutting/derisive aspect (when the mashal is a taunt)
- **The Greek umbrella:** parabole (G3850) — inherits and carries all the above into NT
- **The Greek alt:** paroimia (G3942) — foregrounds the popular-saying / wayside-proverb nuance
