# H5162 — נָחַם (nâcham)

## Core Data
- **Strong's:** H5162
- **Original:** נָחַם
- **Transliteration:** nâcham
- **Pronunciation:** naw-kham'
- **Part of Speech:** Verb (primitive root)
- **Root Sense:** to sigh, breathe strongly — from which derive the twin ideas of "being moved with sorrow" and "consoling another by sighing with them"
- **Occurrences:** 108 verb forms in OT (122 tokens in 100 unique verses; ETCBC parse count 108 excludes the homograph proper name Menahem and the noun H5164 *nocham*)
- **First Occurrence:** Gen 5:29 — "And he called his name Noah, saying, This [same] shall **comfort** us (Piel) concerning our work and toil of our hands"
- **Hapax Legomenon:** No

## Lexicon Definition

A primitive root; properly, to sigh, i.e. breathe strongly; by implication, to be sorry, i.e. (in a favorable sense) to pity, console or (reflexively) rue; or (unfavorably) to avenge (oneself). KJV: comfort (self), ease (one's self), repent(-er, -ing, self).

The single lexical root spans two semantic poles that the Biblical Hebrew verb system keeps separate by **stem**: the active/causative stems (Piel, Pual) consistently render "comfort, console," while the middle/reflexive stems (Niphal, Hithpael) consistently render "be sorry, relent, regret, repent." This stem-based cleavage is the single most important grammatical feature of H5162.

**Key grammatical rule:** The Niphal and Piel are **not** different inflections of the same meaning — they are lexically distinct verbs built on one root. When God is the subject and the translation is "repent/relent/be grieved," the form is virtually always **Niphal** (e.g., Gen 6:6; Ex 32:14; 1 Sam 15:11, 35; Jer 18:8, 10; Amos 7:3, 6; Jon 3:10). When the subject comforts another person or Zion, the form is **Piel** (e.g., Isa 40:1; 49:13; 51:3; 66:13).

## Semantic Categories

### 1. Divine Relenting from Announced Judgment (Niphal, subject = YHWH)
The most theologically charged usage. YHWH, upon repentance or intercession, "relents" from threatened evil.
- **Ex 32:14** — "And the LORD **repented** of the evil which he thought to do unto his people." (after Moses' intercession)
- **Jer 18:8** — "If that nation… turn from their evil, I will **repent** of the evil that I thought to do unto them."
- **Amos 7:3, 6** — "The LORD **repented** for this: It shall not be."
- **Jon 3:10** — "God saw their works… and God **repented** of the evil."

### 2. Divine Relenting Withheld / Divine Constancy (Niphal, negated)
In covenant-lawsuit contexts the same verb is negated to assert God's fixed purpose.
- **Num 23:19** — "God is not a man… that he should **repent**." (Balaam)
- **1 Sam 15:29** — "The Strength of Israel will not lie nor **repent**: for he is not a man, that he should **repent**." (paired with a Niphal statement three verses earlier, v. 11 — "It **repenteth** me that I have set up Saul")
- **Ps 110:4** — "The LORD hath sworn, and will not **repent**, Thou art a priest for ever."
- **Jer 4:28; 20:16** — "I have purposed it, and will not **repent**."
- **Ezek 24:14** — "I will not go back, neither will I spare, neither will I **repent**."

### 3. Divine Grief / Regret Over Creation or Choice (Niphal, subject = YHWH)
- **Gen 6:6–7** — "It **repented** the LORD that he had made man… it grieved him at his heart… I **repent** that I have made them."
- **1 Sam 15:11, 35** — "It **repenteth** me that I have set up Saul." / "The LORD **repented** that he had made Saul king."

### 4. Human Self-Turning / Regret (Niphal, Hithpael)
Rarer than the divine sense, but present.
- **Jer 8:6** — "No man **repented** him of his wickedness, saying, What have I done?"
- **Jer 31:19** — "After that I was turned, I **repented**; and after that I was instructed, I smote upon my thigh."
- **Job 42:6** — "Wherefore I abhor myself, and **repent** in dust and ashes." (Niphal)

### 5. Consoling the Bereaved (Piel)
The paradigmatic human-to-human use: the comforter sits with mourners.
- **Gen 37:35** — "All his sons and all his daughters rose up to **comfort him**; but he refused to be **comforted**."
- **Job 2:11** — Job's three friends came "to mourn with him and to **comfort** him."
- **Job 16:2** — "Miserable **comforters** are ye all."
- **1 Chr 7:22; 19:2–3; 2 Sam 10:2** — comforting after a father's death.
- **Lam 1:2, 9, 16, 17, 21; 2:13** — the refrain "she hath none to **comfort** her" (all Piel participles).

### 6. Divine Comfort of Zion / the Afflicted (Piel, subject = YHWH)
The Isaianic signature, especially in chs. 40–66.
- **Isa 40:1** — "**Comfort ye**, **comfort ye** my people, saith your God." (double Piel imperative)
- **Isa 49:13** — "The LORD hath **comforted** his people."
- **Isa 51:3** — "The LORD shall **comfort** Zion: he will **comfort** all her waste places."
- **Isa 51:12** — "I, even I, am he that **comforteth** you."
- **Isa 66:13** — "As one whom his mother **comforteth**, so will I **comfort** you."
- **Ps 23:4** — "Thy rod and thy staff, they **comfort** me."
- **Ps 119:76, 82** — "Let… thy merciful kindness… be for my **comfort**."

### 7. Vengeance / Self-Ease as Relief (Niphal, rare, context-dependent)
The root's "sigh" nuance can shade into releasing pent-up wrath by executing it.
- **Isa 1:24** — "I will **ease me** of mine adversaries, and avenge me of mine enemies." (Niphal Impf 1cs)
- **Ezek 5:13** — "I will be **comforted**: and they shall know that I the LORD have spoken it in my zeal." (Hithpael — "I will take satisfaction")
- **Ezek 31:16; 32:31** — Pharaoh and the nations "**are comforted**" in Sheol by seeing others share their fate (grim satisfaction).
