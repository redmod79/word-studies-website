## Lexicon Definition

or נְפִל nᵉphil; from H5307 (nâphal); properly, a feller, i.e. a bully or tyrant:—giant.

*Gesenius: the root nâphal (to fall) yields nᵉphîyl as "one who falls upon others" — a violent attacker or one who causes others to fall. English "Nephilim" is the Hebrew plural נְפִילִים (nᵉphîylîym).*

## Semantic Categories

### 1. Antediluvian Giants (Pre-Flood)
- **Gen 6:4** — "There were giants in the earth in those days; and also after that, when the sons of God came in unto the daughters of men, and they bare children to them, the same became mighty men which were of old, men of renown."

The phrase "and also after that" signals the text anticipates a continuation of the nᵉphîyl beyond the flood era. The Nephilim are placed in the same context as the union between "sons of God" (H430 בְּנֵי הָאֱלֹהִים) and human women, though the syntax does not definitively equate them with the offspring.

### 2. Canaanite Giants at Kadesh (Post-Flood Memory)
- **Num 13:33** — "And there we saw the giants (nᵉphîyl), the sons of Anak, which come of the giants (nᵉphîyl): and we were in our own sight as grasshoppers (châgâb), and so we were in their sight."

The word appears twice in this verse — first identifying what the spies saw, then in the relative clause "which come of the giants." The Anakim (descendants of Anak) are here explicitly linked to the Nephilim. This report is part of the Israelite scouts' fearful account that triggered the forty-year wilderness wandering; whether this is literal genealogy or rhetorical exaggeration cannot be determined from the word itself.
