## 3. Clause Structure

```text
v.5  Καὶ ἤκουσα
       τοῦ ἀγγέλου τῶν ὑδάτων λέγοντος
         Δίκαιος εἶ,
         ὁ ὢν καὶ ὁ ἦν,
         ὁ Ὅσιος,
         ὅτι ταῦτα ἔκρινας,

v.6    ὅτι αἷμα ἁγίων καὶ προφητῶν ἐξέχεαν,
       καὶ αἷμα αὐτοῖς δέδωκας πεῖν·
       ἄξιοί εἰσιν.

v.7  Καὶ ἤκουσα
       τοῦ θυσιαστηρίου λέγοντος
         Ναί,
         Κύριε ὁ Θεός ὁ Παντοκράτωρ,
         ἀληθιναὶ καὶ δίκαιαι αἱ κρίσεις σου.
```

**Structural summary.**

- The two outer clauses are auditory vision formulas: `Καὶ ἤκουσα ... λέγοντος` in v. 5 and v. 7. The seer hears a speaking source; the present participle `λέγοντος` introduces the speech content.
- Verse 5 gives the declaration of the angel of the waters: God's righteousness is asserted by nominative predicate adjective + `εἰμί`, then grounded by `ὅτι ταῦτα ἔκρινας`.
- Verse 6 gives the reason inside the speech: blood was shed, blood was given to drink, and the recipients are worthy/deserving.
- Verse 7 gives a second confirming voice associated with the altar: `Ναί` ratifies the preceding declaration, then the altar-speech names God vocatively and declares the judgments true and righteous.

No Greek parser `--similar` matches were found for Rev 16:5 or Rev 16:7 at similarity >= 0.6, so the analysis rests on the direct passage morphology rather than on a retrieved parallel-pattern set.

## 4. Key Grammatical Features

### 1. `τοῦ ἀγγέλου τῶν ὑδάτων λέγοντος` — heard genitive source plus speech-introducing participle

**Morphology.**

- `ἤκουσα` — aorist active indicative 1s of ἀκούω: the seer reports a bounded hearing event.
- `τοῦ ἀγγέλου` — genitive singular masculine: the heard speaker/source.
- `τῶν ὑδάτων` — genitive plural neuter dependent on `ἀγγέλου`: "of the waters."
- `λέγοντος` — present active participle genitive singular masculine, agreeing with `ἀγγέλου`.

**Function.** The construction is not simply "I heard sound"; it identifies a personal speaking source and then gives the content of the speech. The genitive `τῶν ὑδάτων` grammatically marks the angel by relation to the waters. The grammar itself does not decide whether that relation is jurisdiction, assignment, domain, or possession; in the bowl context, the "angel of the waters" is the appropriate speaker over the waters-turned-blood judgment.

**Sanctuary-service relevance.** The waters are not a disconnected nature-image in the grammar. The angel's domain is encoded in the noun phrase before the judgment declaration begins. The one attached to the affected element (`τῶν ὑδάτων`) declares the judgment righteous, creating a syntactic link between plague-domain and verdict.

### 2. `Δίκαιος εἶ ... ὅτι ταῦτα ἔκρινας` — righteous identity grounded in a completed judgment act

**Morphology.**

- `Δίκαιος` — nominative singular masculine predicate adjective.
- `εἶ` — present active indicative 2s of εἰμί.
- `ὁ ὢν καὶ ὁ ἦν` — articular present participle ("the One being") coordinated with articular imperfect indicative ("the One who was").
- `ὁ Ὅσιος` — nominative singular masculine adjective used substantivally/appositionally: "the Holy One."
- `ὅτι` — causal conjunction here: "because."
- `ταῦτα` — accusative plural neuter demonstrative, object or adverbial-object frame: "these things / thus."
- `ἔκρινας` — aorist active indicative 2s of κρίνω: "you judged."

**Function.** The declaration starts with present-stative identity (`Δίκαιος εἶ`) and grounds that declaration in a bounded act (`ἔκρινας`). The present `εἶ` states what God is; the aorist `ἔκρινας` states what God has done in the judgment event. The grammar therefore binds divine character and judicial action without collapsing them: the action manifests the character.

**Text-base caution.** The parser text reads `ὁ Ὅσιος`; the KJV has "O Lord ... and shalt be." The grammar table follows the parser reading. In this text form, the appositional title is holiness (`ὁ Ὅσιος`), not a future-existence clause.

### 3. `αἷμα ... ἐξέχεαν, καὶ αἷμα ... δέδωκας πεῖν` — blood recompense by matched accusatives

**Morphology.**

- First `αἷμα` — accusative singular neuter, direct object of `ἐξέχεαν`.
- `ἁγίων καὶ προφητῶν` — coordinated genitive plural modifiers: "of saints and prophets."
- `ἐξέχεαν` — aorist active indicative 3p of ἐκχέω: "they poured out / shed."
- Second `αἷμα` — accusative singular neuter, direct object of `δέδωκας`.
- `αὐτοῖς` — dative plural masculine, recipient/indirect object: "to them."
- `δέδωκας` — perfect active indicative 2s of δίδωμι.
- `πεῖν` — second aorist active infinitive of πίνω, complementary/result infinitive: "to drink."

**Function.** The verse is built on a recompense pair:

| Human act | Divine recompense |
|-----------|-------------------|
| `αἷμα ... ἐξέχεαν` | `αἷμα αὐτοῖς δέδωκας πεῖν` |
| "they shed blood" | "you have given them blood to drink" |

The repeated fronted `αἷμα` makes blood the lexical hinge of the clause. The aorist `ἐξέχεαν` views the persecutors' act as a bounded deed; the perfect `δέδωκας` presents the divine giving as a completed act with standing result. The infinitive `πεῖν` specifies the purpose/result of the giving: the blood is not merely assigned to them; it is given them to drink.

**Sanctuary-service relevance.** The grammar frames the plague as measured recompense, not arbitrary violence: object mirrors object (`αἷμα` / `αἷμα`), and recipient dative (`αὐτοῖς`) identifies the ones who receive back the blood measure.

### 4. `ἄξιοί εἰσιν` — predicate adjective declaring deservedness

**Morphology.**

- `ἄξιοί` — nominative plural masculine adjective.
- `εἰσιν` — present active indicative 3p of εἰμί.

**Function.** The clause gives the judicial rationale in compact predicate-adjective form: "they are worthy/deserving." The masculine plural subject is implicit from the preceding `ἐξέχεαν` and `αὐτοῖς`; the same group who shed the blood receives the blood-to-drink judgment. The grammar therefore closes v. 6 with a present-tense evaluation of the recipients' fitness for the recompense.

**Important limit.** `ἄξιοί εἰσιν` does not state that the persecutors are morally worthy in a positive sense. In this context, `ἄξιος` marks correspondence/deservedness: the recompense fits the act.

**Grammar reference:** [tense-present-active-indicative](../greek/tense-present-active-indicative.md)

### 5. `τοῦ θυσιαστηρίου λέγοντος` — altar-associated speech and masculine sense-concord

**Morphology.**

- `τοῦ θυσιαστηρίου` — genitive singular neuter: "of the altar."
- `λέγοντος` — present active participle genitive singular masculine.

**Problem.** Strict form-concord would expect a neuter participle if `λέγοντος` agreed directly with `θυσιαστηρίου`. Instead the parser has neuter noun + masculine participle. This creates a live grammatical issue:

1. The participle may reflect **constructio ad sensum**: the altar is not treated as a mere object but as a personal or person-associated speech source.
2. The KJV's "another out of the altar" reflects a text-base where an explicit masculine speaker is present; that would mechanically explain the masculine participle.
3. In the parser text as given, the masculine participle preserves personal agency even though the visible source is the altar.

**Sanctuary-service relevance.** This is the most important sanctuary grammar in the unit. The altar is not an inert prop in v. 7. The grammar makes the altar, or a masculine speaker associated with the altar, a confirming witness to the blood-recompense judgment. That fits the altar's canonical association with sacrificial blood and the blood of martyrs crying for judgment (cf. Rev 6:9-10 conceptually; this entry is only marking the grammar).

### 6. `ἀληθιναὶ καὶ δίκαιαι αἱ κρίσεις σου` — verbless predicate adjectives before the subject

**Morphology.**

- `ἀληθιναὶ` — nominative plural feminine adjective.
- `δίκαιαι` — nominative plural feminine adjective.
- `αἱ κρίσεις` — article + nominative plural feminine noun, subject: "the judgments."
- `σου` — genitive singular pronoun: "your."

**Function.** The clause is verbless/equative: "true and righteous [are] your judgments." The predicate adjectives precede the articular subject, giving the qualities front position. The KJV's bracketed `[are]` correctly supplies the implied copula in English.

**Relation to v. 5.** The adjective `δίκαιος` in v. 5 describes God personally (`Δίκαιος εἶ`); the adjective `δίκαιαι` in v. 7 describes God's judgments (`αἱ κρίσεις σου`). The passage therefore forms a grammatical righteousness arc:

| Verse | Subject | Predicate |
|-------|---------|-----------|
| v. 5 | God addressed directly | `Δίκαιος` |
| v. 7 | God's judgments | `ἀληθιναὶ καὶ δίκαιαι` |

The altar speech ratifies the angel's declaration by applying the same righteousness vocabulary to the judgments themselves and pairing it with `ἀληθινός` ("true, genuine, reliable").

## 6. What the Grammar Constrains

- The speaker in v. 5 is grammatically personal: `τοῦ ἀγγέλου ... λέγοντος`.
- The angel is genitively marked by relation to the waters: `τῶν ὑδάτων`.
- God's righteousness is asserted as present identity (`Δίκαιος εἶ`) and grounded in a completed judgment (`ἔκρινας`).
- Blood recompense is syntactically exact: `αἷμα` shed by the persecutors corresponds to `αἷμα` given by God to those same recipients.
- The perfect `δέδωκας` is stronger than a simple aorist report; the giving is a completed act with an abiding judicial result.
- The altar speech is grammatically weighty because `θυσιαστηρίου` is neuter but `λέγοντος` is masculine; either the text presupposes a personal speaker associated with the altar, or the altar is personified / treated according to sense.
- The final declaration is a verbless predicate-adjective clause: `ἀληθιναὶ καὶ δίκαιαι` are fronted as the qualities of `αἱ κρίσεις σου`.

## 7. What the Grammar Does Not Decide

- Whether "angel of the waters" should be identified with a particular angelic office beyond the phrase's genitive domain relation.
- Whether the altar in v. 7 is itself personified, or whether the masculine participle reflects an omitted/variant personal speaker. The parser/KJV mismatch must be handled text-critically before that question is closed.
- How the bowl-plague sequence maps chronologically. The grammar marks declaration, recompense, and altar ratification; it does not assign a historical date.
- Whether `ταῦτα` in v. 5 should be rendered "these things" or adverbially "thus." Both are compatible with the accusative demonstrative in context.
