**Semantic note:** The LXX translators favored διαφθείρω — a compound of διά (through, thoroughly) + φθείρω (decay, spoil) — because it captures both the processual nature of שָׁחַת (decay as an ongoing condition, Gen 6:11) and its causative force (causing to be ruined, Jer 4:7). This is a stronger word than simple ἀπόλλυμι; it connotes rottenness from within.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same:**
- Both Hebrew and Greek use the word for a process that can be moral (inner corruption), physical (bodily decay, marred objects), or eschatological (destruction of nations, judgment)
- The participial/agentive use ("destroyer", "those who corrupt") is present in both testaments
- The divine restraint motif (God choosing *not* to destroy) is explicit in OT (Ps 78:38, 2 Kgs 8:19) and implicit in NT (God is the one who destroys the destroyers, Rev 11:18)

**What shifts:**
- Hebrew shâchath dominates in historical narrative and prophetic address (Hiphil: causing ruin); the NT picks up mostly the Niphal/passive sense (things/people *becoming* corrupt or *being* destroyed)
- The NT uses διαφθείρω for both physical decay (outward man perishing, 2 Cor 4:16) and the final eschatological act (Rev 11:18), giving the word a full arc from creation to consummation
- Rev 11:18 creates an explicit Flood echo: as the earth was "corrupted" before Noah's flood (Gen 6:11), so the earth-destroyers are themselves destroyed at the end — the same word bracketing both judgments
