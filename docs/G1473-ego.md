## Lexicon Definition

A primary pronoun of the first person, "I." Thayer (summary): the nominative ἐγώ, when joined to a finite verb, generally has force and emphasis, or indicates antithesis (as Mat 3:11; Mar 1:8; Luk 3:16). Oblique cases: genitive ἐμοῦ / enclitic μοῦ; dative ἐμοί / enclitic μοί; accusative ἐμέ / enclitic μέ; plural ἡμεῖς. The full (non-enclitic) forms ἐμοῦ, ἐμοί, ἐμέ are used for emphasis or antithesis. ἰδοὺ ἐγώ corresponds to Hebrew הִנֵּנִי ("behold, here am I," Acts 9:10; 1 Sam 3:8). ἐγώ by itself renders Hebrew אֲנִי as the self-identification "I [am]" (Jhn 1:23; Act 7:32).

**Grammatical note:** Greek verbs encode person in the ending, so the subject pronoun is not required. When ἐγώ is expressed alongside a finite first-person verb, it is pragmatically marked — signaling emphasis, contrast, or solemn self-identification. This makes every occurrence load-bearing for discourse analysis.

## Semantic Categories

### 1. Solemn Self-Identification ("ἐγώ εἰμι" — "I am")
The pronoun + copula εἰμί (G1510) forms the distinctive Johannine "I am" construction. 89 verses NT-wide have both G1473 and G1510; 41 are in John alone. Renders Hebrew אֲנִי הוּא / אֲנִי יהוה when used absolutely. Also used for ordinary predicate self-identification.
- **Jhn 8:58** — "Before Abraham was, I am (ἐγώ εἰμι)"
- **Jhn 8:24** — "if ye believe not that I am (ἐγώ εἰμι) [he], ye shall die in your sins"
- **Jhn 18:5–6** — "I am (ἐγώ εἰμι) [he]. ... they went backward, and fell to the ground"
- **Rev 1:8** — "I am (ἐγώ εἰμι) Alpha and Omega"

### 2. Predicated Self-Disclosure (the metaphorical "I am" sayings)
ἐγώ εἰμι followed by a predicate nominative — Jesus defining his role/identity.
- **Jhn 6:35** — "I (ἐγώ) am the bread of life"
- **Jhn 8:12** — "I (ἐγώ) am the light of the world"
- **Jhn 10:11** — "I (ἐγώ) am the good shepherd"
- **Jhn 11:25** — "I (ἐγώ) am the resurrection, and the life"
- **Jhn 14:6** — "I (ἐγώ) am the way, the truth, and the life"
- **Jhn 15:1** — "I (ἐγώ) am the true vine"

### 3. Emphatic Contrast with Another Subject
The pronoun draws a sharp antithesis between speakers or between the speaker and a different party. Thayer lists this as the primary emphatic use.
- **Mat 3:11** — "I (ἐγώ) indeed baptize you with water... but he that cometh after me..."
- **Mar 1:8** — "I (ἐγώ) indeed have baptized you with water: but he shall baptize you with the Holy Ghost"
- **Mat 5:22** — "but I (ἐγώ) say unto you" (contrasting Jesus' teaching with prior tradition; cf. 5:28, 5:39, and throughout Sermon on the Mount)
- **Gal 2:20** — "I (ἐγώ) am crucified with Christ: nevertheless I live; yet not I (ἐγώ), but Christ liveth in me"

### 4. Apostolic / Authoritative Self-Reference
Writers (esp. Paul) assert identity, authority, or personal stake in a statement.
- **Phm 1:19** — "I (ἐγώ) Paul have written [it] with mine own hand, I will repay [it]"
- **1Co 1:12** — "every one of you saith, I (ἐγώ) am of Paul; and I (ἐγώ) of Apollos"
- **Rev 1:9** — "I (ἐγώ) John, who also am your brother"
- **Eph 3:1** — "For this cause I (ἐγώ) Paul, the prisoner of Jesus Christ"

### 5. Inner Conflict / Ethical "I"
The existential "I" of Romans 7 — the self as moral agent wrestling with sin and Torah.
- **Rom 7:9** — "For I (ἐγώ) was alive without the law once"
- **Rom 7:14** — "but I (ἐγώ) am carnal, sold under sin"
- **Rom 7:17** — "Now then it is no more I (ἐγώ) that do it, but sin that dwelleth in me"
- **Rom 7:25** — "with the mind I (ἐγώ) myself serve the law of God"

### 6. Divine "I" in Old Testament Citations
The pronoun renders the divine אָנֹכִי / אֲנִי of Yahweh when the NT quotes the OT.
- **Heb 1:5** (citing Ps 2:7) — "Thou art my Son, this day have I (ἐγώ) begotten thee"
- **Act 7:32** (citing Exo 3:6) — "I (ἐγώ) [am] the God of thy fathers"
- **2Co 6:16** (citing Lev 26:12 / Eze 37:27) — implicit in "I (ἐγώ) will dwell in them"
- **Heb 10:30** (citing Deut 32:35) — "Vengeance [belongeth] unto me (ἐγώ)"

### 7. Interrogative / Relational Formula τί ἐμοὶ καὶ σοί
Literally "what to me and to thee?" — a Semitism (Heb. מַה־לִּי וָלָךְ) meaning "what have I to do with thee?"
- **Jhn 2:4** — "Woman, what have I (ἐμοί) to do with thee?"
- **Mar 5:7** / **Luk 8:28** — demoniac to Jesus: "What have I (ἐμοί) to do with thee?"
- **Mat 8:29** — "What have we to do with thee, Jesus?" (plural form)
