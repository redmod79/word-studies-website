# G646 — ἀποστασία (apostasía)

## Core Data
- **Strong's:** G646
- **Original:** ἀποστασία
- **Transliteration:** apostasía
- **Pronunciation:** ap-os-tas-ee'-ah
- **Part of Speech:** Feminine noun
- **Root:** From G647 (ἀποστάσιον, *apostasion*, "a writing of divorcement"), itself from the middle of G868 (ἀφίστημι, *aphístēmi*, "to stand off from, withdraw, depart, fall away"); compound of ἀπό ("away from") + ἵστημι ("to stand"). Cognate verbal noun formed with the -σία suffix denoting the state or act of the verbal idea.
- **Occurrences (NT):** 2 unique verses (2 tokens), 2 books
- **First NT Occurrence:** Acts 21:21 — "that thou teachest all the Jews which are among the Gentiles to forsake Moses"
- **Hapax Legomenon:** No (dis legomenon in the NT — only two occurrences)

## Lexicon Definition

Feminine of the same as G647; defection from truth (properly, the state), i.e., (abstractly) apostasy, departure, defection, revolt. The noun denotes not a single act of defection but the condition or event of standing apart from a prior allegiance — religious, political, or covenantal. Both NT uses preserve this core of "abandonment of a previously-held standing position" (Torah-observance in Acts 21:21; obedience to God in 2 Thess 2:3).

Morphologically, ἀποστασία is the nominalized counterpart to ἀφίστημι (G868, the verb "to depart, fall away"). Greek has a parallel form ἀποστάσιον (G647) used for a "certificate of divorce" (Matt 5:31; 19:7; Mark 10:4) — the legal document formalizing a standing-apart. ἀποστασία carries the same underlying image (formal separation from a binding relationship) applied to allegiance rather than marriage.

## Semantic Categories

Given only two NT tokens, the "categories" here are the two distinct applications rather than a semantic range within a populated word field.

### 1. Forsaking a Religious/Covenantal Standard
- **Acts 21:21** — "thou teachest all the Jews which are among the Gentiles **to forsake** (ἀποστασίαν) Moses, saying that they ought not to circumcise their children, neither to walk after the customs."
  - Object of the defection: Moses / Mosaic custom (circumcision, τὰ ἔθη).
  - Speaker's stance: the report is hostile — James and the Jerusalem elders are relaying an accusation against Paul. The word is used to frame a perceived abandonment of ancestral religion.
  - Grammatical form: accusative singular (ἀποστασίαν), without article, as the accusative of content governed by διδάσκεις ("you teach apostasy from Moses").

### 2. An Eschatological Event Preceding the Man of Lawlessness
- **2 Thess 2:3** — "Let no man deceive you by any means: for that day shall not come, except there come **a falling away** (ἡ ἀποστασία) first, and that man of sin be revealed, the son of perdition."
  - Object of the defection: unspecified in the verse itself; the wider context is "the day of the Lord" (v. 2) and the coming of Christ (v. 1, 8).
  - Grammatical form: nominative singular with the definite article (ἡ ἀποστασία).
  - Paired temporally and structurally with the revelation (ἀποκαλυφθῇ, v. 3, 6, 8) of ὁ ἄνθρωπος τῆς ἁμαρτίας / ὁ ἄνθρωπος τῆς ἀνομίας.

## The Definite Article in 2 Thessalonians 2:3

The article ἡ in ἡ ἀποστασία is grammatically significant and is the single most-debated feature of the word's NT usage.

**Article observations (text-level, context-neutral):**

1. **Contrast with Acts 21:21.** Acts 21:21 uses ἀποστασίαν anarthrously as the content of a charge ("teaching apostasy from Moses"). 2 Thess 2:3 uses the articular form ἡ ἀποστασία as a standalone subject: "the apostasy." The article individuates the noun as a definite, identifiable entity rather than the generic abstract concept.
2. **Greek article with abstract nouns.** Greek regularly uses the article with abstract nouns without individuating them (e.g., ἡ ἀγάπη = "love" generically). However, when an abstract noun is the subject of a future verb of arrival and is coordinated with a second articular subject that is clearly a specific individuated referent, the article tends to function as particular rather than generic.
3. **Coordination with ὁ ἄνθρωπος τῆς ἀνομίας.** ἡ ἀποστασία stands in grammatical coordination with ὁ ἄνθρωπος τῆς ἀνομίας via the conjoined ἐὰν μὴ…καί structure ("except the apostasy come first, and the man of lawlessness be revealed"). The man of lawlessness is a specific individuated figure. The coordinated subject would normally share that referential specificity.
4. **Anaphoric possibility.** The article can be anaphoric ("the apostasy [you already know about]"). 2 Thess 2:5 says "Remember ye not, that, when I was yet with you, I told you these things?" — indicating prior oral teaching the Thessalonians possessed but readers today do not. The article may point to content previously delivered.
5. **Monadic/well-known possibility.** The article can mark a noun as monadic or well-known ("the [familiar/expected] apostasy") — a recognized eschatological motif rather than a first-mention introduction.

**What the article does NOT decide (context-neutral):**
The article ἡ by itself does not resolve whether the referent is:
(a) a specific future, climactic, eschatologically-unique apostasy event;
(b) a particular apostasy known to Paul's readers from prior teaching but not climactic-unique;
(c) the generic/abstract act of apostasy as a category marker.

The article establishes definiteness; whether that definiteness is monadic-climactic, anaphoric-known, or generic-abstract must be determined from (i) the immediate literary context (coordinated subjects, temporal markers), (ii) Paul's eschatological teaching elsewhere, and (iii) the OT/Second Temple background of eschatological rebellion motifs. Grammar alone permits all three readings.

**Pairing with "the revelation of the man of lawlessness":**
The syntax ἐὰν μὴ ἔλθῃ ἡ ἀποστασία πρῶτον καὶ ἀποκαλυφθῇ ὁ ἄνθρωπος τῆς ἀνομίας links two subjunctive clauses under a single negated condition. "First" (πρῶτον) attaches to the apostasy's coming; the revelation of the lawless one follows. Whether "first" means (a) "the apostasy precedes the lawless one's revelation as a distinct prior phase" or (b) "both the apostasy and the revelation belong together as the one complex precursor, with the apostasy listed first" is a syntactical question the article itself cannot settle.

**Pattern of the LXX field:** ἀποστασία in the LXX translates three overlapping Hebrew semantic zones:
1. **Covenantal treachery** (מַעַל) — unfaithfulness where allegiance was owed. This is the dominant register.
2. **Backsliding/turning** (מְשׁוּבָה) — habitual drift from YHWH. Jer 2:19 "thy backslidings shall reprove thee" connects the word to Israel's corporate unfaithfulness pattern.
3. **Active rebellion** (מֶרֶד, מְרִי) — overt insurrection.

The 1 Maccabees usage is critical background. 1 Macc 2:15 uses τὴν ἀποστασίαν of the Antiochene persecution — a specific, named, historically-climactic defection from Torah-obedience enforced by a foreign ruler. This establishes a Second Temple usage pattern where ἡ ἀποστασία could refer to a specific, recognized, covenant-breaking historical-prophetic event.

## Contextual Notes (Text-Level, Not Interpretive)

1. **Accusative vs. articular nominative.** Acts 21:21 has the accusative ἀποστασίαν (content of a charge, anarthrous); 2 Thess 2:3 has ἡ ἀποστασία (articular nominative subject). The grammatical profile differs.
2. **Both contexts are polemical.** Acts 21:21 is an accusation; 2 Thess 2:3 is an apocalyptic warning. The word does not appear in neutral narrative.
3. **Both contexts concern a religious community.** Acts 21:21 concerns Jewish Torah-observance; 2 Thess 2:3 concerns the Christian expectation of the day of the Lord. In both, ἀποστασία marks abandonment of a prior covenantal/religious standing.
4. **LXX 1 Macc 2:15 provides the only Second Temple parallel to the articular ἡ ἀποστασία used of a specific, datable covenant-breaking event.** That parallel does not determine the referent of 2 Thess 2:3 but documents that such usage was available in Paul's linguistic environment.
5. **Paul's verbal forms.** Paul uses the cognate verb ἀφίστημι (G868) in the general sense of departing from faith (1 Tim 4:1 — "some shall depart from the faith," ἀποστήσονταί τινες τῆς πίστεως) and from iniquity (2 Tim 2:19). The 2 Thess 2:3 noun is Paul's only use of the nominal form ἀποστασία.
