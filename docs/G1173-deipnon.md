## Lexicon Definition

Strong's: "dinner, i.e. the chief meal (usually in the evening)." The deipnon is the principal meal of the day, as distinct from the morning/midday light meal (ἄριστον, G712). By NT times it was typically taken in the late afternoon or evening, and normally included guests, hence the semantic extension to "banquet, feast." The word covers both ordinary evening meals and formal banquets — birthday feasts, wedding suppers, memorial meals — and in Johannine narrative is the fixed term for the meal of the Last Supper.

**Observations:** Concentrated in Luke (5) and John (4) — the two gospels with the most developed meal-theology. John's four uses cluster around one narrative (the Last Supper: Jhn 13:2, 13:4, 21:20 retrospective, with 12:2 at Bethany anticipating it). The synoptic triple (Mat 23:6 / Mar 12:39 / Luk 20:46) is a single saying. The Pauline pair (1Co 11:20-21) gives the term its definitive ecclesial sense ("the Lord's supper"). Revelation's two uses (Rev 19:9, 19:17) form a deliberate antithetical pair — marriage supper of the Lamb vs. supper of the great God.

| אָרוּחָה (aruchah) | H737 | occasional — "meal/allowance of food" (2Ki 25:30; Jer 40:5) |

The noun deipnon has no dominant single Hebrew equivalent in the LXX because the OT concept of the chief meal/banquet is typically rendered by mishteh (drink-feast) or by verbal constructions with אָכַל (H398, eat) and שָׁתָה (H8354, drink) rather than a single noun. The Rev 19 marriage-supper and judgment-supper imagery draws more from **Isa 25:6** ("the LORD of hosts make unto all people a feast [mishteh] of fat things") and **Eze 39:17-20** ("speak unto every feathered fowl... ye shall eat flesh and drink blood") than from direct lexical borrowing.
