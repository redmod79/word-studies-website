**Semantic narrowing in translation:** The LXX splits galah's three semantic fields across different Greek words: apokalyptō absorbs the "reveal/uncover" sense, metoikizō takes the "exile" sense, and aschēmosynē captures the "nakedness/shame" sense. By the time apokalyptō enters the NT, its physical-uncovering and exile senses have been dropped — it is exclusively divine/spiritual disclosure.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- God is the primary agent of revelation — in both OT galah and NT apokalyptō, the passive voice dominates when the subject is divine truth
- The pattern of selective disclosure — God reveals to some (babes, prophets) and hides from others (the wise, enemies)
- The object revealed includes both God's character (righteousness, wrath) and specific future events (eschatological visions)

**What shifts:**
- **Semantic narrowing:** OT galah covers physical nakedness, exile, and divine revelation; NT apokalyptō is exclusively spiritual/divine disclosure
- **Eschatological concentration:** NT usage clusters around end-time revelation (glory to be revealed, man of sin revealed, salvation revealed) in a way that goes beyond OT galah
- **Christological focus:** NT apokalyptō centers on the revelation of Christ — his identity (Mat 16:17), his gospel (Gal 1:16), his return (Luk 17:30, 2Th 2:8)
