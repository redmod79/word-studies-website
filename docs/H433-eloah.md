## Lexicon Definition

> Probably prolonged (emphatic) from אֵל (H410); a deity or the Deity: — God, god.
> Rarely shortened to אֱלֹהַּ. The plural form is אֱלֹהִים (H430).

Eloah is the singular absolute form lying behind the ubiquitous plural elohim. Where elohim appears 2,600+ times in the OT, eloah appears only 58 times — overwhelmingly in poetic and archaic material.

**Pattern:** Eloah is concentrated overwhelmingly in Job (41 of 58 = 71%). It is otherwise scattered through poetic/archaic texts: the Song of Moses (Deut 32), Habakkuk's theophany (Hab 3), select Psalms, and a handful of wisdom/prophetic passages. The form is absent from the Torah narrative, the Former Prophets, and virtually all non-poetic material — consistent with a high-register/archaic lexical item preserved chiefly in poetry.

## Related Words

### Word Family / Cognates
- **H410 אֵל (ʼel)** — shortened singular form; "God, mighty one." Occurs ~238x.
- **H430 אֱלֹהִים (ʼelohim)** — the standard plural form of H433. ~2,600x. The plural is formed by adding the masculine plural suffix `-im` to the eloah stem (elo(h) + im → elohim).
- **H426 אֱלָהּ (ʼelah)** — Aramaic cognate of eloah; used ~95x in Daniel/Ezra.
- **H433 H430 morphology:** that eloah (singular) actually exists in the Hebrew lexicon is grammatically decisive: it shows elohim is a morphologically regular plural of an attested singular noun, not a frozen pluralis-tantum or loanword.

### Morphological Note — Plurality of Elohim
The coexistence of H410 (el, sg.), H433 (eloah, sg.), and H430 (elohim, pl.) is the key grammatical data point for the "plural of majesty" vs. "numerical plural" debate:

- **Numerical plural ("gods"):** attested — e.g., "other gods" (elohim acherim), Dan 11:37 "any god (eloah)" in juxtaposition with plural idols.
- **Plural of majesty / intensive plural:** when elohim refers to the one God of Israel, it regularly takes singular verbs and singular adjectives (e.g., Gen 1:1 `bara'` sg.), which is the standard grammatical signal that a morphological plural is being used with singular reference.
- **Singular forms remain available:** Hebrew did not need to drift into plural-only usage. The singular eloah is not obsolete — it continues in use through Job, Psalms, Habakkuk, Daniel. Writers who wanted an unambiguously singular "God" had eloah or el at hand and used them.
- **Implication:** the elohim-singular-verb pattern cannot be explained as a lexical accident (i.e., "Hebrew lost the singular"). The singular was preserved and actively used. The plural form's pairing with singular verbs is therefore a grammatical choice, not a lexical necessity — a standard data point in the majestic/intensive-plural analysis.
