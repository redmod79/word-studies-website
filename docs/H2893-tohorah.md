## Lexicon Definition

Feminine of H2892 (ṭôhar); ceremonial purification; moral purity: — is cleansed, cleansing, purification(-fying).

The word denotes both the **process** of purification (the prescribed ritual steps) and the **period** during which purification occurs. It is always tied to a structured, divinely regulated procedure — never a spontaneous or indefinite event.

## Semantic Categories

### 1. Post-partum Purification Period (Lev 12:4–6)

The most frequent single context. Tohorah designates the time-bounded waiting period after childbirth during which the mother transitions from ritual uncleanness back to full access to sacred space.

- **Lev 12:4** — "she shall then continue in the blood of her purifying (bidmey tohorah) three and thirty days; she shall touch no hallowed thing, nor come into the sanctuary, until the days of her purifying (yemey tohoratah) be fulfilled"
- **Lev 12:5** — "she shall continue in the blood of her purifying (demey tohorah) threescore and six days"
- **Lev 12:6** — "when the days of her purifying (yemey tohoratah) are fulfilled, for a son, or for a daughter, she shall bring a lamb... for a burnt offering, and a young pigeon... for a sin offering"

Key phrase: **דְּמֵי טָהֳרָה** (demey tohorah, "blood of purifying") — a construct chain unique to Lev 12, combining "blood" (dam) with "purification." The expression identifies the postpartum bleeding not as contaminating blood but as blood associated with the purification process itself — it is the blood that accompanies the transition back to cleanness.

**Structure:** Uncleanness period (7/14 days) → tohorah period (33/66 days) → culminating sacrifice (burnt offering + sin offering) → full restoration. Tohorah is the intermediate stage: no longer in the initial unclean state, but not yet restored to full access.

### 2. Skin-Disease Cleansing Certification (Lev 13:7, 35; 14:2, 23, 32)

Five occurrences deal with the priestly examination and cleansing process for skin diseases (traditionally "leprosy").

- **Lev 13:7** — "if the scab spread much abroad in the skin, after that he hath been seen of the priest for his cleansing (letohorato), he shall be seen of the priest again"
- **Lev 13:35** — "if the scall spread much in the skin after his cleansing (tohorato)"
- **Lev 14:2** — "This shall be the law (torah) of the leper in the day of his cleansing (yom tohorato): He shall be brought unto the priest"
- **Lev 14:23** — "he shall bring them on the eighth day for his cleansing (letohorato) unto the priest, unto the door of the tabernacle of the congregation, before the LORD"
- **Lev 14:32** — "This is the law of him in whom is the plague of leprosy, whose hand is not able to get that which pertaineth to his cleansing (betohorato)"

Key phrase: **תּוֹרַת הַמְּצֹרָע בְּיוֹם טָהֳרָתוֹ** (torat hametsora beyom tohorato, "the law of the leper in the day of his cleansing") — Lev 14:2 establishes tohorah as a formal legal concept with its own prescribed torah (instruction/law). This is the only instance where a specific "law of X" is explicitly designated for a purification procedure.

**The priest's role:** Tohorah in this context involves both examination (Lev 13:7, 35 — the priest checks whether the condition has worsened after initial clearance) and ceremony (Lev 14:2, 23 — the priest administers the full cleansing ritual with sacrifices). The word encompasses the entire arc from examination through sacrifice.

**Economic provision:** Lev 14:32 explicitly notes that tohorah has material requirements ("that which pertaineth to his cleansing"), and that provision is made for those whose "hand is not able to get" the standard offering — indicating that the purification process required resources.

### 3. Bodily Discharge Purification (Lev 15:13)

- **Lev 15:13** — "when he that hath an issue is cleansed (yithar) of his issue; then he shall number to himself seven days for his cleansing (letohorato), and wash his clothes, and bathe his flesh in running water, and shall be clean"

This verse is notable for containing both the verb taher (H2891, "is cleansed") and the noun tohorah (H2893, "his cleansing") — the verb describes the initial restoration of the clean state, and the noun denotes the formal purification period that follows. Seven days of counted purification, culminating in washing of garments (kabas) and bathing in running water (rachats), then the final declaration: "and shall be clean" (wetaher).

### 4. Nazirite Defilement Purification (Num 6:9)

- **Num 6:9** — "if any man die very suddenly by him, and he hath defiled the head of his consecration; then he shall shave his head in the day of his cleansing (yom tohorato), on the seventh day shall he shave it"

The Nazirite's purification follows a distinct procedure: the consecrated status (nezer) is interrupted by contact with death, and the "day of his cleansing" marks the formal restart of the purification process. The phrase **יוֹם טָהֳרָתוֹ** (yom tohorato, "the day of his purification") parallels Lev 14:2 exactly — both use "day of" + tohorah to mark the appointed time when the formal process begins.

### 5. Levitical Service Duty (1 Chr 23:28)

- **1 Chr 23:28** — "their office was to wait on the sons of Aaron for the service of the house of the LORD, in the courts, and in the chambers, and in the purifying (toharat) of all holy things, and the work of the service of the house of God"

Here tohorah appears in construct form: **טָהֳרַת לְכָל־קֹדֶשׁ** (toharat lekhol-qodesh, "purification of all holy things"). This is the only occurrence where tohorah governs sacred objects rather than persons. The Levites' perpetual duty includes maintaining the ritual purity of everything set apart as holy — vessels, furnishings, offerings.

### 6. Standard for Worship Access (2 Chr 30:19)

- **2 Chr 30:19** — "That prepareth his heart to seek God, the LORD God of his fathers, though he be not cleansed according to the purification of the sanctuary (ketohorat haqqodesh)"

The construct phrase **טָהֳרַת הַקֹּדֶשׁ** (ketohorat haqqodesh, "purification of the sanctuary/holiness") establishes tohorah as a recognized standard — a norm against which actual practice is measured. During Hezekiah's Passover, many northerners ate without meeting this standard. Hezekiah prays for their acceptance despite the gap, and God hears his prayer (v. 20). The passage demonstrates that tohorah as an institution serves as the expected protocol for approaching God, even when grace can cover its absence.

### 7. Institutional Worship Regulation (Neh 12:45)

- **Neh 12:45** — "both the singers and the porters kept the ward of their God, and the ward of the purification (mishmeret hattohorah), according to the commandment of David, and of Solomon his son"

The phrase **מִשְׁמֶרֶת הַטָּהֳרָה** (mishmeret hattohorah, "the ward/charge of the purification") treats tohorah as an ongoing institutional responsibility — a standing charge to be kept, parallel to keeping "the ward of their God." In the post-exilic community, purification is not merely an occasional procedure but a permanent office requiring designated personnel and commanded by royal authority.

### 8. Eschatological Priestly Regulation (Ezek 44:26)

- **Ezek 44:26** — "after he is cleansed (tohorato), they shall reckon unto him seven days"

In Ezekiel's vision of the restored temple, the purification requirement for priests who have contacted the dead mirrors the Levitical pattern but with its own seven-day count. The noun tohorah here denotes the completed state of being cleansed, after which an additional seven-day waiting period is imposed. This is the only usage where tohorah itself seems to denote the completed act rather than the ongoing process, followed by a further waiting period.

### Distinction Between H2893 (tohorah) and H2892 (tohar)
- **H2892 tohar** (masculine) = abstract quality: purity, brightness. Poetic/elevated register ("the clearness of the sky," Exod 24:10).

Both derive from the taher root, but they occupy different semantic spaces: tohar describes what purity looks like; tohorah describes how one gets there.

## Semantic Domain Summary

Tohorah occupies a narrow but well-defined semantic space:

1. **Always ceremonial** — every occurrence involves divinely prescribed ritual procedure, never spontaneous moral renewal
2. **Always temporal** — tohorah has a beginning, a duration ("days of"), and a culmination (sacrifice or declaration)
3. **Always relational** — it belongs to someone (pronominal suffixes) or to something (construct chains)
4. **Always structured** — examination by priest, waiting period, physical washing, culminating sacrifice
5. **Always gateway** — tohorah is what stands between uncleanness and restored access to the holy (qodesh)
