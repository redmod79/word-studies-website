# WG: Angel / Messenger

## Overview
The "angel/messenger" concept spans Hebrew, Aramaic, and Greek with a core meaning: one dispatched by a superior to carry a message or execute a task. The same Hebrew word (malak) covers both divine angels and human envoys; Greek splits the NT usage between ággelos (divine/supernatural messenger) and apóstolos (commissioned human delegate).

## Roles in the Concept

- **Divine Angel (OT):** malak (H4397) — the Angel of the LORD; divine beings who appear, speak, and act for God
- **Divine Angel (NT):** ággelos (G32) — heavenly beings; also Satan and fallen angels; developed angelology with ranks
- **Human Envoy (OT):** malak (H4397) — political/diplomatic messengers sent between kings; same word, different register
- **Human Envoy (OT alt.):** tsîyr (H6735) — a second OT word for envoy/ambassador, less common
- **Commissioned Apostle (NT):** apóstolos (G652) — inherits the "sent human delegate" function of malak; the founding missionaries
- **Aramaic bridge:** malak (H4398) — exact Aramaic cognate, appearing in Dan 3:28; 6:22 for the angel who shut the mouths of lions

## Key Passages Where the Concept Appears in Sharp Focus

- **Mal 3:1** — "I will send my messenger (malak)...even the messenger of the covenant" — single word, two referents (John the Baptist and the Lord himself); quoted in Mat 11:10 using ággelos
- **Hag 1:13** — "Haggai the LORD'S messenger (malak) in the LORD'S message" — prophet as malak
- **Mal 2:7** — "the priest...is the messenger (malak) of the LORD of hosts" — priest as malak
- **2Co 11:14** — "Satan himself is transformed into an angel (ággelos) of light" — the range includes fallen beings
- **Heb 1:4–14** — extended argument for Christ's superiority over the angels (ággelos) — assumes the word's full divine weight
