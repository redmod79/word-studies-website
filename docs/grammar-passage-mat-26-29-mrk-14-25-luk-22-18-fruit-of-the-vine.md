# Grammar Analysis: Matthew 26:29 ‖ Mark 14:25 ‖ Luke 22:18 — "Fruit of the Vine" (τὸ γένημα τῆς ἀμπέλου)

**Scope:** Context-neutral grammatical analysis of the synoptic Last-Supper eschatological-abstention logion. Compares the three parallel forms, documents Jesus's **deliberate lexical choice of τὸ γένημα τῆς ἀμπέλου ("the fruit of the vine")** over the available standard NT term οἶνος ("wine," G3631), catalogues the verb system (present λέγω + double-negative aorist subjunctive οὐ μὴ πίω + present subjunctive πίνω + ἕως-terminus), and surveys the interpretive import of the circumlocution without adjudicating theological positions (communion-bread/wine debates, fermented/unfermented question, eschatological messianic-banquet typology).

## Key Grammatical Features

### 1. The circumlocution τὸ γένημα τῆς ἀμπέλου — noun-phrase analysis

All three synoptics use the **identical four-word head-phrase** `τοῦ γενήματος τῆς ἀμπέλου` (Gen-Sg-Neut + Gen-Sg-Fem) — anarthrous-but-article-bearing in the partitive idiom *ἐκ / ἀπό + genitive*. The phrase consists of:

- **γένημα** (G1081, `N-GSN`) — **neuter noun**, "that which is produced / offspring / fruit / yield / crop." From the root γίν- (γίνομαι, "to come into being / be produced"). Distinct from the **masculine** οἶνος (G3631), which denotes the fermented liquid itself. The neuter gender signals **product-abstract** rather than the concrete substance-name.
  - NT inventory (parser-verified, 8× total): Matt 3:7 `Γεννήματα ἐχιδνῶν`; Matt 12:34; Matt 23:33 (same vocative phrase with Luke 3:7); **Matt 26:29; Mark 14:25; Luke 22:18** (the three Last-Supper occurrences); 2 Cor 9:10 `τὰ γενήματα τῆς δικαιοσύνης` (produce of righteousness).
  - Of the 8 NT occurrences, **3 are the Last-Supper formula** and **4 are the "brood/offspring of vipers"** metaphor; only 2 Cor 9:10 sits outside these two clusters — marking the word as highly distinctive/non-default.
  - Some NA/critical texts spell γένημα (single ν, from γίνομαι, "produce"); TR/Byzantine manuscripts often read γέννημα (double ν, from γεννάω, "beget"). The parser tags all 8 NT occurrences under lemma γέννημα G1081, but the semantic field of "produce / what-is-brought-forth" is stable across both spellings for our phrase. The Greek noun is lexically neutral as to fermentation or state-of-maturation; it denotes *what the vine produces*, whatever form that product takes (juice, must, fermented wine).

- **ἄμπελος** (G288, `N-GSF`) — **feminine noun**, "vine / grapevine" (the plant, not the product). NT inventory (parser-verified, 9× total): the three synoptic Last-Supper passages; John 15:1 ἐγώ εἰμι ἡ ἄμπελος ἡ ἀληθινή; John 15:4 ἡ ἄμπελος; Jas 3:12 ἄμπελος σῦκα; Rev 14:18, 19 (twice — the eschatological vine-harvest). Nine total occurrences, all theologically weighted.

- **τῆς ἀμπέλου** is a **genitive of source / producer** ("fruit *from / belonging to* the vine") — the vine is not the quality attributed to the fruit but the **plant that yielded it**. This stands in contrast to an attributive-genitive reading ("vine-characterized fruit") and resists identification with any particular downstream product. Grammar names the **plant-source**, not the product-state. See [genitive-of-quality-apposition](../greek/genitive-of-quality-apposition.md) for the broader category; this instance is closer to "producer/source genitive" than "quality."

- **The definite article** τοῦ γενήματος τῆς ἀμπέλου — articular in all three synoptics. Matthew alone adds the **near demonstrative** τούτου (`D-GSN`, "this"), grounding the referent in the physical cup Jesus has just handed round (Matt 26:27 ποτήριον); Mark and Luke drop the demonstrative. Matthew's τούτου is therefore ostensive-deictic (pointing to *this very* fruit-of-the-vine just blessed), while Mark's and Luke's articular phrase is **generic/categorical** ("the [class of] fruit of the vine").

### 2. Why γένημα τῆς ἀμπέλου rather than οἶνος — lexical-choice asymmetry

The Evangelists had a ready-to-hand one-word equivalent: **οἶνος** (G3631, 34× in NT per parser). οἶνος occurs in Matthew (9:17 wineskins, 27:34 wine-with-gall offered at crucifixion), Mark (2:22 wineskins; **15:23 wine-mingled-with-myrrh at the crucifixion**), Luke (1:15 John the Baptist drinks no wine; 5:37–38 wineskins; 7:33 same; **10:34 Samaritan's wine poured in wounds**), John (2:3, 9, 10 ×2 Cana; 4:46), Romans, Ephesians (5:18 μὴ μεθύσκεσθε οἴνῳ), Pastorals, and Revelation (many times, including 14:8; 14:10 wine of God's wrath; 17:2; 18:3).

None of the three Last-Supper records uses οἶνος at this saying. The choice of the neuter *produce-of-the-vine* over the masculine *oinos* is unanimous across Matthew–Mark–Luke. The same substitution holds for every verse in the synoptic Last-Supper narrative that names the cup-contents: Matt 26:27, 28; Mark 14:23, 24; Luke 22:17, 20 all speak of the **cup** (ποτήριον, G4221, `N-ASN`, also neuter) or the **blood** (αἷμά μου τῆς διαθήκης, Matt 26:28; Mark 14:24), never οἶνος.

Three context-neutral factors the grammar itself supplies:

1. **Phrase-structure parallel with Hebrew/Aramaic liturgical idiom.** The synoptic wording matches the Jewish *berakhah* formula over the cup preserved in m. Berakhot 6:1: `בורא פרי הגפן` (*bôrēʾ pərî ha-gefen*, "who creates the fruit of the vine"). LXX has no fixed equivalent, but the periphrasis matches γένημα ἀμπέλου in e.g. Isa 32:12 LXX and Deut 22:9 LXX. Jesus's word-choice reproduces the liturgical idiom of the blessing itself rather than substituting a Hellenistic substance-name.
2. **Neuter gender concord.** All three verses pick up the phrase with the **neuter** accusative pronoun αὐτὸ (`P-ASN`) at the ὅταν-clause — "when I drink **it** new." If the antecedent were οἶνος (masculine), the concord would demand αὐτόν (`P-ASM`). The entire syntactic chain is gender-locked to the neuter γένημα, reinforcing that the substitution is not a floating gloss but integrated into the clause.
3. **Lexical openness.** οἶνος in Greek (and LXX usage, e.g. Num 6:3) lexicalizes the fermented product (mellow wine) as distinct from γλεῦκος (G1098, must/new wine, Acts 2:13) and from σίκερα (LXX transliteration of *šēḵār*, strong drink). γένημα τῆς ἀμπέλου denotes the **plant's produce** without specifying its state. Grammar leaves the fermentation/state-of-maturation question unsettled; the phrase is **state-neutral** where οἶνος is **state-specific**.

No theological conclusion follows from the noun-choice alone. The three context-neutral observations merely catalog what the grammar does and does not commit the speaker to. Theological debate over whether the communion element is necessarily fermented, necessarily unfermented, or undetermined by these verses does not turn on γένημα vs. οἶνος alone but on (a) Passover halakhah governing the cup, (b) the parallel τὸ αἷμά μου predication (Matt 26:28), and (c) the eschatological καινόν (see §5 below). Grammar is a constraint, not the verdict.

### 3. Verb system — λέγω (pres) + οὐ μὴ πίω (aor subj) + πίνω (pres subj) + ἔλθῃ (aor subj)

Every finite verb in all three verses clusters around the aspect-contrast between the **aorist subjunctive** (bounded / global act) and the **present subjunctive** (unbounded / in-process act):

| Verb          | Parsing                      | Aspect / Function                                                  | Synoptic spread                         |
|---------------|------------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------------|------------------------------------------|
| λέγω          | `V-PAI-1S` pres.act.ind.1sg  | Present-time declarative speech-frame                              | All three (Matt 26:29; Mark 14:25; Luke 22:18) |
| πίω           | `V-2AAS-1S` aor.act.subj.1sg | Perfective/global "I (will ever) drink" under οὐ μή                | All three                                |
| πίνω          | `V-PAS-1S` pres.act.subj.1sg | Imperfective "I (am) drinking / keep drinking" under ὅταν          | Matthew, Mark (absent from Luke)         |
| ἔλθῃ          | `V-2AAS-3S` aor.act.subj.3sg | Perfective "(the kingdom) should come" under ἕως οὗ                | Luke only                                |

**Three features of this verb system are grammatically load-bearing:**

**(a) Double-negative emphatic future denial (οὐ μή + aor. subj.).** The phrase **οὐ μὴ πίω** (Matt 26:29; Mark 14:25; Luke 22:18 — all three) is the strongest form of negative statement Greek possesses: the "emphatic negation / emphatic future denial" construction (Wallace 468–469; BDF §365; Robertson 929–930). *οὐ* negates indicative reality; *μή* negates subjunctive/possibility; combined with the aorist subjunctive they project a **global/perfective exclusion** into the future. Not "I am not drinking now," not "I will not drink on some occasions," but "there is no occasion whatsoever, conceivable or actual, on which I shall drink." The aorist (not present) subjunctive is the mandatory construction here — an aorist πίω (`V-2AAS-1S`) from πίνω (G4095) seals the exclusion as whole-event-bounded.

Mark sharpens the denial further by stacking **οὐκέτι** (G3765, "no longer") *plus* **οὐ μή**: `οὐκέτι οὐ μὴ πίω` — triple-layered negation marking a before/after rupture at this meal itself ("from here on, by no means ever will I drink"). Matthew and Luke express the same rupture by adverbial phrase rather than a third negation: Matthew's **ἀπ᾿ ἄρτι** (G737, "from now") and Luke's **ἀπὸ τοῦ νῦν** (G3568, "from the now [on]"). Three distinct surface realizations of the same grammatical commitment.

**(b) Aspect contrast: global denial (aor.) vs. future eschatological state (pres.).** Matthew and Mark pair the aorist subjunctive πίω under *οὐ μή* with a **present subjunctive** πίνω under ὅταν ("whenever / at the time when I drink"). The aspect-pair is deliberate: the thing denied is **any instance whatsoever** of drinking (aorist = perfective/whole); the thing affirmed is **ongoing/characteristic drinking** in the restored kingdom-state (present = imperfective). *Not* "I will drink one more cup *someday*" but "there is (will be) a drinking-together-with-you that extends in the kingdom-state." The aspect, not just the tense, carries the promise. Luke drops the πίνω-clause and replaces the entire ὅταν-construction with a simple ἕως-οὗ-clause governed by ἔλθῃ (aor. subj. of ἔρχομαι) on the kingdom itself — Luke's focus is the **arrival of the kingdom** rather than the **drinking-act within the kingdom**.

**(c) Two temporal-boundary systems.** The ἕως-clause in all three marks the terminus of the abstention:
- **Matthew/Mark:** `ἕως τῆς ἡμέρας ἐκείνης ὅταν αὐτὸ πίνω καινὸν ἐν τῇ βασιλείᾳ` — terminus is **a day defined by a drinking-event** (genitive ἡμέρας + demonstrative ἐκείνης + temporal ὅταν-clause + pres.subj.); the kingdom is the locative ἐν-phrase.
- **Luke:** `ἕως οὗ ἡ βασιλεία τοῦ Θεοῦ ἔλθῃ` — terminus is the **coming of the kingdom** as event (relative οὗ + aor.subj. ἔλθῃ); no drinking-event in the terminus, no αὐτὸ / καινὸν clause.

Luke's version is thus the **shortest and event-focused**; Matthew/Mark give the **longer participant-focused** form with the reunion-drinking clause. Context-neutral observation: the three variants are structurally homologous but not identical. Luke's verse-structure emphasizes the kingdom's arrival; Matthew and Mark's emphasize a future table-fellowship within the kingdom.

### 4. The adjective καινός (G2537) — "new" as qualitative not temporal

Matthew and Mark qualify the future drinking with **καινὸν** (`A-ASN`, acc.sg.neut., agreeing with αὐτὸ / γένημα) — "new." Greek has two words for "new": **νέος** (G3501, new in time / young / recent) and **καινός** (G2537, new in quality / unprecedented / of a different kind). The synoptists choose καινός, which in NT usage typically connotes **qualitative novelty**: new covenant (καινὴ διαθήκη, Luke 22:20; 1 Cor 11:25; 2 Cor 3:6; Heb 8:8, 13; 9:15), new creation (καινὴ κτίσις, 2 Cor 5:17), new heaven and new earth (οὐρανὸν καινὸν καὶ γῆν καινήν, Rev 21:1), new name (Rev 2:17; 3:12), new song (Rev 5:9; 14:3).

Grammar by itself does not settle whether καινὸν modifies the kind of drinking ("in a new way") or the substance drunk ("as new produce") — the neuter accusative καινόν predicates over the neuter αὐτὸ back-referring to the neuter γένημα, so all three nominal slots are concord-locked. Adverbial-adjective functioning ("drink it newly / in a new manner") is syntactically available but no more certain than attributive reading ("drink it [as] new [produce]").

Three structural classes of divergence:

- **Matthew alone** keeps the fellowship adjunct **μεθ᾿ ὑμῶν** ("with you") and names the kingdom as **my Father's** (τοῦ Πατρός μου, genitive of possession) — both signaling a more intimate / filial framing.
- **Mark alone** prefaces with the solemnity-particle **ἀμὴν** and doubles the negation with **οὐκέτι**.
- **Luke alone** drops the ὅταν-clause and the καινόν-predicate, reducing the terminus-clause to kingdom-coming, and uses **ἀπό** uniformly where Mark uses ἐκ and Matthew uses both.

The core grammatical invariants across all three — the phrase τὸ γένημα τῆς ἀμπέλου, the οὐ μὴ πίω double-negation, the ἕως-terminus, the eschatological-kingdom anchor — constitute the fixed substrate. The variants do not shift any invariant into a different grammatical category; they reweight discourse-pragmatics (solemnity, intimacy, kingdom-arrival vs. kingdom-location).

### 6. Preceding-clause integration: the cup and the blood (Matt 26:27–28 ‖ Mark 14:23–24 ‖ Luke 22:17)

Each synoptic anchors the γένημα-saying in an immediately preceding cup-act:

- **Matthew 26:27–28** — Aor.Act.Ptcp `λαβὼν` (taking) + Aor.Act.Ptcp `εὐχαριστήσας` (giving thanks) + Aor.Act.Ind.3s `ἔδωκεν` (he gave) + 2pl Aor.Act.Impv `Πίετε` (drink, from πίνω) + ἐξ αὐτοῦ πάντες ("drink of it all of you"); v.28 `τοῦτο γάρ ἐστιν τὸ αἷμά μου τῆς διαθήκης` — predicate-nominal `ἐστιν` (`V-PAI-3S`) identifies the τοῦτο (neuter demonstrative, agreeing by concord with ποτήριον / γένημα) as τὸ αἷμά μου.
- **Mark 14:23–24** — parallel structure, same aorist-participle + aorist-indicative + aorist-imperative sequence; `Πίετε` absent but `ἔπιον ἐξ αὐτοῦ πάντες` (3pl aor. ind. "they all drank of it") renders obedience. v.24 Mark has `τοῦτό ἐστιν τὸ αἷμά μου τῆς διαθήκης τὸ ἐκχυννόμενον ὑπὲρ πολλῶν`.
- **Luke 22:17** — shifts participle to `δεξάμενος` (aor.mid.ptcp of δέχομαι, "having received") + `εὐχαριστήσας` + aor.indic. `εἶπεν` + aor.impv. `Λάβετε` (take) + `διαμερίσατε εἰς ἑαυτούς` (distribute among yourselves). Luke's first-cup saying (22:18 = our verse) precedes the bread-breaking; Luke's second-cup saying (22:20) supplies the `τοῦτο τὸ ποτήριον ἡ καινὴ διαθήκη ἐν τῷ αἵματί μου` — with **καινή** reappearing as qualifier of **διαθήκη**.

The immediate grammatical neighbor of the γένημα-saying is thus the **cup-act and the blood-predication**. Matthew's demonstrative τούτου on γενήματος connects back via concord to the ποτήριον of v.27 and through it to the αἷμα of v.28 (all three are neuter; the pronoun-chain `αὐτοῦ / τοῦτο / αὐτὸ` binds them). Mark and Luke drop the near demonstrative but preserve the neuter concord chain.

Context-neutral observation: the γένημα-saying is **flanked on one side by a present-tense predication identifying the cup-contents as blood-of-the-covenant** (pres. indic. ἐστιν) and **on the other side by a future-aspect aorist-subjunctive denial of drinking** (οὐ μὴ πίω) bounded by a kingdom-event. The grammar alone supplies the temporal horizon (present → indefinite future → kingdom-arrival) and the thematic progression (cup-blood predication → abstention → reunion) without mandating any particular sacramental theology.

## Textual Notes

- **Spelling:** γένημα (single ν, from γίνομαι = "produce") vs. γέννημα (double ν, from γεννάω = "beget"). Modern critical texts (NA28/UBS5) print γένημα in all three verses; Byzantine and Textus Receptus witnesses read γέννημα. Parser tags all 8 NT occurrences under lemma γέννημα G1081. Semantic difference between the two spellings is minimal in agricultural/produce contexts (BDAG sub γένημα); the NT retains both orthographies for the same referent-class.
- **Mark 14:25 ὅτι:** Byzantine and Alexandrian traditions agree on ὅτι after λέγω ὑμῖν (declarative ὅτι introducing indirect or direct speech). No substantive divergence.
- **Matthew 26:29 τούτου:** Matthew's near demonstrative is textually secure; Mark and Luke's absence is equally secure. The synoptic threefold attestation of the γένημα τῆς ἀμπέλου phrase in this order (Matt → Mark → Luke) has no significant variant in the direction of replacement by οἶνος.
- **Luke 22:17–20 longer/shorter text:** The Western text of Luke famously omits parts of 22:19b–20 (the second cup saying), but v. 18 — our verse, the first-cup saying with γένημα τῆς ἀμπέλου — is present in all major text-families.

## Summary of Grammatical Findings

1. **Unanimous lexical choice.** All three synoptics say τὸ γένημα τῆς ἀμπέλου, never οἶνος — despite οἶνος being the standard NT term for wine (34× vs. 8× for γέννημα, only 3 of which name the vine-product). The substitution is grammatically load-bearing, not stylistic.

2. **Liturgical-idiom match.** The phrase mirrors the Jewish *berakhah* over the cup (*pərî ha-gefen*), suggesting the saying reproduces the blessing's own wording rather than translating it into Hellenistic substance-vocabulary.

3. **State-neutral noun-choice.** γένημα denotes *produce of the vine* without specifying fermentation-state; οἶνος lexicalizes the fermented product. Grammar preserves openness where a substitution with οἶνος would close the question.

4. **Neuter concord chain.** The entire ποτήριον–γένημα–αὐτό–τοῦτο concord system across Matt 26:27–29 ‖ Mark 14:23–25 ‖ Luke 22:17–18 is gender-locked to the neuter, bound to γένημα / ποτήριον, not to the masculine οἶνος.

5. **Strongest-form negation.** `οὐ μὴ πίω` (aor.subj. under emphatic double negation) is the most forceful future denial Greek has. Mark reinforces with `οὐκέτι`, Matthew and Luke with `ἀπ᾿ ἄρτι` / `ἀπὸ τοῦ νῦν`. Aspect (aorist) seals the denial as global/whole-event.

6. **Aspectual pair (Matt/Mark).** The aor.subj. denial πίω (perfective, no-event) is matched by the pres.subj. ὅταν-clause πίνω (imperfective, ongoing) in the promised kingdom-drinking — the grammar delivers the promise of *continuing* table-fellowship, not a single eschatological sip.

7. **Kingdom-terminus, two configurations.** Matthew/Mark anchor the terminus in a *day defined by a reunion-drinking event within the kingdom*; Luke anchors it in the *coming of the kingdom* itself. Same temporal-ἕως substrate, different focal emphasis.

8. **Theological adjudication left unsettled by grammar.** No theological position (fermented/unfermented, sacramental substance-theory, eschatological messianic banquet typology) is forced by the grammar alone. The noun-choice constrains — it does not decide. The saying's import lies in the **deliberate preservation of categorical openness at a liturgically central moment**, which grammar documents but does not interpret.
