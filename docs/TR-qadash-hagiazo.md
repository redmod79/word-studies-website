**Semantic note:** The LXX translators chose hagiazō (from *hagios* — set apart, awful, sacred) as the consistent rendering of qâdash. Both words carry the sense of "remove from common use and dedicate to God." The underlying root idea is separation and consecration, not merely moral purity.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same:**
- Core meaning: to set apart from common use and dedicate to God
- God as primary subject who sanctifies (Exo 31:13; John 17:17; 1 Thess 5:23)
- The pattern: Holy name → holy time → holy people → holy place
- Reflexive / corporate call: OT "sanctify yourselves" → NT "sanctify the Lord God in your hearts" (1 Pet 3:15)

**What shifts:**
- OT qadash is heavily Piel (causative-declarative: pronounce holy by ritual/command); NT hagiazō shifts to describing Christ's atoning work as the ground of sanctification
- OT sanctification is primarily ceremonial and tied to Levitical system; NT hagiazō extends to inner moral transformation (1 Thess 4:3-4; 5:23)
- LXX introduced hagiazō into the Greek world where *hagios* carried connotations of awe and dread (from *hágos*); NT writers redirected this toward Christ's person as the source of holiness
- NT adds eschatological dimension: believers are "sanctified" as a completed act (1 Cor 6:11) and are being sanctified progressively
