## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- **Agent → patient structure:** a hostile or contemptuous agent (nation, beast, swine, apostate) tramples a sacred or valuable object (sanctuary, vineyard, pearl, word, Son). The Hebrew and Greek vocabulary name the same relational pattern.
- **Trampling as covenant-reversal:** Israel's worshippers "tread" God's courts (Isa 1:12) with unholy feet; the Gentiles tread Jerusalem (Luke 21:24); the apostate treads the Son (Heb 10:29). Each case is a sacred boundary violated.
- **Passive/substantival usage:** Hebrew mirmâs ("a thing to be trodden") and Greek substantival participial constructions (τὸ καταπατούμενον, "that which is trampled") both objectify the trampled thing — a grammatical continuity preserving the fate-of-the-patient focus.
- **Sanctuary context:** Dan 8:13 mirmâs and Luke 21:24 patéō are joined by the same eschatological logic — a bounded period during which the holy is handed over.

**What shifts:**
- **From nation to person:** OT trampling operates mostly at the corporate level (sanctuary, vineyard, city, nation). NT trampling reaches its climax in the trampling of a person — the Son of God (Heb 10:29). The figure personalises as it intensifies.
- **From God's rod to human contempt:** In Isa 10:6 and 63:3, God himself tramples (directly or via Assyria). In the NT, katapatéō almost always names human contempt toward the holy, with no corresponding divine-warrior trampling verb. The active trampler shifts from God-as-judge to humans-as-profaners.
- **Noun cluster loses its discrete equivalent:** Hebrew has a dedicated trampling-patient noun (mirmâs); Greek expresses the same idea by participial phrasing rather than a single lexeme. The concept is preserved; the lexical compression is lost.
- **Eschatological specification:** Dan 8:13's "how long?" question (bounded trampling of the sanctuary) is echoed by Luke 21:24's "until the times of the Gentiles be fulfilled" — the NT adds an explicit terminus to the OT mirmâs image, converting a lamenting question into a prophetic schedule.
