# H410 — אֵל (ʼêl)

## Core Data
- **Strong's:** H410
- **Original:** אֵל
- **Transliteration:** ʼêl
- **Pronunciation:** ale
- **Part of Speech:** Masculine noun (singular)
- **Root:** Shortened form related to אוּל (ul, "to be strong") / אַיִל (ayil, "mighty one"); cognate to the Semitic root for strength/power
- **Occurrences:** 234 unique verses, 271 tokens in OT
- **First Occurrence:** Gen 14:18 — "Melchizedek king of Salem... was the priest of the most high God (ʼêl)"
- **Hapax Legomenon:** No

## Lexicon Definition

Shortened from a root meaning "strength"; as adjective, mighty; especially the Almighty (but used also of any deity). KJV: God (god), goodly, great, idol, might(-y one), power, strong. Compare compound names ending in `-el`.

The word is grammatically **singular** — the masculine singular noun for "god" or "mighty one." It stands in contrast to H430 אֱלֹהִים (ʼĕlôhîm), which is the same semantic field but formed with the plural ending `-îm`. H433 אֱלוֹהַּ (ʼĕlôahh) is the alternative singular form (36x, mostly in Job).

## Notes for Divine-Names Study

This entry supplies the reference data; the interpretive work belongs to the downstream study. Raw observations from the data:

1. **ʼêl is morphologically singular.** It takes singular verb agreement and has its own plural (ʼêlîm). The plural ʼĕlôhîm is not formed from ʼêl directly but is a distinct lexeme with the plural ending `-îm`.
2. **ʼêl and ʼĕlôhîm collocate in the same verse 50 times** (21% of ʼêl's occurrences), often stacked as coordinate titles (e.g., Josh 22:22; Ps 50:1) — i.e., they are used as equivalent designations, not as distinct deities.
3. **Both ʼêl and ʼĕlôhîm are applied to false gods** (Exo 15:11; Deut 32:21; Isa 44:10; etc.) in the same grammatical constructions used for YHWH.
4. **The plural ʼêlîm exists and is used both for pagan gods and within divine-council imagery** (Exo 15:11; Ps 29:1; 89:6) — so a plural of the singular base form is attested and is *not* treated as a "plural of majesty."
5. **Compound proper names preserve the singular form** (Isra-el, Immanu-el, Dani-el, Micha-el, etc.) — the onomastic evidence shows ancient Israelites treated ʼêl as a singular personal-deity term.
