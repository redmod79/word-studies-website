## Lexicon Definition

μαίνομαι — middle voice from a primary μάω (máō, "to long for"); through the idea of insatiate craving: to rave as a madman, to be out of one's mind, to be insane. The word denotes a state of mental derangement perceived by observers — not clinical diagnosis but social judgment that someone's speech or behavior exceeds the bounds of rationality.

## Semantic Categories

### 1. Accusation of Demonic Madness Against Jesus
- **John 10:20** — "And many of them said, He hath a devil, and is mad (mainetai); why hear ye him?" The charge pairs madness with demon-possession — the two explanations crowds reach for when confronted with speech they cannot assimilate. Jesus' claim to be the Good Shepherd who lays down his life provokes this accusation.

### 2. Disbelief Dismissing an Eyewitness as Delusional
- **Acts 12:15** — "And they said unto her, Thou art mad (mainē). But she constantly affirmed that it was even so. Then said they, It is his angel." Rhoda reports that Peter stands at the gate after his prison escape; the gathered believers dismiss her testimony as madness before proposing a supernatural alternative (his angel). Here μαίνομαι names the reflex of dismissing an incredible but true report.

### 3. Official Charge Against Prophetic/Apostolic Testimony
- **Acts 26:24** — "Festus said with a loud voice, Paul, thou art beside thyself (mainē); much learning doth make thee mad (mania, G3130)." The Roman governor diagnoses Paul's resurrection testimony as scholarly derangement. μαίνομαι (verb) and μανία (noun) appear together — the full madness word family deployed in a single accusation.
- **Acts 26:25** — "But he said, I am not mad (mainomai), most noble Festus; but speak forth the words of truth and soberness (sōphrosýnē, G4997)." Paul's rebuttal sets μαίνομαι against σωφροσύνη — madness vs. sound-mindedness. This is the sharpest antonymic pairing in the NT for both words.

### 4. Outsider Perception of Ecstatic Speech
- **1 Cor 14:23** — "If therefore the whole church be come together into one place, and all speak with tongues, and there come in those that are unlearned, or unbelievers, will they not say that ye are mad (mainesthe)?" Paul uses μαίνομαι to describe how outsiders would perceive uninterpreted glossolalia — not as spiritual gift but as collective insanity. The concern is missional: the appearance of madness repels the very people the church should reach.
