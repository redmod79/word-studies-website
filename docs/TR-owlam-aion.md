**Semantic continuity:** αἰών carries both the temporal ambiguity of ʻôwlâm (age, era, indefinite duration) and its directional flexibility (past: "the former age"; future: "the age to come"). The LXX translation is remarkably consistent, with 287/406 (71%) of ʻôwlâm occurrences rendered by αἰών and another 100 by αἰώνιος.

**Semantic shift:** In Classical Greek αἰών denoted a lifespan or vital force; in LXX usage it expands to encompass the Hebrew concept of boundless, directionally open time. By the NT period αἰών also develops a spatial/cosmological nuance: "this present age (αἰών)" vs. "the age to come."

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same:**
- The fundamental meaning of unlimited, open-ended duration persists from Hebrew through LXX into NT
- The berît ʻôlâm ("everlasting covenant") becomes the "eternal covenant" (Heb 13:20) — same concept, same Greek word
- Doxological "for ever and ever" formulas (εἰς τοὺς αἰῶνας τῶν αἰώνων in NT) directly extend the Hebrew לְעוֹלָם וָעֶד pattern

**What shifts:**
- ʻôwlâm encompasses both past ("of old, ancient") and future ("for ever"); NT αἰών becomes more future-oriented and less retrospective
- αἰών develops a spatial/structural sense in NT ("this present αἰών" = the current world order under sin), which has no precise Hebrew parallel
- The plural αἰῶνες ("the ages") in NT creates a new cosmological concept of sequential eras (Heb 1:2; Eph 3:9) that the Hebrew singular ʻôwlâm does not emphasize
