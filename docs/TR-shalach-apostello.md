**Semantic differentiation in translation:** The LXX translators split shalach's broad semantic range into distinct Greek words. ἀποστέλλω captures the core "commission and send on a mission" sense. ἐξαποστέλλω handles the Piel "send away, release, let go" sense (prominent in Exodus Pharaoh passages and Leviticus scapegoat). ἐκτείνω takes over the "stretch out the hand" physical gesture. This differentiation sharpens what is a single Hebrew verb into specialized Greek terms.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The core pattern of authoritative divine commissioning persists: God sends prophets in the OT, God sends the Son and apostles in the NT
- The "sent with a message" pattern: prophets are sent to speak God's words (Jer 7:25), apostles are sent to preach the gospel (Rom 10:15)
- The word consistently implies the sender's authority invested in the one sent

**What shifts:**
- NT narrows the usage: apostello in the NT almost exclusively means authoritative commissioning — the "let go/release" sense (shalach Piel) is absent, handled by ἀπολύω (apolyo) instead
- The noun ἀπόστολος (apostolos) — "apostle" — becomes a technical title in the NT with no direct Hebrew equivalent; the OT uses shalach as a verb without a corresponding agent-noun for "one who is sent"
- The Leviticus scapegoat "sending away" (shalach Piel → LXX ἐξαποστέλλω) has no direct NT verbal parallel; the NT uses different vocabulary for the sin-bearing and removal concepts
