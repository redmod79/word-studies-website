**Observation:** The yada ↔ ginōskō pairing is one of the strongest single-word LXX bridges in the Hebrew Bible. Greek translators almost always reached for ginōskō or its compound epiginōskō when yada appeared.

**Semantic shift:** Greek ginōskō, in classical usage, leaned more toward perceptual/inferential knowing, while Hebrew yada was broader and more relational. LXX translators kept ginōskō but stretched it — their adoption of ginōskō for sexual-euphemism yada (Gen 4:1 etc.) carried this Hebraic breadth directly into Koine Greek, and into the NT.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- **Breadth of meaning.** Both yada and ginōskō cover cognitive, relational, experiential, and sexual knowing. Greek in general did not do this; biblical Greek does because it inherits yada.
- **Sexual euphemism.** Mat 1:25 and Luk 1:34 use ginōskō for sexual knowing — an un-Greek idiom taken directly from Hebrew via LXX.
- **Covenantal/elective knowing.** "I have known thee" (Amos 3:2, Jer 1:5) → "known of God" (Gal 4:9, 1Co 8:3).
- **Divine recognition formula.** "They shall know that I am the LORD" (Ezekiel) → "that the world may know" (Jhn 17:23).
- **Hiphil causative → gnōrizō.** "Make known" parallels between Psa 103:7 / Pro 1:23 and Rom 16:26, Eph 1:9.

**What shifts:**
- **Christological focalization.** NT knowing often has Christ as its object (Jhn 17:3, Phi 3:10), where OT yada is focused on YHWH.
- **"Knowledge" as a contested term.** NT engages with Greek philosophical/proto-Gnostic gnōsis (1Co 8:1; 1Ti 6:20) in ways the OT does not.
- **Mutual knowing intensified.** NT emphasizes reciprocity ("I know mine own and am known of mine," Jhn 10:14; "then shall I know even as also I am known," 1Co 13:12) more explicitly than OT.
- **Eschatological shift.** OT "they shall know" is largely vindicative (Ezekiel's nations-will-know); NT "they shall know" is largely missional/relational (Jhn 13:35; 17:23).
