## Lexicon Definition

From Thayer's: *To count with pebbles, to compute, calculate, reckon.* Specifically: to reckon up a cost (τὴν δαπάνην, Luke 14:28); to explain by computing a number (τὸν ἀριθμόν, Rev 13:18). In classical Greek, commonly in the middle voice: to give one's vote by casting a pebble into an urn; to decide by voting. Compare compounds: συμψηφίζω (G4860), συγκαταψηφίζομαι (G4785).

*(From ψῆφος — the small worn pebble used in counting boards and voting urns in ancient courts.)*

## Semantic Categories

### 1. Practical Calculation — Counting Cost Before Acting

The word describes the rational, deliberate act of calculating before undertaking a project.

- **Luke 14:28** — "For which of you, intending to build a tower, sitteth not down first, and counteth the cost, whether he have sufficient to finish it?" [V-PAI-3S: Present Active Indicative — ongoing/customary action of reckoning]

Context: Jesus' parable on the cost of discipleship. The act of ψηφίζω is presented as standard prudence — you *count* before you commit.

### 2. Eschatological Computation — Calculating a Symbolic Number

The word is used as an imperative: a command to exercise discernment by calculating.

- **Rev 13:18** — "Here is wisdom. Let him that hath understanding count the number of the beast: for it is the number of a man; and his number is Six hundred threescore and six." [V-AAM-3S: Aorist Active Imperative — a one-time act of discernment]

Context: The reader is called to ψηφίζω — to reckon out, to calculate — the number 666. The pairing with σοφία (wisdom) and νοῦς (understanding) frames calculation as an act of spiritual insight.
