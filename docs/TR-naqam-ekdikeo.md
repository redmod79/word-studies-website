The nqm word family (H5358/5359/5360) maps overwhelmingly to the ek-dik- family (G1556/1557/1558). Combined co-occurrence: 49×.

**Semantic shift in translation:** The Hebrew naqam carries a visceral sense — "to grudge, avenge" — rooted in personal grievance and blood-debt. The LXX translators chose ek-dikeo (ἐκ + δίκη = "execute justice out of"), reframing the concept through a judicial lens. What Hebrew presents as retaliation, Greek frames as vindication. The emotional core of grudge-bearing in the Hebrew yields to procedural justice in the Greek, though both retain the dimension of punitive response.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- Divine vengeance as a prerogative of God, not delegated to individuals (Lev 19:18 → Rom 12:19)
- Blood-avenging as a divine covenant obligation — God avenges the blood of his servants (Deut 32:43 → Rev 6:10, 19:2)
- The "enemy" (oyeb/echthros) as the consistent object of divine naqam/ekdikeo

**What shifts:**
- The NT narrows the scope: naqam in the OT includes divinely authorized human warfare (Num 31:2, Jos 10:13), but NT ekdikeo usage is almost exclusively divine or eschatological — human agents are told "avenge not yourselves" (Rom 12:19)
- The LXX judicial framing (ek-dike = "out of justice") becomes dominant in NT: Luke 18 presents vengeance as vindication of the oppressed rather than punishment of enemies
- 2 Cor 10:6 is the sole NT instance of ekdikeo applied to church discipline ("revenge all disobedience"), retaining the authorized-agent pattern from the OT but in an ecclesiastical rather than military context
