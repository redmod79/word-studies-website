## Semantic Categories

### 1. Sweet vs. Bitter Water (Nature Illustration)
- **James 3:11** — "Doth a fountain send forth at the same place sweet [water] and bitter?"
- **James 3:12** — "Can the fig tree, my brethren, bear olive berries? either a vine, figs? so [can] no fountain both yield salt water and fresh."

James uses glykys to describe wholesome, drinkable water as opposed to bitter (pikros G4089) or salt (halykos G252) water. The point is that a single source cannot produce contradictory outputs — sweet and bitter cannot flow from the same spring. Here "fresh" (KJV 3:12) translates the same word rendered "sweet" in 3:11.

### 2. Sweetness of the Prophetic Word
- **Rev 10:9** — "Take [it], and eat it up; and it shall make thy belly bitter, but it shall be in thy mouth sweet as honey."
- **Rev 10:10** — "And I took the little book out of the angel's hand, and ate it up; and it was in my mouth sweet as honey: and as soon as I had eaten it, my belly was bitter."

In the Ezekiel-patterned prophetic commission (cf. Ezek 3:1-3), the little scroll is glykys "sweet as honey" (glykys hos meli) in the mouth but bitter in the belly. The sweetness describes the initial reception of divine revelation — pleasant to receive — while the bitterness represents the difficult content or consequences of the prophetic message.
