# Grammar Analysis: John 2:1-11 (The Wedding at Cana)

## Overview

The first "sign" (σημεῖον) in John's Gospel. The narrative is built around three distinct classes of tenses:

- **Imperfects and present historical** (background/narrative setup)
- **Aorist imperatives and aorist indicatives** (Jesus' commands and their punctiliar execution)
- **Perfect passive participle and perfect active indicatives** (the abiding result-state of the transformation)

Three content words drive the punch-line: οἶνος (oinos, "wine"), καλός (kalos, "fine/of good quality"), and μεθύω (methyō, "be drunk/have drunk freely"). The architriklinos' speech in v.10 is constructed as a universal proverb contrasted (asyndetic σύ) with an observed exception.

*This beginning of miracles did Jesus in Cana of Galilee, and manifested forth his glory; and his disciples believed on him.*

## Key Grammatical Features

### 1. οἶνος (oinos, G3631) — Generic term for vine-product

- **Lexical range:** Covers the entire vine-product spectrum — fresh juice, new wine, fermented wine. In classical/Koine usage the context (and often a modifier) specifies which is meant; `οἶνος` alone is ambiguous.
- **Morphological pattern in the passage:**
  - v.3: `οἴνου` (GSM, head of the genitive absolute) → `Οἶνον` (ASM, fronted as emphatic object)
  - v.9: `οἶνον γεγενημένον` — accusative with perfect passive participle in predicate position ("water having-become-wine")
  - v.10: `τὸν καλὸν οἶνον` (×2) — articular + adjective + noun "*the* fine wine"
- **Significance:** The article in v.10 (`τὸν καλὸν οἶνον`) is not generic ("good wine") but anaphoric/categorical — "the [kind of] fine wine [that a host sets out first]." The architriklinos is classifying Jesus' product within the known taxonomy of wines.
- **Word study:** [G3631-oinos](../../word-studies/G3631-oinos.md)
- **Related study:** [TR-yayin-oinos](../../word-studies/TR-yayin-oinos.md) (LXX translation patterns)

### 2. καλός (kalos, G2570) — Qualitative "fine," not moral "good"

- **Form in v.10:** `τὸν καλὸν οἶνον` — A-ASM attributive, second-attributive position (article + adj. + noun; second article elided because of the first).
- **Semantic range:** Primarily *qualitatively excellent, fine, beautiful, of superior kind* — not ethically good (which is more typically ἀγαθός). Used of fish, cloth, timber, craftsmanship.
- **Why it matters:** The architriklinos is a professional taster making a **quality judgment**, not a moral one. His assessment certifies the supernatural product as superior within the category of wine. The LXX regularly uses καλός to render טוֹב (ṭôb) in contexts of quality ("it was good") rather than ethics.
- **Contrast in the verse:** `τὸν καλὸν οἶνον` (the fine wine) vs. `τὸν ἐλάσσω` (the inferior/lesser) — comparative adjective with the article standing alone as substantive ("the worse-kind").

### 3. μεθύω / μεθύσκομαι (methyō, G3184) — "drink freely" → "be drunk"

- **Form in v.10:** `μεθυσθῶσιν` — V-APS-3P, aorist **passive** subjunctive (from the related stem μεθύσκω / μεθυσθῆναι). In Koine the passive of μεθύσκω conveys the ingressive or resultative state: "have become drunk" / "have drunk their fill."
- **Lexical range:**
  - *Neutral:* to drink abundantly, to be sated with drink (LXX Gen 43:34; Song 5:1)
  - *Negative:* to be intoxicated, drunk (Eph 5:18 μὴ μεθύσκεσθε οἴνῳ)
- **Subjunctive with ὅταν:** `ὅταν μεθυσθῶσιν` = "whenever they *have* drunk freely" — indefinite-temporal clause. ὅταν + aorist subjunctive portrays an action *prior to* the main verb's time (gnomic present `τίθησιν`), so the picture is: "[at the point when] the guests *have gotten* to the merry stage, *then* [host brings out] the inferior."
- **Syntactic role:** The clause explains *why* hosts downgrade — not a comment on the Cana guests' actual state. It is the premise of a general custom.

### 4. ἀρχιτρίκλινος (architriklinos, G755) — Syntax of the Steward's Statement (v.10)

- **Noun:** literally "ruler of the three-couch (dining) room" — the master of ceremonies / head steward responsible for food, wine, and protocol.
- **Sentence structure of his speech:**

  ```
  [A] Πᾶς ἄνθρωπος  πρῶτον  τὸν καλὸν οἶνον  τίθησιν
      Every man    first    the fine wine    sets out       (gnomic present)

  [B] καὶ ὅταν μεθυσθῶσιν  τὸν ἐλάσσω
      and when they have drunk freely, the inferior        (ellipsis of τίθησιν)

  [C] σὺ   τετήρηκας  τὸν καλὸν οἶνον  ἕως ἄρτι.
      YOU  have-kept  the fine wine    until now            (perfect active)
  ```

  - **[A] and [B]** form a **gnomic proverb** — a universal truth expressed with the present indicative (`τίθησιν`). The second clause [B] omits the verb (verbal ellipsis) and substitutes the comparative adjective `τὸν ἐλάσσω` in the slot where `τὸν καλὸν οἶνον` stood.
  - **[C]** is an **asyndetic contrast** (no connective particle — not even δέ): the abrupt `σὺ` (emphatic nominative pronoun) directly opposes `Πᾶς ἄνθρωπος`. Greek frequently drops the pronoun subject; its *presence* is the contrast marker.
  - **Tense shift from [A] to [C]:** gnomic present (habit/rule) → perfect active indicative `τετήρηκας` (completed action with abiding result). The bridegroom's action *stands as a present reality* — "you stand having kept [it]."

- **The "joke" logic:**
  1. **Premise (proverb):** Everyone serves the good stuff first, then switches to cheap stuff once palates are dulled.
  2. **Implicit reason:** Palates are less discriminating after drinking; no one wastes premium wine on guests who won't notice.
  3. **Observed anomaly:** You have *kept* (τετήρηκας — perfect, deliberate retention) the fine wine until *now* (ἕως ἄρτι — right-now, immediate).
  4. **Punch:** The humor is the inversion — the steward assumes the bridegroom has violated economic sense; the reader knows the source is supernatural. `τετήρηκας` (perfect active) ironically credits the bridegroom with a deliberate stewardship he did not actually perform.

### 5. Verb Tenses Describing the Transformation

The miracle is narrated *entirely through tense choice*, not through explicit description of the act:

| Tense | Verb | Function |
|-------|------|----------|
| Aorist imperative | Γεμίσατε (v.7), Ἀντλήσατε (v.8) | Jesus' punctiliar commands |
| Aorist indicative | ἐγέμισαν (v.7), ἤνεγκαν (v.8), ἐγεύσατο (v.9) | Completed narrative events |
| **Perfect passive participle** | **γεγενημένον** (v.9) | **The completed transformation with abiding state** — "water having-become wine" (and remaining so) |
| **Pluperfect** | **ᾔδει / ᾔδεισαν** (v.9) | **State of knowledge (or ignorance) in the past** — οἶδα forms state-verbs; pluperfect = past-state |
| **Perfect active participle** | **ἠντληκότες** (v.9) | "the ones who had drawn" — still the drawers, their act continues to identify them |
| **Perfect active indicative** | **τετήρηκας** (v.10) | "you stand having kept" — completed action, result continuing into speaker's present |

**The pivot:** `γεγενημένον` (Perf Pass Ptcp) is the syntactic hinge of the whole pericope. Four features converge:
- **Perfect aspect:** the change is already accomplished.
- **Passive voice:** no human agent is named.
- **Participle mood:** subordinate to `ἐγεύσατο` — the steward doesn't witness the change; he tastes its *result*.
- **Predicate accusative `οἶνον`:** the identity of the transformed substance is the point.

Parse it as: *"the water [as something] having-been-made into wine."* The perfect-passive encodes "divine passive" resonance — an agentless change whose agent is obvious to the narrator but invisible to the steward (cf. ᾔδει in the next clause).

### 6. Historical Present — Narrative Drive (λέγει, φωνεῖ)

John uses present-tense `λέγει` (v.3, 4, 5, 7, 8, 10) and `φωνεῖ` (v.9) in narrative past contexts — the **historical (dramatic) present**. This pulls the reader into immediacy and pairs with the aorist event verbs for vividness. Parser output confirms these are V-PAI-3S forms embedded inside a past-time narrative.

## Clause Structure Notes

- **v.3 genitive absolute:** `ὑστερήσαντος οἴνου` — aorist participle in the genitive + genitive subject, functioning adverbially (temporal/causal).
- **v.5 indefinite relative clause:** `Ὅ τι ἂν λέγῃ ὑμῖν, ποιήσατε` — relative pronoun + ἄν + present subjunctive (indefinite) governed by aorist imperative main clause. "Whatever [sort of thing] he may say..."
- **v.6 circumstantial participles:** `κείμεναι` (Pres M/P Ptcp "set/standing") and `χωροῦσαι` (Pres Act Ptcp "containing") — both nominative feminine plural modifying `ὑδρίαι`, descriptive background.
- **v.9 asyndetic comparative clauses:** Three parallel clauses with `δέ` markers plotting knowledge (`ὡς δὲ ἐγεύσατο ... καὶ οὐκ ᾔδει ... οἱ δὲ διάκονοι ᾔδεισαν`) before the main verb `φωνεῖ` — a deliberate narrator's aside contrasting steward's ignorance with servants' knowledge.
- **v.10 asyndeton:** `σὺ τετήρηκας` — no conjunction, creating the punchline effect.
- **v.11 correlative structure:** `ἐποίησεν ... καὶ ἐφανέρωσεν ... καὶ ἐπίστευσαν` — three aorists forming the theological result chain: sign-done → glory-revealed → disciples-believed.
