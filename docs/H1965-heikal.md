## Lexicon Definition

(Aramaic) corresponding to H1964; a palace or temple: — palace, temple.

**Brown-Driver-Briggs:** Noun masculine, palace, temple (Assyrian loan-word; Biblical Hebrew H1964; also Palmyrene היכלא and הכלא). Forms: emphatic הֵיכְלָא (Ezra 4:14); construct הֵיכַל (Dan 4:29); suffixed הֵיכְלִי (Dan 4:4), הֵיכְלֵה (Dan 6:18).

Two semantic senses in BDB:
1. **Palace** of a king — Dan 4:4; 4:29; 5:5; 6:18; Ezra 4:14.
2. **Temple** — (a) in Jerusalem: Dan 5:2, 5:3; Ezra 5:14a; 5:15; 6:5 (2x); (b) in Babylon: Ezra 5:14b–c.

The word occurs exclusively in the Aramaic sections of the OT (Ezra 4:8–6:18; 7:12–26 and Daniel 2:4b–7:28). In Ezra the sense is almost entirely "temple" (the Jerusalem sanctuary and the Babylonian temple where its vessels were stored); in Daniel it is primarily "palace" (of Nebuchadnezzar, Belshazzar, and Darius), with the exception of Dan 5:2–3 where the Jerusalem temple vessels are in view.

## Semantic Categories

### 1. Royal Palace (residence of a pagan king)
- **Dan 4:4** — "I Nebuchadnezzar was at rest in mine house, and flourishing in my palace (heikal)"
- **Dan 4:29** — "he walked in the palace (heikal) of the kingdom of Babylon"
- **Dan 6:18** — "Then the king went to his palace (heikal), and passed the night fasting"

### 2. Pagan Temple (of Babylonian deity)
- **Ezra 5:14** — Nebuchadnezzar "brought them into the temple (heikal) of Babylon... Cyrus the king take out of the temple (heikal) of Babylon"

### 3. Temple in Jerusalem (house of YHWH)
- **Dan 5:2** — "the golden and silver vessels which his father Nebuchadnezzar had taken out of the temple (heikal) which [was] in Jerusalem"
- **Dan 5:3** — "the golden vessels that were taken out of the temple (heikal) of the house of God which [was] at Jerusalem"
- **Ezra 5:15** — "carry them into the temple (heikal) that [is] in Jerusalem"
- **Ezra 6:5** — "brought forth out of the temple (heikal) which [is] at Jerusalem... brought again unto the temple (heikal)"

### 4. Scene of Divine Judgment / Theophany
- **Dan 5:5** — "wrote over against the candlestick upon the plaister of the wall of the king's palace (heikal)" — the palace becomes the setting of the handwriting on the wall.
