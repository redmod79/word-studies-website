**Semantic continuity:** The LXX translates tsaddiq as dikaios with exceptional consistency (182/197 primary uses). The Hebrew adjective's dual meaning — describing both persons of moral rectitude and their standing before God — carries directly into the Greek. The antonymous pairing tsaddiq/râshâʻ (righteous/wicked) becomes dikaios/ásebēs or dikaios/hamartōlós in both LXX and NT.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The word describes persons of moral and covenant rectitude (Noah, Abel, Lot → Joseph, Simeon, Cornelius)
- The righteous/wicked antithesis (tsaddiq/râshâʻ → dikaios/hamartōlós) persists unchanged
- God is called righteous in both testaments, especially in his judgments (Exo 9:27; Rev 16:5)

**What shifts:**
- NT adds a messianic titular use — "the Just One" as a specific designation for Jesus (Acts 3:14; 7:52; 22:14)
- The Hab 2:4 quotation becomes a doctrinal centerpiece: faith as the basis for the righteous standing before God
- Rom 3:10 ("none righteous") applies tsaddiq-language universally to indict all humanity — a more sweeping use than typical OT application
