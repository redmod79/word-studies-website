**Semantic continuity:** The LXX rendering is remarkably stable. The compound προ-φήτης ("forth-speaker") captures the same core function as nābîyʼ (from the root nāba — "to bubble forth, announce"). Both emphasize the communicative role — one who delivers a message from God — rather than the predictive element. The PMI of 33.88 and 236 co-occurrences make this one of the most reliable role-title bridges in the entire LXX, comparable to the H3548 kōhēn → G2409 hiereus (priest) bridge.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The prophet as God's authorized spokesperson — one called and sent to deliver a divine message
- The pattern of prophets being rejected and persecuted (OT: Jer 26:20–23, 2Ch 36:16; NT: Mat 23:37, 1Th 2:15)
- The prophets as a body of authoritative Scripture ("the law and the prophets")
- The expectation of "the Prophet" as an eschatological figure (Deu 18:15 → Jhn 1:21, Acts 3:22)

**What shifts:**
- The NT adds a church-office sense: prophets as a recognized charismatic role alongside apostles and teachers (1Co 12:28, Eph 4:11), distinct from the classical OT calling
- The NT formula "spoken by the prophet" treats OT prophets primarily as Scripture authors whose words are now fulfilled, whereas the OT more often presents prophets as living confrontational figures
- The feminine form prophētis (G4398) appears in the NT (Luk 2:36 Anna, Rev 2:20 Jezebel) while the OT uses nᵉbîyʼāh (H5031) for prophetesses
