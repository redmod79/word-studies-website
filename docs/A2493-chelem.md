## Lexicon Definition

(Aramaic) From a root corresponding to H2492; a dream: — dream. The Aramaic cognate of the Hebrew noun חֲלוֹם (chalom, H2472), both derived from the root חלם "to dream." Confined in biblical Aramaic to Daniel, where it denotes a dream experienced as a medium of divine revelation to Gentile kings (Nebuchadnezzar in Dan 2, 4) and to Daniel himself (Dan 7:1). The word carries revelatory weight: in every occurrence the dream originates from or is decoded by God, and its interpretation (פְּשַׁר, pᵉshar) is the decisive issue.

## Semantic Categories

### 1. The Dream as Withheld Test (Nebuchadnezzar Demands Recall + Interpretation)
- **Dan 2:5** — "make known unto me **the dream**, with the interpretation thereof, ye shall be cut in pieces"
- **Dan 2:6** — "if ye shew **the dream**, and the interpretation thereof, ye shall receive of me gifts"
- **Dan 2:9** — "if ye will not make known unto me **the dream**, there is but one decree for you"

The king's demand that the Chaldeans recount the chêlem itself, not merely interpret it, frames chêlem as a divinely withheld content that only a revealing God can supply.

### 2. The Dream Revealed by the God of Heaven
- **Dan 2:26** — "Art thou able to make known unto me **the dream** which I have seen, and the interpretation thereof?"
- **Dan 2:28** — "there is a God in heaven that revealeth secrets... **thy dream**, and the visions of thy head upon thy bed"
- **Dan 2:36** — "This is **the dream**; and we will tell the interpretation thereof before the king"
- **Dan 2:45** — "the great God hath made known to the king what shall come to pass... and **the dream** is certain"

Here chêlem is tied explicitly to divine disclosure — the dream is "certain" (yatstsiyb) and its interpretation "sure" because its source is God.

### 3. The Dream as Source of Dread
- **Dan 4:5** — "I saw **a dream** which made me afraid, and the thoughts upon my bed and the visions of my head troubled me"
- **Dan 4:18** — "This **dream** I king Nebuchadnezzar have seen. Now thou, O Belteshazzar, declare the interpretation"
- **Dan 4:19** — "My lord, **the dream** be to them that hate thee, and the interpretation thereof to thine enemies"

The chêlem provokes fear in the dreamer even before its meaning is understood — the numinous quality precedes interpretation.

### 4. The Dream Told to Expert Interpreters
- **Dan 4:6** — "I made a decree to bring in all the wise men of Babylon before me, that they might make known unto me the interpretation of **the dream**"
- **Dan 4:7** — "I told **the dream** before them; but they did not make known unto me the interpretation thereof"
- **Dan 4:8** — "at the last Daniel came in before me... and before him I told **the dream**"
- **Dan 4:9** — "tell me the visions of **my dream** that I have seen, and the interpretation thereof"

chêlem becomes the verbal content told (ʼămar) to interpreters — the professional class fails while the Spirit-endowed Daniel succeeds.

### 5. The Dream as Daniel's Prophetic Gift
- **Dan 5:12** — "an excellent spirit... interpreting **of dreams**, and shewing of hard sentences... were found in the same Daniel"

The plural pointer (chelmîn) — Daniel's gift is generalized to dream-interpretation as a category.

### 6. The Dream as Received Prophetic Vision (Daniel Himself)
- **Dan 7:1** — "Daniel had **a dream** and visions of his head upon his bed: then he wrote **the dream**, and told the sum of the matters"

Notably Daniel, the interpreter of others' chalmîn in Dan 2 and 4, now receives his own. The chêlem is paired with chezvê rêʼsh ("visions of the head") and is written down — chêlem and prophetic apocalyptic converge.

**Key distinction:** chêlem and chălôwm are the same word in two dialects. The OT systematically uses chălôwm in Hebrew narrative and chêlem in Aramaic (Daniel's court narrative). Within Daniel, chêlem is the dream-event, while chêzêv is the specific visionary content perceived during it; the two are juxtaposed in Dan 2:28, 4:5, 4:9–10, and 7:1.
