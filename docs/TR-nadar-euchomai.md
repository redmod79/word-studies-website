**Semantic shift in translation:** In the LXX εὔχομαι retains the vow-making sense (it is the standard rendering of nadar and is paired with εὐχή for neder). By the NT period the word has drifted toward the more general "pray, wish, desire" — the specifically votive sense has thinned. Greek εὔχομαι becomes a catch-all for religious wishing, while the Hebrew verb's technical "conditional vow to God" specificity is lost. Where the NT wants the OT vow sense, it uses different vocabulary (e.g., Acts 18:18, 21:23 use εὐχή for the cut-hair vow).

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The word is consistently directed toward God (never human-to-human contract).
- The speech-act character persists: an utterance that binds the speaker.
- The obligation-to-perform ethic is carried forward (cf. Eccl 5:4 paralleled by Matt 5:33–37 against rash oaths).

**What shifts:**
- **Conditionality dissolves.** OT nadar is typically "If God does X, I will do Y." NT euchomai has no conditional structure — it expresses present desire or intercession.
- **Materiality fades.** Hebrew vows usually involved sacrifice, votive offering, Nazarite hair, or livestock. NT euchomai is verbal/attitudinal.
- **Specialization lost.** The Hebrew verb is a technical cultic term; the NT verb is general religious wishing. The Nazarite/votive sense survives in the NT mainly via εὐχή (G2171), not the verb.
- **Frequency collapse.** 28 OT uses → 7 NT uses. The concept is largely absorbed into προσεύχομαι (prayer) and into the theology of Christ's fulfilled self-offering.
