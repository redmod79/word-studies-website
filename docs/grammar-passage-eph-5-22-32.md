# Grammar Analysis: Ephesians 5:22–32

Paul's marriage-paraenesis, hinged to the broader "submit-to-one-another" participle of v.21 (ὑποτασσόμενοι ἀλλήλοις ἐν φόβῳ Χριστοῦ), and ending in the declaration that the whole matter — wife-to-husband and husband-to-wife — is a μέγα μυστήριον "into Christ and into the church" (v.32). The passage is built as a **paired chiastic exhortation**: wives-clause (vv.22–24) / husbands-clause (vv.25–30) / shared Genesis citation (v.31) / apostolic interpretation (v.32). Each clause is bound to the Christ–church analogy by comparative adverbs (ὡς, ὡς καί, καθὼς καί, οὕτως καί) and by the repeated lexical triad **κεφαλή / σῶμα / ἐκκλησία**. The passage reaches its grammatical climax in v.31's LXX-citation-turned-prophecy, then Paul's own gloss in v.32 — τὸ μυστήριον τοῦτο μέγα ἐστίν, ἐγὼ δὲ λέγω εἰς Χριστὸν καὶ εἰς τὴν ἐκκλησίαν.

## Key Grammatical Features

### 1. Ellipsis in v.22 — the wives-clause has no verb

- **Form:** The NA28/THGNT text of v.22 — Αἱ γυναῖκες τοῖς ἰδίοις ἀνδράσιν ὡς τῷ Κυρίῳ — contains **no finite verb and no participle**. The verb must be supplied from the immediately preceding v.21 **ὑποτασσόμενοι ἀλλήλοις ἐν φόβῳ Χριστοῦ** (V-PPP-NPM, present middle-passive participle nominative plural masculine of ὑποτάσσω).
- **Textual variation:** Byzantine witnesses read ὑποτάσσεσθε (V-PPM-2P middle imperative); other Byzantine witnesses ὑποτασσέσθωσαν (3P imperative). These are **later clarifications** of an elliptical original. The KJV's "submit yourselves" reflects that Byzantine supplement. The shortest reading (no verb) is accepted on internal grounds — it is the reading that best explains the rise of the others.
- **Grammatical consequence:** Without a verb of its own, v.22 is **grammatically dependent on v.21's participle**. This makes the mutual-submission participle of v.21 (ὑποτασσόμενοι ἀλλήλοις "submitting yourselves to one another") the **syntactic frame** for the entire marriage-discourse. The wives-exhortation is not introduced as a freestanding imperative but as a **specification** of the already-stated mutual-submission posture of believers in the Spirit (cf. v.18 πληροῦσθε ἐν πνεύματι, the whole v.19–22 string hanging from it as participial consequents).
- **Voice of the supplied ὑποτάσσω:** The participial form in v.21 is **ὑποτασσόμενοι** — middle-passive morphology, but the sense of ὑποτάσσω in NT ethical contexts is regularly **middle-voice reflexive**: "to order/arrange oneself under." This is not passive "be-subjected-to" (the external-agent passive of Rom 8:20 ὑπετάγη) but self-voluntary "take-rank-under." Same middle sense carries through v.24 ὑποτάσσεται (V-PPI-3S) and the supplied verb of v.22.

### 2. ὡς τῷ Κυρίῳ / ὡς καὶ ὁ Χριστός / καθὼς καὶ — the comparative-analogical adverbs

- **Form:** Four analogical comparisons are chained through the paraenesis, each using a comparative adverb with a datival or nominal correlate:
  - v.22 **ὡς** τῷ Κυρίῳ ("as [you would submit] to the Lord")
  - v.23 **ὡς καί** ὁ Χριστὸς κεφαλή ("just as also Christ [is] head")
  - v.24 **ὡς** ἡ ἐκκλησία ὑποτάσσεται … **οὕτως καί** αἱ γυναῖκες ("as the church submits … so also the wives")
  - v.25 **καθὼς καί** ὁ Χριστὸς ἠγάπησεν ("just as also Christ loved")
  - v.29 **καθὼς καί** ὁ Χριστὸς τὴν ἐκκλησίαν ("just as also Christ [does] the church")
- **Significance:**
  - **ὡς** (G5613) is a basic comparative adverb ("as, like, just as"), used here to introduce a **paradigm-comparison**: wife-to-husband is patterned *on* wife-(and-whole-church)-to-the-Lord. The dative τῷ Κυρίῳ in v.22 is the datival complement of the *unstated* ὑποτάσσω supplied from v.21 — the dative of the person-to-whom one submits.
  - **καθώς** (G2531) intensifies — "in-the-exact-manner-that, according-as." Where ὡς gives simple likeness, καθώς gives **conformity**: Christ's love is not merely compared to husbandly love; husbandly love is commanded to *conform itself to* Christ's pattern.
  - The paired **ὡς … οὕτως καί** construction of v.24 is a classical **correlative pair** (protasis-apodosis), typical in legislative/ethical discourse: *as* X *behaves*, *so-also* Y *must behave*. The οὕτως καί is the logical bridge that turns the indicative (the church submits) into the normative pattern for the addressees (so the wives).
  - The repeated καί after each comparative ("just-as *also*") does not add a second item; it is **adverbial-ascensive καί** ("just as *indeed*, just as *truly*"), intensifying the comparison by asserting the reality of the compared term.

### 3. ἀνήρ ἐστιν κεφαλὴ τῆς γυναικός (v.23) — anarthrous predicate nominative

- **Form:** ἀνήρ (N-NSM, **anarthrous**) is the subject; ἐστιν (V-PAI-3S) is the copula; κεφαλή (N-NSF, **anarthrous**) is the predicate nominative. The genitive τῆς γυναικός modifies κεφαλή as a **relational genitive** ("head of the wife"). The parallel clause Χριστὸς [ἐστιν] κεφαλὴ τῆς ἐκκλησίας has identical structure: **articular** subject (ὁ Χριστός) + [elided copula] + **anarthrous** predicate κεφαλή + **relational genitive**.
- **Significance:**
  - Both κεφαλή instances are anarthrous in predicate position. This is standard for **qualitative/class-assigning predicate nominatives** (Colwell's rule context, BDF §273): the predicate describes the *kind of thing* the subject is, not uniquely identifies it. "A husband *is* head-of-wife" describes the relational role; it does not assign definite-article identity "*the* head."
  - The two κεφαλή-clauses are syntactically **parallel**: ἀνήρ : γυνή :: Χριστός : ἐκκλησία. The **analogy is structural, not lexical equivalence** — the genitive relation is identical (*kephalē* + genitive-of-reference), but the analogy asserts similarity of *relational role*, not identity of *metaphysical rank*. Husbands are not divine; the analogy is about the *shape* of the relationship.
  - αὐτὸς σωτὴρ τοῦ σώματος closes v.23: αὐτός (P-NSM emphatic pronoun) + anarthrous predicate σωτήρ + relational genitive τοῦ σώματος. The emphatic αὐτός restricts "saviour of the body" to Christ alone — *he and no other*. This is the only element of the analogy that **breaks symmetry**: the husband-wife clause does not get a parallel "αὐτὸς σωτὴρ" line. Paul deliberately stops short of applying σωτήρ to husbands.

### 4. ἀγαπᾶτε (v.25) — present imperative grounded in aorist indicative

- **Form:** ἀγαπᾶτε is **V-PAM-2P** (present active imperative 2nd plural of ἀγαπάω). The grounding καθώς-clause uses two **aorist indicatives**: ἠγάπησεν (V-AAI-3S, aorist active indicative 3rd sg. of ἀγαπάω) and παρέδωκεν (V-AAI-3S, aorist active indicative 3rd sg. of παραδίδωμι, "handed over, delivered up").
- **Significance:**
  - The **present imperative** commands **durative / continuous / habitual action**: not "love [at some moment]" but "be-loving, keep-loving, make-loving-your-ongoing-mode." Same force as John 15:12 ἀγαπᾶτε ἀλλήλους and 1 Cor 16:14 ἐν ἀγάπῃ γινέσθω. Contrast the **aorist imperative** (ἀγαπήσατε) which would command a single decisive act.
  - The **aorist indicatives** of the καθώς-clause refer to the **historical act of the cross**: Christ's loving-the-church and giving-himself-up-for-her are viewed as a single completed whole. The aorist is **the aspect proper for the cross-event** — a definite, once-for-all act in redemptive history (cf. Rom 5:8 Χριστὸς ὑπὲρ ἡμῶν ἀπέθανεν; Gal 2:20 τοῦ ἀγαπήσαντός με καὶ παραδόντος ἑαυτὸν ὑπὲρ ἐμοῦ — same word-pair).
  - The aspectual contrast is doctrinally weighted: **the aorist indicative of the cross grounds the present imperative of the life**. The unrepeatable once-for-all act of Christ (ἠγάπησεν … παρέδωκεν) becomes the ongoing norm of the husband (ἀγαπᾶτε). The grammar itself says: *the definite-past act is the continuing-present pattern*.
  - παραδίδωμι ("hand over, deliver up") is the standard word for the handing-over of Jesus to death (Matt 17:22; 20:18; Rom 4:25; 8:32 **τὸν ἰδίον υἱὸν οὐκ ἐφείσατο ἀλλ' ὑπὲρ ἡμῶν πάντων παρέδωκεν αὐτόν**; Gal 2:20). Its object here is **ἑαυτόν** — Christ **handed over himself**, active voluntary self-offering (against any adoptionist or passive-victim reading).
- **Word study:** [G25-agapao](../../word-studies/G25-agapao.md); [G26-agape](../../word-studies/G26-agape.md); gap flagged for **G3860-paradidōmi**.
- **Cross-reference:** [mood-aorist-imperative](../greek/mood-aorist-imperative.md) (for the aorist-vs-present imperative contrast); gap flagged for canonical **present-imperative-continuing-command** entry.

### 5. The ἵνα-chain of vv. 26–27 — telescoped purposive subordination

- **Form:** Three ἵνα-clauses, each introducing an aorist subjunctive (or final present subjunctive), chained in nested purpose:
  - v.26 **ἵνα** αὐτὴν **ἁγιάσῃ** (V-AAS-3S, aorist active subjunctive of ἁγιάζω) — purpose of παρέδωκεν (v.25)
  - v.27 **ἵνα** **παραστήσῃ** αὐτὸς ἑαυτῷ ἔνδοξον τὴν ἐκκλησίαν (V-AAS-3S aorist active subjunctive of παρίστημι) — purpose advanced to its further stage
  - v.27c ἀλλ' **ἵνα** **ᾖ** ἁγία καὶ ἄμωμος (V-PAS-3S present active subjunctive of εἰμί) — result/purpose of παραστήσῃ
- **Significance:**
  - The **three ἵνα-clauses form a telescoping purpose-chain**: [Christ loved and gave himself] → (in-order-that-he-might-sanctify) → (in-order-that-he-might-present) → (in-order-that-she-might-be holy-and-blameless). Each ἵνα moves the purpose **one step further along** — the cross aims at sanctification; sanctification aims at presentation; presentation aims at a state of holiness-and-blamelessness.
  - The first two ἵνα use **aorist subjunctive** (single decisive acts of sanctifying / presenting); the third uses **present subjunctive** ᾖ (continuous state: "in-order-that-she-*might-be* holy"). The aspect shift from aorist (acts) to present (state) is deliberate: the acts produce a **standing state**.
  - The **two aorist subjunctives after ἵνα** both show purpose. Paul's normal syntax — ἵνα + aorist subjunctive is the default **purpose/final construction** in Koine (Wallace 471–474; BDF §369). No other interpretation is grammatically available.
  - παραστήσῃ ἑαυτῷ ("he might present *to himself*") uses the **reflexive dative** ἑαυτῷ — Christ is both the presenter and the one-to-whom-the-church-is-presented. The imagery is **bridal** (cf. 2 Cor 11:2 παρθένον ἁγνὴν παραστῆσαι τῷ Χριστῷ; Rev 19:7 ὁ γάμος τοῦ ἀρνίου) — the bridegroom presenting the bride to himself. The reflexive ἑαυτῷ is the grammatical marker of the closed-circle: Christ is not presenting the church to the Father but to himself.
- **Cross-reference:** gap flagged for canonical **hina-purpose-chain** / **telescoped-purpose-subjunctives** entry; gap flagged for canonical **aorist-to-present-subjunctive-aspect-shift** entry.

### 6. καθαρίσας (v.26) — aorist participle of attendant circumstance or means

- **Form:** καθαρίσας is **V-AAP-NSM**, aorist active participle nominative singular masculine of καθαρίζω ("cleanse"). It agrees with the subject of the aorist subjunctive ἁγιάσῃ (Christ). Tense: aorist, both participle and main verb. Dative τῷ λουτρῷ τοῦ ὕδατος is **dative of instrument/means** ("by the washing-of-water"); the following ἐν ῥήματι is **ἐν of instrument/accompaniment** ("with/in [connection with] the word").
- **Significance:**
  - **Aorist participle + aorist main verb**: the standard rule (Wallace 614–615, 622–630) is that an aorist participle preceding an aorist main verb expresses **antecedent action** ("having cleansed, he might sanctify") or **coincident action / means** ("he might sanctify *by cleansing*"). Both readings are grammatically available; the specific reading depends on context and on the logical relation of *cleansing* and *sanctifying* in Paul's theological vocabulary.
  - **Antecedent reading**: cleansing is the logically prior event; sanctification follows. Order: cleansing-at-the-washing-of-water → subsequent sanctification.
  - **Means reading**: cleansing *is* the means by which sanctification is accomplished. "That he might sanctify her *by cleansing* her with the washing of water in the word."
  - **Double instrumental**: the two instrumental phrases stack — τῷ λουτρῷ τοῦ ὕδατος (dative-instrument, the washing-of-water) and ἐν ῥήματι (ἐν-instrument, the word). Grammatically both are instruments of the participle καθαρίσας; semantically "washing" and "word" are the paired means, not sequential.
  - τοῦ ὕδατος is a **genitive of content/material**: "the washing consisting of water" — the washing whose substance *is* water. This is distinct from an objective genitive (*washing of-the-water*) which would be odd here.
- **Cross-reference:** gap flagged for canonical **aorist-participle-coincident-vs-antecedent** entry; word study gap flagged for **G2511-katharizo** (exists: [G2511-katharizo](../../word-studies/G2511-katharizo.md)); gap flagged for **G3067-loutron** word study.

### 7. ὀφείλουσιν … ἀγαπᾶν (v.28) — obligation verb + complementary infinitive

- **Form:** ὀφείλουσιν is **V-PAI-3P** (present active indicative 3rd plural of ὀφείλω, "owe, ought, be obligated"); ἀγαπᾶν is **V-PAN** (present active infinitive of ἀγαπάω) functioning as the **complementary infinitive** to ὀφείλουσιν.
- **Significance:**
  - ὀφείλω + complementary infinitive is the standard Koine construction for **moral obligation** ("ought, owe, are-duty-bound to…"). It is **stronger than δεῖ** (impersonal "it-is-necessary"): ὀφείλω imports a **debt-metaphor** — the husband *owes* love to his wife as a standing obligation, not merely is-obliged-to in some general moral sense. Same debt-sense in Rom 13:8 μηδενὶ μηδὲν ὀφείλετε εἰ μὴ τὸ ἀλλήλους ἀγαπᾶν.
  - The **present infinitive** ἀγαπᾶν (rather than aorist ἀγαπῆσαι) preserves the same durative aspect as v.25's ἀγαπᾶτε — ongoing, habitual loving, not a single decisive act.
  - The οὕτως ("so, thus") at the head of v.28 resumes the pattern of Christ's love just described (vv. 25b–27) and applies it to "the husbands *also*" (καὶ οἱ ἄνδρες), with the ascensive καί again marking comparative conformity.
- **Cross-reference:** gap flagged for canonical **opheilō-complementary-infinitive** entry.

### 8. ὁ ἀγαπῶν (v.28) — articular present participle as substantival class-subject

- **Form:** ὁ ἀγαπῶν is **T-NSM + V-PAP-NSM** (definite article + present active participle nominative singular masculine of ἀγαπάω), functioning **substantivally** — "the-one-who-loves." The main verb of the clause is ἀγαπᾷ (V-PAI-3S): "the-one-who-loves his own wife loves himself."
- **Significance:**
  - **Articular participle = substantival** (Wallace 619–621). The article converts the participle from a verbal modifier into a noun-equivalent naming a class or type. Same construction as John 3:16 ὁ πιστεύων, Rom 1:17 ὁ … δίκαιος ἐκ πίστεως ζήσεται, 1 John 4:7 πᾶς ὁ ἀγαπῶν ἐκ τοῦ θεοῦ γεγέννηται.
  - The **present participle** gives *durative / characterizing* aspect: not "the-one-who-has-loved-[once]" but "the-one-who-is-continually-loving" — a settled, ongoing orientation. The grammatical force is *generic class*: any man whose orientation-toward-his-wife is love is (by the principle of v.28) loving himself.
  - The clause ὁ ἀγαπῶν τὴν ἑαυτοῦ γυναῖκα **ἑαυτὸν** ἀγαπᾷ is a **gnomic / sententious present indicative**, expressing a standing principle rather than a report of an event. This feeds into the gnomic pattern that continues through vv. 29–31.
- **Cross-reference:** gap flagged for canonical **articular-participle-substantival** entry.

### 9. οὐδεὶς … ἐμίσησεν, ἀλλὰ ἐκτρέφει καὶ θάλπει (v.29) — gnomic aorist vs. habitual present

- **Form:** ἐμίσησεν is **V-AAI-3S** (aorist active indicative 3rd sg. of μισέω); ἐκτρέφει (V-PAI-3S of ἐκτρέφω, "nourish, bring-up") and θάλπει (V-PAI-3S of θάλπω, "cherish, warm, keep-warm") are both **present actives**. ποτε ("ever") modifies ἐμίσησεν.
- **Significance:**
  - **Gnomic aorist**: ἐμίσησεν + οὐδεὶς … ποτε ("no-one-ever-hated") is a **gnomic aorist of universal negation** — a timeless generalization expressed in the aorist because the principle is viewed as a **single complete truth** about human nature. Standard Koine idiom (Wallace 562; BDF §333).
  - **Habitual/iterative presents** ἐκτρέφει καὶ θάλπει describe the ongoing pattern that replaces the hated alternative. Aspectual contrast: **aorist for the rejected timeless generalization; present for the positive ongoing habit**. The same aspectual contrast recurs in Paul's ethical argumentation (Rom 6:14; 1 Cor 13).
  - The two verbs **ἐκτρέφω + θάλπω** form a **hendiadys** — "nourish and cherish" = "lovingly-sustain." θάλπω is the rarer word ("keep-warm, foster like a mother-bird," cf. 1 Thess 2:7 ὡς ἐὰν τροφὸς **θάλπῃ** τὰ ἑαυτῆς τέκνα — maternal imagery). Paul selects the pair to import the nurturing-domestic register into the Christ-church analogy.
- **Cross-reference:** gap flagged for canonical **gnomic-aorist** entry.

### 10. v.31 — the LXX-citation as legislative-prophetic future

- **Form:** v.31 is Paul's near-verbatim quotation of **Gen 2:24 LXX**:
  - **LXX:** ἕνεκεν τούτου καταλείψει ἄνθρωπος τὸν πατέρα αὐτοῦ καὶ τὴν μητέρα αὐτοῦ καὶ προσκολληθήσεται πρὸς τὴν γυναῖκα αὐτοῦ καὶ ἔσονται οἱ δύο εἰς σάρκα μίαν.
  - **Paul:** ἀντὶ τούτου καταλείψει ἄνθρωπος τὸν πατέρα καὶ τὴν μητέρα καὶ προσκολληθήσεται πρὸς τὴν γυναῖκα αὐτοῦ, καὶ ἔσονται οἱ δύο εἰς σάρκα μίαν.
  - **Differences**: (1) ἀντὶ τούτου (G473 ἀντί) for LXX ἕνεκεν τούτου — Paul's ἀντί ("for this reason, in exchange/return for this") is a less common rendering of the Hebrew עַל־כֵּן ("therefore") than ἕνεκεν, but its preposition-type ("in-return-for, because-of") fits; (2) Paul drops both instances of αὐτοῦ after πατέρα and μητέρα, keeping only the third αὐτοῦ (after γυναῖκα).
- **Verbs:** Three **futures**:
  - καταλείψει (V-FAI-3S of καταλείπω, "leave-behind")
  - προσκολληθήσεται (V-FPI-3S of προσκολλάω, "be-glued-to"; **future passive** with middle/reflexive sense, "shall-cleave-to")
  - ἔσονται (V-FDI-3P of εἰμί, "they-shall-be")
- **Significance:**
  - The LXX futures render the Hebrew **imperfect / prefix-conjugation** of Gen 2:24 (יַעֲזָב … וְדָבַק … וְהָיוּ) as **gnomic / legislative futures** in Greek — a futurum legis, expressing a standing rule rather than a prediction of a specific event. This is the Koine Greek equivalent of the modal Hebrew imperfect ("a man *shall* leave…" = "a man is-to-leave, a man does-leave as-a-matter-of-principle").
  - προσκολληθήσεται is **future passive in form, reflexive/middle in sense** — "shall-glue-himself-to, shall-cleave-to." This is a **deponent-like use** of the future passive, a frequent Koine pattern where certain verbs of union, motion, and affect take passive morphology with active/middle semantics (compare ἀποκριθήσεται "he-shall-answer," φανήσεται "he-shall-appear"). The Hebrew is Qal וְדָבַק — **active**; the LXX renders the active Hebrew by the morphologically-passive future that carries reflexive force.
  - ἔσονται οἱ δύο εἰς σάρκα μίαν — ἔσονται is **future middle/deponent** of εἰμί; εἰς + accusative σάρκα μίαν is the **predicate εἰς construction** — a Semitism imported from the Hebrew לְבָשָׂר אֶחָד (lamed-of-product), where the preposition marks **transformation/result**, not direction. The idiom "X shall-be-*into* Y" = "X shall-become Y" / "X shall-turn-into Y." Same εἰς-of-result in Matt 21:42 (ἐγενήθη εἰς κεφαλὴν γωνίας), Acts 4:11, 1 Cor 4:13, 6:16 (same Gen 2:24 citation applied to *porneia*).
  - The number-concord οἱ δύο … εἰς σάρκα μίαν is pointed: **two → one**. The numeral δύο is not treated as a distributive ("each of the two…") but as a collective subject *becoming* a single entity.
- **Cross-reference:** gap flagged for canonical **legislative-future / futurum-legis** entry; gap flagged for canonical **predicate-εἰς-Semitic** entry; gap flagged for canonical **future-passive-reflexive-sense** entry; word study gap flagged for **G4347-proskollaō** and **G2641-kataleipō**.

### 11. τὸ μυστήριον τοῦτο μέγα ἐστίν (v.32) — near-demonstrative framing and anaphoric reference

- **Form:** τὸ μυστήριον (T-NSN + N-NSN) is the **articular subject**; τοῦτο (D-NSN, near demonstrative) is **predicate-attributive** to μυστήριον, pointing back to *the immediately preceding* Gen 2:24 citation. μέγα (A-NSN) is the predicate adjective ("great"); ἐστίν is the copula. Word order: article-noun-demonstrative is **second-attributive position** for the demonstrative.
- **Significance:**
  - τοῦτο is **anaphoric** — it points **backward**, not forward. Its antecedent is the Gen 2:24 citation of v.31. Grammatically, near-demonstratives (οὗτος / αὕτη / τοῦτο) point to the nearer (usually immediately preceding) referent; far-demonstratives (ἐκεῖνος) point to the more remote. The phrase is therefore "**this mystery**" = "**this [just-cited Gen 2:24 word] is a great mystery.**"
  - μέγα is **anarthrous predicate adjective** — qualitative/class-assigning ("is great [in magnitude/significance]"), not "*the* great [mystery]" (which would require the article). The greatness is predicated, not assumed.
  - The δέ of the next clause (ἐγὼ δὲ λέγω) is **contrastive/clarifying**: "**but** I — I am *speaking* [i.e., *applying it*] with reference to Christ and the church." The δέ does not deny the plain sense of Gen 2:24 as applied to human marriage; it signals that Paul's own apostolic *application* (λέγω, first-person emphatic) is to a second referent — the Christ-church union.
  - **ἐγὼ δὲ λέγω** with emphatic **ἐγώ** (P-1NS) is a **formula of apostolic interpretation** — the first-person-emphatic plus λέγω with εἰς + accusative marks *Paul's own reading of the scriptural text*. Compare Rom 10:18–20 where Paul cites OT texts and marks his own commentary with ἀλλὰ λέγω / ἐγὼ λέγω formulas. The emphatic ἐγώ is not self-referential vanity; it is **apostolic-exegetical warrant** — Paul naming himself as the authoritative interpreter of the cited text.
  - **εἰς Χριστὸν καὶ εἰς τὴν ἐκκλησίαν** — λέγω + εἰς + accusative = "I speak with reference to / with application to / pointing toward." This εἰς is the **εἰς of reference or direction-of-application**, not goal-εἰς (as in v.31's σάρκα μίαν, where εἰς is transformative). Paul double-names the referent (εἰς Χριστόν … εἰς τὴν ἐκκλησίαν) — one εἰς per member of the analogical pair. The double εἰς **grammatically preserves both halves of the Christ-church union** as distinct referents of the citation.
- **Word study:** [G3466-mysterion](../../word-studies/G3466-mysterion.md); [G1577-ekklesia](../../word-studies/G1577-ekklesia.md); [G2776-kephale](../../word-studies/G2776-kephale.md); [G4561-sarx](../../word-studies/G4561-sarx.md).
- **Cross-reference:** gap flagged for canonical **anaphoric-near-demonstrative** entry; gap flagged for canonical **legō-eis-apostolic-interpretation-formula** entry; gap flagged for canonical **emphatic-nominative-pronoun-egō** entry.

### 12. μέλη ἐσμὲν τοῦ σώματος αὐτοῦ (v.30) — predicate noun with partitive genitive

- **Form:** μέλη (N-NPN, "members") is the **anarthrous predicate-nominative plural** of ἐσμέν (V-PAI-1P, present active indicative 1st plural of εἰμί). τοῦ σώματος αὐτοῦ is **partitive genitive** ("members *of his body*"). ὅτι (G3754) is causal — "**because** we are members of his body."
- **Significance:**
  - **Partitive genitive** (Wallace 84–86): σώματος is the **whole**; μέλη are the **parts**. "We *are* parts-of-the-whole-that-is-his-body." Same idiom as 1 Cor 6:15 μέλη Χριστοῦ ἐστιν, 1 Cor 12:27 ὑμεῖς δέ ἐστε σῶμα Χριστοῦ καὶ μέλη ἐκ μέρους. The partitive-genitive here **anchors the analogy**: husbands loving their own bodies (v.28 ὡς τὰ ἑαυτῶν σώματα) is right because the church (which the husband's love mirrors) is Christ's body (whole) of which we are members (parts).
  - The **ὅτι** (v.30) is the second ὅτι-grounding in the passage (first at v.23). Both ground the analogy-claims in **Christ-church ontology**:
    - v.23 ὅτι ἀνήρ ἐστιν κεφαλή … ὡς καὶ ὁ Χριστός (grounding the wives' submission)
    - v.30 ὅτι μέλη ἐσμὲν τοῦ σώματος αὐτοῦ (grounding the husbands' self-love / church-love)
  - The 1st-person **ἐσμέν** shifts the referent — Paul stops saying "Christ … the church" (third-person) and drops into "**we** are members." This is a **register shift** from didactic third-person analogy to **confessional first-person inclusion** — the author-and-audience as the members in question. The shift prepares the reader for the universal-bride imagery of v.32.
- **Word study:** [G4983-soma](../../word-studies/G4983-soma.md) (gap flagged if absent); gap flagged for **G3196-melos**.
- **Cross-reference:** gap flagged for canonical **partitive-genitive** entry (also relevant to Col 1:15 πρωτότοκος πάσης κτίσεως discussion).

### Repeated lexical and syntactic features binding the analogy

- **The triad κεφαλή / σῶμα / ἐκκλησία** appears across vv. 23, 24, 29, 30 — the same triad as Col 1:18 αὐτός ἐστιν ἡ κεφαλὴ τοῦ σώματος, τῆς ἐκκλησίας and Eph 1:22–23.
- **ἐκκλησία (G1577) occurs six times** (vv. 23, 24, 25, 27, 29, 32) — the highest local density in Ephesians.
- **σῶμα / σάρξ doublet**: σῶμα (v.23 σωτὴρ τοῦ σώματος; v.28 τὰ ἑαυτῶν σώματα; v.30 τοῦ σώματος αὐτοῦ) pairs with σάρξ (v.29 τὴν ἑαυτοῦ σάρκα; v.31 εἰς σάρκα μίαν). The σῶμα-σάρξ alternation is deliberate: σῶμα in the Christ-church analogy (ecclesial organism), σάρξ in the Genesis-citation register (the "one flesh" of creation order). Paul thereby **binds the two frames**: the σάρξ-union of Gen 2:24 = the σῶμα-union of Christ-and-church.
- **ἑαυτοῦ / ἑαυτῶν cluster** (vv. 25, 27, 28×3, 29, 31): the reflexive pronoun recurs as the structural marker of the **self-referential loop** that the analogy turns on — loving-the-wife *is* loving-oneself *because* she and he are *one*, *as* Christ-and-church are one.
- **Four comparative-adverb hinges** (ὡς v.22, 23, 24, 28; καθώς v.25, 29; οὕτως καί v.24, 28) structurally fasten every exhortation to the Christ-church referent.

### The final-position position of the citation

Paul does not begin with Gen 2:24 and derive the analogy; he **begins with the analogy and ends with the citation**. The Genesis quotation is *the conclusion* to which the paraenesis has been building. This placement makes v.32's τὸ μυστήριον τοῦτο grammatically and rhetorically **anaphoric to v.31's citation** — "this [Gen 2:24 word] is a great mystery." The near-demonstrative τοῦτο cannot grammatically reach back past the citation to some more remote referent; the antecedent of τοῦτο is the Genesis word itself.

## Textual Notes

- **v.22** — NA28/THGNT print the shortest reading (no verb). The verb ὑποτάσσεσθε is absent from ℙ⁴⁶, B, and the earliest witnesses; it is present in Byzantine witnesses and in early translations. The shorter reading is the critical text's choice because (a) it is the reading that *explains the rise of the other readings* (copyists supplying a verb), and (b) it is attested in the earliest and most diverse witnesses. The KJV's "submit yourselves" reflects the Byzantine gloss.
- **v.30** — some Byzantine witnesses add ἐκ τῆς σαρκὸς αὐτοῦ καὶ ἐκ τῶν ὀστέων αὐτοῦ ("of his flesh and of his bones," KJV "of his flesh, and of his bones"). NA28/THGNT omit the addition on early-manuscript grounds (ℙ⁴⁶, א*, A, B, etc.). The addition is a scribal assimilation to Gen 2:23 LXX ὀστοῦν ἐκ τῶν ὀστέων μου καὶ σὰρξ ἐκ τῆς σαρκός μου. The shorter reading leaves v.30's μέλη ἐσμὲν τοῦ σώματος αὐτοῦ more clearly grounded in ecclesial body-language (Paul's standard) rather than in a second Genesis-allusion.
- **v.31** — Paul's ἀντὶ τούτου for LXX ἕνεκεν τούτου is either an independent rendering of the Hebrew עַל־כֵּן ("therefore, on-account-of-this") or Paul's own paraphrase. Both ἀντί and ἕνεκεν can render the same Hebrew in different LXX traditions; the variation is not substantive.
