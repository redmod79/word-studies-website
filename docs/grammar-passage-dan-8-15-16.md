# Grammar Analysis: Daniel 8:15–16 (Gabriel Commissioned)

**Context-neutral passage analysis.** This entry describes the Hebrew morphology and clause architecture of Daniel 8:15–16 — the hinge verses that close Daniel's narrative witness of the 2,300-evening-morning oracle (vv. 13–14) and open the angelic commissioning block (vv. 16–26). The two verses introduce the **interpreter-figure Gabriel** and command **understanding of the marʾeh**. They are the pivot where three features converge: (a) the perception-vocabulary alternation between **ḥāzôn** and **marʾeh**, (b) the introduction of Gabriel as named interpreter, and (c) the Hiphil imperative of בין initiating Daniel's instruction.

Five grammatical features drive the analysis:

1. **Compound wayhî + bə-infinitive-construct temporal frame** — v.15 opens with *wa-yhî bi-rʾōṯî ʾănî Dāniyyēʾl ʾeṯ-he-ḥāzôn* ("and it was, when I saw, I Daniel, the vision"), nesting a subordinate infinitive-construct clause inside the wayhî macro-syntactic opener. This is the canonical Danielic vision-framing pattern (cf. 8:2; 9:21; 10:7).
2. **Piel wayyiqtol 1cs with paragogic-ה** — *wā-ʾăḇaqšāh ḇînāh* ("and I earnestly sought understanding"). The Piel stem of בקשׁ *intensifies* the Qal "seek" into deliberate pursuit; the paragogic-ה is Danielic long-form 1cs wayq (cf. 8:13 וָאֶשְׁמְעָה, 8:17 וָאֶפְּלָה).
3. **The marʾeh/chazon lexical switch** — v.15 couples *he-ḥāzôn* (object of seeing, accusative with ʾeṯ) with *kə-marʾēh ḡāḇer* (construct simile describing the standing figure); v.16 names *ham-marʾeh* (definite direct object) as the thing Gabriel is to explain. The two nouns are not interchangeable: ḥāzôn = the whole prophetic-vision complex; marʾeh = the specific visible element seen. This distribution is decisive for interpreting vv. 17–26.
4. **Hiphil imperative 2ms הָבֵן with prepositional-dative addressee** — *hāḇēn lə-hallāz ʾeṯ-ham-marʾeh* ("make this-one understand the marʾeh") — Hiphil (causative) of בין addressed to Gabriel *about* Daniel (the demonstrative pronoun הַלָּז as the one-to-be-instructed) with the marʾeh as the direct object (ʾeṯ-marker).
5. **Hēnnēh + active participle presentative construction** — *wə-hinnēh ʿōmēḏ ləneḡdî kə-marʾēh ḡāḇer* ("and behold, standing before me like the appearance of a mighty man") — the classic vision-report presentative pattern (hinneh + Qal ptca) introducing a new visible referent without finite verb.

**Indefiniteness of כְּמַרְאֵה־גָבֶר.** Both nouns in v.15 are absolute-without-article, so the chain is morphologically indefinite: "like *an* appearance of *a* gever." This is not the titular definite ("the appearance of *the* man"), as contrasted with v.26 מַרְאֵה הָעֶרֶב וְהַבֹּקֶר where both absolutes are articled. V.15's comparative particle כְּ ("like, as") further softens the identification: Daniel does not say "a gever stood" but "one stood, like a gever-appearance." The grammar marks a **visual simile**, not a definite identification.

## Key Grammatical Features

### 1. וַיְהִי + בִּרְאֹתִי — The wayhî-InfCon temporal frame

**Morphology.**
- וַיְהִי — Verb.Qal.Wayq.3ms of היה; the canonical narrative-opener "and it came to pass."
- בִּרְאֹתִי — Prep בְּ + Qal.InfCon of ראה + 1cs pronominal suffix as logical subject. This is the standard BH temporal-subordinate construction: בְּ + infinitive-construct with suffixed subject = "when/while I saw" (Joüon §124; Waltke-O'Connor §36.2.2b).

**Function.** The wayhî opener + bə-InfCon subordinate forms a two-clause temporal wrapping: macro-syntactic frame (clause 1, "and it was") + subordinate "when"-clause (clause 2, "when I was seeing the vision"). This is Daniel's signature vision-hinge pattern. Compare:

- Dan 8:2 *wā-ʾereh ba-ḥāzôn wa-yhî bi-rʾōṯî* — same structure, reversed order, opening the vision itself.
- Dan 9:21 *wə-ʿôḏ ʾănî məḏabbēr…wə-hāʾîš Gaḇrîʾēl ʾăšer rāʾîṯî ḇe-ḥāzôn ba-ttəḥillâ muʿāp̄ bîʿāp̄ nōḡēaʿ ʾēlay kə-ʿēṯ minḥaṯ-ʿāreḇ* — Gabriel-returns frame, same "while I was speaking" temporal nesting.
- Dan 10:7 *wə-rāʾîṯî ʾănî Dāniyyēʾl ləḇaddî* — with emphatic *ʾănî Dāniyyēʾl* (same as 8:15).

The emphatic apposition **אֲנִי דָנִיֵּאל** (ʾănî Dāniyyēʾl, "I, Daniel") is *casus pendens* / left-dislocation of the pronominal-subject of the infinitive-construct (Joüon-Muraoka §177). It is semantically redundant (the 1cs suffix on בִּרְאֹתִי already encodes the subject), but pragmatically it places the seer's identity in focus — a personal authentication marker. Daniel uses this form at 7:15, 8:1, 8:15, 9:2, 10:2, 10:7, 12:5 as a first-person authentication/signature.

See [vision-introduction-formulas](../hebrew/vision-introduction-formulas.md) for the full canonical distribution of the wayhî-seeing-frame across the Hebrew Bible.

### 2. וָאֲבַקְשָׁה בִינָה — Piel intensification and paragogic-ה wayyiqtol

**Morphology.**
- וָאֲבַקְשָׁה — Conj + **Piel.Wayq.1cs** of בקשׁ (H1245) with **paragogic ה** (long-form 1cs wayq — the -āh ending rather than bare 1cs wayq vowel pattern). See parser output: `Verb.Piel.Wayq.1s`.
- בִינָה — Noun.fs.Abs of בינה (H998) "understanding, discernment." *Not* a verb form — ETCBC tags it Noun.fs.Abs. It is cognate to the verb בין (H995) but is the substantive noun.

**The Piel force.** The Qal of בקשׁ is not attested in Biblical Hebrew (the root occurs ~225× but always in Piel); the Piel is the lexical base-stem for this root, meaning "seek, look for, pursue, require." See [H995-biyn](../../word-studies/H995-biyn.md) for the related cognitive root (the verb "to understand," not the noun "understanding").

While the Piel here is not *contrastively* intensified vs. a Qal counterpart (because no Qal exists), the stem itself carries an inherent *volitional/deliberative* sense — active pursuit rather than passive reception. Compare the parallel construction with דרשׁ (H1875, "seek, inquire") which has both Qal "seek-after" and Piel "search-out intensively." The choice of בקשׁ (Piel) over דרשׁ (Qal) registers **deliberate inquiry** — Daniel is actively seeking to grasp what he has just seen, not merely waiting for revelation.

**The paragogic-ה wayyiqtol.** BH 1cs wayyiqtol has two forms:
- Short: וָאֶבְקֵשׁ (1cs wayq with characteristic-vowel pattern but no suffix) — the bare form.
- Long (paragogic): וָאֲבַקְשָׁה (same stem with appended -āh) — the cohortative-like extended form.

The long-form wayyiqtol is a Danielic stylistic feature — especially concentrated in first-person vision-reports. See parser-verified examples in the chapter:
- Dan 8:13 וָאֶשְׁמְעָה ("and I heard") — long-form Qal.Wayq.1s of שׁמע.
- Dan 8:15 וָאֲבַקְשָׁה (this verse) — long-form Piel.Wayq.1s of בקשׁ.
- Dan 8:17 וָאֶפְּלָה ("and I fell") — long-form Qal.Wayq.1s of נפל.
- Dan 10:16, 10:19 recurrent.

The paragogic-ה is not simply stylistic; it frequently marks first-person volition/emotive-coloration (Waltke-O'Connor §31.7.1). The seer is both *reporting* the event and *communicating* his internal engagement with it. In v.15 the -āh marks Daniel's seeking as intentional and heartfelt, not merely sequential-narrative.

**Object בִינָה.** The direct object of the seeking is the noun "understanding" — Daniel does not seek *the vision* (he has already seen it per clause 2), but its **interpretation**. This sets up the precise need that v.16's Hiphil הָבֵן (same root בין as the noun בִינָה) is sent to supply. The wordplay is deliberate: Daniel seeks *ḇînâ*, and Gabriel is commanded to *hāḇēn* — "cause to understand" — with the **marʾeh** as direct object of the causing-to-understand.

### 3. וְהִנֵּה עֹמֵד לְנֶגְדִּי כְּמַרְאֵה־גָבֶר — The hinneh + participle presentative

**Morphology.**
- וְהִנֵּה — Conj + Interj הנה "behold." The waw + hinneh combination marks a *presentative* — a visual/narrative pointing gesture introducing a new referent into the discourse.
- עֹמֵד — **Qal active participle masc.sg.Abs** of עמד "stand." Unarticled and subject-less — the hinneh supplies the pragmatic pointer and the participle supplies the predicate.
- לְנֶגְדִּי — Prep לְ + Noun.ms.Abs of נגד "in-front-of, opposite" + 1cs suffix → "before me, facing me, over-against me."
- כְּמַרְאֵה־גָבֶר — Prep כְּ ("as, like") + construct chain (see §Construct Chains above).

**Function.** The *hinneh + ptca* pattern is BH's standard **vision-presentative** — introducing a new figure into the visionary scene without finite verb. The participle is stative/imperfective — the figure *is standing*, an ongoing state Daniel observes. Examples:

- Zech 1:8 רָאִיתִי הַלַּיְלָה וְהִנֵּה־אִישׁ רֹכֵב עַל־סוּס אָדֹם ("I saw in the night, and behold a man riding upon a red horse").
- Gen 28:12 וַיַּחֲלֹם וְהִנֵּה סֻלָּם מֻצָּב אַרְצָה ("and he dreamed, and behold a ladder set up on the earth").
- Dan 10:5 וָאֶשָּׂא אֶת־עֵינַי וָאֵרֶא וְהִנֵּה אִישׁ־אֶחָד לָבוּשׁ בַּדִּים ("I lifted up my eyes and saw, and behold a certain man clothed in linen").

The participle's subject is typically *the thing-just-beheld* — grammatically recoverable from the hinneh-pointing rather than from explicit syntax. Here the referent is introduced immediately as the indefinite simile "like a gever-appearance" (כְּמַרְאֵה־גָבֶר); v.16 will name him as Gabriel.

**Lexical note: גָבֶר vs. אִישׁ vs. אָדָם.** Hebrew has three principal "man"-words:
- אָדָם (H120) — generic human, often with species/creaturely connotation (used in v.16 קוֹל־אָדָם).
- אִישׁ (H376) — male individual, the default "a man" (used extensively in Daniel 10).
- גֶּבֶר (H1397) — strong/mighty male, warrior-class, often with connotation of virility or valor (from גבר "be strong"). This is the rarer, *marked* term.

The choice of **gever** in v.15 — rather than אִישׁ — signals *virile/mighty* quality in the figure's appearance, consistent with Gabriel's name (גַּבְרִיאֵל = *gever* + ʾēl, "mighty-man of God" or "God is my hero"). The grammar itself marks a name-echo: Daniel sees "one-like-a-gever" and immediately hears God call him "Gabri-el." See [WG-man](../../word-studies/WG-man.md) for the distribution and registers of the three terms.

### 4. The marʾeh / chazon distribution in vv. 15, 16 — and its continuation through v. 27

**The decisive observation.** Verses 15–16 initiate a deliberate **two-term vocabulary split** that runs through the remainder of Dan 8. The same chapter uses both *ḥāzôn* (H2377) and *marʾeh* (H4758) — words that in many biblical contexts are interchangeable and both rendered "vision" in English — with a grammatically-traceable differentiation. The word studies [H2377-chazon](../../word-studies/H2377-chazon.md) and [H4758-mareh](../../word-studies/H4758-mareh.md) document this distinction; [dan-8-26](dan-8-26.md) §1 works it out in detail. This entry traces how vv. 15–16 set up the pattern.

**The chapter's distribution (parser-verified lemmata):**

| 8:27 | הַמַּרְאֶה וְאֵין מֵבִין | מראה | Daniel was astonished at the marʾeh and there was no understander |

**The pattern.** Within Dan 8, **chazon** consistently labels the **whole vision-prophecy complex** — the macro-revelation opened in v.1 and sealed in v.26b. **Marʾeh** consistently labels a **specific visible element** within that complex — the standing-figure (v.15), the thing-to-be-explained (v.16), the evening-morning oracle (v.26a), and the unresolved revelation that remains opaque (v.27). The switch in v.16 from *chazon* (which Gabriel could interpret if commanded) to *marʾeh* (which is what he is explicitly commanded to explain) is grammatically significant:

- The voice between Ulai does **not** command Gabriel to explain *he-ḥāzôn* (the whole vision); it commands him to explain *ham-marʾeh* (the specific visible element).
- The interpretation Gabriel proceeds to give in vv. 17–25 therefore **has a delimited object** — the marʾeh — not the entire chazon.
- V.26a confirms this by re-using **marʾeh** for the specific evening-morning oracle, while using **chazon** for the sealing command (applied to the whole).
- V.27 closes the chapter with Daniel's astonishment at the **marʾeh** — the specific element whose understanding Gabriel's explanation has not yet fully supplied.

**What is "the marʾeh" in Dan 8?** The grammar of v.16 (ʾeṯ-ham-marʾeh, definite direct object) presupposes an already-identified referent. Two candidates from the prior text are grammatically available:

- **(a)** The figure just introduced in v.15 *kə-marʾēh ḡāḇer* — the "gever-appearance" Daniel has just seen standing. Reading (a) makes v.16 self-reflexive: the voice commands Gabriel to make Daniel understand *himself* (the gever-appearance). This is grammatically workable but interpretively strained — Daniel doesn't need explanation of who the standing one is; he needs explanation of the oracle.
- **(b)** The 2,300 evening-mornings oracle of v.14, retrospectively identified as **marʾeh** in v.26a (*u-marʾēh hā-ʿereḇ wə-hab-bōqer ʾăšer neʾĕmar*). Reading (b) treats v.16's *ham-marʾeh* as prospectively-definite, anticipating v.26a's explicit titular use. This is the dominant reading in commentaries and is supported by v.26a's retrospective identification.

Both readings can be held compatibly: the figure (gever/Gabriel) needs no explanation, but the *content* of what Daniel has just seen — the climactic "unto 2,300 evening-mornings, then shall the holy one be vindicated" — does. Gabriel's subsequent speech in vv. 17–25 focuses on the ram/goat/little-horn sequence and their terminus at the קֵץ — which is precisely the question raised by v.14's number. Thus Gabriel's explanation of *ham-marʾeh* targets the temporal-and-agent content of the 2,300-oracle, even while the full disclosure awaits Dan 9:21–27 (where Gabriel *returns*, explicitly to supply what was not yet fully unsealed — see [dan-9-24-27](dan-9-24-27.md)).

**Caveat: the chazon/marʾeh distinction is not rigid across the whole Hebrew Bible.** See [H4758-mareh](../../word-studies/H4758-mareh.md) §5 and [H2377-chazon](../../word-studies/H2377-chazon.md) §"Easily Confused Words": in general usage, *marʾeh* covers physical beauty, priestly inspection, theophanic appearances, AND prophetic visions; *chazon* is narrower (prophetic revelation exclusively). What makes Dan 8's distribution distinctive is its **intra-chapter systematicity** — the chapter deploys the two terms with a *structural* distinction even though their general semantic fields overlap. This is not a lexicon-level distinction but a **discourse-level one**.

### 5. הָבֵן לְהַלָּז אֶת־הַמַּרְאֶה — Hiphil imperative with prepositional-dative addressee

**Morphology.**
- הָבֵן — **Qal.Hiphil.Impv.2ms** of בין (H995). The Hiphil of בין has the distinctive *hā-* prefix + short-i second-syllable pattern characteristic of hollow-root Hiphil imperatives (Joüon §80p; GKC §72). See parser: `Verb.Hiphil.Impv.2ms`.
- לְ — Prep; with personal pronominal complement, functions as the **dative of the indirect-object recipient** ("to this-one").
- הַלָּז — **demonstrative pronoun** (lemma לז, H1975 or variant), masc.sg, "this one, yonder one." The definite-article הַ is orthographically attached but is part of the pronoun's form (*hal-lāz* is the fused article+pronoun form; cf. הַלָּזֶה, הַלֵּזוּ). The demonstrative is proximal/deictic — "that one there," typically with a physical-pointing context.
- אֶת־הַמַּרְאֶה — accusative-marker + article + Noun.ms.Abs of מראה (H4758).

**The Hiphil causative force.** The Qal of בין = "discern, perceive, understand" (see word study for full range). The Hiphil is causative: "cause to understand / make understand / inform" — exactly parallel to Hebrew's standard Hiphil causative pattern (ידע Qal "know" → Hiphil "make-known"; ראה Qal "see" → Hiphil "show"; בוא Qal "come" → Hiphil "bring"). See [stem-hiphil](../hebrew/stem-hiphil.md) and [hiphil-imperfect](../hebrew/hiphil-imperfect.md) for the full taxonomy.

The Hiphil imperative הָבֵן is standardly addressed to a cognitive-agent who is to *enable another's understanding*. In Dan 8, the command goes:

- **8:16** הָבֵן לְהַלָּז אֶת־הַמַּרְאֶה — voice → Gabriel, with Daniel as indirect-object ("*to* this-one") and marʾeh as direct object.
- **8:17** הָבֵן בֶּן־אָדָם — Gabriel → Daniel, as vocative-addressed command (no indirect-object; Daniel himself is the cognizer).

The shift in addressee between v.16 and v.17 is grammatically tracked: v.16 is 2ms Hiphil spoken *to* Gabriel *about* Daniel; v.17 is 2ms Hiphil spoken *to* Daniel directly. See [dan-8-17-19](dan-8-17-19.md) §5 for analysis of v.17's use.

**The demonstrative הַלָּז.** This is a rare form (occurring ~11× in the Hebrew Bible: Gen 24:65; 37:19; Judg 6:20; 1 Sam 14:1, 17:26; 2 Kgs 4:25, 23:17; Ezek 36:35; Dan 8:16; Zech 2:8 [Eng 2:4]; Ps 104:25 as *zeh*). It carries a deictic-pointing force: "*that* one, yonder one, the one over there." The form signals *physical visibility* — the addressee is being pointed to, not merely referred to. In Dan 8:16, the voice *points* to Daniel from across the Ulai, singling him out as the recipient of understanding.

The three-part structure of the imperative clause is:

| Syntactic role | Element | Referent |
|---|---|---|
| Predicate (Hiphil causative) | הָבֵן | the "make-understand" action commanded of Gabriel |
| Dative indirect-object | לְהַלָּז | Daniel (the one-to-be-taught) |
| Accusative direct-object | אֶת־הַמַּרְאֶה | the content to be explained |

The prepositional-dative addressee construction (ל + indirect-object pronoun) is standard for Hebrew causatives whose direct-object is the information/thing conveyed and whose recipient is human. Cognate patterns:

- Dan 9:22 הֵבִין לְהַשְׂכִּיל לְךָ בִינָה (Hiphil inf.abs. + Hiphil inf.cst. + dative לְךָ + direct-object בִינָה) — "he instructed [me], to give you understanding."
- Dan 10:14 לַהֲבִינְךָ — same Hiphil root with pronominal suffix direct-object ("to make *you* understand").

### 6. קוֹל־אָדָם בֵּין אוּלָי — Disembodied voice between the Ulai banks

**Morphology.**
- קוֹל־אָדָם — construct chain (Noun.ms.Cst + Noun.ms.Abs) "voice of (a) man" — indefinite, generic "a human voice."
- בֵּין — Noun.ms.Cst "between" — functioning as preposition. ETCBC tags it Noun.ms.Cst morphologically (it is etymologically a noun, but syntactically a preposition).
- אוּלָי — PropN.s.Abs "Ulai" (the river that forms the setting of the vision — cf. 8:2 עַל־אוּבַל אוּלָי "by the Ulai canal").

**The locative puzzle.** בֵּין ("between") typically governs two nouns ("between X and Y"), but here it governs only one — Ulai. The KJV supplies "between [the banks of] Ulai" to capture the sense. Three grammatical options:

- **(a) Ellipsis of coordinate:** between [this bank and that bank] of Ulai — an elided pair whose second element is recoverable from the river-geography.
- **(b) Locative extension:** בֵּין אוּלָי = "in the middle of Ulai" — bēn as an intensive locative, "right-in-the-midst-of."
- **(c) Dual implicit:** the Ulai has two banks inherently, and בֵּין governs the implicit dual — "between the (two banks of) Ulai."

All three are grammatically workable; the visual picture is the same: the voice speaks *from the middle of the river* (or from one bank projecting to the other). The effect is theophanic — the voice is not in Daniel's spatial vicinity but *across* or *within* the river.

**Agent-suppression of the voice.** The construct chain *qôl-ʾāḏām* names the voice as "human-sounding" but suppresses the speaker's identity. The speaker is never grammatically named — subsequent 3ms verbs (*wa-yyiqrāʾ wa-yyōʾmar*, v.16) bind to an unexpressed subject that the narrative leaves anonymous. Is it (a) God, (b) the "prince of the host" of v.11/25, (c) another unnamed heavenly voice, or (d) the gever/Gabriel himself (in which case he would be commanding his own task)? The grammar is silent. The authoritative nature of the voice is implied by its ability to **command Gabriel** (named angelic figure), but the speaker's identity is withheld.

This pattern — authoritative voice commanding a named angel, speaker unidentified — parallels Dan 12:6–7 (the voice speaking to "the man clothed in linen" above the waters) and Rev 10:4, 10:8, 11:1 (voices from heaven commanding John). Grammatically, the suppression of the speaker-subject is a feature, not a gap: it prevents the reader from localizing authority in a named intermediary.

### 7. The auditory/visual parallel: v.15 seeing → v.16 hearing

The two verses form a matched sensory diptych:

| v.15 (seeing) | v.16 (hearing) |
|---|---|
| וָאֶשְׁמְעָה (elided — implicit from ch.) | **וָאֶשְׁמַע** "and I heard" |
| **וַיְהִי בִּרְאֹתִי** "when I saw" | |
| אֶת־הֶחָזוֹן (object of sight: the whole chazon) | קוֹל־אָדָם (object of hearing: a human voice) |
| וָאֲבַקְשָׁה בִינָה (sought understanding — 1cs action) | הָבֵן לְהַלָּז (command to make-understand — delegated to Gabriel) |
| וְהִנֵּה עֹמֵד (visual presentative — the gever-appearance) | וַיִּקְרָא וַיֹּאמַר (auditory narrative — calling + speaking) |
| כְּמַרְאֵה־גָבֶר (indefinite simile) | גַּבְרִיאֵל (definite proper name) |

V.15 is predominantly visual + introspective ("I saw, I sought"); v.16 is predominantly auditory + commissioning ("I heard, the voice called"). The two verses together deliver the full bilateral commissioning: Daniel sees the interpreter-figure, and hears the authoritative voice that commissions him.

The **marʾeh/chazon switch** tracks this transition: what Daniel *saw* was the whole *chazon* (v.15a); what Gabriel is commissioned to explain is the specific *marʾeh* (v.16c). The act of seeing encompasses the entire vision-complex; the act of understanding targets the specific content.

### Repetition of the Hiphil imperative הָבֵן

The verb בין in Hiphil imperative recurs three times in the Dan 8–9 complex, and the repetition tracks Gabriel's commissioned task:

- **8:16** הָבֵן לְהַלָּז אֶת־הַמַּרְאֶה — voice commands Gabriel to make Daniel understand the marʾeh.
- **8:17** הָבֵן בֶּן־אָדָם — Gabriel commands Daniel to understand, addressing him as "son of man."
- **9:23** (continuation via cognate inf.cst. לְהַשְׂכִּילְךָ) — Gabriel returns in Dan 9 to supply what 8:16's command did not complete: "I am now come to give you understanding."

The 8:27 coda *wə-ʾên mēḇîn* ("and there was no understander"; Qal.Ptcp of בין) grammatically acknowledges that the v.16 command was not fully executed within ch.8 — preparing for Gabriel's return in Dan 9 with the cognate roots שׂכל and בין in repeated Hiphil form. ## What the Grammar Constrains vs. Leaves Open

**The grammar constrains:**

1. V.15's *chazon* is the whole vision complex (anaphoric to 8:1).
2. V.15's *gever-appearance* is introduced indefinitely via a comparative simile — Daniel sees "one like a gever," not "the gever."
3. V.16's *marʾeh* is definite (articulated) and is a specific object distinct from the whole *chazon* — it is what Gabriel is commissioned to explain.
4. The voice in v.16 is grammatically anonymous — its authority is presupposed but its identity is unexpressed.
5. Gabriel is commissioned with a Hiphil causative imperative, indicating that the explanation is *his specific task* (not just a general utterance Daniel overhears).
6. The Hiphil הָבֵן answers Daniel's Piel בקשׁ בִינָה — the voice hears Daniel's seeking and dispatches an interpreter.

**The grammar leaves open:**

1. The specific referent of *ham-marʾeh* in v.16 — whether it is the just-seen gever (reading a) or the 2,300-oracle (reading b, supported by v.26a's retrospective identification). Both readings are grammatically licensed.
2. The identity of the voice between the Ulai — God, an unnamed heavenly figure, or another angelic speaker.
3. Whether the *gever-appearance* of v.15 is the same figure as *Gabriel* named in v.16 (the indefinite simile + the name-echo *gever*/*Gabri-el* strongly suggests yes, but the text does not grammatically *assert* the identity; it merely juxtaposes them).
4. The degree to which Gabriel's subsequent speech in vv. 17–25 constitutes full execution of the v.16 command vs. partial execution completed only in Dan 9:22–27. The grammar permits either reading; the 8:27 coda *wə-ʾên mēḇîn* and the 9:22–23 resumption chain favor the partial-execution reading.

## Note on the Central Exegetical Question: What does Gabriel explain in vv. 17–26?

The grammar of vv. 15–16 delimits the commissioned task by lexical-semantic choice:

- V.16 specifies ***ham-marʾeh*** (not *he-chazon*) as direct object of הָבֵן.
- V.26a retrospectively identifies the *marʾeh* as *hā-ʿereḇ wə-hab-bōqer* — the evening-morning oracle.
- V.26b then commands sealing of *he-chazon* (the whole) — because its full disclosure awaits *yāmîm rabbîm*.
- V.27 records Daniel's astonishment at *ham-marʾeh* — the specific element still unresolved.

This lexical pattern — when read with the grammatical distinction — strongly supports the reading that **Gabriel's vv. 17–25 speech explains the narrative-sequence portion** of the chazon (ram = Media-Persia, goat = Greece, little-horn career) **while the specific numeric terminus (2,300 evening-mornings, the marʾeh proper) remains partially sealed until Dan 9:21–27**.

The grammar does not *prove* this reading (both (a) and (b) are grammatically available for *ham-marʾeh*), but the **cumulative evidence**:
1. V.16's lexical choice of *marʾeh* where *chazon* was available,
2. V.26a's titular retrospective use of *marʾeh* for the evening-morning oracle specifically,
3. V.27's "no understander" coda despite vv. 17–25's interpretation,
4. Dan 9:21–23's explicit resumption (הַמַּרְאֶה בַּתְּחִלָּה "the vision at the first"),

— jointly point toward the reading that **ham-marʾeh in v.16 = the 2,300-oracle**, and that its full disclosure is deferred across the ch. 8 → ch. 9 hinge. This reading is consistent with the intra-Danielic three-part reveal pattern documented in [dan-8-26](dan-8-26.md) §4 (authentication → sealing → partial disclosure) and the Dan 9:23 angelic return analyzed in [dan-9-24-27](dan-9-24-27.md).

Note that this grammatical observation does not adjudicate *chronological* schemes (Antiochene / pagan Rome / papal / futurist) for the 2,300 evening-mornings. It only constrains the *referent* of *ham-marʾeh*: what Gabriel is commissioned to explain in v.16 is most plausibly the specific time-oracle of v.14, with its full disclosure deferred to the Dan 9 supplement. The chronological application of that time-oracle is a separate question analyzed at [dan-8-14](dan-8-14.md), [dan-8-14-evening-mornings/CONCLUSION.md](../../dan-8-14-evening-mornings/CONCLUSION.md), and [dan-9-24-27](dan-9-24-27.md).
