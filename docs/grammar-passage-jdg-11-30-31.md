# Grammar Analysis: Judges 11:30–31 (Jephthah's Vow)

## Key Grammatical Features

### 1. Wayyiqtol narrative chain: וַיִּדַּר… וַיֹּאמַר (v. 30)

- **Form:** Two consecutive **Qal wayyiqtols**, 3ms (Waltke-O'Connor's "waw-relative" / "waw-resultative" of the prefix conjugation).
- **Function:** Sequentially advances the storyline — "and Jephthah vowed a vow… and said…" The second wayyiqtol does not introduce a new independent event but marks the **natural narrative hand-off from act to speech**: the vow is then unpacked in direct discourse (IBHS §33.2.1). This is the classic narrative backbone frame within which the vow is embedded.
- **Paronomastic object:** נדר + נֶדֶר ("vowed a vow") is cognate-accusative paronomasia — a stylistic device that raises the rhetorical weight of the vow. Compare Num 6:2; 30:3; 1 Sam 1:11.
- **Significance:** The narrator marks the vow as a solemn speech-act *before* quoting it. Everything that follows inside direct speech leaves the wayyiqtol register and shifts to the **votive register** of protasis (אִם + imperfect) + weqatal apodosis — a register switch that carries the exegetical weight of the passage.

### 2. Infinitive-absolute paronomasia: נָתוֹן תִּתֵּן (v. 30)

- **Form:** Qal **infinitive absolute** נָתוֹן preceding the finite Qal imperfect 2ms תִּתֵּן of the same root נתן ("give, deliver"). Standard "paronomastic" or "tautological" infinitive construction (IBHS §35.3.1; GKC §113l–n; BDB-Grammar p.118 notes ~470 of the ~875 OT inf. absolutes occur this way).
- **Function:** Emphatic confirmation of the finite verb — "if thou wilt *indeed* give," "if thou wilt *surely* deliver." Waltke-O'Connor (§35.3.1h), following Muraoka, stresses that the emphasis lies in the doubling itself, not in the inf. abs. as such.
- **Significance:** Inside the **protasis** of the conditional (אִם-clause), this construction lends solemn rhetorical weight to the *condition* Jephthah sets: he is not hedging but underscoring the seriousness of the request he places before YHWH. This matches the paronomasia already seen in נדר נֶדֶר — the whole oath is framed by doubled-root emphasis.

### 3. Conditional vow structure: אִם-protasis + weqatal apodosis (vv. 30–31)

- **Form (protasis, v. 30):** אִם + (inf. abs. +) imperfect — standard real-future conditional. Clause type in the parser is `xYq0` in quotation domain.
- **Form (apodosis, v. 31):** a chain of three **weqatal** (waw-consecutive perfect) clauses — וְהָיָה… וְהָיָה לַיהוָה… וְהַעֲלִיתִיהוּ עוֹלָה. The initial וְהָיָה functions as an **apodosis-marker** ("then it shall be…"; IBHS §32.2.3–5, §38.2; GKC §112ff, §143d).
- **Construction:** The apodosis opens with a fronted nominative absolute (casus pendens) הַיּוֹצֵא אֲשֶׁר יֵצֵא — "the one going out who goes out" (articular participle + relative clause headed by a cognate yiqtol of יצא). This topic-fronting gives the protasis' reward-object its own syntactic slot before the apodosis resolves it with a 3ms pronoun (implicit in וְהָיָה לַיהוָה, then overtly in the +3ms suffix of וְהַעֲלִיתִיהוּ).
- **Significance:** This is **the canonical Hebrew vow template** (Gen 28:20–22 [Jacob]; Num 21:2 [Israel]; 1 Sam 1:11 [Hannah]). Jephthah's vow conforms to it with one unusual feature: the referent of הַיּוֹצֵא is left radically unspecified — the construction names *whatever* comes out, not *whoever*. The grammar does not settle whether animal or human is in view; that is a lexical/pragmatic question the syntax deliberately leaves open.
- **Grammar reference:** [syntax-conditional-vow](../hebrew/syntax-conditional-vow.md)

### 4. Casus pendens + cognate-verb relative: הַיּוֹצֵא אֲשֶׁר יֵצֵא (v. 31)

- **Form:** Definite Qal active participle הַיּוֹצֵא ("the one going out") fronted as a **casus pendens** (parser clause type `CPen`), then re-specified by a relative clause אֲשֶׁר יֵצֵא with a cognate yiqtol of the same root יצא ("who will go out").
- **Function:** The doubling is not redundant. The **participle** names the class (anyone/anything characterized by "going out"), and the **relative + imperfect** locates the event in the future contingent frame of the vow: "whatever shall actually come out." This is parallel in construction-type to the inf-abs paronomasia of v. 30 — a rhetorical doubling that broadens rather than narrows the referent.
- **Significance:** Grammatically, the subject of the apodosis is maximally indefinite. Translations that render "whatsoever" (KJV) are tracking the syntax correctly; the Hebrew is not committed to a human referent.

### 5. The disjunctive-waw question on וְהַעֲלִיתִיהוּ עֹולָה (v. 31)

- **Form:** A third **weqatal** clause headed by וְ, continuing the apodosis chain — Hiphil perfect 1cs with 3ms object suffix (הַעֲלִיתִיהוּ, "I will cause it/him to go up"), followed by the adverbial accusative עוֹלָה ("as a burnt-offering").
- **The exegetical question:** Is the וְ at the head of וְהַעֲלִיתִיהוּ a **conjunctive waw** ("and I will offer it up"), so that the vow promises two things of the same referent — *dedicate to YHWH and offer as a burnt-offering*? Or is it a **disjunctive waw** ("or I will offer it up"), so that the vow offers YHWH two alternatives — *if a person comes out, it will be dedicated to YHWH (as a lifelong servant); or if an animal comes out, I will offer it up as a burnt-offering*?
- **Grammatical assessment:** IBHS §39.2.1–3 (Waltke-O'Connor p.650ff) and GKC §154 treat disjunctive ("or") waw as a recognized but *marked* and context-dependent sense of simple waw. Hebrew has no morphologically distinct "or" particle in this position — אוֹ is available but is not used here. The default value of waw + weqatal in apodosis is **coordinate-sequential**: each weqatal advances the apodosis chain in the same direction. The two preceding apodosis weqatals (וְהָיָה, וְהָיָה לַיהוָה) are uncontroversially conjunctive; a third weqatal in the same series, with no shift of subject or domain, defaults to the same relation.
- **Lines of argument for disjunctive reading:** (a) human sacrifice was forbidden and repugnant in Israelite religion (Deut 12:31; Lev 18:21); (b) הָיָה לַיהוָה in other votive contexts (Lev 27; 1 Sam 1:11, 22, 28) can denote dedication, not immolation; (c) GKC §143d and IBHS §38.2 acknowledge that waw-apodosis can be epexegetical rather than strictly additive. On this reading the two weqatals explicate one reward with two possible execution modes.
- **Lines of argument for conjunctive reading:** (a) the default force of continuing weqatal in an apodosis chain is *and*, not *or*; (b) the referent of הַיּוֹצֵא is marked as maximally indefinite and the narrative (vv. 34–40) *treats* the vow as sacrificial, not dedicatory; (c) nothing in the morphosyntax of וְהַעֲלִיתִיהוּ signals disjunction — no אוֹ, no particle of alternatives, no shift in clause type.
- **Significance:** The grammar does not unilaterally decide the question. The **default, unmarked** reading of the waw is conjunctive ("and"); the disjunctive reading is grammatically possible but must be *imported* from theological or lexical considerations (Lev 27 dedication parallels, prohibition of human sacrifice). The exegetical weight therefore falls on (a) the scope of הַיּוֹצֵא and (b) whether *dedication vs. immolation* can be read into הָיָה לַיהוָה + הַעֲלִיתִיהוּ עוֹלָה — not on the waw itself. The **semantic grammar** search (Waltke-O'Connor, Gesenius on waw) supports treating "or"-waw as the marked reading that bears the burden of proof.
