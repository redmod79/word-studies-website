## Lexicon Definition

From ἀντί (G473, "opposite, instead of, in place of") and λύτρον (G3083, "something to loosen with, a redemption-price"); a redemption-price, a ransom given in exchange. The compound prefix ἀντι- intensifies the substitutionary dimension: not merely a ransom, but a ransom *in the place of* another.

## Semantic Categories

### 1. Substitutionary Ransom — Self-Giving of Christ
- **1 Tim 2:5–6** — "For there is one God, and one mediator between God and men, the man Christ Jesus; who gave himself a ransom (antilytron) for (hyper) all, to be testified in due time."

This single occurrence establishes the word's full semantic range: Christ as mediator gives *himself* — not an external payment — as the substitute-ransom on behalf of all humanity. The construction ὁ δοὺς ἑαυτὸν ἀντίλυτρον ὑπὲρ πάντων chains three substitutionary markers: (1) ἑαυτόν "himself" as the ransom content, (2) ἀντι- prefix denoting exchange/substitution, (3) ὑπέρ "on behalf of" denoting beneficiaries.

## Grammatical Note

The sole occurrence is accusative singular neuter (ἀντίλυτρον, [N-ASN]), functioning as an appositive to ἑαυτόν ("himself") — Christ gave himself *as* a ransom. The participle δούς (aorist active of δίδωμι, G1325) marks a completed, once-for-all act of giving.

## Hebrew Source

No direct LXX mapping exists for this NT-only compound. However, the lexicon notes LXX usage of the word at Psalm 48:9 (LXX numbering) = Psalm 49:8 (MT/KJV), where the Hebrew underlying the "ransom" concept is כֹּפֶר (kopher, H3724) and פִּדְיוֹם (pidyom, H6306). The conceptual OT background — that no human ransom suffices to redeem a soul (Ps 49:7–8) — is the theological problem that 1 Tim 2:6 answers with Christ as the antilytron.
