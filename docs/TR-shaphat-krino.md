*Reverse: κρίνω (G2919) also translates H1777 דִּין (diyn) 20x, H7378 רִיב (riyb) 23x, and H6664 צֶדֶק (tsedeq) 16x — showing the Greek word absorbs several Hebrew legal-action concepts.*

**Semantic continuity:** The LXX translators chose κρίνω (from PIE *krei-, "to sift/separate") for shaphat's core sense of discerning and pronouncing. Both words cover the full judicial arc: hearing a case, deliberating, and rendering a verdict. The LXX also preserves shaphat's participial form (the judge as civil ruler) via κριτής (37x), keeping the concrete agent separate from the abstract action.

**Semantic shift:** In the LXX, δικαιόω appears 9x for shaphat when the judicial act produces vindication rather than condemnation. This reflects Hebrew law's understanding that judgment can mean either acquittal or punishment, and the LXX began pulling the vindicating sense toward the separate Greek semantic sphere of δίκη (justice as norm). The NT will sharpen this distinction further.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The subject of ultimate judgment is God / Christ — the LXX consistently renders the divine-judgment passages with κρίνω, and the NT adopts this directly
- The eschatological scope: "judge the earth/nations" language (Ps 9:8; 96:13; 82:8) passes intact into NT proclamation (Acts 17:31; Rev 19:11)
- The participial agent: shophet (judge as ruler/deliverer) appears as κριτής in both the LXX and NT

**What shifts:**
- The NT largely drops shaphat's *governing/civil-ruler* sense (the "Judges of Israel" strand); κρίνω in the NT is almost exclusively judicial/evaluative, not administrative
- The NT introduces κατακρίνω (G2632, condemn) as a sharpened form for the punitive side of judgment, which Hebrew shaphat covered broadly by context
- The Niphal "be judged" strand (passive, 17x in Hebrew) is greatly amplified in NT: "do not judge, lest you be judged" (Matt 7:1), "we shall all stand before the judgment seat" (Rom 14:10) — the reflexive/communal implications are foregrounded
