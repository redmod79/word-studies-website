**Semantic split in translation:** The LXX fragments ʻâbar's wide semantic range across multiple Greek verbs. ## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- Diabaino consistently means "to cross a boundary" — physical geography in both testaments
- The faith-and-crossing pattern (Israel crossing by divine action) carries from OT narrative into Hebrews 11 as an exemplar of faith
- The idea that God enables impossible crossings (Jordan, Red Sea) persists

**What shifts:**
- The massive OT range of ʻâbar (transgression, removal of sin, wrath, transience) does NOT carry through diabaino into the NT. These senses are served by different Greek words (parabaino for transgression, parerchomai for passing away)
- Diabaino appears only 3x in the NT, making it a minor word. The theological weight of ʻâbar distributes across several Greek word families in the NT
- Luke 16:26 introduces a new sense not present in OT usage: an *impassable* boundary that *cannot* be crossed — inverting the OT pattern where God enables crossing
