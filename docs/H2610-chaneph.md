## Lexicon Definition

A primitive root; to soil, especially in a moral sense: corrupt, defile, greatly, pollute, profane.

Brown-Driver-Briggs notes the Arabic cognate meaning "to incline away from the right way," which illuminates the word's core sense: a fundamental turning aside from what is right, producing moral contamination. The word describes pollution that is specifically moral in nature — not ritual or ceremonial impurity (which is the domain of H2930 tame), but the soiling of land, persons, or institutions through sin, especially bloodshed, sexual immorality, and covenant-breaking.

## Semantic Categories

### 1. Land-Defilement by Bloodshed
The foundational usage: innocent blood shed on the land produces a moral pollution (chaneph) that cannot be cleansed except by the blood of the murderer. This is distinct from ritual contamination (tame) — it is an irreversible moral stain on the land itself.
- **Num 35:33** — "So ye shall not pollute [tachaniyphu, Hiphil] the land wherein ye are: for blood it defileth [yachaniph, Hiphil] the land: and the land cannot be cleansed of the blood that is shed therein, but by the blood of him that shed it"
- **Ps 106:38** — "And shed innocent blood, even the blood of their sons and of their daughters, whom they sacrificed unto the idols of Canaan: and the land was polluted [wattechenap, Qal] with blood"

### 2. Land-Defilement by Sexual Immorality / Spiritual Adultery
The prophetic extension: the land is polluted not only by literal bloodshed but by Israel's spiritual adultery — whoredom with idols, treated as a violation of the marriage covenant between God and Israel.
- **Jer 3:1** — "shall not that land be greatly polluted [chanoph techenap, emphatic Qal]? but thou hast played the harlot with many lovers"
- **Jer 3:2** — "and thou hast polluted [wattachaniyphi, Hiphil] the land with thy whoredoms and with thy wickedness"
- **Jer 3:9** — "she defiled [wattechenap, Qal] the land, and committed adultery with stones and with stocks"

### 3. Earth-Defilement by Covenant-Breaking
Extended to a universal scale: the entire earth is polluted because its inhabitants have violated God's laws and broken His eternal covenant.
- **Isa 24:5** — "The earth also is defiled [chanephah, Qal] under the inhabitants thereof; because they have transgressed the laws, changed the ordinance, broken the everlasting covenant"

### 4. Profaneness of Persons (Religious Leaders)
Applied to persons rather than land: prophets and priests who are morally polluted in character, bringing their wickedness into God's house itself.
- **Jer 23:11** — "For both prophet and priest are profane [chanephu, Qal]; yea, in my house have I found their wickedness, saith the LORD"

### 5. Moral Corruption by External Influence
The Hiphil used for deliberate seduction away from covenant faithfulness through flattery and deception.
- **Dan 11:32** — "And such as do wickedly against the covenant shall he corrupt [yachaniph, Hiphil] by flatteries"

### 6. Hostile Nations' Desire to See Zion Defiled
Nations gathered against Zion express the desire that she be polluted — moral defilement as an intended humiliation.
- **Mic 4:11** — "Now also many nations are gathered against thee, that say, Let her be defiled [techenap, Qal], and let our eye look upon Zion"

### Semantic Distinction: chaneph vs. tame

The pairing in Num 35:33-34 is the clearest window into the distinction:
- **v.33** uses chaneph (Hiphil): "ye shall not **pollute** the land... for blood it **defileth** the land" — moral pollution caused by bloodshed
- **v.34** uses tame (Piel): "**Defile** not therefore the land which ye shall inhabit, wherein I dwell" — ritual contamination of God's dwelling

Both words describe what happens to the land, but from different angles: chaneph is the moral soiling that results from sin; tame is the ritual unfitness that excludes God's presence. The prophets (especially Jeremiah) extend chaneph beyond bloodshed to include spiritual adultery and covenant-breaking, while tame remains anchored in the Levitical purity system (though Ezekiel extends tame to idolatry contexts as well).

## The Adjective H2611 in Job and the Prophets

The adjective chaneph appears 13 times, translated "hypocrite" (KJV) in every occurrence. However, the Hebrew meaning is broader than the English "hypocrite" (which implies pretending). The Hebrew adjective describes a person who is genuinely morally polluted — godless, profane, irreverent:

- **Job 8:13** — "the hypocrite's hope shall perish" (the godless person has no lasting hope)
- **Job 13:16** — "an hypocrite shall not come before him" (the profane cannot stand in God's presence)
- **Job 15:34** — "the congregation of hypocrites shall be desolate" (the community of the godless faces ruin)
- **Job 20:5** — "the joy of the hypocrite but for a moment" (godless joy is transient)
- **Job 27:8** — "what is the hope of the hypocrite, though he hath gained, when God taketh away his soul?"
- **Job 34:30** — "that the hypocrite reign not, lest the people be ensnared"
- **Job 36:13** — "the hypocrites in heart heap up wrath"
- **Isa 9:17** (Heb 9:16) — "every one is an hypocrite and an evildoer" (paired with meraʿ, "evildoer")
- **Isa 10:6** — "against an hypocritical nation" (Assyria sent against godless Israel)
- **Isa 33:14** — "fearfulness hath surprised the hypocrites" (the godless in Zion fear God's consuming fire)
- **Prov 11:9** — "an hypocrite with his mouth destroyeth his neighbour"
- **Ps 35:16** — "with hypocritical mockers in feasts"

Job uses chaneph 8 times — more than any other book — always to describe a moral category of person whose godlessness determines their destiny. The word is a character descriptor, not merely a behavioral one.

## Key Verses with Analysis

### Numbers 35:33 — The Foundational Land-Pollution Text
> "So ye shall not pollute the land wherein ye are: for blood it defileth the land: and the land cannot be cleansed of the blood that is shed therein, but by the blood of him that shed it."

Both verb occurrences are Hiphil (causative): "do not cause the land to be polluted" and "blood causes the land to be polluted." The verse establishes that (1) bloodshed produces an indelible moral pollution on the land, and (2) only the blood of the murderer can cleanse it. This is not ritual impurity with washing rites — it is a moral contamination requiring justice.

### Jeremiah 3:1-2, 9 — Spiritual Adultery Pollutes the Land
> "shall not that land be greatly polluted? but thou hast played the harlot with many lovers" (3:1)

Jeremiah extends the chaneph concept from literal bloodshed (Num 35) to spiritual adultery. Israel's unfaithfulness to the covenant is treated as producing the same kind of land-pollution as murder. The Deuteronomy 24:1-4 law (a divorced woman who remarries cannot return to her first husband) is applied to Israel's relationship with God — her idolatry has polluted the land beyond normal remedy. Yet God invites return ("yet return again to me").

### Isaiah 24:5 — Universal Earth-Defilement
> "The earth also is defiled under the inhabitants thereof; because they have transgressed the laws, changed the ordinance, broken the everlasting covenant."

The scope expands from Israel's land to the whole earth. Three causes are given: transgressing laws, changing the ordinance, and breaking the everlasting covenant. The earth itself bears the moral stain of humanity's rebellion. This is the broadest application of chaneph in Scripture.

### Daniel 11:32 — Corruption by Flattery
> "And such as do wickedly against the covenant shall he corrupt by flatteries."

The Hiphil here describes deliberate seduction — making covenant-violators even more profane through flattering speech. This is the only occurrence where chaneph describes interpersonal moral corruption rather than land-pollution.
