**Semantic shift in translation:** Essentially none. Onoma in the LXX functions as a transparent calque for shem across every major sense — personal/place identifier, reputation, character, memorial, and the divine-name idiom. The LXX also imports the Hebrew idioms directly: *epikaleo to onoma kyriou* ("call on the name of the Lord") in Gen 4:26 LXX is literal and becomes the template for NT usage.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The formula "call on the name of YHWH / the Lord" (qara b'shem → epikaleo to onoma) — patriarchal altar piety (Gen 4:26; 12:8) becomes apostolic confession (Act 2:21; Rom 10:13)
- "Name" = character/reputation, not merely label (Exo 34:5–7 name-proclamation → Joh 17:6 name-manifestation)
- "Name" = authority in which one acts / speaks (Deu 18:5, 22 prophets speak "in the name" → Mat 28:19; Act 3:6 disciples act "in the name")
- "Name" as site of presence (Deu 12:5 "put his name there" → Mat 18:20 "gathered together in my name")
- Blessing/cursing by the name (2 Sam 6:18 → Jam 5:14)
- Holiness / profanation of the name (Lev 22:32; Eze 36:23 → Mat 6:9)

**What shifts:**
- In the OT, shem YHWH = the name YHWH and its associated attributes. In the NT, the divine name is increasingly attached to Jesus: Acts applies Joel 2:32 ("call on the name of YHWH") to Jesus; Phil 2:9–11 claims that God has given him "the name above every name" in terms echoing Isa 45:23 where YHWH swears by himself.
- "In my name" language (Joh 14:13–14; 16:23) extends the OT authority-formula from prophetic speech to prayer and discipleship.
- "Name" becomes almost a technical term for confession and identity ("Christian" = one of "the Name" — Act 5:41; 1 Pe 4:14–16).
