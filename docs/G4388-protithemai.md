## Lexicon Definition

Middle voice from πρό (G4253) and τίθημι (G5087); to place before, i.e. (for oneself) to exhibit; to propose (to oneself), i.e. to purpose, determine.

The middle voice is significant: the subject acts on or for himself — either placing something before others (public display) or placing something before oneself (resolving, purposing).

## Semantic Categories

### 1. Human Purpose / Intention
- **Rom 1:13** — "Now I would not have you ignorant, brethren, that oftentimes I purposed to come unto you, (but was let hitherto,) that I might have some fruit among you also, even as among other Gentiles."

Paul uses protithēmi to describe a personal resolve — a deliberate intention formed repeatedly ("oftentimes") but prevented from fulfillment. The middle voice captures the reflexive sense: Paul placed this plan before himself.

### 2. Divine Public Display
- **Rom 3:25** — "Whom God hath set forth [to be] a propitiation through faith in his blood, to declare his righteousness for the remission of sins that are past, through the forbearance of God."

God is the subject; Christ is the object publicly displayed as hilastērion. The middle voice indicates God acting for himself — displaying Christ as his own provision. The context of "declare his righteousness" reinforces the public-display sense: the act makes something visible that was previously unseen.

### 3. Divine Eternal Purpose
- **Eph 1:9** — "Having made known unto us the mystery of his will, according to his good pleasure which he hath purposed in himself."

God purposes "in himself" (ἐν αὐτῷ) — the middle voice naturally suits the reflexive: God set this plan before himself. The collocation with "mystery" (mystērion) emphasizes that this was a hidden resolve now made known, bridging the "purpose beforehand" and "display" senses.
