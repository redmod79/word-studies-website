**Semantic shift in translation:** The LXX divides mishpat's range across two Greek words: krima (the verdict rendered) and krisis (the act of judging). This split is useful — krima emphasizes outcome, krisis emphasizes process — but neither alone covers the full OT range of mishpat, which also includes covenant legislation (ordinance sense) and social entitlement (right sense). Those senses map to δικαίωμα (statute) and δικαιοσύνη (righteousness) respectively.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same:**
- Both OT mishpat and NT krima/krisis carry the core judicial sense: a verdict pronounced, a process of judging, consequences rendered
- Both can be applied to God's governance: mishpat as foundation of God's throne (Ps 89:14) ↔ God's krima being "according to truth" (Rom 2:2)
- Both demand action from humans: OT "do mishpat" ↔ NT warnings about receiving adverse krima

**What shifts:**
- Mishpat's rich covenant-legislation sense (ordinances to be observed) fades in NT usage of krima/krisis — that sense migrates to νόμος (nomos, law) and δικαίωμα (ordinance)
- Mishpat's social-justice dimension (rights of the vulnerable) is mostly absent from NT krima/krisis, which trend toward forensic/eschatological verdict
- NT krima gains a stronger "adverse verdict / damnation" coloring; mishpat was more neutral in valence
