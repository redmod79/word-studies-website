## Lexicon Definition

From Strong's: "akin to γλυκύς (sweet); sweet wine, i.e. (properly) must (fresh juice), but used of the more saccharine (and therefore highly inebriating) fermented wine."

The term denotes grape juice at the sweet end of the fermentation spectrum — either freshly pressed "must" or wine retaining high residual sugar. In classical and Hellenistic Greek usage it is distinguished from ordinary οἶνος (oinos) by its sweetness rather than by absence of alcohol.

## Semantic Categories

Because gleukos appears only once in Scripture, semantic range is reconstructed from the single biblical occurrence, the word's Louw-Nida neighborhood, and its cognate γλυκύς.

### 1. Sweet / Fresh-Pressed Wine (Must)
- **Acts 2:13** — "Others mocking said, These men are full of new wine (gleukos)."

The single biblical usage. The mockers attribute the apostles' ecstatic speech to intoxication from gleukos. The force of the accusation requires that gleukos in first-century Jerusalem usage be understood as capable of producing drunkenness — otherwise the mockery is nonsensical and Peter's counter-defense in Acts 2:15 ("these are not drunken, as ye suppose, seeing it is but the third hour of the day") would not need to address drunkenness at all. Peter refutes the timing of the charge, not its premise.

### 2. Extra-Biblical Usage — Sweet Fermented Wine
In Greek agricultural and medical writers (Aristotle, Nicander, Dioscorides, Plutarch), gleukos consistently denotes wine retaining natural grape sweetness — either recently fermented (weeks to months old) or preserved sweet by methods such as cold storage, boiling down, or sealing in a submerged jar. It is not unfermented juice in ordinary Greek usage; it is young or sweet wine, still capable of intoxication (Aristotle, Meteorologica 388a; Plutarch, Quaestiones Convivales 3.7.1).

### 3. Pentecost Festal Context
Pentecost (Shavuot) fell in late spring / early summer (May–June), well before the autumn grape harvest (Tishri, Sept–Oct). Truly fresh "must" of that year's vintage was not available in Jerusalem at Pentecost. What was available under the name gleukos was therefore either the previous year's wine still retaining its sweetness, or sweet wine specially preserved — in both cases fermented.
