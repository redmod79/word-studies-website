## Lexicon Definition

**Strong's:** "From [chamar, H2560]; wine (**as fermenting**):—pure, red wine."

**BDB / Gesenius:** חֶמֶר noun masculine — "wine" (in poetry and late), from the root חמר I "to boil up, ferment, foam." The noun names wine from the visible action of its fermenting: the bubbling, foaming, reddening must. The Aramaic cognate חֲמַר is the ordinary prose word for wine in Ezra 6:9; 7:22 and throughout the Belshazzar's-feast narrative (Dan 5:1, 2, 4, 23), where it is unambiguously the intoxicating beverage that renders the king and his court drunk.

**Key semantic note:** The lexical core of chemer is **fermentation itself**. The word is built on the verb that describes wine's active working — boiling up, throwing off foam/scum, glowing red. Unlike yayin (H3196), which is a generic noun for "wine," and unlike tirosh (H8492), which can denote the fresh must of the vintage, chemer is **named for the fermenting process**. The Strong's definition ("wine as fermenting") and Gesenius ("in poetry and late") converge: chemer is the poetic word that foregrounds wine's alcoholic working.

## Semantic Categories

Only one Hebrew occurrence, so "categories" here are really the one semantic cluster plus the illuminating Aramaic parallels and the cognate-verb field.

### 1. Fermented Grape-Wine in Covenant Blessing (Deut 32:14)
In the Song of Moses, chemer stands at the climax of a catalog of the richest products of the land that YHWH fed to Jeshurun — butter, milk, fat of lambs and rams, kidney-fat of wheat, and finally "the blood of the grape, chemer." The poetic parallelism is **dam-enav** ("blood of the grape") || **chemer** — identifying chemer with the red fermented juice of the vine. The preceding verse names honey and olive oil; the whole list is festal plenty, matured agricultural produce. Grape juice would not be a poetic climax beside cultivated dairy fats and matured grain; the covenant blessing is the full-strength fermented wine of the harvest.
- **Deut 32:14** — "Butter of kine, and milk of sheep, with fat of lambs, and rams of the breed of Bashan, and goats, with the fat of kidneys of wheat; and thou didst drink the pure blood of the grape [dam-enav tishteh chamer]"

### 2. Aramaic Royal/Cultic Wine (H2562 chamar — cognate reference field)
Every Aramaic occurrence names wine that is unambiguously alcoholic:
- **Dan 5:1** — "Belshazzar the king made a great feast... and drank wine [chamar] before the thousand"
- **Dan 5:2** — "Belshazzar, whiles he tasted the wine [chamar], commanded to bring the golden and silver vessels"
- **Dan 5:4** — "They drank wine, and praised the gods of gold, and of silver" (drunken idolatry)
- **Dan 5:23** — "thy lords, thy wives, and thy concubines, have drunk wine [chamar] in them"
- **Ezra 6:9** — daily priestly provision: "wheat, salt, wine [chamar], and oil"
- **Ezra 7:22** — Artaxerxes' decree of temple stores: "wine [chamar] to an hundred baths"

### 3. The Verb-Field of חמר (H2560 chamar) — "Boiling, Foaming, Reddening"
The cognate verb is the conceptual home of chemer and illuminates what the noun names. It occurs 6x with these glosses (KJV):
- "**are troubled**" (Ps 46:3 — waters roaring/foaming; Lam 1:20; 2:11 — bowels in turmoil)
- "**is foul**" (Job 16:16 — face reddened/inflamed with weeping)
- "**is red**" (Ps 75:8 — see below)
- "**and daubed it**" (Ex 2:3 — Moses' ark daubed with chemar-bitumen/pitch)

The verb's core image is **a boiling-up, foaming, reddening action** — whether of stormy water, a face swollen with tears, bowels churning in grief, bitumen bubbling in pitch-pots, or wine fermenting in the vat. A noun built on this root and applied to wine straightforwardly names wine in its **active, fermenting, foaming** condition.

## The "Foameth" / Ps 75:8 Context

Psalm 75:8 (English v. 8 = Hebrew v. 9) is not a chemer-noun verse but is its strongest cognate witness:

**"For in the hand of the LORD there is a cup, and the wine [yayin H3196] is red [chamar H2560 — Qal perf. 3ms]; it is full of mixture [mesek H4538]; and he poureth out of the same: but the dregs [shemarim] thereof, all the wicked of the earth shall wring them out, and drink them."** (Ps 75:8 KJV)

The parsed Hebrew (Psalm 75:9 MT):
- יין (yayin) — wine
- חָמַר (chamar, H2560, Qal perfect 3ms) — the verb is the exact root of chemer

KJV renders chamar here as "**is red**," but the verb's full field is "boil up / foam / ferment / glow red." Many commentators and modern translations (LXX ἄκρατος "unmixed"; JPS "foams"; NET "foams up"; HCSB "foaming") render it **"foameth" / "foams up."** The same root that produces the noun chemer (Deut 32:14) here describes the cup of YHWH's wrath as **a churning, foaming, full-strength fermented wine mixed with spices**, whose dregs the wicked must drain. The image requires an actively fermented, inebriating wine — the judgment figure trades on wine's power to intoxicate and disable, then on the bitterness of the lees that remain.

**Implication for the water-into-wine question:** Where Scripture wishes to name wine as fermented — as actively alcoholic, foaming, full-strength — it has a ready word family (the חמר root) that points straight at fermentation. The poetic tradition (Deut 32; Ps 75; Isa 27 textual variant) uses this root for wine at its fullest richness and potency, whether in covenant blessing (Deut 32:14) or in judgment (Ps 75:8).
