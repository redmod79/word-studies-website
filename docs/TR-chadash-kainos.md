## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The qualitative nuance: châdâsh in the key prophetic texts already pointed to something qualitatively unprecedented (Jer 31:31 explicitly contrasts the new covenant with the Sinai covenant: "not according to the covenant that I made with their fathers"). The LXX and NT preserve this with καινός.
- The "new song" formula (Psalms → Revelation) is a continuous literary chain: Psa 33:3, 40:3, 96:1, 98:1, 144:9 → Rev 5:9, 14:3.
- The eschatological scope: both châdâsh and καινός point toward a divinely inaugurated reordering — new covenant, new heavens/earth, new heart (Ezek 36:26).

**What shifts:**
- In the Hebrew OT, châdâsh frequently describes mundane objects (a new cart, new bottles, a new house). In the NT, this ordinary usage still appears (new tomb: Mat 27:60; new garment: Luk 5:36) but the theological weight shifts almost entirely to the eschatological and covenantal uses.
- The contrast with παλαιός (old/worn) becomes theologically loaded in the NT: Heb 8:13 explicitly uses καινός to declare the Mosaic covenant παλαιός (obsolete). This deprecation of the old by the new is more explicit than in the OT.
