**Semantic continuity in translation:** The LXX preserves the core meaning well. Both mishkan and skene denote a physical tent-structure that serves as a dwelling. The LXX does not distinguish between mishkan (the inner wooden-walled structure) and ohel (the outer tent covering) — both become skene. This merger simplifies the Hebrew architectural distinction but preserves the theological point: God's portable dwelling among his people.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The core concept of God's portable, accessible dwelling among his people persists from Exodus through Revelation
- The skene word family in the NT consistently refers back to the wilderness tabernacle as its reference point
- The association between divine presence and the tent-structure remains intact (cloud/glory in OT; incarnation/eschaton in NT)

**What shifts:**
- In the OT, mishkan is overwhelmingly a concrete physical structure (90%+ of uses); in the NT, skene carries increasing figurative weight (the body as a tent in 2 Cor 5:1-4, using skenos)
- The NT introduces the concept of a heavenly "true tabernacle" (Heb 8:2) of which the earthly mishkan was a copy — a typological layer not explicit in the Pentateuchal construction texts
- John 1:14 uses the verbal form (eskenosen, "tabernacled") to describe the incarnation, extending the dwelling-concept from structure to person
