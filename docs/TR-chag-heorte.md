**Semantic shift:** The Hebrew chag carries the specific nuance of circular/processional movement (root châgag = to move in a circle). Greek ἑορτή captures the festival occasion but loses this kinetic/processional connotation. The concept of pilgrimage (going up) is preserved in context but not in the word itself.

**Dominance:** H2282 is the *primary* Hebrew source for G1859 (PMI 31.28), though H4150 môwʻêd also maps to ἑορτή (34x, PMI 19.41). The LXX essentially collapses chag and a portion of moed under the single word heortḗ.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same:**
- The three annual pilgrimage festivals remain the referents of heortḗ throughout the NT
- The Passover and Feast of Tabernacles are the most prominent individual feasts in both OT and NT
- The corporate/public character of chag (all Israel assembled) continues in NT usage
- The tension between formal observance and genuine worship (see Amos 5:21) echoes in NT critique of external religion

**What shifts:**
- Hebrew chag implies processional movement and pilgrimage to a central sanctuary; NT heortḗ is used in contexts where Jesus himself becomes the locus of the feast (Jhn 2:23; 7:37–38)
- Paul's use in Col 2:16 and 1 Cor 5:8 applies the festival vocabulary to the new-covenant community, decoupling it from the Jerusalem pilgrimages
- The eschatological trajectory in Zec 14 (nations keeping feast of tabernacles) appears to be in view in NT passages about universal worship
