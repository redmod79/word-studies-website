# WG: Writing / Scripture

## Overview

The writing/scripture concept in the NT involves a cluster of words spanning the act of writing (γράφω), the product of writing (γραφή, γράμμα, βιβλίον), and the act of reading (ἀναγινώσκω), plus compound verbs for specialized inscriptions. These words are theologically central: the NT's authority rests on appeal to "what is written" (γέγραπται), the OT scriptures (γραφαί), and the new apostolic writings.

## Relationships

### Shared Root (γραφ-)
All γράφ- words derive from the same root as γράφω:
- **γράφω** (G1125) — the primary verb
- **γραφή** (G1124) — noun: a specific writing → by metonymy "the Scriptures"
- **γράμμα** (G1121) — noun: an individual character, letter, or brief document
- **γραπτός** (G1123) — adjective: "written" (Rom 2:15 only)
- **ἐγγράφω** (G1449) — compound: write in/into a medium
- **ἐπιγράφω** (G1924) — compound: write upon a surface as a superscription

**βιβλίον** (G975) and **ἀναγινώσκω** (G314) belong to the same Louw-Nida domain (033.005 — Written Language) but have different roots.

## Roles in the Concept

- **Verb (act):** γράφω — the act of writing or inscribing anything
- **Authoritative corpus:** γραφή — the body of sacred writings collectively ("the scriptures")
- **Individual text unit:** γράμμα — a letter, bill, or short written piece
- **Physical medium:** βιβλίον — the scroll or book as physical object
- **Reception:** ἀναγινώσκω — reading what has been written
- **Inscribed into:** ἐγγράφω — writing upon/within a recipient (heart, stone tablet)
- **Labeled upon:** ἐπιγράφω — inscribing a title or superscription on a surface
- **Legal instrument:** χειρόγραφον — a hand-signed bond/debt certificate

- **Rev 5:1–4** — "a book (βιβλίον) written (γράφω) within … no man was found worthy to open and to read (ἀναγινώσκω) the book (βιβλίον)"

## The γέγραπται Formula

The theologically distinctive feature of γράφω in the NT is its use in the **perfect passive indicative** (γέγραπται — "it stands written"). This formula appears 67 times (35% of all γράφω occurrences) as the standard NT appeal to OT authority. The perfect tense signals that the writing is a completed past act whose result permanently abides.

Pattern: **γέγραπται** + quotation of OT text → used by Jesus (temptation, temple), Paul (throughout Romans, Corinthians), and Luke's narrative voice.
