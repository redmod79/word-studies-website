# Grammar Analysis: John 3:28-30

## Text

**Greek (v. 28):** αὐτοὶ ὑμεῖς μοι μαρτυρεῖτε ὅτι εἶπον Οὐκ εἰμὶ ἐγὼ ὁ Χριστός, ἀλλ' ὅτι Ἀπεσταλμένος εἰμὶ ἔμπροσθεν ἐκείνου.

**Greek (v. 29):** Ὁ ἔχων τὴν νύμφην νυμφίος ἐστίν· ὁ δὲ φίλος τοῦ νυμφίου ὁ ἑστηκὼς καὶ ἀκούων αὐτοῦ, χαρᾷ χαίρει διὰ τὴν φωνὴν τοῦ νυμφίου. αὕτη οὖν ἡ χαρὰ ἡ ἐμὴ πεπλήρωται.

**Greek (v. 30):** ἐκεῖνον δεῖ αὐξάνειν, ἐμὲ δὲ ἐλαττοῦσθαι.

**KJV (v. 28):** Ye yourselves bear me witness, that I said, I am not the Christ, but that I am sent before him.

**KJV (v. 29):** He that hath the bride is the bridegroom: but the friend of the bridegroom, which standeth and heareth him, rejoiceth greatly because of the bridegroom's voice: this my joy therefore is fulfilled.

**KJV (v. 30):** He must increase, but I [must] decrease.

## Key Grammatical Features

### 1. Ἀπεσταλμένος εἰμὶ (v. 28) — Perfect Passive Periphrastic State

- **Form:** Perfect passive participle (Ἀπεσταλμένος) + present indicative of εἰμί = periphrastic perfect. The participle is fronted for emphasis.
- **Significance:** John does not merely say "I was sent" (aorist ἀπεστάλην); he says "I stand in the condition of having been sent." The perfect stresses the abiding state that results from a prior commissioning. His current ministry is the continuing outworking of that completed divine sending. The passive leaves the agent unnamed but is conventionally construed as divine (cf. John 1:6, ἀπεσταλμένος παρὰ Θεοῦ).
- **Grammar reference:** [participle-perfect-passive-state.md](../greek/participle-perfect-passive-state.md); [divine-passive.md](../greek/divine-passive.md)

### 2. ὁ ἑστηκὼς καὶ ἀκούων (v. 29) — Paired Participles with Tense Contrast

- **Form:** Two articular attributive participles modifying φίλος, joined by καί, but in DIFFERENT tenses. ἑστηκώς is perfect active ("having taken his stand / standing there"); ἀκούων is present active ("listening"). Both are nominative singular masculine, agreeing with φίλος.
- **Significance:** The tense shift is deliberate. The perfect ἵστημι has a stative force — the friend of the bridegroom has positioned himself and remains at his post. The present ἀκούων pictures ongoing, attentive listening in that posture. Together they depict a fixed stance of readiness plus continuous attention to the bridegroom's voice. The genitive αὐτοῦ is the normal construction with ἀκούω when hearing a person (genitive of source/person heard), as distinct from hearing a message (accusative).
- **Grammar reference:** Perfect of ἵστημι — [G2476-histemi.md](../../word-studies/G2476-histemi.md) (note the stative perfect ἕστηκα "I stand" is functionally present in meaning)

### 3. χαρᾷ χαίρει (v. 29) — Cognate Dative of Manner (Semitic Infinitive Absolute)

- **Form:** Dative singular noun χαρᾷ ("with joy") modifying the cognate verb χαίρει ("rejoices"). Both derive from the same root χαρ-.
- **Significance:** This is the standard Septuagintal rendering of the Hebrew infinitive absolute construction (e.g., שָׂמוֹחַ יִשְׂמַח "rejoicing he rejoices"). The cognate dative is NOT a mere instrument; it intensifies the verbal action. KJV captures it as "rejoiceth greatly." The idiom colors John the Baptist's speech with OT solemnity appropriate to prophetic testimony. See LXX Isa 61:10 (χαρᾷ χαρήσεται) for the closest parallel — the bride/bridegroom context strongly suggests conscious allusion.
- **Grammar reference:** See dative-of-manner/cognate-dative pattern; akin to the Hebrew infinitive-absolute intensification found in [lxx-disjunctive-waw-vows.md](../greek/lxx-disjunctive-waw-vows.md) context

### 4. πεπλήρωται (v. 29) — Perfect Passive Indicative of Completed State

- **Form:** Third singular perfect passive indicative of πληρόω ("fill up, complete").
- **Significance:** Not "is being fulfilled" (present) nor "was fulfilled" (aorist) but "has been brought to full measure and stands filled." The perfect stresses present resultative state: the joy is now full and abides so. John is not striving toward joy; he has it in its completion at this very moment — precisely because the bridegroom has come and is being heard. The passive again sidesteps naming an agent, though theologically the filling agent is the unfolding messianic event itself.
- **Grammar reference:** [G4137-pleroo.md](../../word-studies/G4137-pleroo.md); [participle-perfect-passive-state.md](../greek/participle-perfect-passive-state.md)

### 5. ἐκεῖνον δεῖ αὐξάνειν, ἐμὲ δὲ ἐλαττοῦσθαι (v. 30) — Impersonal δεῖ + Accusative-Subject Infinitives

- **Form:** Impersonal δεῖ ("it is necessary") governing TWO infinitives in antithetical parallelism:
  - αὐξάνειν — present active infinitive ("to be increasing")
  - ἐλαττοῦσθαι — present passive infinitive ("to be being diminished")
  The logical subjects are in the accusative (ἐκεῖνον, ἐμέ), which is the normal construction with δεῖ + infinitive.
- **Significance:** Three features combine:
  1. **δεῖ** expresses divine/eschatological necessity, not mere propriety. This is the same δεῖ used of the Son of Man's passion (Mark 8:31; Luke 24:26). John frames his decrease as part of God's ordained plan, not personal resignation.
  2. **Voice contrast.** Christ's increase is active (αὐξάνειν) — He grows by his own agency / by what He intrinsically is. John's decrease is passive (ἐλαττοῦσθαι) — he is to be made less, his diminishment is something done to him (by the advance of the Messianic era).
  3. **Present tense in both infinitives** indicates ongoing, progressive action. Not one completed handover but a continuous trajectory: Christ continually increasing, John continually being reduced.
  4. **Word order chiasm:** demonstrative pronoun (ἐκεῖνον) : verbal action :: verbal action : pronoun (ἐμὲ). The fronting of each accusative pronoun heightens the antithesis.
- **Grammar reference:** [G1163-dei.md](../../word-studies/G1163-dei.md) for δεῖ as divine necessity

## Clause Structure

**v. 28** is a testimonial reminder framed by μαρτυρεῖτε ὅτι ... (indirect discourse). Two ὅτι clauses in antithetic parallelism: "you witness [1] that I said I am not the Christ, but [2] that I have been sent before him." The second ὅτι is best read as a second object of μαρτυρεῖτε (you witness THAT…), with εἶπον implicit — though some editors read the second ὅτι as continuing the content of εἶπον. Either way the adversative ἀλλά structures it as NOT-X / BUT-Y.

**v. 29** is a wedding-parable illustration in three movements:
- (a) Role identification: Ὁ ἔχων τὴν νύμφην νυμφίος ἐστίν — "the one who has the bride IS the bridegroom." Substantival participle as subject, predicate nominative νυμφίος.
- (b) Role of the friend: ὁ ... φίλος τοῦ νυμφίου ὁ ἑστηκὼς καὶ ἀκούων αὐτοῦ, χαρᾷ χαίρει διὰ τὴν φωνὴν τοῦ νυμφίου. Complex noun phrase (φίλος + genitive + double articular participle) serving as subject of χαίρει.
- (c) Personal application: αὕτη οὖν ἡ χαρὰ ἡ ἐμὴ πεπλήρωται — inferential οὖν draws the conclusion; note the double-articular attributive construction (ἡ χαρὰ ἡ ἐμή, "the joy, the mine"), which emphasizes possession and makes ἐμή attributive rather than predicative.

**v. 30** is a single clause with one governing verb (δεῖ) and two antithetical infinitives, joined by δέ. No finite verb in the second half — the δεῖ is gapped.

## Exegetical Summary

The grammar of John 3:28-30 is built on aspectually loaded perfect verbs (Ἀπεσταλμένος εἰμί, ἑστηκώς, πεπλήρωται) that depict abiding states rather than point actions, paired with a climactic use of impersonal δεῖ + present infinitives to frame an ongoing, divinely-ordained trajectory. John the Baptist presents himself as one who (1) stands commissioned in a settled state of having-been-sent, (2) stands at his post listening with full attention, (3) stands now in fullness of joy, and (4) is to be continually diminished as the active increase of Christ continues — a voice-contrast (active/passive) that makes Christ the agent and John the recipient of the eschatological reordering.
