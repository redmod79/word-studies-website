# WG: Image / Likeness — NT Representation Language

## Overview
Five Greek words share the semantic space of "image, likeness, form, and representation" in the NT. Together they describe how persons, objects, or realities correspond to, resemble, or derive from an original — spanning Christology (Christ as God's image), anthropology (humans as bearers of God's image), typology (law as shadow; fulfillment as image), and eschatology (image of the beast).

## Roles in the Concept

- **εἰκών** — the derived representation; used for God's image in man, Christ as God's visible image, coins, and the beast's idol
- **χαρακτήρ** — the precise replication; the Son as exact counterpart of the Father's substance (Heb 1:3 only)
- **ὁμοίωμα** — resemblance in external form; emphasizes similarity without identity (Christ in human likeness; Adam as type)
- **τύπος** — the prefigurative pattern; OT figures/events as types that shape NT reality
- **μορφή** — inherent essential form; used for Christ's pre-existent divine form (Phl 2:6) and servant form (Phl 2:7)

## Key Passages Where Multiple Group Words Appear

- **Rom 1:23** — εἰκών + ὁμοίωμα: "changed... into an image [eikōn] made like [homoiōma] to corruptible man"
- **Phl 2:6–7** — μορφή + ὁμοίωμα: "in the form [morphē] of God... in the likeness [homoiōma] of men"
- **Heb 10:1** — implicit contrast: σκιά (shadow) vs. εἰκών (true image) — from WG-shadow.md
