**Semantic shift in translation:** The LXX chose proselytos ("one who has come to") rather than xenos ("foreigner") or allotrios ("belonging to another"). This is a deliberate interpretive move: the ger is defined by arrival and integration, not by foreignness. The word already implies crossing over from outsider to insider — a trajectory that the NT era would narrow to mean religious conversion.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The proselyte is someone who has left their own people and joined the community of YHWH's worshippers
- Both ger and proselytos carry legal/covenantal obligations, not just physical presence
- Both require full inclusion: the ger under "one law" (Exo 12:49), the proselyte at Pentecost (Acts 2:10)

**What shifts:**
- OT ger is primarily a socio-legal category (resident alien with rights and duties); NT proselytos is primarily a religious category (convert to Judaism)
- The OT ger did not need to become ethnically Israelite — circumcision was required only for Passover participation (Exo 12:48); by the NT period, proselyte conversion involved circumcision, immersion, and sacrifice as a complete package
- Jesus' critique in Matt 23:15 targets the Pharisaic conversion system itself, suggesting the institutionalized proselyte process had become burdensome rather than welcoming — a reversal of the OT ethic of loving the ger
- The early church's Gentile inclusion debate (Acts 15) essentially asks: must Gentile believers become proselytes? The answer (no full proselyte conversion required) breaks the ger→proselytos chain and creates a new category
