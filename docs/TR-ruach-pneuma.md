**Semantic handling in translation:** The LXX translators split ruach's semantic range across multiple Greek words. Pneuma received the theological weight (divine Spirit, human spirit, life-breath), while anemos absorbed the purely meteorological sense. This division is cleaner than Hebrew, where a single word covers the entire range from physical wind to the Spirit of God.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The Spirit of God as active agent in creation, prophecy, and empowerment (Gen 1:2 → Acts 2:4)
- Ruach/pneuma as divine presence that fills, rests upon, and empowers chosen individuals (Exo 31:3 → Eph 5:18)
- The association of spirit with life — departure of spirit = death (Ecc 12:7 → James 2:26)
- Evil/unclean spirits as agents of deception (1 Sam 16:14 → Mark 1:23)

**What shifts:**
- **Scope of Spirit-giving widens:** OT Spirit comes upon select individuals (judges, prophets, kings); Joel 2:28/Acts 2 promises Spirit poured out on "all flesh"
- **Wind/spirit ambiguity narrows:** In the NT, anemos handles "wind" (31×), so pneuma skews heavily toward "spirit" (only John 3:8 clearly means wind). In the OT, ruach covers the full range without a separate "wind" word
- **Trinitarian identity:** NT progressively identifies the Spirit as a divine person alongside Father and Son (Matt 28:19, 2 Cor 13:14) — a development beyond the OT's "spirit of God/LORD" language
- **Ecclesiastes "vexation of spirit":** This distinctive pessimistic use of ruach (pursuit of wind/futility) has no NT parallel; the LXX itself shifted it to mataiotēs (vanity)
