## Lexicon Definition

(Thayer / Strong) From G264 (hamartanō); a sin (properly abstract): offence, sin(-ful). The word denotes (a) the act of missing or erring, (b) the resulting state of guilt/error, and (c) in Pauline usage, sin as an enslaving power.

## Textual Variant — 2 Thessalonians 2:3

The phrase ὁ ἄνθρωπος τῆς ἁμαρτίας ("the man of sin") is one of the most famous textual divergences involving G266.

| Text Tradition | Reading | Witnesses (representative) |
|----------------|---------|---------------------------|
| Textus Receptus / Byzantine / Majority | ὁ ἄνθρωπος τῆς **ἁμαρτίας** — "man of sin" | Byzantine mss majority, K, L, Ψ, minuscule tradition, Vulgate, Peshitta, KJV, NKJV |
| Critical Text (NA28/UBS5) / Alexandrian | ὁ ἄνθρωπος τῆς **ἀνομίας** — "man of lawlessness" | ℵ, B, 0278, 33, 81, 1739, 1881, co (Coptic), Origen; ESV, NIV, NASB, RSV |

**Semantic difference:**
- **ἁμαρτία (G266)** — sin as general category; broad ethical/theological failure
- **ἀνομία (G458)** — specifically "lawlessness," absence of/rebellion against νόμος (law)

The readings are near-synonyms in biblical usage — 1 John 3:4 explicitly equates them (ἡ ἁμαρτία ἐστὶν ἡ ἀνομία) — but ἀνομία carries a sharper connotation of antinomian rebellion, which pairs the figure in 2 Thess 2:3 with ὁ ἄνομος ("the lawless one") in 2 Thess 2:8 (where the text is undisputed). The critical text reading yields internal cohesion between v. 3 and v. 8. The Byzantine reading harmonizes with the broader NT idiom "man of X" for moral character.

Either reading preserves the same referent (an end-time figure opposed to God); the difference is rhetorical emphasis, not identity.

**Translation pattern:** The LXX overwhelmingly uses ἁμαρτία as a flat equivalent for the Hebrew sin-nouns. The Hebrew triad חטאת / עון / פשע (sin / iniquity / rebellion) is routinely collapsed into ἁμαρτία (+ ἀνομία, ἀδικία, παράπτωμα as secondary options), dissolving finer Hebrew distinctions. The Hebrew "sin offering" sense of חַטָּאת survives in NT formulas like περὶ ἁμαρτίας (Rom 8:3; Heb 10 cluster).

## First Occurrence

**NT:** Matt 1:21 — "thou shalt call his name JESUS: for he shall save his people from their sins."

The NT opens the word's usage by placing sin directly opposite the name "Jesus" (Ἰησοῦς / Yeshua, "YHWH saves"). Salvation is defined as salvation *from sins*, framing the entire NT's use of G266 around a rescue verb.
