**Note on G4633 as a shared bridge:** The LXX uses σκηνή to translate multiple Hebrew tent/dwelling words — H168 אֹהֶל (ohel, 230x), H4908 מִשְׁכָּן (mishkan, 88x), and H5521 sukkah (20x). The word is not uniquely "derived from" sukkah, but the Feast of Tabernacles (σκηνοπηγία, G4634) is specifically grounded in sukkah's meaning.

**Semantic shift:** Hebrew sukkah has a range from mundane temporary shelter (animal booth, watchman's hut, military tent) to divine pavilion. The LXX σκηνή preserves the structural/physical sense but in NT contexts the word gravitates toward the sacred — the Mosaic tabernacle and its heavenly counterpart.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The physical image of a temporary booth/tent as a place of divine meeting or protection
- The corporate, memorial character — people dwelling together in temporary structures before God
- The eschatological forward-pointing: a sukkah is inherently temporary, awaiting something more permanent

**What shifts:**
- Hebrew sukkah spans mundane (animal stall, military tent) to sacred (God's pavilion); NT σκηνή gravitates almost entirely toward the sacred — the Mosaic tabernacle, the heavenly sanctuary, the body as tent
- The Feast of Tabernacles pattern (sukkah → σκηνοπηγία) shifts from historical commemoration (exodus) to eschatological fulfillment (Rev 21:3) and present typology (Heb 8–9)
- The bodily dimension: NT introduces σκῆνος (G4636) as the body-as-tent metaphor (2 Cor 5:1–4), absent from sukkah usage
