**Semantic shift in translation:** Remarkably little shift. The LXX translators consistently mapped the entire qodesh word family to the hagios word family. The Hebrew noun qodesh (which can mean "holiness," "sanctuary," or "holy thing") is flattened to the Greek adjective hagios, losing some of the noun's flexibility. The superlative "qodesh qodashim" (holy of holies) is rendered as "hagia hagion" or "to hagion ton hagion" in Greek.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- Holiness as fundamentally a divine attribute that God shares with people, places, and things
- The spatial holiness gradient (holy → most holy) preserved in Hebrews 9
- The command for God's people to be holy because God is holy (Lev 19:2 → 1 Pet 1:16)
- "Saints" (hagioi) as the NT term for God's set-apart people, continuous with OT Israel as qodesh

**What shifts:**
- The NT substantivizes hagios far more: "saints" (hagioi) becomes a standard term for believers (47 of 219 occurrences), where the OT rarely used qodesh for persons
- The sanctuary language shifts: OT qodesh refers to the physical tabernacle/temple; NT hagios in Hebrews reinterprets the sanctuary as heavenly and Christ's ministry
- Holiness becomes more explicitly ethical/moral in the NT (1 Pet 1:15, Rom 12:1), while OT qodesh has a stronger ritual/ceremonial dimension
