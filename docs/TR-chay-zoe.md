| chay (H2416) | ψυχή (G5590) | 62x | Overlap with nephesh chayyah constructions |

**Semantic shift in translation:** Hebrew chay is fundamentally an adjective meaning "living, alive" — it describes vitality. The LXX splits this into a noun (ζωή = life as a substance) and a verb (ζάω = to be alive). This Greek split is significant: ζωή takes on the weight of "life" as an abstract quality or gift, while ζάω retains the verbal sense of being biologically alive. The NT inherits ζωή in its elevated, substantival sense.

Phrases like מַיִם חַיִּים ("living waters") → ὕδωρ ζῶν, and עֵץ הַחַיִּים ("tree of life") → ξύλον τῆς ζωῆς, carry into Revelation unchanged.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same:**
- Life belongs to and flows from God — both testaments present life as God's possession bestowed on creatures
- The oath formula "as I live" (חַי אָנִי) carries the same force as NT uses of the "living God"
- Concrete images (water, tree) maintain the same vocabulary from LXX through Revelation

**What shifts:**
- Hebrew chay is primarily adjectival (describing the state of being alive); NT ζωή becomes a fully substantival noun — something one can possess, receive, or lose
- The eschatological dimension is greatly intensified in the NT: ζωή αἰώνιος ("eternal life") as a distinct category appears almost entirely in the NT, developed beyond OT chay's primarily biological sense
- In John's Gospel, ζωή is christologically concentrated — Jesus does not merely give life, he *is* life (John 11:25; 14:6), a claim with no direct Hebrew-OT parallel in the chay word group
