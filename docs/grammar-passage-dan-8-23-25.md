# Grammar Analysis: Daniel 8:23–25

**Context-neutral passage analysis.** This entry describes grammatical forms and clause architecture in the Hebrew of the little-horn recapitulation oracle (Dan 8:23–25). It documents the interpretive options the grammar permits. It does not adjudicate them.

---

## Key Grammatical Features

### 1. מֶלֶךְ עַז־פָּנִים + וּמֵבִין חִידוֹת — Two stacked characterizations of the "king"

**Morphology.**
- עַז־פָּנִים — adjective in construct (`Adj.ms.Cst`) + masc. plural noun (`Noun.mp.Abs`). This is the **adjectival construct** pattern, in which a quality is predicated of a body-part (GKC §128p; Joüon §129i). Parallels: עַז־נֶפֶשׁ "greedy of soul" (Isa 56:11), קְשֵׁה־עֹרֶף "stiff of neck" (Ex 32:9). The lexeme עַז denotes "fierce, strong, bold, hard, harsh, impudent."
- פָּנִים is the standard plural-only form for "face" (lemma פנה, H6440). See word study [H6440-paniym](../../word-studies/H6440-paniym.md) — and its Greek counterpart [TR-paniym-prosopon](../../word-studies/TR-paniym-prosopon.md).
- וּמֵבִין חִידֹות — waw + Hiphil active participle (`Verb.Hiphil.Ptcp.ms.Abs`) of בין "to understand, discern," with a plural f. direct object חִידֹות (lemma חידה, H2420 "riddle, dark saying, enigma"). The two phrases are coordinated attributes of the subject מֶלֶךְ — both flag that the king is being *characterized*, not just named.

**The phrase עַז־פָּנִים elsewhere.** The only other OT occurrence is Deut 28:50 גֹּוי עַז־פָּנִים "a nation of fierce countenance" — describing the distant invading judgment-nation that shows no favor to old or young. The lexical/syntactic echo is exact and widely noted by commentators.

**מֵבִין חִידוֹת elsewhere.** The idiom "understanding riddles/enigmas" recalls the Joseph tradition (Gen 41; "interpret dreams") and Solomon (1 Ki 10:1 = 2 Chr 9:1 חִידוֹת; Prov 1:6). In Numbers 12:8 חִידֹת is contrasted with God's direct speech to Moses. The semantic field includes deception, political maneuvering, mysteries, and crafty speech. The participle form describes a settled, characterizing competence (not a one-off act).

**Note on the combined portrait.** Verses 23 opens the king with two qualities — harsh face *and* cunning mind — a bi-polar characterization ("fierce + sly") that the rest of the pericope unpacks: v. 24 = the fierce/destructive side, v. 25 = the sly/deceitful side.

### 2. וְעָצַם כֹּחוֹ וְלֹא בְכֹחוֹ — "And his power shall be mighty, but not by his (own) power"

**Morphology.**
- וְעָצַם — waw + Qal perfect 3ms (`Verb.Qal.Perf.3ms`) of עצם "be mighty, numerous, strong." In this WQtX-chain (weqatal + X) functioning in predictive discourse, the form carries future sense.
- כֹּחֹו — noun masc. sg. (lemma כח "strength, power") + 3ms suffix: "his strength."
- וְלֹא בְכֹחוֹ — waw + negative + prep. בְ + noun + 3ms suffix.

**The verbless/nominal clause.** ETCBC analyzes וְלֹא בְכֹחוֹ as `NmCl` — a **verbless nominal clause** with a negated prepositional adjunct and no predicate verb. Verbless clauses in Hebrew take their subject and temporal reference from context; here the tacit subject is the foregoing כֹּחֹו / עָצַם כֹּחֹו. In English this is usually rendered "but not by his (own) power," treating the waw as disjunctive/adversative (see [syntax-waw-conjunction](../hebrew/syntax-waw-conjunction.md) on conjunctive vs. disjunctive waw).

**Force of the construction.** The clause is a **disjunctive nominal correction** of the preceding predicate — a Hebrew idiom for "X is true, but not in the sense you'd expect." The repetition of כח (his power … not by his power) is deliberate paronomasia: the very word supplying the strength-claim is repeated to deny its origin. Grammatically this says two things at once:

1. His strength is great (affirmative, v. 24 clause 1).
2. The source of that strength is *not* himself (negative, v. 24 clause 2).

The clause leaves the *actual* source of the power unspecified grammatically — it is a gap for context, parallels, or theology to fill. Commentators read this either as:
- Another agent (divine permission — passive/agentless backdrop; cf. the ἐδόθη pattern in Dan 7 LXX and Rev 13; see Greek grammar entry [divine-passive](../greek/divine-passive.md))
- A borrowed political/military base (a predecessor or coalition)
- Supernatural malevolent backing (e.g., Dan 10:13's princely warfare; or 2 Thess 2:9 κατ' ἐνέργειαν τοῦ Σατανᾶ — see [energeia-subjective-genitive](../greek/energeia-subjective-genitive.md))

**The grammar does not name the agent.** It only denies that the agent is the king himself.

### 3. וְנִפְלָאֹות יַשְׁחִית — "He shall destroy wondrously / marvelous destruction"

**Morphology.**
- נִפְלָאֹות — Niphal participle feminine plural (`Verb.Niphal.Ptcp.fp.Abs`) of פלא "be wonderful, extraordinary." The Niphal of this root is regularly substantivized as a plural abstract: "wondrous acts, marvels, extraordinary things" (cf. Ps 9:1; 26:7; 40:5; Job 37:14). See [stem-niphal](../hebrew/stem-niphal.md) on the stem's functions.
- יַשְׁחִית — Hiphil imperfect 3ms (`Verb.Hiphil.Impf.3ms`) of שׁחת, the causative stem: "cause to ruin / destroy." See word study [H7843-shachath](../../word-studies/H7843-shachath.md) and its Greek mapping [TR-shachath-diaphtheiro](../../word-studies/TR-shachath-diaphtheiro.md).
- The clause is **`WxY0`** — conjunction + fronted object (NIPH.ptcp substantivized) + yiqtol. The object is pre-verbal, placing emphasis on *what kind* of destruction occurs.

**Semantic puzzle.** The word נִפְלָאֹות in the Hebrew Bible is almost always used of **God's marvelous works** — salvific, creational, theophanic wonders (Ex 3:20; Jdg 6:13; Ps 72:18; 86:10; 98:1; 106:22). For this word to appear as the object of a **destructive** Hiphil (שׁחת) is striking. Three readings are discussed in the grammars:

1. **Adverbial accusative:** "he shall destroy wondrously" — the substantivized ptcp. plural functions as manner-accusative (GKC §117q). KJV "destroy wonderfully."
2. **Internal/object accusative:** "he shall cause marvelous destruction," i.e., what he destroys are things that are themselves extraordinary, or the destruction itself is of marvelous scope.
3. **Ironic reuse of divine vocabulary:** deliberate intertextual shock — the king mimics or usurps divine prerogative-language (cf. the same root at Dan 11:36 יְדַבֵּר נִפְלָאֹות "he shall speak wondrous things" against the God of gods).

The grammar tolerates all three; English versions split between "wonderfully" (KJV/ASV/NASB) and "fearful/astounding destruction" (NIV/ESV).

### 4. וְהִצְלִיחַ וְעָשָׂה — "And shall prosper and practise"

**Morphology.**
- וְהִצְלִיחַ — waw + Hiphil perfect 3ms (`Verb.Hiphil.Perf.3ms`) of צלח. Qal = "succeed"; Hiphil = transitive-causative "cause to succeed / prosper" OR intransitive-declarative "be successful." Both uses are attested.
- וְעָשָׂה — waw + Qal perfect 3ms (`Verb.Qal.Perf.3ms`) of עשׂה "do, make, act." Both verbs are **absolute** — no object specified.

**The "absolute" collocation.** The pair וְהִצְלִיחַ וְעָשָׂה is unusual for having **no object**. Compare Dan 8:12 וְעָשְׂתָה וְהִצְלִיחָה (reverse order, same intransitive absolute use, ascribed there to the little-horn's earlier acts), and the same Hiphil used twice more in the immediate pericope:
- v. 25 וְהִצְלִיחַ מִרְמָה בְּיָדֹו — "he shall cause deceit to prosper in his hand" (transitive, with object מִרְמָה)

The objectless וְהִצְלִיחַ וְעָשָׂה is therefore **summary predication** — "he shall succeed and act" / "he shall carry things out and prosper" — a telegraphic declaration that whatever he undertakes, he gets away with. The two weqatals are consecutive (sequential future: see [weqatal-apodosis-vow](../hebrew/weqatal-apodosis-vow.md) for weqatal as sequential-future, and [syntax-wx-qatal-circumstantial](../hebrew/syntax-wx-qatal-circumstantial.md) on its interaction with x-yiqtol in prose).

**Why it matters.** The pairing creates a rhetorical counterpoint to the final clause וּבְאֶפֶס יָד יִשָּׁבֵר: success-success-success … then abrupt passive cessation. The pattern is **unopposed action → sudden external termination** (Dan 2:34–35 paradigm).

### 5. וְעַל־שַׂר־שָׂרִים יַעֲמֹד — "And shall stand up against the Prince of princes"

**Morphology.**
- וְעַל — waw + preposition על. With יַעֲמֹד this preposition characteristically means "against / in opposition to" (BDB עמד, §1.e; cf. Dan 8:25; 11:14). With יַעֲמֹד in v. 23 (no prep.) the same verb means simply "arise, appear." The preposition shifts the semantics.
- שַׂר־שָׂרִים — construct chain: singular construct + plural absolute, the classic **superlative of governance** ("prince of princes" = supreme prince). Parallels: מֶלֶךְ מְלָכִים "king of kings" (Ezr 7:12 Aram.; 1 Tim 6:15; Rev 17:14), אֱלֹהֵי הָאֱלֹהִים "God of gods" (Deut 10:17), קֹדֶשׁ הַקֳּדָשִׁים "holy of holies," שִׁיר הַשִּׁירִים "song of songs." See word study [H8269-sar](../../word-studies/H8269-sar.md) and Greek mapping [TR-sar-archon](../../word-studies/TR-sar-archon.md).
- יַעֲמֹד — Qal imperfect 3ms of עמד "stand" — the same verb as v. 23's יַעֲמֹד. The same verb brackets the oracle: in v. 23 the king "arises" (no prep.); in v. 25 he "takes his stand against" a superior figure. The inclusio is deliberate.

**Referent of שַׂר־שָׂרִים.** Elsewhere in Daniel שַׂר is used for:
- Angelic princes / patron-princes of nations — Michael "one of the chief princes" (Dan 10:13 אַחַד הַשָּׂרִים הָרִאשֹׁנִים); "the prince of Persia," "the prince of Greece" (10:13, 20); "Michael your prince" (10:21); "Michael, the great prince" (12:1).
- The "Prince of the host" (שַׂר־הַצָּבָא) in 8:11, from whom the little-horn takes away the tamid.
- The "Prince of princes" here (שַׂר־שָׂרִים) in 8:25.

Grammatically the superlative construct-chain permits (a) the top of the angelic-prince hierarchy (Michael as "prince of princes"), (b) a divine reference (God as commander-in-chief of all שָׂרִים), or (c) a Messianic/Christological identification (cross-referencing Dan 9:25 מָשִׁיחַ נָגִיד "messiah-prince"). The grammar alone does not decide between these; the lexical field is consistent with all three.

### 6. וּבְאֶפֶס יָד יִשָּׁבֵר — "But he shall be broken without hand"

**Morphology.**
- אֶפֶס — noun masc. sg. in construct (`Noun.ms.Cst`) — root meaning "cessation, end, nothing, nothingness." Nominalized and used as a quasi-preposition "without" when in construct with another noun. BDB אֶפֶס II.c "without." Glossed by the parser as "end" (of the construct sense).
- יָד — "hand" (lemma יד, H3027). See word study [TR-yad-cheir](../../word-studies/TR-yad-cheir.md) for the hand-metonymy semantics (agency/power/intervention).
- אֶפֶס יָד — construct chain: "cessation of hand" → idiomatic "no human agency," "without (anyone's) hand."
- יִשָּׁבֵר — **Niphal imperfect 3ms** (`Verb.Niphal.Impf.3ms`) of שׁבר "break, shatter" → Niphal passive: "be broken, be shattered." See [stem-niphal](../hebrew/stem-niphal.md) for the passive function of the Niphal. The same stem pattern ends Dan 8:14's oracle (וְנִצְדַּק) — both are Niphals in clause-final position describing the fate of the oracle's central action.

**The "agent-less passive" construction.** The two key grammatical moves work together:

1. **Niphal voice** (`יִשָּׁבֵר`) = the subject receives the action; agent need not be named.
2. **אֶפֶס יָד** = idiom explicitly *denying* human agency.

The Niphal alone would have been ambiguous about the source. The **addition of the אֶפֶס־יָד adjunct** deliberately closes off the "human hand" reading and thereby **identifies, by exclusion, a non-human agent** — exactly the inverse grammatical move of v. 24 (עָצַם כֹּחֹו וְלֹא בְכֹחֹו: "strong, but not by his own strength"). Both pairs operate by negation: v. 24 denies the king's *own* power as source; v. 25 denies *human* power as the destroyer. The grammar of the pericope thus brackets the king between two non-human forces — an ambiguous empowering agent at the start, and a supernatural terminating agent at the end.

**Intra-Danielic parallel — Dan 2:34, 45.**
- 2:34 (Aram.) אִתְגְּזֶרֶת אֶבֶן דִּי־לָא בִידַיִן — "a stone was cut out without hands" (di-la vidayin, "that-not by-hands").
- 2:45 טוּרָא אִתְגְּזֶרֶת אֶבֶן דִּי־לָא בִידַיִן — same formula, with the stone's destruction of the image.

The Aramaic דִּי־לָא בִידַיִן (Dan 2) and the Hebrew בְאֶפֶס יָד (Dan 8) are distinct idioms but make the same grammatical point: a passive verb whose agent is explicitly *not* human. Daniel opens (ch. 2) and climaxes the ram-goat vision (ch. 8) with the same theological grammar.

**Inter-testamental parallel — 2 Thessalonians 2:8.** Paul's οὗτος ὁ ἄνομος … ὃν ὁ κύριος [Ἰησοῦς] ἀνελεῖ τῷ πνεύματι τοῦ στόματος αὐτοῦ καὶ καταργήσει τῇ ἐπιφανείᾳ τῆς παρουσίας αὐτοῦ uses two future active verbs (ἀνελεῖ "will slay," καταργήσει "will abolish") with explicit divine agent (ὁ κύριος) and instrument (τῷ πνεύματι τοῦ στόματος, τῇ ἐπιφανείᾳ τῆς παρουσίας). The Greek names what Hebrew grammatically refuses to name: the agent *is* God/the Lord, acting personally at the παρουσία. Where Dan 8:25's grammar denies human agency and leaves divine agency implicit, 2 Thess 2:8 supplies the explicit agent-and-means.

For the Greek parallel's grammar see [textual-variant-2thess-2-3](../greek/textual-variant-2thess-2-3.md) (for the ἄνομος / ἁμαρτία identification) and [energeia-subjective-genitive](../greek/energeia-subjective-genitive.md) (on the agency/working vocabulary of 2 Thess 2).

## Placement: how 23–25 relates to 8:9–14

The oracle in **Dan 8:9–14** narrates the vision as vision — a little horn arises, grows, reaches heaven, casts truth to the ground, takes away the tamid, and asks "how long?" The visionary verbs are a mix of wayyiqtol narrative (in vision-report mode) and yiqtol description. The pericope culminates in the 2,300 evening-mornings oracle at v. 14 (see [dan-8-14](dan-8-14.md) for that verse's grammar).

**Dan 8:23–25** is not a second-wave prediction. It is Gabriel's *interpretation* of 8:9–14 — the angel giving Daniel the key to the vision he has seen. The shift is marked by:

| 8:9–14 | 8:23–25 |
|--------|---------|
| Vision-narration: wayyiqtol-dominant | Angelic oracle: weqatal / x-yiqtol (predictive prose) |
| Imagery: "horn," "host," "truth," "tamid," "sanctuary" | Interpretation: "king," "power," "holy people," "Prince of princes" |
| Temporal deictic: 2,300 evening-mornings → תצדק קדש | Temporal deictic: "in the latter time of their kingdom" (אַחֲרִית מַלְכוּתָם); "at the time of the end" (v. 17) |
| Acts listed in *visionary* sequence | Acts listed in *thematic* pairs (fierce/cunning, prosper/destroy, against-Prince/broken) |
| Open question: "how long?" (v. 13) | Terminal closure: "he shall be broken without hand" (v. 25) |

Thus 23–25 is the **recap-and-expansion** of 9–14, not a different referent or a second horn. The same grammatical subject ("he") is carried through; v. 17 and v. 19 frame the whole block as an oracle about "the time of the end" / "the last end of the indignation" (עֵת־קֵץ, אַחֲרִית הַזָּעַם). The Hebrew verbal morphology shifts genre (narrative vision → predictive prose) but the referent is continuous.

A concrete lexical bridge: יַעֲמֹד ("shall stand up") occurs in v. 22 (of the four kings arising from the broken Greek horn), v. 23 (of the fierce-faced king arising), and v. 25 (of his standing against the Prince of princes). The threefold repetition of עמד knits the interpretation to the vision's verbs (גָּדַל, עָצַם, רָם, עָשָׂה — 8:9–12) by *substituting* a political-stance verb (עמד "take one's stand") for the visionary growth verbs (גדל, עצם).

## Interpretive Options for מֶלֶךְ עַז־פָּנִים

The **grammar permits multiple referents**. The following schools of interpretation are all represented in historical commentary. This entry catalogs them without adjudicating.

### A. Antiochus IV Epiphanes (Seleucid, d. 164 BC)

**Grammatical fit.**
- אַחֲרִית מַלְכוּתָם — the "latter time of their kingdom" (=Seleucid, the last of the Diadochi dynasties per v. 22). Natural reading if the four-horn king-successors of v. 22 are the Diadochi and "their kingdom" is collectively the Hellenistic kingdoms.
- עַז־פָּנִים matches Deut 28:50's invading-nation typology; Antiochus' reputation for harshness in 1 Macc 1 / 2 Macc 5 matches the idiom.
- וּמֵבִין חִידוֹת — Antiochus' diplomatic-political craft (double-dealing with Jerusalem high priests; staged "peaceful" coups).
- וְלֹא בְכֹחוֹ — the Seleucid empire of Antiochus IV was in fact in decline and depended on Roman tolerance / client-state manipulation.
- יַשְׁחִית עֲצוּמִים וְעַם־קְדֹשִׁים — persecution of the Maccabean-era pious (the חֲסִידִים, עַם־קְדֹשִׁים).
- וְעַל־שַׂר־שָׂרִים יַעֲמֹד — opposition to YHWH / the high priest / the Jerusalem cultus.
- וּבְאֶפֶס יָד יִשָּׁבֵר — Antiochus' sudden disease-death in Persia (1 Macc 6; 2 Macc 9; Polybius 31.9) with no human conqueror.

**Grammatical strain.**
- The chronology demanded by 2,300 "evening-mornings" (8:14) and the "time of the end" (עֵת־קֵץ) framing (8:17, 19) stretches the historical fit.
- "Prince of princes" as YHWH directly is supportable but requires reading past the angelic-prince semantic field of chs. 10–12.

### B. Rome (pagan + papal continuum)

**Grammatical fit.**
- אַחֲרִית מַלְכוּתָם — read "their" as the collective Greek succession, with Rome as the successor-kingdom that "stands up" (v. 23 יַעֲמֹד) in Greece's "latter time." The little horn of 8:9 emerges *from* one of the four Greek winds, but its growth ("exceeding great," גָּדַל יֶתֶר) into the "glorious land" and sanctuary matches Rome's Eastern incorporation.
- Deut 28:49–50 עַז־פָּנִים גֹּוי is read typologically of Rome ("from the end of the earth … swift as the eagle").
- וּמֵבִין חִידוֹת — Roman political-ecclesiastical statecraft.
- וְלֹא בְכֹחוֹ — in the continuum reading, the papal phase is understood as drawing civil power from converted states (cf. Rev 13:2 "the dragon gave him his power") rather than its own military base. The ἐνέργεια / ἐδόθη pattern of NT apocalyptic applies: see [energeia-subjective-genitive](../greek/energeia-subjective-genitive.md) and [aorist-passive-indicative](../greek/aorist-passive-indicative.md).
- יַשְׁחִית עֲצוּמִים — destruction of Jewish polity and temple (70 AD, pagan phase); persecution of saints over the long durée (continuum view).
- וְעַל־שַׂר־שָׂרִים יַעֲמֹד — crucifixion of the Messiah / "Prince of princes" in the pagan phase; or doctrinal/claim-to-mediation opposition to Christ's priestly ministry in the papal phase.
- וּבְאֶפֶס יָד יִשָּׁבֵר — read as the eschatological destruction at the Second Advent (parallel to 2 Thess 2:8 τῇ ἐπιφανείᾳ τῆς παρουσίας and Dan 2:34 stone-cut-without-hands).

**Grammatical strain.**
- אַחֲרִית מַלְכוּתָם with plural suffix "their" reads most naturally of the immediately preceding plural subject — the four Greek kings of v. 22. Extending "their" to include Rome is a typological extension.

### C. Papal Succession (papal-only, distinct from pagan Rome)

**Grammatical fit.** Reads v. 9's little horn as emerging through Rome-the-vehicle but referring specifically to the post-imperial ecclesiastical-political power that succeeded it.
- Same grammatical cues as B, but restricted to the ecclesiastical phase.
- וְלֹא בְכֹחוֹ — papal reliance on the "kings of the earth" for civil enforcement (classic Reformation reading; cf. Rev 17).
- וְעַל־שַׂר־שָׂרִים יַעֲמֹד — opposition to Christ's heavenly priestly ministry (cf. Heb 9; Dan 8:11 "prince of the host" and the tamid).
- וּבְאֶפֶס יָד יִשָּׁבֵר — eschatological destruction "not by human hand" at the Second Advent (parallel to 2 Thess 2:8; Dan 2:34).

### D. Eschatological-only / other

Various non-historicist schemes read מֶלֶךְ עַז־פָּנִים as a purely end-time figure (e.g., an eschatological antichrist not identifiable with any historical regime until his appearing). The grammar of v. 17 (לְעֵת־קֵץ הֶחָזוֹן) and v. 19 (לְאַחֲרִית הַזָּעַם) is taken literally terminal.

### What the grammar does and does not decide

**What the grammar does decide.**
- The king's power is mighty — and not self-sourced (v. 24).
- His destruction is characterized as נִפְלָאֹות, a word from the divine-wonder vocabulary (v. 24).
- He stands up against the highest Prince (v. 25) — "prince of princes" is a superlative, not just "a great prince."
- His own end is a Niphal-passive + agent-denying adjunct: **not by human hand** (v. 25).
- The agent of his destruction is left grammatically unnamed in Hebrew; named in Greek at 2 Thess 2:8 (ὁ κύριος at the παρουσία) and imaged in Dan 2:34 (the stone cut without hands).

**What the grammar does not decide.**
- Who the king is (Antiochus, Rome pagan, Rome papal, purely eschatological, etc.).
- The temporal span between his rise and his breaking.
- Whether "Prince of princes" is Michael, YHWH directly, or the Messiah-prince.
- Whether "their kingdom" (אַחֲרִית מַלְכוּתָם) refers narrowly to the four Greek successor-kingdoms or typologically to the fourfold sweep of Dan 2 / Dan 7.

These are theological and historical-referential questions; the grammar constrains the options but does not close them.
