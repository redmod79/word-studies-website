**Semantic shift:** Minimal. *Pheídomai* ("to be chary of, refrain, spare") captures the core châmal sense — withholding what one has power to inflict. The Greek verb collapses the Hebrew near-synonyms (*châmal / chûs / châsak*) into a single lexeme, so the fine distinctions between "pity-that-withholds" (châmal), "eye-that-pities" (chûs), and "hand-that-withholds" (châsak) blur in Greek. When the emotional/merciful nuance of châmal is foregrounded, LXX shifts to *eleéō*.

**Key NT passages:**

- **Rom 8:32** — "He that spared (*pheídomai*) not his own Son, but delivered him up for us all" — a direct intertextual echo of the Aqedah (Gen 22:12 LXX "ouk epheisō tou huiou sou" / Heb. "lo châsaktâ"), but with God in Abraham's role and a decisive reversal.
- **2 Pet 2:4–5** — "if God spared (*pheídomai*) not the angels that sinned... spared not the old world" — YHWH-style judgment language ported directly: the God who "did not spare" of Lam 2 and Ezek 7 reappears in the NT apocalyptic register.
- **Acts 20:29** — "grievous wolves enter in among you, not sparing the flock" — the classic châmal-as-predator-restraint idiom applied to false teachers.
- **2 Cor 1:23** — "to spare you I came not as yet unto Corinth" — Paul inverts the judgment-sparing idiom to describe pastoral restraint.
- **1 Cor 7:28** — "I spare you" — apostolic compassion-restraint.
- **Rom 11:21** — "if God spared not the natural branches, take heed lest he also spare not thee" — Paul applies châmal-style warning to Gentile believers.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The core action-concept: *withholding an otherwise-warranted blow or destruction*.
- The negated form ("did NOT spare") as the rhetorically loaded construction — equally dominant in châmal and pheídomai usage.
- The divine-subject framework: God as the one who either spares or declines to spare.
- Collocation with destruction-verbs (OT: *hârag*, *châram*, *nâkâh*; NT: "deliver up," "cast down," "destroy").

**What shifts:**
- The Hebrew triplet *châmal / chûs / châsak* — with fine distinctions between pitying-restraint, eye-pity, and hand-withholding — collapses into Greek *pheídomai*, flattening the distinctions.
- The emotional-pity nuance of châmal migrates in many LXX contexts to *eleéō* (G1653, whence NT ἔλεος / eleos), so in the NT the "mercy" side of châmal's semantic field is largely carried by the *eleos* word-group rather than by *pheídomai*.
- The gospel reversal in Rom 8:32: the châmal/châsak pattern of "a father spares his son" (Mal 3:17; Gen 22) is decisively broken — God does NOT spare — precisely to enact the deepest possible mercy. The withholding of châmal becomes the means of ultimate mercy.
