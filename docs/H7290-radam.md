## Lexicon Definition

> A primitive root; to stun, i.e. stupefy (with sleep or death):—(be fast a-, be in a deep, cast into a dead, that) sleep(-er, -eth).
> — Strong's H7290

The gloss "stun / stupefy" is significant: rādam is not ordinary sleep (yāšēn, H3462) but an *overwhelmed* state, something imposed on the subject — whether by exhaustion, by a divine agent, or as a metaphor for death.

All 7 occurrences are Niphal — the root does not appear in Qal or other active stems in the MT. The Niphal is semantically stative/passive: the subject is *acted upon* (stupefied) rather than actively sleeping. BHSA parses 6 of the 7 as Niphal participle (ongoing state: "being stunned/in deep sleep") and 1 as Niphal perfect 1cs (Dan 8:18, `nirdamtî`). Jonah 1:5 is Niphal wayyiqtol 3ms.

## Semantic Categories

### 1. Deep sleep induced by divine encounter (prophetic stupor)
The Niphal state that falls on a prophet in the presence of a heavenly being — a functional equivalent of the `tardēmāh` (H8639) that fell on Abram (Gen 15:12), Adam (Gen 2:21), and Saul's camp (1 Sam 26:12).
- **Dan 8:18** — "Now as he was speaking with me, I was in a deep sleep (`nirdamtî`) on my face toward the ground: but he touched me, and set me upright."
- **Dan 10:9** — "when I heard the voice of his words, then was I in a deep sleep (`nirdām`) on my face, and my face toward the ground."

### 2. Deep sleep as divinely imposed judgment
The stupor that God casts on enemies, rendering them unable to resist.
- **Ps 76:6 [MT 76:7]** — "At thy rebuke, O God of Jacob, both the chariot and horse are cast into a dead sleep (`nirdām`)." The parallelism with v.5 ("they have slept their sleep... none of the men of might have found their hands") shows rādam here = battlefield paralysis imposed by YHWH's rebuke (`gǎʿǎrāh`).

### 3. Deep sleep of exhaustion preceding death
A stupor of vulnerability that opens the subject to destruction.
- **Jdg 4:21** — "he was fast asleep (`nirdām`) and weary (`wayyāʿap`). So he died." Sisera's rādam state — an exhausted collapse — is the precondition for Jael's tent-peg. The text yokes rādam directly to `ʿāyēp` (weary) and then to `mût` (he died).

### 4. Deep sleep of negligence / moral failure
In Wisdom and prophetic narrative, being found `nirdām` at the critical moment is shameful.
- **Prov 10:5** — "He that gathereth in summer is a wise son: but he that sleepeth (`nirdām`) in harvest is a son that causeth shame." Sleeping through the harvest season.
- **Jonah 1:5** — "But Jonah was gone down into the sides of the ship; and he lay, and was fast asleep (`wayyērādam`)."
- **Jonah 1:6** — "So the shipmaster came to him, and said unto him, What meanest thou, O sleeper (`nirdām`)? arise, call upon thy God..." The shipmaster's rebuke — "What are you doing in this stupor? Get up!" — treats the rādam state as culpable abdication.

## Grammatical Note: The Niphal-Only Pattern

That all 7 occurrences are Niphal is itself a lexical fact: rādam exists in the MT only as a state that *befalls* the subject. There is no active "to deeply-sleep someone" — the causative must be supplied by another agent (God in Ps 76:6; exhaustion in Jdg 4:21; angelic presence in Dan 8:18 / 10:9). This makes every rādam occurrence implicitly ask: *what agent or cause induced this stupor?*

The Niphal participle (6 of 7 forms) conveys ongoing state ("[he is] in a stupefied condition"), while the single Niphal perfect `nirdamtî` (Dan 8:18) is Daniel's first-person recollection of the completed event: "I fell into / entered the stupor." The wayyiqtol at Jonah 1:5 (`wayyērādam`) frames the onset narratively: "and he [went and] fell into deep sleep."
