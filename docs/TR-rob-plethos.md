**Semantic profile in translation:** The LXX strongly favors plēthos as the primary rendering of rob (95 of 144 OT verses, ~66%). The Hebrew rob is abstract — "abundance in any respect" (quantity, size, extent). Greek plēthos concretizes this toward "a group, a body, a mass of people." The shift is from uncountable abundance to a collective noun that can serve as a grammatical subject. This is why NT plēthos typically denotes a gathered body of persons rather than abstract quantity.

**Competing Greek renderings:** πολύς (polys, "much/many") captures the adjectival sense of rob where plēthos captures the nominal/collective sense. The LXX thus splits rob's semantic range: plēthos for "the multitude of X" and polys for "many X."

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The core quantitative sense of "large number, abundance" carries through intact from Hebrew rob to Greek plēthos
- The divine-promise formula ("innumerable as stars/sand for multitude") bridges directly from OT to Hebrews 11:12
- The wisdom-tradition maxim about covering sins (Prov 10:12) carries through to James and 1 Peter unchanged

**What shifts:**
- OT rob is predominantly abstract and inanimate ("abundance of wealth/years/sins/chariots"); NT plēthos is predominantly concrete and personal ("the multitude of believers/disciples/people")
- NT plēthos acquires a technical ecclesial sense absent from the OT: the gathered believing community as a decision-making body (Acts 6:2, 5; 15:12, 30) — this reflects Lukan usage of the LXX word in a new institutional context
- The grammatical shift from masculine rob to neuter plēthos creates the NT constructio ad sensum pattern where a neuter singular collective takes plural verbs (Luke 19:37, Acts 4:32, Acts 21:36)
