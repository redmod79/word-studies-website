**Semantic shift:** The Hebrew gowy is purely ethno-national (no moral loading in itself). The LXX preserves this with ethnos, but the increasing use of ethnos as a contrast to "the circumcision" in Jewish literature gave the word a secondary religious valence by the NT era. The NT thus uses ethnos in both registers — neutral (all peoples) and loaded (pagan outsiders).

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same:**
- The word refers to ethno-national groups throughout both testaments
- Israel is distinct from the nations (gōyim/ethnē) — this binary persists from Genesis to Revelation
- The nations can be objects of both judgment and redemption (prophetic dual vision in Isaiah, Psalms → fulfilled in Acts, Romans, Revelation)

**What shifts:**
- In the OT, gowy is primarily a neutral descriptor; moral/religious loading comes from context
- By NT times, ethnos has acquired a technical religious sense ("those without the Law/circumcision") that operates semi-automatically in Paul
- The NT's most significant move: ethnos-peoples are now *included* in the covenant people rather than perpetually outside it (Eph 3:6 — "fellowheirs")
- Revelation redeems the eschatological nations entirely — from angry enemies (Rev 11:18) to healed, worshipping peoples (Rev 21:24–26; 22:2)
