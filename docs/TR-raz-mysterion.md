The râz→mystērion bridge is one of the cleanest 1:1 loan-equivalences in biblical translation: every occurrence of râz in Daniel 2 and 4:9 is rendered μυστήριον in the Theodotionic Greek Daniel (the form of Daniel quoted in the NT).

μυστήριον in the Greek OT is overwhelmingly a Daniel-word. Outside Daniel (Theodotion), it appears in Wisdom, Sirach, 2 Maccabees, and Tobit — nearly always in apocalyptic or wisdom contexts — but it never translates a Hebrew word in the protocanonical books outside of Daniel. The lexical footprint of NT "mystery" is therefore almost entirely Danielic.

**Semantic continuity:** The conceptual frame — *a matter hidden in God's counsel, concerning the future of kingdoms, disclosed by divine initiative to a chosen servant* — is preserved intact in the Greek. No semantic shift occurs at the LXX step; μυστήριον fully carries the Danielic râz.

No NT writer cites Daniel with a formula-citation for the word "mystery," but the *structure* of Pauline and Johannine mystery-language — hidden-in-God's-counsel, concerning kingdoms/ages, disclosed by revelation, made known through an authorized interpreter — is Danielic.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The word denotes *content*, not merely a condition of hiddenness.
- Disclosure is a divine prerogative; human wisdom cannot reach the mystery on its own (Dan 2:27; 1 Cor 2:7–10).
- The mystery is regularly paired with *revelation* verbs (gᵉlâh → apokalyptō; yᵉdaʻ → gnōrizō).
- The mystery has an eschatological horizon: "what shall be in the latter days" (Dan 2:28) / "before the world began… now made manifest" (1 Cor 2:7; Col 1:26).

**What shifts:**
- **Scope broadens from dream to gospel.** In Daniel the râz is the specific content of Nebuchadnezzar's dream about world empires. In the NT, the mystērion becomes the whole economy of salvation in Christ (Eph 3:3–6), the inclusion of Gentiles, the resurrection transformation (1 Cor 15:51), and the final consummation (Rev 10:7).
- **Recipient expands.** In Daniel the recipient is a single prophet (Daniel) acting for the king. In the NT the recipients are the apostles and, through them, the church (Eph 3:5; Col 1:26–27).
- **"Mystery of iniquity"** (2 Thess 2:7) is a distinctive NT development: the term is applied not only to God's saving counsel but also, by contrast, to a concealed eschatological evil — still within the Danielic framework of end-time disclosure.
