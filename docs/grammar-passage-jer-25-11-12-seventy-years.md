# Grammar Analysis: Jeremiah 25:11-12 + 29:10 — The Seventy-Year Babylonian Captivity

## Summary

Jeremiah's seventy-year Babylonian captivity prophecy (Jer 25:11-12; 29:10; cf. 2 Chr 36:21; Dan 9:2) is a **CONTROL CASE** for the day-for-year question. It is a prophetic time-prediction that is **NOT** day-for-year. The grammar, genre, and reception history all converge on literal years.

**Four reinforcing signals:**
1. **Plain `šānāh`** — the word "year" appears four times across these verses as a straight accusative-of-duration with the numeral `šiḇʿîm` ("seventy"). There is **no `yôm laššānāh`** ("a day for a year") declaration anywhere in the context.
2. **No apocalyptic genre markers** — no vision, no symbolic beast, no angelic interpreter, no numbered period riding on a symbolic time-unit.
3. **No seal-command** — contrast Dan 8:26; 12:4, 9 where the text flags its own temporal opacity.
4. **Daniel reads it literally (Dan 9:2)** — while exiled in Babylon Daniel computes `mispar ha-šānîm` ("the number of the years") from Jeremiah's scrolls as the actual years Jerusalem would lie desolate, and prays at the terminal end of that period. His reading is **canonically authoritative** for how the prophecy was meant to function.

The 70 years are fulfilled within one lifetime at the literal-year scale: 605 BC (first deportation) → 539 BC (Cyrus decree) ≈ 66-70 years depending on reckoning; or 586 BC (temple destruction) → 516 BC (temple rebuilt) = 70 years exactly. Either terminus works at literal scale; no year-day expansion is required or grammatically invited.

This entry supplies the hermeneutic baseline: **day-for-year is genre-triggered, not universally applied** to every prophetic time-reference. The very prophet (Ezekiel) who declared the `yôm laššānāh` rule at Ezek 4:6 uses plain `šānāh` literally at Ezek 29:11-13 for Egypt's forty-year desolation. Jeremiah's seventy years operate the same way: ordinary calendrical speech in a non-apocalyptic oracle.

## Clause Structure

### Jeremiah 25:11 — two coordinated weqatal clauses

- **Clause 1** (WQtX, quotation domain): *wə-hāyəṯāh kol-hā-ʾāreṣ haz-zōʾṯ lə-ḥorbāh lə-šammāh*
  - Pred: `hāyəṯāh` | Subj: `kol hā-ʾāreṣ haz-zōʾṯ` | PreC: `lə-ḥorbāh lə-šammāh`
- **Clause 2** (WQtX, quotation domain): *wə-ʿāḇəḏû hag-gôyim hā-ʾēlleh ʾeṯ-meleḵ Bāḇel šiḇʿîm šānāh*
  - Pred: `ʿāḇəḏû` | Subj: `hag-gôyim hā-ʾēlleh` | Objc: `ʾeṯ-meleḵ Bāḇel` | **Time: `šiḇʿîm šānāh`**

The BHSA parser classifies `šiḇʿîm šānāh` explicitly as a **Time adjunct** of the predicate `ʿāḇəḏû` ("they shall serve"). Grammatically this is an accusative of temporal duration — the ordinary way Hebrew says "for X years." There is no trace of sign-act framing, no interpretive formula, no symbolic substitution. It is the same construction used of Jacob serving Laban "seven years" (Gen 29:20, 30), Israel's forty-year wilderness (Num 14:33-34 non-sign-act uses), etc.

### Jeremiah 25:12 — apodosis with infinitive-construct temporal clause

- **Clause 1:** *wə-hāyāh* — formulaic "and it shall come to pass"
- **Clause 2** (InfC): *ḵi-məlōʾṯ šiḇʿîm šānāh* — "when seventy years are fulfilled"
  - Pred: `məlōʾṯ` (Qal infinitive construct of מלא) | Subj: `šiḇʿîm šānāh`
- **Clause 3** (ZYq0): *ʾep̄qōḏ ʿal-meleḵ Bāḇel…* — "I will punish the king of Babylon…"

The `kə + infinitive construct` construction (`ḵi-məlōʾṯ`) is a standard Hebrew temporal clause: "at the fulfilling of…" / "when X is completed." The subject of the infinitive is `šiḇʿîm šānāh` — the seventy years themselves are what becomes "full" (מלא). This is the same verb used in 2 Chr 36:21 and Dan 9:2 to describe the seventy years being **completed** as literal calendar time.

### Jeremiah 29:10 — `lə-p̄î + infinitive construct`

- **Clause 1** (xQtX): *kî ḵōh ʾāmar YHWH* — messenger-formula
- **Clause 2** (xYq0): *kî lə-p̄î… ʾep̄qōḏ ʾeṯḵem* — "for according to… I will visit you"
  - Time: `lə-p̄î` ("according to the mouth of" / "in accordance with the measure of")
- **Clause 3** (InfC): *məlōʾṯ lə-Bāḇel šiḇʿîm šānāh*
  - Pred: `məlōʾṯ` | Cmpl: `lə-Bāḇel` | Subj: `šiḇʿîm šānāh`
- **Clause 4** (WQt0): *wa-hăqîmōṯî ʿălêḵem ʾeṯ dəḇārî haṭ-ṭôḇ* — "and I will establish my good word upon you"
- **Clause 5** (InfC): *lə-hāšîḇ ʾeṯḵem ʾel ham-māqôm haz-zeh* — "to cause you to return to this place"

The idiom `lə-p̄î məlōʾṯ` ("according to the mouth of the fulfillment of") is an unusual but unambiguous construction. `lə-p̄î` is a prepositional idiom meaning "according to the measure of / in proportion to." Combined with `məlōʾṯ` ("the fulfilling/completing of"), the sense is: **when the full measure of seventy years for Babylon has been completed**. The emphasis is on quantitative completion — a literal exhausting of the seventy-year period — not on symbolic expansion.

## Key Grammatical Features

### 1. **`šānāh` (Noun.fs.Abs) — plain "year" with numeral**

- **Form:** Feminine singular absolute noun, Strong's H8141 *šānāh*.
- **Construction:** The Hebrew idiom with round-decade numerals places the counted noun in the singular absolute (`šiḇʿîm šānāh` literally "seventy year"), standing as an accusative of temporal duration.
- **Significance:** This is the ordinary, calendrical word for "year" in its plainest syntactic frame. There is no modifier, no appositional decoder, no `yôm` ("day") paired in substitution, no genitive relation linking it to a symbolic referent.
- **Contrast with Ezek 4:6:** Ezekiel's sign-act declaration uses the **explicit formula** `yôm laššānāh yôm laššānāh` ("a day for a year, a day for a year") — a double-stamped interpretive rule that redefines the time-unit. **Jeremiah has nothing like this.** The absence is the datum.
- **Word study:** [H8141 shaneh](../../word-studies/H8141-shaneh.md)
- **Grammar reference:** [day-year-formula](../hebrew/day-year-formula.md) — documents the explicit formula that would be required to trigger day-for-year and shows it is absent here.

### 2. **`məlōʾṯ` / `mallōʾṯ` (infinitive construct of מלא) — "fulfilling / completing"**

- **Form:** Qal infinitive construct (`məlōʾṯ`, Jer 25:12; 29:10) and Piel infinitive construct (`mallōʾṯ`, 2 Chr 36:21; Dan 9:2) of the root **מלא** ("be full, fill, complete").
- **Function:** In all four passages this verb governs `šiḇʿîm šānāh` — the seventy years are the thing being *completed*. The semantic field is **quantitative exhaustion of a measured period**, not symbolic expansion.
  - Jer 25:12: `ḵi-məlōʾṯ šiḇʿîm šānāh` — "when seventy years are fulfilled"
  - Jer 29:10: `lə-p̄î məlōʾṯ lə-Bāḇel šiḇʿîm šānāh` — "according to the fulfilling of seventy years for Babylon"
  - 2 Chr 36:21: `lə-mallōʾṯ šiḇʿîm šānāh` (Piel) — "to fulfill seventy years"
  - Dan 9:2: `lə-mallōʾṯ lə-ḥārəḇôṯ Yərûšālāim šiḇʿîm šānāh` (Piel) — "to fulfill seventy years for the desolations of Jerusalem"
- **Significance:** The verb **demands a literal, countable terminus**. You cannot "fill up" a symbolic unit — you fill up actual calendar time. The Piel intensifies the completion sense (2 Chr, Dan). This verbal framing is diagnostic: where Daniel uses מלא-Piel with `šānāh`, he reads the number as arithmetic; where Daniel's own apocalyptic visions give time-numbers (Dan 8:14; 12:7), he is told the vision is **sealed** (Dan 8:26; 12:9) and receives no similar computational cue.
- **Word study:** [TR-male-pleroo](../../word-studies/TR-male-pleroo.md)

### 3. **`ʿāḇəḏû` (Verb.Qal.Perf.3p) — concrete geopolitical servitude**

- **Form:** Qal perfect 3rd-common-plural (here functioning as a weqatal future after the opening weqatal chain) from root **עבד** ("serve, work, labor").
- **Significance:** The verb denotes real political-military vassalage — the same root used of slaves, corvée labor, and tribute-paying nations. Its subject is `hag-gôyim hā-ʾēlleh` ("these nations") and its indirect object is `meleḵ Bāḇel` ("the king of Babylon"). This is **not visionary or symbolic language**; it is the vocabulary of imperial subjugation.
- **Significance for the day-year question:** The genre is concrete geopolitical prophecy, not apocalyptic vision. Apocalyptic time-units ride on symbolic carriers (a little horn, a ram's horns, a visionary sequence); here the "time" sits on a plain historical verb describing nations paying tribute. There is nothing to symbolically decode.

### 4. **`rāṣəṯāh hā-ʾāreṣ ʾeṯ-šabbəṯôṯêhā` (2 Chr 36:21) — the sabbatical-rest rationale**

- **Form:** `rāṣəṯāh` (Qal perfect 3fs from רצה, "pay off / satisfy / enjoy"); `šabbəṯôṯêhā` (plural noun "her sabbaths" with 3fs suffix).
- **Construction:** The land (feminine subject) "pays off" her sabbaths — meaning the accumulated unobserved sabbatical years (Lev 25; 26:34-35, 43) are now being compensated by an enforced rest. `kol-yəmê ha-šammāh šāḇāṯāh lə-mallōʾṯ šiḇʿîm šānāh` — "all the days of the desolation she kept sabbath, to fulfill seventy years."
- **Significance:** The Chronicler **anchors the seventy years in Leviticus 26:34-35** — a land-rest arithmetic. Seventy sabbatical years (one every seven years) corresponds to 490 years of neglected sabbath-years. This is a **calendrical, not symbolic, calculation.** It is impossible to sustain a day-for-year reading here: you cannot multiply sabbath-year debt by 360 without producing absurdities (25,200 sabbatical years ≈ 176,400 years of pre-exilic occupation).
- **Significance for the day-year question:** The Chronicler's inspired commentary fixes the denomination at literal years. Any hermeneutic that would convert Jeremiah's seventy into prophetic-day years (70×360 = 25,200 years) breaks the sabbath-debt rationale that the canon itself supplies.

### 5. **`mispar ha-šānîm` (Dan 9:2) — Daniel's arithmetic reception**

- **Form:** Construct chain: `mispar` (ms.Cst "number-of") + `ha-šānîm` (fp.Abs "the years"). From the word study H4457 *mispar* — a quantitative numerical count.
- **Significance:** In Dan 9:2 Daniel says he "understood by the books [ba-səp̄ārîm] the *number of the years* whereof the word of the LORD came to Jeremiah." `mispar` is the Hebrew word for an enumerated arithmetic count — inventories, census totals, troop musters. Daniel is **computing** the seventy years as calendar time and prays at its terminus (Dan 9:3-19).
- **Significance for the day-year question:** This is the **genre-distinction baseline**. The same prophet who receives the apocalyptic 2300 "evenings-mornings" (Dan 8:14) — where he cannot understand the vision and is told it is sealed for the distant future (Dan 8:26-27) — here **reads Jeremiah's seventy years without any symbolic operation**, arithmetically, as literal years. Daniel himself treats the two kinds of prophetic time-reference differently. The canonical reader's distinction is the hermeneutic.
- **Passage reference:** [dan-9-2-jeremiah-reading](dan-9-2-jeremiah-reading.md)

## Absence of Day-for-Year Triggers

The day-for-year hermeneutic — where it is grammatically licensed — comes with identifiable textual signatures. **None of them appear in Jer 25:11-12 / 29:10:**

| Signature | Present in Num 14:34 / Ezek 4:6 | Present in Dan 8:14 / 9:24-27 | Present in Jer 25:11-12 / 29:10 |
|-----------|--------------------------------|------------------------------|--------------------------------|
| Explicit `yôm laššānāh` declaration | **Yes** (both) | No (but sign-act analog via covenantal frame) | **No** |
| Sign-act performance (prophet as symbol) | **Yes** (Ezek 4) | No | **No** |
| Apocalyptic vision context | No (sign-act) | **Yes** | **No** |
| Seal-command or concealment formula | No | **Yes** (Dan 8:26; 12:9) | **No** |
| Symbolic time-carrier (horn, beast, etc.) | No | **Yes** | **No** |
| Terminus beyond one lifetime | No (40 yr) | **Yes** (centuries–millennia) | **No** (one lifetime) |
| Canonical reader decodes symbolically | n/a | **Yes** (Dan 12:9 preserves the seal; cf. later apocalyptic tradition) | **No** — Daniel reads it literally (Dan 9:2) |

Jeremiah's seventy years hit **zero** of the hermeneutic triggers. They are ordinary calendrical time.

## Fulfillment at Literal-Year Scale

Two conventional reckonings both land inside a seventy-year window:

| Terminus a quo | Terminus ad quem | Years |
|----------------|-------------------|-------|
| 605 BC — Nebuchadnezzar's first deportation (Dan 1:1-2) | 539 BC — Cyrus captures Babylon / decree (2 Chr 36:22-23; Ezra 1:1) | ~66-67 |
| 586 BC — destruction of the temple | 516 BC — second temple dedicated (Ezra 6:15) | ~70 |
| 605 BC — first deportation | 536 BC — return under Zerubbabel begins | ~69-70 |

Biblical authors themselves cite the prophecy as fulfilled:
- **2 Chr 36:21-22** — explicit fulfillment notice at Cyrus's decree
- **Ezra 1:1** — repeats the citation: "that the word of the LORD by the mouth of Jeremiah might be fulfilled"
- **Dan 9:2** — Daniel praying at the terminal end of the seventy years
- **Zech 1:12; 7:5** — the 70-year period referenced as ongoing/just-concluded in real historical time

Every one of these fulfillment notices treats `šiḇʿîm šānāh` as literal calendar years. No canonical witness converts the number to prophetic-day years.

## Hermeneutic Takeaway

**Day-for-year is genre-triggered, not universally applied.** Three cumulative lines of evidence here:

1. **Same-author control.** Ezekiel declares the rule (Ezek 4:6) and then writes a plain-year forty-year prophecy (Ezek 29:11-13) using the same word. The author distinguishes.
2. **Canonical-reader control.** Daniel — the premier apocalyptic seer in the Tanakh — receives sealed apocalyptic numbers (Dan 8:14; 12:7) and prays literally over a non-apocalyptic seventy (Dan 9:2). The reader distinguishes.
3. **Lexical control.** The verb מלא ("fulfill, complete") with `šānāh` functions arithmetically in Jer 25:12; 29:10; 2 Chr 36:21; Dan 9:2 — the years are *counted*, not decoded. The grammar itself distinguishes.

Jeremiah's seventy-year prophecy is therefore a **clean baseline case**: a prophetic time-prediction that is demonstrably NOT day-for-year. It establishes that the Bible does not uniformly symbolize prophetic time-references — and thereby validates the genre-triggered approach by which some prophetic time-numbers (e.g., Dan 8:14; 9:24-27; 12:7) *are* read at day-for-year scale while others (Jer 25:11-12; 29:10; Ezek 29:11-13) are read at literal scale.

The hermeneutic is supplied by the text, not imposed on it.
