This means the Greek reader of the LXX encountered árchōn for Daniel's angelic princes — a usage that directly primed the NT's supernatural application.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same:**
- Árchōn, like sar, covers political rulers from local officials to cosmic governors
- Daniel's sar for angelic/national princes (10:13, 12:1) flows directly into NT usage of árchōn for supernatural powers
- The messianic "Prince of Peace" (sar-shalom, Isa 9:6) resurfaces in Rev 1:5's "árchōn of the kings of the earth"

**What shifts:**
- In the OT, sar is largely neutral — a functional title. The NT concentrates árchōn's supernatural uses in a *negative* direction (prince of this world, princes of this age who crucified the Lord — 1 Cor 2:8). The cosmic-ruler sense from Daniel is retained but theologically inverted: these archons are now the powers Christ overcomes.
- The LXX's fragmentation of sar into specialized military terms (hekatontarches, chiliarchos) means the NT's árchōn inherits primarily the civic and cosmic register, not the military one.
