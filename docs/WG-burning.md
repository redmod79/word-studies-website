# WG: Burning (Hebrew Fire Vocabulary)

## Overview
Hebrew uses distinct verbs for different types of burning. The key distinction is between total consumption fire (saraph), general burning/consuming (baar), and altar-smoke offering (qatar). These are complemented by the noun esh (fire) and the derived noun serephah (cremation/burning).

## Roles in the Concept

- **Element:** esh (אֵשׁ) — fire as substance/phenomenon
- **Total consumption:** saraph (שָׂרַף) — burning to ashes; ritual disposal of sin-offering remains outside camp; military destruction of cities
- **General burning:** baar (בָּעַר) — fire kindled and consuming; altar fire kept burning; also figurative for divine anger
- **Sacrificial smoke:** qatar (קָטַר) — the ascending smoke of altar offerings; incense; a worship act, not destruction
- **Resulting state:** serephah (שְׂרֵפָה) — the burning event itself, cremation
- **Injury:** kavah (כָּוָה) — being burned/scorched (rare; Prov 6:28, Isa 43:2)

## Critical Distinction: saraph vs. qatar

In Levitical sacrificial contexts, these two verbs describe fundamentally different acts:
- **qatar** — burning on the altar, sending up smoke/aroma to God (Lev 1:9, 13, 15, 17; 2:2; 3:5)
- **saraph** — burning the remains outside the camp, disposing of what cannot go on the altar (Lev 4:12, 21; 16:27; cf. Exo 29:14)

The distinction maps to different Greek verbs in the LXX: qatar → θυμιάω (offer incense), saraph → κατακαίω (burn down).
