**Semantic shift in translation:** The LXX's consistent choice of παραβολή collapses several distinct Hebrew wisdom/speech categories — proverb, allegory, riddle, taunt, oracle — into a single Greek term. This broadens παραβολή well beyond classical Greek usage (where it meant only "comparison" / "juxtaposition") and gives it the range of mashal. Greek παροιμία (paroimia) appears alongside as a secondary rendering, especially for circulating popular sayings.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The core sense of figurative, oblique, interpretation-requiring speech
- The speech-act idiom: "take up" (nasa) / "spake in" (laleo en) a parable — elevated, authoritative utterance
- The hidden/revealed polarity: a mashal/parabole both conceals (from outsiders) and reveals (to the initiated) — explicit in both Ps 78:2 / Ezek 17:2 and Matt 13:11–13
- Use for popular sayings (1 Sam 10:12 ↔ Luke 4:23)

**What shifts:**
- "Taunt-song against a foreign nation" sense (Isa 14, Hab 2, Mic 2) largely drops out of NT parabole; prophetic-oracle function recedes
- "Byword / object of reproach" sense (Deut 28:37; 1 Kgs 9:7) not carried into NT parabole
- NT parabole develops a narrative-form emphasis (story parables of Jesus) that mashal had only in embryonic form (Ezek 17, 24; Judg 9:8–15)
- Greek paroimia (G3942) carries the "popular proverb" nuance more narrowly, while parabole expands to absorb the rest
