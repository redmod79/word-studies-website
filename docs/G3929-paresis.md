## Lexicon Definition

From G3935 παρίημι (pariēmi); praetermission, i.e. toleration — remission. Literally "a passing by" or "a letting go past." The word denotes a temporary overlooking or passing over of something, not its permanent removal or cancellation. The KJV translation "remission" is potentially misleading, as it conflates paresis with the distinct word ἄφεσις (aphesis, G859), which denotes full release and permanent forgiveness.

## Semantic Categories

### 1. Divine Forbearance Toward Past Sins
The sole occurrence places paresis within an argument about God's justice. The "passing over" (paresis) of formerly committed sins (ἁμαρτημάτων προγεγονότων, hamartēmatōn progegonotōn) was a temporary divine response during the period of God's forbearance (ἀνοχή, anochē), prior to the definitive act of atonement in Christ:
- **Rom 3:25** — "to declare his righteousness for the remission [paresis] of sins that are past, through the forbearance [anochē] of God"

The logical structure of Paul's argument (Rom 3:25–26) depends on the distinction between paresis (temporary passing over) and permanent resolution: God passed over sins in the past era, and now demonstrates his righteousness through the hilastērion (propitiation) of Christ's blood.

### Morphological Contrast: paresis vs. aphesis
Both words end in -εσις (-esis), the action-noun suffix, and derive from compounds of ἵημι (hiēmi, "to send"). The distinction lies in the prefix:
- **ἀπό** (apo, "away from") + ἵημι → ἄφεσις: a sending *away* — complete removal
- **παρά** (para, "beside, past") + ἵημι → πάρεσις: a sending *past* — letting something go by without dealing with it
