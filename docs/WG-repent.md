# WG: Repentance

## Overview

The repentance concept in Scripture involves a cluster of words that together express the full range of turning from sin toward God. The Greek metanoeō/metanoia pair is the primary NT vocabulary; its Hebrew background comes from two distinct streams — nâcham (emotional sorrow/relenting) and shûwb (volitional turning/returning). The confused word metamellomai marks an important contrast: regret without transformation.

### Hebrew Bridge — shûwb (H7725)
H7725 shûwb is the primary OT conversion verb (952v). It bridges to NT through two Greek words:
- → G1994 ἐπιστρέφω (PMI 24.70, 322x) — the normal LXX rendering
- → G3340 μετανοέω (PMI 8.28, 9x) — secondary overlap in repentance contexts

This means NT "repent" (metanoeō) draws on both the mind-change of nâcham and the directional turn of shûwb, while "turn" (epistréphō) more directly carries shûwb's relational/directional force.

## Roles in the Concept

- **Cognitive reorientation:** metanoeō (G3340) — the deliberate change of mind and will
- **State/disposition:** metanoia (G3341) — the ongoing posture of repentance
- **Physical/relational movement:** epistréphō (G1994) — turning from one direction toward another
- **Emotional sorrow (Hebrew):** nâcham (H5162) — the grief and sighing that precedes change
- **Directional turning (Hebrew):** shûwb (H7725) — the volitional turn back toward God
- **Contrast (regret without change):** metamellomai (G3338) — remorse that does not produce transformation (cf. Judas, Matt 27:3)
