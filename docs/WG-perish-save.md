# WG: Perish / Save (Eschatological Antithesis)

## Overview

The NT frames final human destiny as a binary: one either *perishes* or *is saved*. Louw-Nida groups these under domain 21.006 ("Save in a Religious Sense") and 20.003 ("Destroy"). The pairing is so consistent that the verbs routinely share the same verse, usually as direct contrast (Mat 16:25; Jhn 3:16; 1 Cor 1:18; 2 Cor 2:15). Around this core antithesis cluster the abstract result-nouns (perdition / salvation), the agent-noun (Saviour), the positive end-state (life eternal), and its qualifier (eternal).

## Roles in the Concept

- **Negative verb (perish):** apóllymi (G622) — to perish, be destroyed, be lost. Middle voice is the typical eschatological form.
- **Negative result-noun (perdition):** apṓleia (G684) — the destination/state of having perished.
- **Positive verb (save):** sōzō (G4982) — to rescue, deliver, save. The direct antonym.
- **Positive result-noun (salvation):** sōtēría (G4991) — the destination/state of having been saved.
- **Agent (Saviour):** sōtḗr (G4990) — the one who saves; title for God and Christ.
- **Content of the positive state (life):** zōē (G2222) — the substantive good of being saved.
- **Temporal qualifier (eternal):** aiōnios (G166) — intensifies both poles, pushing the antithesis beyond this age.

## Practical Disambiguation

- **apóllymi vs apṓleia** — verb (the action) vs. noun (the resulting state). Use apóllymi when the focus is on perishing as an event; apṓleia when the focus is on perdition as a destination ("vessels of wrath… fitted to destruction," Rom 9:22).
- **sōzō vs sōtēría vs sōtḗr** — verb / result-noun / agent-noun pattern parallels apóllymi / apṓleia / (no agent-noun for the perish pole — the closest is Ἀπολλύων, Apollyon, G623, in Rev 9:11).
- **zōē + aiōnios** — the fixed phrase "eternal life" is the single most common way the positive pole is stated in John (17 of the 45 co-occurrences).
