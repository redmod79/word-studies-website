## OT Usage (Hebrew)

| Strong's | Word | Occurrences | Primary Meaning |
|----------|------|-------------|-----------------|
| H639 | אַף (ʼaph) | 269 | nose, nostril; anger, wrath |

The Hebrew *ʼaph* spans a concrete-to-abstract spectrum: literally "nose/nostril" (Gen 2:7), metonymically "face" (Gen 19:1), and predominantly "anger/wrath" (Exo 4:14). The anger sense derives from the physical image of nostrils flaring in passion. It is the broadest and most frequent Hebrew anger term, used of both divine and human subjects.

**Key OT passages:**
- **Exo 34:6** — "The LORD God, merciful and gracious, longsuffering" (*ʼerek ʼappayim*, "long of nostrils")
- **Num 25:3-4** — "the anger (ʼaph) of the LORD was kindled... that the fierce anger (ʼaph) of the LORD may be turned away"
- **Psa 78:38** — "he, being full of compassion, forgave their iniquity... many a time turned he his anger (ʼaph) away"

**Semantic convergence in translation:** The LXX collapses seven distinct Hebrew anger words into two Greek families: orgē for settled/judicial wrath, thymos for passionate/burning fury. The Hebrew word *ʼaph* maps to both — orgē slightly more often (135x vs. 126x). This means NT readers encountering orgē are receiving a signal that was distributed across the full Hebrew wrath vocabulary.

In the NT, orgē retains the judicial character established in the LXX: it is God's settled verdict against sin, not a passionate outburst. It appears in Paul's theology of justification (Rom 1:18, 2:5, 5:9), in Revelation's eschatological judgments (Rev 6:16-17, 11:18, 14:10, 19:15), and once of Jesus's human anger (Mar 3:5).

**Key NT passages:**

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- Divine wrath as a response to covenant-breaking and sin — from Num 25:3 ("anger kindled against Israel" for Baal-worship) through Rom 1:18 ("wrath revealed against all ungodliness")
- The pattern of wrath averted through intercession or atonement — Num 25:4 (turning away God's *ʼaph*) finds its NT counterpart in 1 Thess 1:10 (Jesus "delivered us from the wrath to come")
- The "slow to anger" divine attribute — Exo 34:6 *ʼerek ʼappayim* becomes Rom 2:4 "the riches of his goodness and forbearance and longsuffering"

**What shifts:**
- The literal anatomical sense (nose/nostril) is entirely absent from NT *orgē*, which is exclusively abstract
- The face/prostration sense mapped through a different Greek word (proskyneō) rather than orgē, splitting *ʼaph*'s semantic range
- Seven Hebrew anger words collapsed into mainly orgē and thymos, losing the nuanced distinctions between burning (chemah), fierce heat (charon), outburst (qetseph), overflow (ebrah), and indignation (zaʻam)
- NT orgē tends toward eschatological and final judgment (Rev 6:16-17, 14:10) more than the repeated cyclical anger-relenting pattern common in the OT historical books
