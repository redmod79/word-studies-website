**Semantic continuity in translation:** The LXX translators chose ᾅδης with exceptional consistency (PMI 33.87). Hades in Greek mythology denoted the unseen realm of the dead — semantically parallel to Hebrew sheol as the underworld destination for all who die. The LXX renders sheol's directional descent idioms (yārad + sheol) with καταβαίνω / κατάγω (to go/lead down), preserving the spatial metaphor.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- Hades/sheol is the realm of the dead — not a place of torment specifically but the domain where the dead exist
- The pairing with death (māvet / θάνατος) persists: Hebrew "sheol and death," LXX "hades and thanatos," NT "death and hades" (Rev 6:8; 20:13–14)
- Directional language: people go *down* to hades/sheol (Mat 11:23; Luke 10:15 echo OT descent idiom)
- God has authority over it: Ps 16:10 → Acts 2:27; Rev 1:18 ("keys of hell and of death")

**What shifts:**
- OT sheol receives all the dead (righteous and wicked); NT háidēs appears to distinguish more sharply as a realm contrasted with resurrection (Rev 20:13–14: hades gives up its dead for judgment)
- NT introduces the concept of Christ descending into hades and holding its keys (Rev 1:18) — active authority over the realm, not just passive entry
- The final fate of háidēs itself appears in Rev 20:14: "death and hell were cast into the lake of fire" — sheol/hades ends at the last judgment, replaced by the lake of fire
