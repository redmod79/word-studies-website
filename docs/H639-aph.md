## Lexicon Definition

From H599; properly, the nose or nostril; hence, the face, and occasionally a person; also (from the rapid breathing in passion) ire: — anger(-gry), before, countenance, face, forebearing, forehead, (long-)suffering, nose, nostril, snout, worthy, wrath.

## Semantic Categories

### 1. Divine Wrath Kindled Against Sin
The dominant usage. The formula "the anger of the LORD was kindled" (*wayyichar ʼaph YHWH*) recurs throughout the historical and prophetic books.
- **Exo 4:14** — "And the anger of the LORD was kindled against Moses"
- **Num 25:3** — "And Israel joined himself unto Baalpeor: and the anger of the LORD was kindled against Israel"
- **Jdg 2:14** — "And the anger of the LORD was hot against Israel, and he delivered them into the hands of spoilers"
- **2Sa 6:7** — "And the anger of the LORD was kindled against Uzzah"

### 2. Human Anger
Used for the anger of individuals — patriarchs, kings, military leaders. The same verb *charah* ("was kindled") applies.
- **Gen 30:2** — "And Jacob's anger was kindled against Rachel"
- **Gen 49:7** — "Cursed be their anger, for it was fierce; and their wrath, for it was cruel" (Simeon and Levi)
- **1Sa 20:30** — "Then Saul's anger was kindled against Jonathan"
- **1Sa 20:34** — "Jonathan arose from the table in fierce anger"

### 3. Physical Nose / Nostril (Literal Anatomical Sense)
The original concrete meaning, from which all figurative senses derive.
- **Gen 2:7** — "breathed into his nostrils (ʼaph) the breath of life"
- **Gen 7:22** — "All in whose nostrils was the breath of life"
- **Job 27:3** — "the spirit of God is in my nostrils"
- **Isa 2:22** — "Cease ye from man, whose breath is in his nostrils"

### 4. Face / Countenance (Metonymic Extension)
The nose as the prominent feature of the face, extended to mean the whole face — especially in prostration.
- **Gen 19:1** — "Lot... bowed himself with his face (ʼaph) toward the ground"
- **Gen 48:12** — "he bowed himself with his face to the earth"
- **1Sa 25:23** — "Abigail... fell before David on her face"
- **2Sa 14:33** — "Absalom... bowed himself on his face to the ground before the king"

### 5. The Fierce Anger of God in Prophetic Judgment
Especially in the doublet *charon ʼaph* ("fierceness of anger") and in stacked wrath vocabulary.
- **Num 25:4** — "that the fierce anger of the LORD may be turned away from Israel"
- **Isa 13:9** — "the day of the LORD cometh, cruel both with wrath and fierce anger"
- **Jer 4:8** — "the fierce anger of the LORD is not turned back from us"
- **Lam 4:11** — "he hath poured out his fierce anger, and hath kindled a fire in Zion"

### 6. Slowness to Anger (ʼerek ʼappayim)
The dual form *ʼappayim* ("two nostrils") in the idiom "slow to anger" (*ʼerek ʼappayim*, lit. "long of nostrils"), part of the divine self-revelation formula.
- **Exo 34:6** — "The LORD God, merciful and gracious, longsuffering"
- **Num 14:18** — "The LORD is longsuffering, and of great mercy"
- **Psa 103:8** — "The LORD is merciful and gracious, slow to anger, and plenteous in mercy"
- **Nah 1:3** — "The LORD is slow to anger, and great in power"

### 7. Theophanic Imagery (Breath/Blast of God's Nostrils)
The literal nostril sense combined with divine warrior imagery — God's breath as destructive force.
- **Exo 15:8** — "with the blast of thy nostrils the waters were gathered together"
- **2Sa 22:9** — "There went up a smoke out of his nostrils"
- **2Sa 22:16** — "at the blast of the breath of thy nostrils"
- **Psa 18:15** — "at the blast of the breath of thy nostrils"

The LXX splits *ʼaph* across two Greek families. When *ʼaph* denotes settled, judicial anger, it becomes ὀργή; when it denotes passionate burning anger (especially alongside *chemah*), it becomes θυμός. The prostration/worship scenes where *ʼaph* means "face" are rendered with προσκυνέω.
