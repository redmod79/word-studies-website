**Semantic continuity:** The LXX makes no attempt to translate the name's theophoric meaning — it transliterates. Greek-speaking Jews read Ἰησοῦς for Joshua, Jeshua, and later for the Messiah without orthographic distinction. The etymology ("YHWH saves") is preserved only through explicit exposition, as in Mat 1:21.

**Semantic narrowing in NT:** Of the 935 NT uses of Ἰησοῦς, only two unambiguously refer to OT Joshua (Act 7:45; Heb 4:8). One refers to a companion of Paul called Justus (Col 4:11). The remaining ~932 all refer to Jesus of Nazareth.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- Phonetic form: Yeshua → Iēsous (transliteration preserved across LXX and NT)
- Theophoric meaning: "YHWH saves" remains the semantic core — even when speakers/hearers no longer parse the Hebrew
- Association with deliverance: Joshua delivers into land; High Priest Jeshua leads post-exilic restoration; Jesus delivers from sin

**What shifts:**
- Referent narrows from multiple bearers (Joshua, Jeshua, others) to one dominant referent (the Messiah) — 99% of NT uses
- Theological weight intensifies: the name becomes "above every name" (Phl 2:9), an object of confession (Rom 10:9), baptism (Act 2:38), and prayer (Jhn 14:13)
- From proper noun denoting a savior-figure (OT) to proper noun denoting the Savior embodied (NT)
- OT theophoric compound name (YHWH + yasha) finds its christological rationale made explicit: the one who bears the name *is* the one whom the name names — YHWH saves in person
