## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- Triple semantic range (atmosphere / celestial realm / divine dwelling) is preserved intact.
- Merism "heaven and earth" survives as the standard totality idiom (γῆ καὶ οὐρανός).
- "Host of heaven" (*tsᵉbāʾ hashshâmayim* → στρατιὰ τοῦ οὐρανοῦ, Acts 7:42; Luke 2:13 uses στρατιὰς οὐρανίου).
- "Stars of heaven" / "fowl of the heaven" / "God of heaven" idioms all carry over.

**What shifts:**
- **Dual → singular/plural fluidity.** Hebrew *shâmayim* is grammatically dual-only. Greek *ouranos* allows singular and plural, and the NT freely uses both. Matthew uses the plural οὐρανοί systematically (esp. "kingdom of heaven"), likely reflecting Semitic sensibility. Other NT writers prefer singular.
- **"Heaven" as divine circumlocution.** In Second Temple Judaism (already visible in Dan 4:26 "heaven does rule" and 1 Macc), "heaven" began to stand in for God. Matthew's βασιλεία τῶν οὐρανῶν extends this; Luke writes "I have sinned against heaven" (15:18, 21). Hebrew OT does not use *shâmayim* this way.
- **"Third heaven" / heavenly places.** NT develops a stratified cosmology (2 Cor 12:2; Eph 1:3, 20; 2:6; 3:10; 6:12 use ἐπουράνιος / ἐν τοῖς ἐπουρανίοις). The Hebrew "heaven of heavens" (Deut 10:14; 1 Kgs 8:27) is the seed, but the NT multiplies levels.
- **Eschatological intensification.** "New heavens and new earth" is a prophetic trajectory (Isa 65:17) but becomes a dominant NT horizon (2 Pet 3; Rev 21).
- **Aramaic bridge.** Dan 2–7 uses H8065 *shâmayin* (Aramaic), and Dan 4:26 "heaven rules" seeds the later Jewish/NT use of "heaven" for God.
