## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The narrow moral-legal focus is preserved: both ratsach and phoneúō denote unlawful killing of a human being rather than killing in war, judicial execution, or animal slaughter.
- The Sixth Commandment is transmitted verbatim: "Thou shalt not kill / ou phoneúseis" in every NT citation.
- The word-family pairing is clean: Hebrew retsach (noun) / ratsach (verb) corresponds to Greek phónos (noun) / phoneús (agent) / phoneúō (verb).

**What shifts:**
- The OT concentrates this vocabulary in *legal-procedural* contexts (cities of refuge, avenger of blood, judicial witness). The NT concentrates it in *ethical-catechetical* contexts (Decalogue citation, catalogues of vice, "do not murder" as summary of neighbor-love).
- The OT uses the same verb for accidental manslaughter ("the slayer") and premeditated murder ("the murderer") — distinguished only by context and motive. The NT narrows phoneúō almost entirely to the premeditated sense.
- Jesus radicalizes the commandment in Matt 5:21-22 by equating unjustified anger with the forbidden ratsach/phoneúō — an internalization the OT text did not explicitly make.
- The OT refuge-city institution (the largest single context for ratsach) finds no direct NT reflex; the ethical-forensic apparatus around the word drops out and only the prohibition remains.
