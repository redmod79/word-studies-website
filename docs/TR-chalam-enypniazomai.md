**Semantic continuity:** The LXX preserves the Hebrew verb/noun cognate pair almost perfectly — ἐνυπνιάζομαι (verb) matches חָלַם, and ἐνύπνιον (noun) matches חֲלוֹם. Greek even replicates the Hebrew cognate accusative ("dream a dream" → ἐνυπνιάζομαι ἐνύπνιον).

**Semantic shift:** The health/plumpness sense of חָלַם (Job 39:4, Isa 38:16) drops out in the Greek; ἐνυπνιάζομαι in the LXX is exclusively about dreaming. The "be healthy/recover" meaning is filled by other Greek words (ὑγιαίνω, ἀναλαμβάνω, etc.) in those specific verses.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The verb/noun cognate pair (חָלַם/חֲלוֹם → ἐνυπνιάζομαι/ἐνύπνιον) translates with near one-to-one fidelity.
- The two principal value-polarities of dreaming remain intact: **positive/revelatory** (Joseph → Acts 2:17) and **negative/false-prophetic** (Deut 13 → Jude 1:8).
- The cognate-accusative construction "dream a dream" is preserved even into the Greek NT (Acts 2:17).

**What shifts:**
- Frequency collapses: 25 OT verses → only 2 NT. Dreaming is no longer a dominant revelatory mode once the incarnation has occurred; the NT's "visionary" vocabulary shifts toward ὅραμα, ὀπτασία, ἀποκάλυψις.
- The "health/plump" sense of H2492 (Job 39:4; Isa 38:16) disappears entirely from the Greek chain; ἐνυπνιάζομαι is semantically narrower than חָלַם.
- In Matthew's infancy/passion narratives, dream-revelation appears but uses ὄναρ (G3677) rather than ἐνυπνιάζομαι — suggesting that G1797 carries a slightly more technical/prophetic flavor (Joel, Jude) while ὄναρ handles narrative dreams.
