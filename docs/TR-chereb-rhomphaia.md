**Semantic split in translation:** The LXX translators divided chereb between two Greek words. Rhomphaia (a large Thracian broadsword) and machaira (a short sword or dagger) both render the same Hebrew word. The split is not random — rhomphaia tends toward the more dramatic, judgment-laden contexts, while machaira covers ordinary martial usage. This distinction becomes theologically significant in the NT: rhomphaia is reserved exclusively for the apocalyptic sword from Christ's mouth, while machaira covers all other sword references.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The sword as God's instrument of judgment on the nations — from "my sword shall be bathed in heaven" (Isa 34:5) to "out of his mouth goeth a sharp sword, that with it he should smite the nations" (Rev 19:15)
- The association with decisive, inescapable divine action

**What shifts:**
- In the OT, chereb is both a literal weapon and a metaphor for divine judgment. In the NT, rhomphaia is exclusively metaphorical — it proceeds from Christ's mouth, identifying the sword with his spoken word
- The LXX split between rhomphaia and machaira becomes a fixed NT distinction: rhomphaia = the divine judgment-word, machaira = the ordinary blade (including Eph 6:17, "the sword of the Spirit, which is the word of God," which uses machaira rather than rhomphaia)
