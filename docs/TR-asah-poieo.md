## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- Both ʻasah and poieō function as the default, context-determined "do/make" verb.
- The "doer of [the law/the word]" nominal construction carries unchanged: Hebrew *ʻoseh ha-torah* → Greek *poiētēs tou nomou*.
- The moral idioms "do good/evil," "do what is right," "do judgment," and "do a feast/sacrifice" transfer with their idiomatic weight intact.
- Both words describe divine creation (Gen 1:7; John 1:3) and human covenant response (Deut 6:18; Jas 1:22) without semantic loss.

**What shifts:**
- The OT's dense stock of idiomatic expressions ("do kindness," "do a covenant," "do judgment") are sometimes flattened in NT Greek, which prefers more specific verbs (δίδωμι, ποιέω + object, κρίνω).
- In the NT, ποιέω picks up a Christological dimension not native to ʻasah alone — Jesus as both the one who "does the works of the Father" (John 5:36) and the one through whom God "has made" everything (Heb 1:2, using ποιέω for cosmic creation that in OT Hebrew would use בָּרָא).
- The cognate noun ποίημα (G4161) in Eph 2:10 ("we are his **workmanship**") imports ʻasah's productive sense into a distinctly NT theology of new creation.
