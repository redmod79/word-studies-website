# Grammar Analysis: Deuteronomy 14:22–27 — The Tithe Law and the Weqatal Chain

**"Thou shalt truly tithe ... and thou shalt eat ... and thou shalt turn it into money ... and thou shalt bestow that money for whatsoever thy soul lusteth after ... and thou shalt eat there ... and thou shalt rejoice"**

The tithe-feast pericope of Deuteronomy's central-sanctuary code. Its five 2ms imperatival predicates (one Piel inf-abs-intensified imperfect + four weqatals) form the classic Deuteronomic covenant-obligation chain. The chain is triggered in v. 22 by the *figura etymologica* עַשֵּׂ֣ר תְּעַשֵּׂ֔ר and carried through vv. 23–26 by a sequence of וְ+perfect 2ms forms, then closed by the prohibition of v. 27.

The passage is controlling for one ancient-near-eastern-law question: when Moses names יַיִן ("wine") and שֵׁכָר ("strong drink") in v. 26, are those two nouns **direct objects of the verb** ("thou shalt buy wine and strong drink"), or are they **items enumerated inside a permissive בְּכֹל-PP** that functions as the **complement** of the verb נָתַן "give/spend"? This entry answers that question with the BHSA clause-role tags and a clause-by-clause map of the whole pericope.

**LXX (Rahlfs, Deut 14:26) — tool-aligned via LXX mapping in [H3196-yayin](../word-studies/H3196-yayin.md) and [H7941-shekar](../word-studies/H7941-shekar.md):**

> καὶ δώσεις τὸ ἀργύριον **ἐπὶ** παντός, οὗ ἂν ἐπιθυμῇ ἡ ψυχή σου, **ἐπὶ** βουσὶν ἢ **ἐπὶ** προβάτοις, **ἐπὶ** οἴνῳ ἢ **ἐπὶ** σικερᾷ ἢ **ἐπὶ** παντός, οὗ ἂν ἐπιθυμῇ ἡ ψυχή σου…

Five-fold distributive ἐπί + dative, preserving the Hebrew בְּ-PP structure word-for-word. **οἶνος** renders יַיִן; **σίκερα** (direct Greek calque of שֵׁכָר, per word-studies LXX alignment) renders שֵׁכָר. Both Greek nouns stand in the **dative** as complements of ἐπί — not in the accusative as direct objects of δώσεις. The LXX thus grammatically reinforces the Hebrew syntactic tree: wine and strong drink are items **under** a preposition, not **objects of** a verb.

## Key Grammatical Features

### 1. עַשֵּׂ֣ר תְּעַשֵּׂ֔ר (v. 22) — Piel Inf-Abs + Piel Impf. 2ms

- **Form:** Cognate infinitive-absolute immediately preceding the finite imperfect of the same root עשׂר ("to take a tenth") in the same stem (Piel). The ETCBC tag on עַשֵּׂר is `Verb.Piel.InfAbs`; on תְּעַשֵּׂר is `Verb.Piel.Impf.2ms`.
- **Significance:** Paronomastic intensification — GKC §113n, Joüon-Muraoka §123d–l, IBHS §35.3.1. In second-person paraenetic genre this produces an **emphatic, non-negotiable command**: "thou shalt **certainly / surely / fully** tithe." This construction is the **anchoring trigger** of the weqatal chain that runs through vv. 23–26. Same construction at Gen 2:17 מוֹת תָּמוּת; Deut 7:2 הַחֲרֵם תַּחֲרִים (see [deut-7-1-6](deut-7-1-6.md)).
- **Grammar reference:** [infinitive-absolute-intensification](../hebrew/infinitive-absolute-intensification.md)

### 2. Weqatal Chain (vv. 23–26) — וְאָכַלְתָּ, וְנָתַתָּה ×2, וְצַרְתָּ, וְהָלַכְתָּ, וְאָכַלְתָּ, וְשָׂמַחְתָּ

- **Form:** Seven Qal perfects 2ms prefixed with waw, morphologically identical to plain qatal but functioning here as future/directive 2ms commands in a Deuteronomic legal-paraenetic chain.
- **Significance:** Each weqatal inherits imperatival force from the v. 22 inf-abs + imperfect trigger. The classical stress shift (וְנָתַתָּ֣ה ultimate-syllable stress in v. 26) is the morphological diagnostic. The ETCBC BHSA tagger classes each as clause-type `WQt0` (w-qatal, no extra constituents) — the signature Deuteronomic command-chain clause-type.
- **Significance for the passage:** The chain collapses the whole cultic journey into one continuous covenant obligation: **tithe → eat → (or if-far) convert → bind → go → spend → eat → rejoice.** Every node is a **divine command binding the addressee**. No node is a description of permissible commodities.
- **Grammar reference:** [weqatal-legal-imperatival](../hebrew/weqatal-legal-imperatival.md) (explicitly cites Deut 14:26 וְנָתַתָּ֣ה as an example of the stress-shifted weqatal)

### 3. נָתַן + בְּ (v. 25, v. 26) — "give money INTO/FOR X" idiom

- **Form:** Both weqatal instances of נתן in this passage take the 2ms addressee as subject, with an `[Objc]` slot filled by כסף (silver/money) and a `[Cmpl]` slot filled by a ב-PP.
- **v. 25:** `וְנָתַתָּ֖ה בַּכָּ֑סֶף` — "thou shalt convert [the produce] into silver" (ב-pretii / ב-of-exchange); `בַּכָּסֶף` is `[Cmpl]`.
- **v. 26:** `וְנָתַתָּ֣ה הַכֶּ֡סֶף בְּכֹל֩ אֲשֶׁר־תְּאַוֶּה נַפְשְׁךָ בַּבָּקָר וּבַצֹּאן וּבַיַּיִן וּבַשֵּׁכָר וּבְכֹל אֲשֶׁר תִּשְׁאָלְךָ נַפְשֶׁךָ` — "thou shalt give the silver for/in whatsoever thy soul desires, [namely] in the cattle, in the flock, in the wine, in the strong drink, and in whatsoever thy soul asks of thee" (ב-pretii / ב-of-transaction).
- **Significance:** נָתַן + כֶּסֶף + בְּ is the classic Biblical Hebrew **purchase idiom** — "pay money for X." The ב-PP marks **what the money is exchanged for**, not a second object of נתן. Cross-compare Gen 23:9 (Machpelah: בְּכֶסֶף מָלֵא יִתְּנֶנָּה לִי), Deut 2:6 (בַּכֶּסֶף תִּשְׁבְּרוּ מֵאִתָּם אֹכֶל וַאֲכַלְתֶּם). The verbal complement is the ב-phrase; the items inside the ב-phrase are **not** direct objects of the verb. They are items inside a prepositional phrase.
- **Grammar reference:** no dedicated entry yet for "ב-pretii / ב of exchange" — gap flagged below.

### 4. בְּכֹל אֲשֶׁר־תְּאַוֶּה נַפְשֶׁךָ — head-then-list permissive idiom

- **Form:** `בְּ + כֹל (Noun.ms.Cst) + אֲשֶׁר + Piel.Impf.3fs + נַפְשְׁךָ (Subj)`. The construct noun כֹל heads a nomen-rectum-less construct whose rectum is the relative clause itself (GKC §130d; Joüon §129q). The opening בְּכֹל is then **elaborated** by an appositional list of four items, each prefixed by a separate בְּ + ה (בַּבָּקָר / וּבַצֹּאן / וּבַיַּיִן / וּבַשֵּׁכָר), then closed by a mirror-parallel בְּכֹל אֲשֶׁר־תִּשְׁאָלְךָ נַפְשֶׁךָ.
- **Significance:** The four explicit items are **instantiations** of the permissive category "whatsoever thy soul desireth." They are **not** independently commanded. They are grammatically **exemplars under a בְּכֹל umbrella** that opens and closes the list (inclusio). The sense is "for whatever your appetite dictates — some cattle, some sheep, some wine, some shekar, and anything else your appetite asks for." This is not a command to buy these things; it is a **permission to buy them if the taxpayer so chooses.**
- **Grammar reference:** no dedicated entry yet for "בְּכֹל enumerative-permissive" — gap flagged below.

### 5. Casuistic כִּי Protasis (v. 24) vs. Weqatal Apodosis (v. 25)

- **Form:** v. 24 stacks three כִּי clauses (all with impf. verbs) — "when the way is too long, when thou canst not carry it, when the place is too far, when YHWH has blessed thee." v. 25 resumes the weqatal chain with וְנָתַתָּה / וְצַרְתָּ / וְהָלַכְתָּ. There is no אָז or other particle marking the protasis-apodosis hinge.
- **Significance:** This is the Deuteronomic casuistic sub-case: distance and abundance collectively license the produce-to-money conversion without breaking the central-sanctuary rule. The conversion itself is still a command, not a permission; the weqatal apodosis carries full imperatival force.

### 6. Chain-breaker לֹא + Yiqtol (v. 27)

- **Form:** `לֹא תַעַזְבֶנּוּ` — negative particle לֹא + Qal Impf. 2ms with 3ms suffix.
- **Significance:** **Categorical, permanent prohibition** (contrast אַל + jussive situational prohibition; GKC §107o, Joüon §160f — see [deut-7-1-6](deut-7-1-6.md) Feature 3). The switch from weqatal-chain to לֹא+yiqtol also **terminates** the main paragraph; the motive כִּי-clause explains why (the Levite has no landed inheritance).

## **The Central Question — Is Wine/Shekar a Direct Object of an Imperative?**

Given the user's explicit framing: **"Is wine/shekar a direct object of an imperative verb, or is it an item enumerated inside a permissive בְּכֹל clause?"**

### The Tool-Verified Answer

**יַיִן (yayin) and שֵׁכָר (shekar) in Deut 14:26 are NOT direct objects of any imperative or imperatival verb. They are items enumerated inside the permissive בְּכֹל אֲשֶׁר־תְּאַוֶּה נַפְשְׁךָ complement of the verb נתן.**

### The Six Lines of Tool Evidence

**1. Morphology of יין and שכר (hebrew_parser.py --verse "Deu 14:26"):**
- בַּיַּ֨יִן֙ = `ב (Prep) + ה (Art) + יַיִן (Noun.ms.Abs)` — a prepositional phrase, not a bare object marker.
- בַּשֵּׁכָ֔ר = `ב (Prep) + ה (Art) + שֵׁכָר (Noun.ms.Abs)` — a prepositional phrase, not a bare object marker.
- No אֵת direct-object marker anywhere in the clause except upstream on הַכֶּסֶף (implicit — article-marked as definite direct object).

**2. BHSA clause role of the entire wine-containing phrase (hebrew_parser.py --clause "Deu 14:26"):**
- Clause 1 slot assignments:
  - `[Pred]` = וְנָתַתָּ֣ה (the weqatal verb)
  - `[Objc]` = הַכֶּ֡סֶף (the silver / money — **sole direct object**)
  - `[Cmpl]` = בְּכֹל֩ ... בַּבָּקָ֣ר וּבַצֹּ֗אן **וּבַיַּ֨יִן֙ וּבַשֵּׁכָ֔ר** וּבְכֹ֛ל

The ETCBC BHSA tagger groups **the entire בְּכֹל...וּבַשֵּׁכָר sequence as one `[Cmpl]` constituent**, a prepositional complement of נתן. Wine and strong drink fall inside that single constituent alongside cattle and sheep and "whatsoever."

**3. Contrast with v. 23, where a true direct object exists (hebrew_parser.py --clause "Deu 14:23"):**

In v. 23 the main clause is:
```
Type: WQt0
  [Conj] וְ
  [Pred] אָכַלְתָּ֞                              ← weqatal "thou shalt eat"
  [Adju] לִפְנֵי יְהוָה אֱלֹהֶיךָ
  [Loca] בַּמָּקוֹם
  [Objc] מַעְשַׂר דְּגָנְךָ תִּירֹשְׁךָ וְיִצְהָרֶךָ וּבְכֹרֹת בְּקָרְךָ וְצֹאנֶךָ
```

Here the construct-chain `מַעְשַׂר דְּגָנְךָ תִּירֹשְׁךָ וְיִצְהָרֶךָ וּבְכֹרֹת...` is tagged `[Objc]` — **a direct object of אכל**. The chain contains תִּירֹשׁ (*tirosh*, must/new-wine) — the **raw agricultural tithe-product itself**, brought in kind. Tirosh in v. 23 **is** a grammatical direct object of a command verb.

But in v. 26, the grammatical frame flips entirely. The weqatal-commanded verb is נָתַן ("give/spend money"), its direct object is הַכֶּסֶף, and *yayin* + *shekar* sit downstream inside a ב-PP describing **what the money may be exchanged for if the purchaser's appetite so chooses**. They are not direct objects; they are nominal heads under a preposition, inside a complement, inside a permissive list headed by בְּכֹל.

**4. Contrast of vocabulary — tirosh (v. 23) vs. yayin + shekar (v. 26):**

The two verses name **different items altogether**. v. 23's תירש (H8492 *tirosh* — agricultural must, vine-firstfruits, the raw tithe product) is a direct object of eat. v. 26's יין (H3196 *yayin* — fermented grape-wine) and שכר (H7941 *shekar* — fermented grain/date/honey-drink) are inside the permissive list. The text distinguishes lexically as well as syntactically between what the tithe **is** (tirosh, corn, oil, firstlings) and what the redemption-money **may be spent on** (yayin, shekar, cattle, sheep, anything).

**5. LXX grammatical ratification:**

The LXX (Rahlfs, Deut 14:26) preserves the Hebrew syntactic tree verbatim:
- δώσεις ("thou shalt give") — future indicative active 2s, rendering weqatal
- τὸ ἀργύριον — accusative direct object = הַכֶּסֶף
- ἐπὶ παντός... ἐπὶ βουσίν... **ἐπὶ οἴνῳ ἢ ἐπὶ σικερᾷ**... — five ἐπί + dative phrases, each a **prepositional complement**, not an accusative object.

Both οἶνος (for יין) and σίκερα (for שכר) stand in the **dative** — the case required by ἐπί when it marks the domain on which the money is spent. They are **not** in the accusative. Even a translator who knew no Hebrew would read from the Greek that wine and strong drink are items-spent-upon, not items-commanded.

**6. Whole-passage chain-logic:**

Every weqatal command in the pericope names an **action** performed by the addressee: tithe, eat, convert, bind, go, spend, eat, rejoice. **No weqatal takes wine or strong drink as its object.** The only verb in the entire pericope that stands in any syntactic relation to יין / שכר at all is נתן, and its direct object is already filled by הַכֶּסֶף.

### The Consequence

**Grammatically, v. 26 licenses the possibility that a distant taxpayer's redeemed tithe money might be spent on wine or strong drink, along with cattle and sheep, in a joyful covenant meal.** But the verse does not **command** such a purchase, and it does not place wine or shekar in any verbal argument-slot that an imperative or weqatal governs directly.

The whole category of "whatsoever thy soul desireth" is a **permission within a command**: the command is "spend the money at the central sanctuary"; the permission is "for whatever your appetite dictates, be that cattle, sheep, wine, shekar, or anything else." The items inside are enumerated exemplars under a בְּכֹל umbrella, not independently-commanded purchases.

This grammatical finding is **context-neutral** on broader hermeneutical and theological questions (Nazirite exceptions, priestly prohibitions, prophetic rebuke of drunkenness, Adventist temperance hermeneutics, *two-wine* theory, covenant-meal typology). Grammar tells us what the verse **syntactically permits**, not how that permission should be applied theologically. Those questions must be decided on other grounds.
