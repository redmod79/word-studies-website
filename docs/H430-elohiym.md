# H430 — אֱלֹהִים (ʼĕlôhîym)

## Core Data
- **Strong's:** H430
- **Original:** אֱלֹהִים
- **Transliteration:** ʼĕlôhîym (elohim)
- **Pronunciation:** el-o-heem'
- **Part of Speech:** Masculine noun (morphologically plural, grammatically often singular)
- **Root:** Plural of H433 אֱלוֹהַּ (ʼĕlôwahh); cognate with H410 אֵל (ʼêl) "God, strength, mighty"
- **Occurrences:** 2,689 tokens in 2,246 unique verses
- **Books:** 35 of 39 OT books
- **First Occurrence:** Gen 1:1 — "In the beginning God (Elohim) created the heaven and the earth."
- **Hapax Legomenon:** No — the second most common noun in the OT

## Lexicon Definition

Strong's: *Plural of אֱלוֹהַּ (H433); gods in the ordinary sense; but specifically used (in the plural thus, especially with the article) of the supreme God; occasionally applied by way of deference to magistrates; and sometimes as a superlative:—angels, exceeding, God (gods) (-dess, -ly), (very) great, judges, mighty.*

Morphologically a masculine plural (-im ending), but functions grammatically as **either singular or plural depending on referent**. The plural form with singular agreement is widely classified by grammarians as a "plural of majesty," "plural of intensity/fullness," or "honorific/abstract plural" (GKC §124; Joüon-Muraoka §136d).

## Grammatical Agreement Patterns

This section is central. Morphologically, אֱלֹהִים is a masculine plural noun (-îm ending, like *melakhim* "kings," *devarim* "words"). But grammatical agreement — the actual number of accompanying verbs, adjectives, and pronouns — varies by referent.

### Pattern A: Elohim = true God → SINGULAR agreement (the overwhelming norm)

When Elohim refers to the God of Israel, accompanying verbs, adjectives, and pronouns are **almost always grammatically singular**, even though the noun itself is morphologically plural.

| Reference | Elohim form | Governing verb/pronoun | Parse |
|-----------|-------------|------------------------|-------|
| Gen 1:1 | אֱלֹהִים (mp) | בָּרָא *bara* "created" | Qal Perf **3ms** (singular) |
| Gen 1:3 | אֱלֹהִים (mp) | וַיֹּאמֶר *va-yomer* "and he said" | Qal Wayq **3ms** |
| Gen 1:5 | אֱלֹהִים (mp) | וַיִּקְרָא *va-yiqra* "and he called" | Qal Wayq **3ms** |
| Deut 4:35 | הָאֱלֹהִים (mp + art) | הוּא *hu* "he is" | PersPron **3ms** |
| Deut 6:4 | אֱלֹהֵינוּ *eloheinu* (mp + 1cp) | אֶחָד *echad* "one" | adj **ms** |
| Isa 45:5 | אֱלֹהִים (mp) | אֲנִי *ani* "I [am]" | PersPron **1s** (God's self-predicate) |
| Ps 7:10 | אֱלֹהִים (mp) | שׁוֹפֵט *shofet* "judging" | Qal Ptc **ms** |
| 2Ki 19:15 | אֱלֹהִים (mp) | אַתָּה־הוּא *atta-hu* "thou art he" | PersPron **2ms + 3ms** |

**Diagnostic example — Deut 4:35:** יְהוָה הוּא הָאֱלֹהִים — "YHWH, **he** (3ms) is the Elohim (mp)." The singular pronoun *hu* governs the plural noun.

Across the ~2,000 verses where Elohim refers to YHWH, the verb is singular in virtually every case.

### Pattern B: Elohim = pagan gods (plural referents) → PLURAL agreement

When Elohim refers to actual multiple deities, agreement is plural, matching the morphology.

| Reference | Elohim form | Governing verb | Parse |
|-----------|-------------|----------------|-------|
| Exo 20:3 | אֱלֹהִים אֲחֵרִים | אֲחֵרִים *acherim* "other" | adj **mp** |
| Exo 32:4 | אֱלֹהֶיךָ "thy gods" | הֶעֱלוּךָ *he'elukha* "brought thee up" | Hiph Perf **3p** |
| Exo 32:8 | אֱלֹהֶיךָ | הֶעֱלוּךָ | Hiph Perf **3p** |
| Deut 13:2 | אֱלֹהִים אֲחֵרִים | אֲחֵרִים | adj **mp** |
| Jdg 10:14 | אֱלֹהִים | בְּחַרְתֶּם "ye have chosen" — context plural | plural |
| Jer 10:11 | אֱלָהַיָּא (Aram. plural, ref. pagan gods) | יֵאבַדוּ "shall perish" | plural |

**Diagnostic contrast — Exo 32:4 vs Gen 1:1:** Same noun form (*elohim*), but in Exo 32:4 the verb is *he'elukha* (3p plural), while in Gen 1:1 it is *bara* (3ms singular). The difference is **referential**, not theological: plural verb = actually-multiple deities; singular verb = one God.

### Pattern C: The anomalous plural-verb cases with the true God (the famous crux verses)

A small handful of passages use Elohim of the true God with a plural verb or plural modifier. These are the verses most frequently adduced in Trinity arguments.

| Reference | Elohim form | Plural element | Parse |
|-----------|-------------|----------------|-------|
| Gen 1:26 | אֱלֹהִים (mp) | נַעֲשֶׂה *na'aseh* "let us make" | Qal Impf **1cp** (cohortative) |
| Gen 3:22 | אֱלֹהִים (mp) | כְּאַחַד מִמֶּנּוּ "as one of us" | PersPron **1cp** |
| Gen 11:7 | (YHWH speaks) | נֵרְדָה *neredah* "let us go down" | Qal Impf **1cp** |
| Gen 20:13 | אֱלֹהִים (mp) | הִתְעוּ *hit'u* "caused to wander" | Hiph Perf **3p** |
| Gen 35:7 | הָאֱלֹהִים (mp + art) | נִגְלוּ *niglu* "were revealed" | Niph Perf **3p** |
| 2Sa 7:23 | אֱלֹהִים (mp) | הָלְכוּ *halkhu* "went" | Qal Perf **3p** |
| Josh 24:19 | אֱלֹהִים קְדֹשִׁים | קְדֹשִׁים *qedoshim* "holy (ones)" | adj **mp**, followed by הוּא *hu* **3ms** |
| Ps 58:11 | אֱלֹהִים שֹׁפְטִים | שֹׁפְטִים *shoftim* "judging" | Qal Ptc **mp** |

Josh 24:19 is notable: the adjective is plural (*qedoshim*) but the pronoun in the same clause is singular (*hu*) — mixed agreement within one sentence. The "plural of majesty" / "concord by sense" grammatical analyses both attempt to explain this.

Gen 1:26, 3:22, 11:7 are plural verbs (*cohortative* "let us") but not predicated of Elohim as subject — they are quotations of God speaking in first-person plural.

### Pattern D: Elohim of non-divine humans

| Reference | Referent | Agreement |
|-----------|----------|-----------|
| Exo 7:1 | Moses ("a god/Elohim to Pharaoh") | Singular context; Moses is one man |
| Exo 21:6; 22:8–9, 28 | Human judges | Debated; LXX renders κριτήριον / θεός |
| 1Sa 28:13 | Samuel's ghost ("I saw *elohim* coming up") | ὁἐλעֹלִים *olim* participle **mp** ("ascending"), though one individual |
| Ps 82:1, 6 | Heavenly assembly / human judges (disputed) | Plural adjectives in immediate context |

1Sa 28:13 is another crux: one figure (Samuel), but the participle *olim* is plural masc. Some read this as agreement with the plural morphology regardless of referent; others as "a divine being of the kind called elohim."

### Summary of the grammatical pattern

- The **form** of Elohim is plural (-im), always.
- The **agreement** is controlled by referent, not form:
  - One true God → singular verb / adj / pronoun (~99% of cases)
  - Multiple actual deities → plural agreement
  - Individual human/being called elohim → usually singular; sometimes plural
- The plural form with singular agreement is cross-linguistically well-attested as an "honorific / intensive / abstract plural" (compare *baalim* "master" of one person, *adonim* "lord" of one, Egyptian royal "we"). It does **not** of itself indicate plurality of persons.
- The handful of plural-verb cases with the true God (Gen 1:26; 3:22; 11:7; 20:13; 35:7; 2Sa 7:23) are exegetically debated and are the central data for interpretive questions about intra-divine plurality.

## Grammatical Classification Notes

The standard grammars classify Elohim-for-YHWH as one or more of:

- **Plural of majesty / excellence** (GKC §124g–i; Joüon-Muraoka §136d) — a plural noun with singular reference intensifying quality, not quantity. Compare *baalim* "master" (singular person), *adonim* "lord" (singular person, e.g., Gen 24:9–10), *qedoshim* "the Holy One" (Prov 9:10; 30:3).
- **Plural of abstraction / intensity** — "godness," "the fullness of deity."
- **Honorific / royal plural** — comparable to "we" in royal decrees.
- **Numerical plural** (only when referring to actual multiple deities, angels, judges, etc.).

Whether the plural form carries additional theological weight (intra-divine plurality, proto-Trinitarian hinting) is an **interpretive question beyond grammatical classification**. The grammar itself requires only that plural-for-one-referent be read as honorific/intensive unless the context (plural verb + plural adjectives + plural referents) indicates otherwise.
