## Lexicon Definition

From a presumed compound of υἱός (huios, G5207 "son") and a derivative of τίθημι (tithemi, G5087 "to place"); the placing as a son, i.e. adoption (figuratively, Christian sonship in respect to God).

## Semantic Categories

### 1. Present Spiritual Status — Received Through the Spirit
- **Rom 8:15** — "ye have not received the spirit of bondage again to fear; but ye have received the Spirit of adoption, whereby we cry, Abba, Father"
- **Gal 4:5** — "To redeem them that were under the law, that we might receive the adoption of sons"

The adoption is something already "received" (λαμβάνω) — a present reality, not merely future. Paired with Spirit reception and the intimate cry "Abba, Father."

### 2. Future Eschatological Completion — Bodily Redemption
- **Rom 8:23** — "ourselves also, which have the firstfruits of the Spirit, even we ourselves groan within ourselves, waiting for the adoption, to wit, the redemption of our body"

Here adoption is still "waited for" (ἀπεκδέχομαι) — identified with the future redemption of the body. The Spirit is the "firstfruits" (ἀπαρχή) guaranteeing this completion.

### 3. Israel's Covenantal Privilege
- **Rom 9:4** — "Who are Israelites; to whom pertaineth the adoption, and the glory, and the covenants, and the giving of the law, and the service of God, and the promises"

Adoption listed among Israel's corporate privileges alongside glory, covenants, law, worship, and promises. Refers to Israel's historic status as God's "son" (cf. Exod 4:22, Hos 11:1).

### 4. Predestined Divine Purpose
- **Eph 1:5** — "Having predestinated us unto the adoption of children by Jesus Christ to himself, according to the good pleasure of his will"

Adoption as the predetermined goal of God's elective purpose, accomplished "by" (διά) Jesus Christ and motivated by divine good pleasure (εὐδοκία).

## Hebrew Source

No Hebrew source via LXX mapping. The concept of huiothesia is a Greco-Roman legal institution (adoptio) without a direct Hebrew equivalent. The OT expresses the concept through relational declarations ("Israel is my son, my firstborn," Exod 4:22) rather than a technical adoption term.
