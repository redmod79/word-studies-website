**Semantic alignment:** The LXX translators consistently chose laleo as the primary rendering of dabar, making this the strongest Hebrew-Greek speech equivalence. The LXX distinguished dabar-as-verb (→ laleo, the act of speaking) from dabar-as-noun (→ logos/rhema, the content spoken). This verb-noun distinction carried into NT usage where laleo and logos frequently co-occur in the same verse.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- Both dabar and laleo are the standard, general-purpose words for speaking in their respective testaments
- Both are used for divine speech, prophetic utterance, and ordinary conversation
- Both carry the nuance of "producing articulate words" (as opposed to crying out, shouting, or being silent)
- The Hebrews author explicitly bridges the testaments: "God spake [laleo] in time past...hath spoken [laleo] unto us" (Heb 1:1-2)

**What shifts:**
- NT adds the tongues-speaking dimension (Acts 2, 1 Cor 12-14) — laleo is the exclusive verb for this phenomenon, which has no direct OT counterpart using dabar
- In the OT, dabar is the dominant speech verb; in the NT, lego (G3004) is actually more frequent than laleo, with laleo occupying a more specific semantic niche (the act/process of speaking vs. lego's focus on content/declaration)
- The NT occasionally uses laleo for non-human "speech" (Rev 13:15 — the image of the beast "should speak"), extending beyond dabar's typical usage
