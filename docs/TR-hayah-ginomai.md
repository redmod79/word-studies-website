These contextual co-occurrences reflect ginomai absorbing Hebrew temporal formulas ("the word came," "in the year," "in the month").

**Semantic split in translation:** Hebrew hayah is a single lexeme covering the entire range from static existence to dynamic becoming. The LXX translators systematically divided this range: ginomai for narrative event markers ("it came to pass"), transformations ("became"), and happening; eimi for predication ("was"), identity ("I am"), and existence. This split is a genuine translation decision, not merely a grammatical one. Greek requires the distinction; Hebrew does not.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- Both hayah and ginomai serve as narrative event markers ("it came to pass" / "it happened")
- Both express transformation and becoming (Gen 2:7 "became" / John 1:14 "was made")
- Both are used for creative/causative events (Gen 1:3 / John 1:3)

**What shifts:**
- The existential/identity sense of hayah ("I AM" in Exo 3:14) is carried exclusively by eimi in the NT, not ginomai
- Ginomai in the NT develops a stronger theological sense of "coming into being" (creation, incarnation) than the more general hayah
- The "came to pass" narrative formula (wayehi) that dominates Hebrew narrative becomes less prominent in NT Greek, replaced by other narrative devices
