**Semantic shift in translation:** The LXX bridge is highly faithful. Hebrew tumʼah and Greek akatharsía both denote the abstract quality of ritual/moral impurity (not the adjective "unclean" nor the verb "to defile"). The noun-to-noun mapping is near-exclusive, preserving both the ritual (Levitical) and moral (prophetic) senses without significant semantic shift. The alpha-privative formation of akatharsía (a- + katharos "clean") mirrors the Hebrew derivation from ṭâmêʼ.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The abstract-noun status: both tumʼah and akatharsía denote the condition/quality of impurity, not the person or the act
- The antithetical pairing with cleanness/holiness: OT tumʼah opposes ṭohŏrâh (H2893) / qodesh (H6944); NT akatharsía opposes hagiasmos (G38 "holiness/sanctification") at 1 Thess 4:7
- The connection to sexual conduct: OT tumʼah covers menstrual and genital discharges (Lev 15); NT akatharsía regularly appears in vice lists alongside porneia ("fornication") and aselgeia ("lasciviousness")

**What shifts:**
- **Ritual → moral dominance:** OT tumʼah is overwhelmingly ritual-cultic (Levitical bodily impurity, sanctuary pollution). NT akatharsía is overwhelmingly moral-ethical (sexual sin, greed, hypocrisy). The Ezekiel passages (36:17, 25, 29) form the transitional bridge — tumʼah as metaphor for national moral failure.
- **Cult → character:** OT tumʼah is something contracted and removed by priestly ritual (washing, sacrifice, waiting). NT akatharsía is a disposition of the heart (Rom 1:24 "lusts of their own hearts"), overcome by sanctification (1 Thess 4:7), not by ceremony.
- **Jesus' pivotal reframing:** Matt 23:27 applies the classic Levitical imagery (corpse-contact impurity from Num 19:13) to expose internal moral corruption, collapsing the ritual-moral distinction.
