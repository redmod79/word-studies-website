## Lexicon Definition

Strong's: "an emphatic form of [H113 adon]; the Lord (used as a proper name of God only): — (my) Lord."

The form is morphologically a plural of majesty (pluralis majestatis) of H113 אָדוֹן (adon, "lord, master") with a first-person singular suffix — literally "my lords" but functioning as a singular honorific title. By the post-exilic period, אֲדֹנָי was treated as a proper divine title distinct from ordinary adon (H113), which is used of human masters.

## Qere Perpetuum — Oral Substitute for YHWH

Adonai's most distinctive function in the Masoretic tradition is as the *qere perpetuum* ("perpetual read-aloud") substitute for the Tetragrammaton יהוה (H3068 YHWH). To avoid pronouncing the divine name, Jewish readers orally substituted אֲדֹנָי whenever the text wrote יהוה. The Masoretes signaled this by pointing יהוה with the vowels of אֲדֹנָי (shewa-holem-qamets), yielding the familiar hybrid pointing *Yəhōwāh*.

When Christian readers of the later medieval period combined the consonants of יהוה with these Adonai vowels — ignoring the qere convention — the artificial Latinized form "Iehouah / Jehovah" was produced. "Jehovah" is thus a vowel-point hybrid: YHWH consonants + Adonai vowels, not an authentic pronunciation of either word.

When יהוה and אֲדֹנָי stood together in the text (אֲדֹנָי יהוה), reading יהוה as "Adonai" would produce "Adonai Adonai"; to avoid this, the Masoretes pointed the second word with the vowels of אֱלֹהִים (Elohim), and the reader said "Adonai Elohim." This is why KJV renders such compounds "Lord GOD" (with GOD in small caps).

## Historical / Theological Notes

- **Pluralis majestatis:** The plural form אֲדֹנָי with first-person singular suffix ("my lords") is usually analyzed as an honorific plural, analogous to Elohim. Some grammarians read the qamets as a distinguishing marker separating the divine form from the human "my lords" (which would take patach).
- **Second Temple usage:** By the late Second Temple period, Adonai had fully taken over as the spoken divine name in synagogue reading. The Qumran scrolls show scribes writing the Tetragrammaton in paleo-Hebrew or substituting אדני to avoid profanation.
- **"Jehovah" origin:** The word Jehovah first appears in 13th-century Christian Latin texts (Raymundus Martini, Pugio Fidei, ~1270), formed by combining the consonants YHWH with the Adonai pointing (shewa-holem-qamets → e-o-a). It is a reading error of the qere-perpetuum convention, not an ancient pronunciation.
- **NT kyrios:** Because the LXX rendered both H3068 and H136 as κύριος, the NT's Christological use of "Lord" for Jesus inherits the full semantic weight of both — YHWH's exclusive identity and Adonai's sovereign-master role — without formal distinction.
