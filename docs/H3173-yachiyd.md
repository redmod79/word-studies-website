## Lexicon Definition

From H3161; properly, *united*, i.e. *sole*; by implication, *beloved*; also *lonely*; (feminine) *the life* (as not to be replaced):—darling, desolate, only (child, son), solitary.

The word carries a tight cluster of senses built on the idea of "one-and-only":
1. **Sole / unique** (of a child with no sibling — the only-begotten of a parent)
2. **Beloved / precious** (one-of-a-kind → treasured → darling)
3. **Solitary / alone** (one by oneself → lonely, desolate)
4. **The life** (feminine substantive in Ps 22:20, 35:17 — the one irreplaceable self)

## Semantic Categories

### 1. The Only (and Beloved) Son Offered in Sacrifice — the Akedah Pattern
- **Gen 22:2** — "Take now thy son, thine only [son] Isaac, whom thou lovest, and... offer him there for a burnt offering."
- **Gen 22:12** — "thou hast not withheld thy son, thine only [son] from me."
- **Gen 22:16** — "thou hast... not withheld thy son, thine only [son]."

The threefold repetition across one chapter is unique in the Hebrew Bible — *yâchîyd* is the narrative drumbeat of the Akedah. Ishmael existed, so *yâchîyd* here does not mean biologically "only child" but rather "one-of-a-kind, the son of promise, uniquely beloved." The LXX fixes this as ἀγαπητός ("beloved") — a translation choice that will be picked up at the baptism and transfiguration of Jesus.

### 2. The Only Child as Victim of Sacrifice — Jephthah's Daughter
- **Jdg 11:34** — "she [was his] only child; beside her he had neither son nor daughter."

The narrator frames Jephthah's daughter with *yâchîyd* explicitly to echo Isaac; the LXX renders it with **both** μονογενής *and* ἀγαπητή — the only verse in the OT to do so — fusing the two Gen-22 associations (sole-born + beloved) in a single line. Unlike the Akedah, here there is no ram in the thicket.

### 3. Mourning for an Only Son (Prophetic Simile of Deepest Grief)
- **Jer 6:26** — "make thee mourning, [as for] an only son, most bitter lamentation."
- **Amos 8:10** — "I will make it as the mourning of an only [son], and the end thereof as a bitter day."
- **Zech 12:10** — "they shall mourn for him, as one mourneth for [his] only [son], and shall be in bitterness for him, as one that is in bitterness for [his] firstborn."

*Yâchîyd* becomes the standard prophetic shorthand for inconsolable grief: the death of an only son is the maximum horizon of human sorrow. Zech 12:10 pairs it with בְּכוֹר (firstborn), and the NT cites this verse at the pierced Messiah (John 19:37; Rev 1:7).

### 4. The Beloved/Precious Life (Feminine Substantive, "Soul" Parallel)
- **Ps 22:20** — "Deliver my soul (nephesh) from the sword; my darling (yâchîydâh) from the power of the dog."
- **Ps 35:17** — "rescue my soul from their destructions, my darling from the lions."

A poetic parallelism in which *yâchîyd* stands in the B-colon matching *nephesh* in the A-colon — "my one-and-only (life)." The feminine form personifies the speaker's single irreplaceable self. LXX renders both as μονογενῆ (μου). Ps 22 is the Passion psalm; the convergence of "pierced hands and feet" (v. 16), "my darling/only-one" (v. 20), and the mourning of Zech 12:10 tightens the sacrificial thread.

### 5. Lonely / Desolate / Solitary (Isolated as "One Alone")
- **Ps 25:16** — "have mercy upon me; for I [am] desolate (yâchîyd) and afflicted."
- **Ps 68:6** — "God setteth the solitary (yâchîyd) in families."
- **Pro 4:3** — "I was my father's son, tender and only [beloved] in the sight of my mother."

When the relational opposite "family / companions" is absent, *yâchîyd* slides from "uniquely cherished" to "left alone." Pro 4:3 keeps the cherished sense (cp. LXX ἀγαπώμενος); Ps 25:16 and 68:6 bend toward isolation. Same word, opposite emotional valence, determined by context.

## LXX Mapping

*Yâchîyd* has one of the cleanest and most theologically freighted LXX profiles in the Hebrew Bible. All 12 Hebrew occurrences are rendered by just four Greek lexemes, and the split by context is consistent:

| Greek Equivalent | Strong's | Count | KJV-H3173 Contexts |
|------------------|----------|-------|--------------------|
| ἀγαπητός (agapētós) — beloved | G27 | 7 | Gen 22:2, 22:12, 22:16; Jdg 11:34 (paired); Jer 6:26; Amos 8:10; Zech 12:10 |
| μονογενής (monogenḗs) — only-born, sole | G3439 | 4 | Jdg 11:34 (paired); Ps 22:20; Ps 25:16; Ps 35:17 |
| ἀγαπώμενος (passive ptc. of ἀγαπάω) — beloved | G25 | 1 | Pro 4:3 |
| (paraphrased, not rendered word-for-word) | — | 1 | Ps 68:6 — "solitary" sense handled via broader clause |

**Two streams, one source.**
- Where *yâchîyd* modifies the **son/child in a sacrifice or mourning frame**, the LXX reaches for ἀγαπητός. This is the Greek word the voice from heaven uses of Jesus at his baptism and transfiguration (Matt 3:17; 17:5; Mark 1:11; 9:7; Luke 3:22; 9:35 var.) and that Paul applies to the Son in Rom 8:32 ("he that spared not his own Son," verbally echoing Gen 22:16 LXX οὐκ ἐφείσω τοῦ υἱοῦ σου τοῦ ἀγαπητοῦ).
- Where *yâchîyd* describes the **psalmist's one-irreplaceable-life** (Pss 22, 25, 35) or an **only child** abstractly, the LXX reaches for μονογενής. This is the Greek word John uses of the Son (John 1:14, 18; 3:16, 18; 1 John 4:9) and Hebrews 11:17 uses of Isaac.

**Hebrews 11:17 is the keystone.** Hebrews calls Isaac Abraham's μονογενής even though Isaac was not Abraham's only biological son — the author is importing the Hebrew *yâchîyd* sense ("sole, uniquely-one-of-a-kind, son of promise") directly into Greek via a word the LXX already used to render *yâchîyd* in the Psalms. The semantic bridge runs yâchîyd → μονογενής / ἀγαπητός → the Christological titles "beloved Son" and "only-begotten Son."

## Semantic-Domain Note: "Only Child in a Sacrifice Context"

Of the 12 OT occurrences, **5** (42%) stand in a context of actual or imagined sacrificial death of the only child:
- Gen 22:2, 12, 16 — Isaac on Moriah (averted)
- Jdg 11:34 — Jephthah's daughter (not averted)
- Zech 12:10 — the pierced one mourned as an only son (future / Messianic)

Add the 3 mourning-simile verses (Jer 6:26; Amos 8:10; the other half of Zech 12:10) and **over half** the attestations cluster in the semantic field of *grief-over-the-loss-of-one's-sole-heir*. This tight pattern is the reason the word exerts such gravity on NT Christology: when John and Hebrews and Paul reach for "only/beloved Son" language, they are reaching for a Hebrew word whose dominant associative field is **a father giving up the one he cannot afford to lose**. The Akedah is the master-type; Jephthah is the tragic foil; Zech 12:10 is the prophetic forward-echo; and the LXX's split rendering (ἀγαπητός for the son-contexts, μονογενής for the soul-contexts) seeds both halves of the NT vocabulary.
