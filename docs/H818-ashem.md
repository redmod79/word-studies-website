### 1. Guilt-State as Acknowledged Condition
- **Gen 42:21** — "And they said one to another, We [are] verily guilty concerning our brother, in that we saw the anguish of his soul, when he besought us, and we would not hear; therefore is this distress come upon us."
  Joseph's brothers use *ashem* as a predicate adjective describing their standing before God — a guilt-state they recognize as the cause of their present distress. The adverbial intensifier "verily" (אֲבָל) underscores the self-evident nature of the condition.

### 2. Culpability Requiring Redress
- **2 Sam 14:13** — "And the woman said, Wherefore then hast thou thought such a thing against the people of God? for the king doth speak this thing as one which is faulty, in that the king doth not fetch home again his banished."
  The wise woman of Tekoa applies *ashem* to king David — rendered "as one which is faulty" — framing his failure to restore Absalom as a guilt-condition against God's people. Here the adjective describes liability that demands corrective action.

### 3. Guilt-State Preceding Sacrificial Offering
- **Ezra 10:19** — "And they gave their hands that they would put away their wives; and [being] guilty, [they offered] a ram of the flock for their trespass."
  The returned exiles who married foreign women are described as *ashem* — in a state of guilt — and from that condition they present a ram as a trespass offering (cf. H817 ʼasham). The adjective establishes the legal-ritual status that necessitates the offering.
