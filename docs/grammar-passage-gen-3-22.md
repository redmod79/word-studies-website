# Grammar Analysis: Genesis 3:22

## Text

**Hebrew:** וַיֹּ֣אמֶר׀ יְהוָ֣ה אֱלֹהִ֗ים הֵ֤ן הָֽאָדָם֙ הָיָה֙ כְּאַחַ֣ד מִמֶּ֔נּוּ לָדַ֖עַת טֹ֣וב וָרָ֑ע וְעַתָּ֣ה׀ פֶּן־יִשְׁלַ֣ח יָדֹ֗ו וְלָקַח֙ גַּ֚ם מֵעֵ֣ץ הַֽחַיִּ֔ים וְאָכַ֖ל וָחַ֥י לְעֹלָֽם׃

**KJV:** And the LORD God said, Behold, the man is become as one of us, to know good and evil: and now, lest he put forth his hand, and take also of the tree of life, and eat, and live for ever:

The clause is structured as: **narrative speech frame → divine assertion (XQtl) → purpose infinitive → pivot (*w'attah*) → apprehension clause (*pen* + yiqtol) → three weqatal consequences.** The weqatal chain after *pen* extends the negative-purpose force across the whole sequence ("lest he... and... and..."). See [syntax-waw-conjunction.md](../hebrew/syntax-waw-conjunction.md).

## Key Grammatical Features

### 1. כְּאַחַד מִמֶּנּוּ — "as one of us" (Prep + noun in construct + Prep with 1cp suffix)

- **Form:** אַחַד is *Noun.s.Cst* — the cardinal number "one" in the **construct state**, governed by the preposition *k'* ("as, like"). מִמֶּנּוּ is the preposition *min* with a **1st-person common plural pronominal suffix** ("from us / of us").
- **Morphological note:** *min* fuses with pronominal suffixes through gemination of *nun*: מִמֶּנִּי "from me" (1cs), מִמֶּנּוּ (either 1cp "from us" *or* 3ms "from him"), מִמֶּנָּה "from her." The 3ms and 1cp forms are **consonantally identical** (מִמֶּנּוּ), distinguished only by context. See GKC §103c, §103f n. 4; Joüon-Muraoka §103f.
- **Construct + partitive *min*:** The phrase אַחַד מִמֶּנּוּ is a partitive construction — "one of / one from among us." Both the construct state of *echad* and the prepositional *min* signal partitivity (IBHS §15.2.1d on partitive *min*; §9.6 on the partitive construct). Compare Gen 42:13 אַחַד אֵינֶנּוּ "one [of us] is not," Judges 17:11, 1 Sam 26:15 — idiomatic "one of N" formula.
- **Significance:** The grammar unambiguously locates the divine speaker within a group that includes more than one referent. The man has become "like one (member) of us." The question is not *whether* plurality is in view (it is), but *which* plurality.

### 2. הָיָה כְּאַחַד מִמֶּנּוּ — Qal perfect + comparative *kaph* (stative/resultative)

- **Form:** הָיָה is Qal perfect 3ms of היה "to be/become." With the comparative כְּ, the construction *hayah k-* means "he has become like / he is now like" (stative resultative). See IBHS §30.5.3; Williams §255.
- **Significance:** The perfect is **not** describing a potential or hypothetical state — it asserts an accomplished fact: the man *has become* like one of us. Whatever "like one of us" means theologically, the grammar presents it as a present reality after the fall, not a boast, wish, or fear. This grounds the divine council / Trinity / angelic-address debate in an actual post-fall condition, not a rhetorical exaggeration.
- **Word study:** See [WG-becoming.md](../../word-studies/WG-becoming.md) for *hayah* as existential/resultative "become."

### 3. First-person plural divine self-reference (morphology + concord)

Four data points from the verse establish the plural self-reference:

1. **מִמֶּנּוּ** — preposition *min* + 1cp suffix ("from us / of us") — carries the plural number morphologically.
2. **אֱלֹהִים** — formally masculine plural noun (Noun.mp.Abs) but construed here with singular verb (יֹּאמֶר 3ms) and singular proper name יְהוָה. This is the standard "plural of majesty / intensive plural" pattern for the one true God (GKC §124g; Joüon-Muraoka §136d; IBHS §7.4.3).
3. **יֹּאמֶר** — Qal wayyiqtol **3ms singular**. YHWH-Elohim is grammatically singular as subject.
4. **כְּאַחַד** — "as one" presupposes a group of at least two from which one can be chosen.

The tension is real and structural: a **grammatically singular speaker** (singular verb + proper name YHWH) who refers to himself with a **plural partitive** ("one of us"). This is the same pattern found in Gen 1:26 (נַעֲשֶׂה "let us make"), Gen 11:7 (נֵרְדָה "let us go down"), and Isa 6:8 (מִי יֵלֶךְ־לָנוּ "who will go for us"). Gen 3:22 is the **only one of these four that uses a pronominal suffix** rather than a plural verb form, so it is the least ambiguous morphologically — *min* + 1cp suffix cannot be explained by cohortative hortatory self-address.

### 4. Interpretive Options for the 1cp Reference

Standard grammars and commentators survey four options. The grammar rules out the weakest; the others remain exegetically open.

**(a) Plural of majesty / *pluralis majestatis*.** Applied to *Elohim* (the noun) this works (GKC §124g; Joüon-Muraoka §136d-e). Applied to the **pronominal suffix** it does not — Gesenius explicitly notes that the *pluralis majestatis* "has been almost universally given up" as an explanation for divine plural *verbs* and *pronouns*, because Hebrew has **no genuine parallels**: kings in the Hebrew Bible use 1cs, not 1cp, of themselves (cf. GKC §124g n. 2; Joüon-Muraoka §114e n. 2). The royal "we" is a Latin / medieval European convention, not a Hebrew one. IBHS §7.4.3f treats the divine plural of *Elohim* as "honorific" but does not extend this to 1cp verbs/suffixes.

**(b) Address to the divine/heavenly council.** God speaks to the assembled *bene Elohim* (cf. 1 Kgs 22:19-22; Job 1:6; Ps 82:1; Isa 6:8). This is the dominant option in critical scholarship (so Westermann, *Genesis 1-11*, 161; Gunkel; von Rad). Strength: Isa 6:8 pairs singular and plural self-reference in an explicit throne-room setting — "Whom shall I send, and who will go for us?" Weakness for Gen 3:22: the text does not narrate any council; and the "one of us" in **knowing good and evil** places the council members on the knowledge-level the man has just attained — which is theologically awkward if the referents are creaturely angels.

**(c) Address to angels.** Essentially a softened version of (b). Rashi, Ibn Ezra, and some Jewish commentators take this route (so Sarna, *JPS Torah Commentary: Genesis*, 29). Same grammatical strength as (b); same theological difficulty about angels' knowledge of good and evil.

**(d) Intra-Trinitarian / plurality-within-God reference.** The speaker is YHWH-Elohim (one singular God) who refers to himself using a plural that reflects genuine internal plurality. Defended as grammatically coherent by Keil-Delitzsch (*Pentateuch*, 1:105), Hengstenberg, and modern evangelical commentators (Hamilton, *Genesis 1-17*, NICOT, 208; Wenham, *Genesis 1-15*, WBC, 84-85, surveys options). Strength: explains the tight binding of singular verbs/names to plural suffixes without positing unmentioned bystanders; coincides with Christian reading of Gen 1:26 and Isa 48:16. Weakness: no pre-Christian Jewish text reads Gen 3:22 Trinitarianly; the explicit doctrine is anachronistic for the author's context (though many hold the text is compatible with progressive revelation).

**Grammar's verdict (what the morphology alone settles):** The plural reference is real (ruling out pure *pluralis majestatis* of the pronoun); the speaker is singular at the verb-concord level (ruling out pure polytheism); and the speaker includes himself in the group ("of *us*"), so the "council" must somehow include YHWH. Everything else is exegetical/theological judgment.

### 5. לָדַעַת טוֹב וָרָע — Infinitive construct with *lamed* (purpose / result)

- **Form:** לָדַעַת is *lamed* + Qal infinitive construct of ידע "to know." Followed by two absolute adjectives functioning as objects (טוֹב וָרָע "good and evil"). See [word-studies/H3045-yada.md](../../word-studies/H3045-yada.md).
- **Function:** The *lamed* + infinitive construct is typically purpose ("in order to know") or result ("so as to know"); here it defines the **content** of what being "like one of us" consists in — "like one of us *with respect to / in terms of* knowing good and evil." See Joüon-Muraoka §124l (infinitive construct with *le* of specification); Williams §277.
- **Significance:** The divine likeness the man has seized is specifically the *moral-discriminating* kind (see the word study on *yada*). It is not ontological deification, not general omniscience, but the **experiential/evaluative knowledge of good and evil** — which Gen 2:17 had forbidden. The grammar thus focuses the "like us" claim narrowly: not "humans are now gods," but "humans now presume to judge moral categories on their own authority."

### 6. פֶּן + Qal imperfect followed by weqatal chain (negative purpose / apprehension)

- **Form:** פֶּן־יִשְׁלַח יָדוֹ וְלָקַח... וְאָכַל וָחַי — *pen* + yiqtol (apprehension clause) governing a chain of three weqatal consequential perfects.
- **Function:** *Pen* introduces negative purpose / apprehension clauses ("lest," "in order that... not"). The initial yiqtol carries the apprehension; subsequent weqatals extend the same modal force — "lest he stretch out, and take, and eat, and live forever." See GKC §152w; IBHS §38.4a; Joüon-Muraoka §168g. The weqatal chain after yiqtol is the normal way Hebrew sustains modal / future / volitional force across successive actions.
- **Significance:** The verse ends not with a statement but with **divine self-deliberation**. The expulsion of v. 23-24 is presented as preventive action motivated by the *pen* clause. Eternal life in a fallen moral state is the danger — and God acts to prevent it. The grammar turns the expulsion from Eden into an act of mercy (blocking sinful immortality), not merely of punishment. See [weqatal-apodosis-vow.md](../hebrew/weqatal-apodosis-vow.md) for weqatal chain dynamics (here after *pen*).

## Discourse Flow

```
Narrative frame   :  wayyiqtol  (WayX)         — "YHWH Elohim said"
  │
  ├─ Assertion    :  XQtl / stative perfect    — "the man has become as one of us"
  │     └─ purpose infinitive — "to know good and evil"
  │
  ├─ Pivot        :  w'attah                   — "and now"
  │
  └─ Apprehension :  pen + yiqtol
        │          — "lest he stretch out his hand"
        ├─ weqatal — "and take from the tree of life"
        ├─ weqatal — "and eat"
        └─ weqatal — "and live forever"
```

The *w'attah* ("and now") is a classical **discourse-pragmatic pivot**: the first half diagnoses the condition (man's new state); the second half announces the response (preventive expulsion). See Joüon-Muraoka §177n on *we'attah* as a rhetorical hinge.
