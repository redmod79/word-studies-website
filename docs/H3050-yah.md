## Lexicon Definition

Contraction for יְהֹוָה (YHWH), and meaning the same; **Jah, the sacred name**. Compare names in '-iah,' '-jah.' Strong's gloss: "Jah, the Lord, most vehement." Strong's explicitly directs readers to compare the many theophoric personal names ending in *-yah* / *-iah* / *-jah*, which preserve this shortened form as a suffix element.

## Relationship to H3068 (YHWH)

Yah is a poetic/liturgical short form of the Tetragrammaton יהוה (YHWH, H3068). It preserves the first and last consonants/vowel of the longer name:

- **H3068 יְהֹוָה** — 6,828x, full sacred name, prose and narrative
- **H3050 יָהּ** — 46x, liturgical/poetic contexts (overwhelmingly Psalms)

The two co-occur in the same verse 20 times (43% of H3050's uses) — most strikingly in the paired expressions where both forms appear adjacently:

- **Exo 17:16** — "the LORD (Yah) hath sworn [that] the LORD (YHWH) [will have] war..."
- **Isa 12:2** — "the LORD JEHOVAH" (Yah YHWH)
- **Isa 26:4** — "in the LORD JEHOVAH [is] everlasting strength" (Yah YHWH)

When KJV renders "the LORD JEHOVAH," the underlying Hebrew is the compound יָהּ יְהוִה (Yah + alternate pointing of YHWH).

## Semantic Categories

## Theophoric -yah Suffix in Personal Names

Strong's lexicon note explicitly invokes this feature: "Compare names in '-iah,' '-jah.'" Though H3050 as an independent word appears only 46 times, it lives on as the most common theophoric element in Hebrew onomastics. The short form Yah (or the fuller -yahu) terminates the names of prophets, kings, and officials, declaring "Yah is ___" or "___ of Yah":

| **Yeshayah, Adonijah, Ahaziah, Josiah, Hezekiah**, etc. | all end in -iah / -yahu | — |

The short form -יָה and the longer theophoric -יָהוּ are orthographic variants of the same divine element. Every such name is a miniature confession of YHWH's character.

**LXX treatment summary:** The Septuagint most often translates H3050 as κύριος (Lord), the same default rendering used for the Tetragrammaton — dissolving the short/long distinction. However, the *halelu-Yah* formula is preserved through direct transliteration as ἀλληλουϊα (allēlouïa), keeping the Hebrew divine name audible in Greek. This transliteration is the only LXX case where Yah survives as a recognizable proper name in the Greek text.

### New Testament Reflex
ἀλληλουϊα (G239) appears 4 times in the NT, all clustered in **Revelation 19:1, 3, 4, 6** — the heavenly multitude's cry at the fall of Babylon and the marriage of the Lamb. This is the only NT liturgical borrowing of H3050. Revelation preserves the Hebrew divine name Yah in worship contexts fifteen centuries after the Exodus 15 song, forming a canonical inclusio: Yah praised at the exodus from Egypt (Exo 15) and Yah praised at the exodus from Babylon (Rev 19).
