## Lexicon Definition

From ἀ- (negative particle) + a derivative of κεράννυμι ("to mix"). Literally "unmixed, undiluted, pure" — applied especially to wine not diluted with water. Louw-Nida gloss: "at full strength | undiluted | pure." In Greek usage from Homer onward, ἄκρατος οἶνος denotes wine drunk neat, without the customary water mixture, carrying the connotation of overwhelming strength and intoxicating potency.

## Semantic Categories

Because ἄκρατος is a biblical hapax with a single attestation, its meaning is fixed by (a) its cognate structure (α-privative + κεράννυμι) and (b) extra-biblical Greek usage and LXX parallels. Rather than multiple semantic categories within Scripture, the word carries a single technical sense expressed through one image:

### 1. Undiluted Wine of Judgment
- **Rev 14:10** — "The same shall drink of the wine of the wrath of God, which is poured out without mixture (ἀκράτου) into the cup of his indignation"

The adjective qualifies wine within the cup-of-wrath complex. Ordinary Greco-Roman and Jewish table wine was habitually cut with water (commonly 2–3 parts water to 1 part wine); drinking it ἄκρατος was considered barbaric excess and produced rapid, overwhelming intoxication. Applied to divine wrath, the image removes the tempering element: the cup contains θυμός (thumos, G2372 — burning anger) at full strength.

### 2. Background: Cup-of-Wrath Tradition
The Septuagint translators used related vocabulary to render the Hebrew cup-of-wrath passages (Ps 75:8; Jer 25:15; Isa 51:17, 22). Jer 25:15 LXX uses the cognate noun κέρασμα/κεράννυμι family to describe mixed wine of wrath; Ps 75:8 speaks of wine "full of mixture" (πλῆρες κεράσματος). Revelation inverts or intensifies this by specifying the opposite — ἄκρατος — signaling that the eschatological cup is not the tempered draft of historical judgments but judgment at undiluted strength.

## Interpretive Notes on Rev 14:10 Syntax

The same verse contains both κεκερασμένου ἀκράτου ("mixed undiluted") — an apparent contradiction. Greek interpreters resolve it as follows: κεκερασμένου refers to the preparation/pouring of the cup (the standard verb for serving wine), while ἀκράτου specifies that what was poured in was the wrath itself, at full strength, with no water added. The wine is "served up" but not "watered down." This reverses the normal Greco-Roman drinking practice where κεράννυμι presupposes dilution.
