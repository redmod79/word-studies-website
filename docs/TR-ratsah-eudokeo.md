**Semantic continuity in translation:** Strong continuity. Both the Hebrew râtsâh and the Greek eudokeō express evaluative pleasure — a subject finding something or someone worthy of approval. The LXX chose eudokeō specifically for the divine-pleasure sense of râtsâh (God being pleased with persons, offerings, or conduct), while using prosdechomai and dechomai for the more transactional "receive/accept" sense. This division means eudokeō entered the NT pre-loaded with specifically divine evaluative connotations.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The core sense of evaluative divine pleasure — God finding someone or something acceptable — carries intact from OT râtsâh through LXX eudokeō into the NT
- The sacrificial context persists: OT "accepted for him to make atonement" → NT "in burnt offerings thou hast had no pleasure" (Heb 10:6)
- The personal relational sense persists: OT "my soul delighteth" (Isa 42:1) → NT "in whom I am well pleased" (Matt 3:17)

**What shifts:**
- The NT intensifies the christological application: the baptismal voice (Matt 3:17, Mark 1:11, Luke 3:22, 2 Pet 1:17) concentrates divine eudokeō on a single person — the Son — whereas OT râtsâh is distributed across Israel, individuals, offerings, and land
- The NT expands to human willing/choosing: Paul uses eudokeō for human decisions ("we were willing," 1 Thess 2:8; "it pleased them of Macedonia," Rom 15:26), a usage rare in the OT where râtsâh is overwhelmingly God-directed (37% YHWH collocation)
- Hebrews deploys the negative form ("no pleasure in burnt offerings") to argue that the entire sacrificial system anticipated its own replacement — turning the OT Niphal "be accepted" on its head
