# Grammar Analysis: 1 Corinthians 10:14-22

**Focus:** Cup of blessing vs cup of demons — why believers cannot drink both.

## Text

**Greek (NA28 base):**

## Key Grammatical Features

### 1. φεύγετε (v14) — Present Active Imperative, 2nd Plural

- **Form:** Pres Act Impv 2Pl of φεύγω, "flee."
- **Significance:** The present imperative commands ongoing, habitual action — not a one-time decision but a continuing pattern: "keep on fleeing." Paul does not say "stop committing" (which would use μή + present) but rather demands a continuing withdrawal. Contrast with v15's κρίνατε (aorist imperative: "render a single, decisive judgment on what I say").
- **Grammar reference:** `greek/mood-aorist-imperative.md` (for the aorist contrast)

### 2. κρίνατε (v15) — Aorist Active Imperative, 2nd Plural

- **Form:** Aor Act Impv 2Pl of κρίνω.
- **Significance:** Aorist imperative calls for a discrete, complete act of judgment. Paul is not commanding ongoing deliberation but asking the Corinthians to reach a single verdict on his argument. The aorist-present contrast within vv14-15 frames the whole unit: one act of judgment (v15) that leads to a continuous lifestyle of fleeing (v14).
- **Grammar reference:** `greek/mood-aorist-imperative.md`

### 3. κοινωνία + Genitive (vv16, 18, 20) — Genitive of Participation/Sharing

- **Form:** κοινωνία (G2842, N-NSF) governing objective/partitive genitives: τοῦ αἵματος, τοῦ σώματος, τοῦ θυσιαστηρίου (v18 uses cognate κοινωνοί + gen.), τῶν δαιμονίων (v20).
- **Significance:** κοινωνία with the genitive denotes real participation in a shared reality, not mere symbolic association. The structure is repeated in parallel — blood of Christ / body of Christ / altar (Israel) / demons — forcing readers to see the table-fellowship as ontologically parallel. The choice of genitive (partitive/objective) makes the participants share in that very thing. This is the load-bearing grammar for the whole argument: the same construction applied to Christ's blood and to demons implies that idol-meals are not neutral.
- **Grammar reference:** (new entry candidate — no existing canonical entry)
- **Word study:** (candidate G2842-koinonia — not yet in library)

### 4. οὐχὶ ... ἐστιν; (v16) and οὐχ ... εἰσίν; (v18) — Rhetorical Questions Expecting "Yes"

- **Form:** οὐχί (G3780, strengthened form of οὐ) and οὐχ with the indicative of εἰμί, in interrogative function.
- **Significance:** Negative particles with a question mark expect an affirmative answer. Paul is not asking whether the cup is communion — he is compelling the Corinthians to affirm that it is. The strengthened οὐχί in v16 is emphatic ("is it not indeed ...?"). This rhetorical grammar drives assent step by step: *communion with Christ's blood* (yes), *communion with Christ's body* (yes), *partakers of the altar* (yes) — therefore the parallel claim about demons in v20 must also be accepted.

### 5. ἐκ τοῦ ἑνὸς ἄρτου μετέχομεν (v17) — ἐκ + Genitive of Source/Partition

- **Form:** ἐκ (G1537) + genitive τοῦ ἑνὸς ἄρτου ("out of the one loaf") governing μετέχομεν (Pres Act Ind 1Pl of μετέχω, G3348).
- **Significance:** μετέχω ("share in, partake") ordinarily takes the genitive directly (as in v21 — τραπέζης ... μετέχειν). Here Paul inserts ἐκ to emphasize the single source: the one bread is the ground *from which* the many share. The grammar itself images the unity argument: ἓν σῶμα οἱ πολλοί (one body, the many) because ἐκ τοῦ ἑνὸς (out of the one). The repeated εἷς/ἓν/ἑνὸς across cases is a rhetorical refrain.

### 6. δαιμονίοις ... οὐ Θεῷ θύουσιν (v20) — Dative of Indirect Object + Negated Dative in Parallel

- **Form:** δαιμονίοις (Dat Pl N) / Θεῷ (Dat Sg M) as datives of indirect object ("to whom") with θύουσιν (Pres Act Ind 3Pl of θύω, G2380).
- **Significance:** The parallel datives set up a binary: pagans sacrifice *to demons*, not *to God*. Paul does not say the idol exists (v19) — he says the *direction* of the act is toward real demonic beings. The dative flags the recipient, and the οὐ between the two datives is absolute: the two destinations are mutually exclusive. This sets up the "you cannot" of v21.

### 7. οὐ δύνασθε ... πίνειν / μετέχειν (v21) — Impossibility + Complementary Present Infinitives

- **Form:** δύνασθε (V-PNI-2P of δύναμαι, G1410 — present middle/passive indicative) + complementary present active infinitives πίνειν (G4095) and μετέχειν (G3348).
- **Significance:** δύναμαι + infinitive normally denotes capacity. οὐ δύνασθε is not "you may not" (permission) but "you are not able" (ontological/spiritual impossibility). The present infinitives πίνειν and μετέχειν mark ongoing participation — Paul is not banning a single act but declaring the two patterns of life incompatible. The double καὶ ("and also") linking cup-of-Lord to cup-of-demons shows the impossibility lies in the *combination*, not in either drink alone. This is the grammatical climax: the two κοινωνία relationships (Christ / demons) cannot coexist because they are the same *kind* of participation aimed at opposite realities.
- **Grammar reference:** (candidate — δύναμαι + complementary infinitive entry)

### 8. ποτήριον Κυρίου / ποτήριον δαιμονίων — Objective vs Possessive Genitive in Parallel

- **Form:** Both phrases are bare anarthrous genitives modifying ποτήριον. Κυρίου and δαιμονίων are unmarked — no article, no preposition.
- **Significance:** The genitives are deliberately ambiguous between possessive ("the Lord's cup / the demons' cup") and objective ("the cup for/dedicated to the Lord / demons"). By using the same syntactic form for both, Paul enforces a symmetry: whatever the Lord's cup is *to* the Lord, the demons' cup is *to* the demons. The lack of article heightens the categorical force (cup-of-Lord vs cup-of-demons as two identifiable realities). Same syntax is used with τραπέζης Κυρίου / τραπέζης δαιμονίων — tables likewise set in genitive parallel.

### 9. γίνεσθαι (v20) — Present Middle/Passive Infinitive after θέλω

- **Form:** γίνεσθαι (V-PNN) of γίνομαι, G1096, as complementary infinitive to οὐ θέλω.
- **Significance:** γίνομαι ("become") rather than εἶναι ("be") — Paul's concern is the *process* of becoming κοινωνοὺς τῶν δαιμονίων. The present tense marks ongoing state; the middle/passive voice hints at something the Corinthians undergo, not simply choose. ὑμᾶς is accusative subject of the infinitive; κοινωνούς is accusative predicate (double-accusative construction with γίνομαι). This grammar reveals what Paul fears: not an act but a transformation — they would *become* partners with demons.

### 10. παραζηλοῦμεν ... ἰσχυρότεροι αὐτοῦ (v22) — Rhetorical Questions with μή + Comparative + Genitive of Comparison

- **Form:** παραζηλοῦμεν (Pres Act Ind 1Pl of παραζηλόω, G3863, "provoke to jealousy") — indicative used as rhetorical question. Then μή + ἐσμεν (indicative) — μή in a question expects "no." ἰσχυρότεροι is the comparative of ἰσχυρός (G2478, A-NPM-**C**omparative), with αὐτοῦ as genitive of comparison ("than he").
- **Significance:** The μή question is not asking for information; it presupposes the answer "no, of course not." The comparative form with genitive of comparison is classical Greek for "stronger than X." Paul is not speculating — he is stunning the Corinthians with the absurdity: if you share the demons' table, you are challenging the Lord to a contest of strength. The allusion is to Deuteronomy 32:16, 21 (LXX), where Israel provoked God by sacrificing to demons (δαιμονίοις, exactly the word Paul uses in v20). The grammar thus links 1 Cor 10:20-22 directly to the Song of Moses: the same verb (παραζηλόω / הִקְנִיא hiphil) and the same direct object (δαιμονίοις) bind the two texts.

## Clause Structure Notes

- **vv14-15** form a chiastic command-pair: continuous flight (pres impv) bracketing decisive judgment (aor impv).
- **v16** is structured as two parallel rhetorical questions (cup | bread), each following **subject-relative clause-negative particle-predicate-genitive modifier** order. The chiasm cup-blood // bread-body mirrors the Last Supper institution.
- **v17** gives the ὅτι ("because") ground: one bread ⇒ one body ⇒ unity through shared participation (ἐκ τοῦ ἑνὸς).
- **v18** introduces an analogy from Israel (κατὰ σάρκα — "ethnic Israel"): sacrifice-eaters are κοινωνοὶ of the altar.
- **vv19-20** use a diatribe structure (τί οὖν φημι; — self-questioning) to preempt objection: Paul is not conceding that idols exist, but he is asserting that the demonic is real behind pagan sacrifice.
- **v21** draws the conclusion with double οὐ δύνασθε — the grammatical climax of the unit.
- **v22** closes with a pair of rhetorical questions (ἤ / μή) that assume negative answers and push the Corinthians to recognize the gravity of their position.
