**Semantic shift in translation:** The LXX narrows H6031's broad range. Hebrew anah covers affliction, oppression, sexual violation, and cultic self-denial. The LXX splits these: ταπεινόω takes the humbling/lowering sense (the majority), while κακόω takes the mistreatment/oppression sense. The sexual violation sense and the Ecclesiastes "exercise" sense are rendered with other Greek words entirely. The net effect is that ταπεινόω in the NT carries the "humble/bring low" meaning without the darker associations of force or oppression that attach to the Hebrew.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The core meaning of deliberate lowering/humbling persists from OT to NT
- The reflexive/voluntary sense ("humble yourself") is prominent in both testaments — OT Hithpael of H6031 parallels NT middle/reflexive usage of G5013
- The exalt/humble reversal pattern appears in both: Deut 8:2-3 (God humbles then exalts) → Mat 23:12, Luk 14:11, Jas 4:10 (self-humbling leads to exaltation)

**What shifts:**
- OT usage is predominantly active/imposed (Piel: "afflict others") — ~72% of H6031 tokens; NT usage is predominantly reflexive/voluntary ("humble yourself") — the majority of G5013 occurrences
- The oppression, sexual violence, and national affliction senses of H6031 do not carry through to G5013 in the NT
- The cultic "afflict your souls" (fasting) formula becomes the broader ethical "humble yourselves before God"
- Php 2:8 (Christ "humbled himself") creates a Christological center for the word that has no direct OT counterpart — the suffering servant of Isa 53:7 uses H6031 Niphal (passive: "he was afflicted") while Php 2:8 uses the active voice (he chose to humble himself)
