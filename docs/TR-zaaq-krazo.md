## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The core semantic: an intense vocal outburst that demands response — whether from God, from a healer, or from a crowd
- The deliverance pattern: oppressed people cry → God acts. OT Judges cycle (zaʻaq → deliverer raised) reappears in blind men crying to Jesus (krazō → healing given)
- The collocation with "loud voice" (qol gadol / phōnē megalē) persists across testaments

**What shifts:**
- OT zaʻaq is predominantly human-to-God (distress prayer). NT krazō expands to include demonic shrieking, mob violence, and Spirit-enabled worship ("Abba, Father")
- OT zaʻaq addresses YHWH through covenant relationship. NT krazō addresses Jesus directly as Son of God/Son of David — applying the zaʻaq-to-YHWH pattern to Jesus
- OT zaʻaq never appears in the mouth of the divine. NT krazō is used for Jesus' own cries — teaching proclamations (John 7:28, 37) and his death-cry (Matt 27:50), a striking reversal where the one who answers the cry also makes the cry
