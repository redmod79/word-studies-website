**Semantic shift in translation:** The LXX translators consistently chose agapao (144x) over phileo (12x) for ahab, even though Hebrew ahab covers the full range from erotic love to divine covenant love. The Greek distinction between agapao (volitional, moral) and phileo (affectionate, personal) represents a narrowing: the LXX already funnels most OT love-language through agapao, establishing it as the "religious" love word before the NT writers inherit it.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The God→human direction: God loves his people (Deut 4:37 → John 3:16, Eph 2:4)
- The human→God command: "love the LORD thy God" (Deut 6:5 → Mat 22:37)
- The neighbor command: Lev 19:18 is quoted verbatim six times in the NT
- Love linked to commandment-keeping: Exo 20:6 pattern continues in John 14:15, 1 John 5:2-3

**What shifts:**
- **Scope broadens:** OT ahab includes erotic love (Song of Solomon), personal preference (Gen 25:28), and appetite for things (Gen 27:4 "savoury meat"). NT agapao concentrates on moral/relational love — the erotic and appetite senses are absent
- **Enemy love emerges:** OT "love thy neighbour" gets extended to "love your enemies" (Mat 5:44) — a usage ahab never carried
- **Reciprocal community ethic:** "Love one another" as mutual obligation among believers (John 13:34) goes beyond anything in OT ahab usage
- **The agapao/phileo distinction:** Hebrew has one primary love verb (ahab); the NT works with two, sometimes in deliberate contrast (John 21:15-17)
