## Lexicon Definition

From the same root as ἄγκος (ankos, "a bend"); an "anchor" (as crooked). A device for mooring a ship, shaped with curved flukes to grip the seabed.

## Semantic Categories

### 1. Nautical Equipment — Physical Anchoring of a Ship
- **Acts 27:29** — "they cast four anchors out of the stern, and wished for the day"
- **Acts 27:30** — "under colour as though they would have cast anchors out of the foreship"
- **Acts 27:40** — "when they had taken up the anchors, they committed themselves unto the sea"

All three literal uses describe the deployment, pretended deployment, and retrieval of anchors during Paul's shipwreck voyage. The word functions as standard nautical terminology.

### 2. Metaphor for Stability and Security
- **Hebrews 6:19** — "Which hope we have as an anchor of the soul, both sure and stedfast, and which entereth into that within the veil"

The sole metaphorical use identifies Christian hope as an anchor — something that provides security and prevents drifting. The metaphor is modified by two adjectives: ἀσφαλής (asphales, "sure/safe") and βέβαιος (bebaios, "firm/steadfast").
