## Lexicon Definition

A primitive root (compare H2583); properly, to bend or stoop in kindness to an inferior; to favor, bestow; causatively to implore (i.e. move to favor by petition):—beseech, fair, (be, find, shew) favour(-able), be (deal, give, grant (gracious(-ly), intreat, (be) merciful, have (shew) mercy (on, upon), have pity upon, pray, make supplication, very.

The core image is a superior stooping/bending in kindness toward an inferior. The active voice (Qal, Piel) expresses the act of showing favor; the reflexive (Hithpael) inverts the posture into entreaty — the inferior petitioning the superior to stoop.

## Semantic Categories

### 1. Petitionary Cry — "chânnênî" ("be gracious to me")
The signature Qal imperative `חָנֵּנִי` (Pss 4:1; 6:2; 9:13; 25:16; 26:11; 27:7; 30:10; 31:9; 41:4, 10; 51:1; 56:1; 57:1; 86:3, 16; 119:29, 58, 132; 123:3) is the most characteristic form of the word — a compact one-word prayer opening.
- **Ps 51:1** — "Have mercy upon me (channênî), O God, according to thy lovingkindness"
- **Ps 4:1** — "have mercy upon me (channênî), and hear my prayer"
- **Ps 123:3** — "Have mercy upon us (chonnênû), O LORD, have mercy upon us"

### 2. Divine Self-Revelation and Sovereign Grace
- **Exod 33:19** — "I will be gracious (chanan) to whom I will be gracious, and will shew mercy on whom I will shew mercy" — the foundational declaration of sovereign, uncompelled favor
- **Num 6:25** — "The LORD make his face shine upon thee, and be gracious (chanan) unto thee" — Aaronic benediction
- **Isa 30:18-19** — "therefore will the LORD wait, that he may be gracious unto you... he will be very gracious unto thee at the voice of thy cry"

### 3. Human Supplication to God (Hithpael)
The reflexive stem: to place oneself in the posture of seeking favor.
- **Deut 3:23** — "I besought (etchannan) the LORD at that time"
- **1 Kgs 8:33, 47** — Solomon's temple dedication: "pray and make supplication (hithchannenu) unto thee"
- **Hos 12:4** — Jacob "made supplication (yithchannen)" to the angel
- **Job 8:5** — "make thy supplication (tithchannan) to the Almighty"

### 4. Covenant-Level Divine Favor Toward a People
- **2 Kgs 13:23** — "the LORD was gracious unto them (wayyâḥon), and had compassion on them... because of his covenant with Abraham"
- **Mal 1:9** — "beseech God that he will be gracious (yechannenu) unto us"
- **Amos 5:15** — "it may be that the LORD God of hosts will be gracious (yechenan) unto the remnant of Joseph"

### 5. Human-to-Human Favor and Deliverance from Enemies
- **Gen 33:5, 11** — Jacob: "the children which God hath graciously given (chanan)... God hath dealt graciously with me (channanî)"
- **Gen 43:29** — "God be gracious (yechonka) unto thee, my son"
- **Deut 7:2** — "thou shalt smite them... thou shalt make no covenant with them, nor shew mercy (techânnêm) unto them"
- **Deut 28:50** — a nation that "shall not regard the person of the old, nor shew favour (yâchon) to the young"
- **Judg 21:22** — "be favourable unto them (chânnûnû) for our sakes"

### 6. Wisdom: Mercy to the Poor as Religious Act
Proverbs links chanan to social ethics — showing favor to the needy is reckoned as lending to YHWH.
- **Prov 14:21** — "he that hath mercy (chonên) on the poor, happy is he"
- **Prov 14:31** — "he that honoureth him hath mercy (chonên) on the poor"
- **Prov 19:17** — "He that hath pity (chonên) upon the poor lendeth unto the LORD"
- **Prov 28:8** — gathered wealth will go "for him that will pity (chonên) the poor"

### 7. Withholding of Favor — Negated chanan
- **Deut 7:2** — "thou shalt make no covenant with them, nor shew mercy (lô-techânnêm)" (the Deut 7:2 "show no mercy" clause)
- **Deut 28:50** — a nation that does not chanan
- **Isa 27:11** — "he that made them will not have mercy (lô-yechunnennû)"
- **Lam 4:16** — "they favoured (lô-chânânû) not the elders"
- **Job 19:21** — "Have pity upon me" (appeal precisely because pity is being withheld)

The negated form marks the withdrawal or refusal of grace — a judicial or punitive posture. The Deut 7:2 prohibition of chanan toward the Canaanites functions within Israel's unique conquest mandate (ḥērem), removing the ordinary covenantal posture of mercy toward outsiders in that specific context.

The LXX distributes chanan primarily between eleeō (the act of mercy) and oikteirō (the emotion of pity). NT-era usage of χάρις (G5485, charis) = "grace" corresponds more closely to Hebrew ḥēn (H2580, the noun-gift), while chanan (the verb-act) surfaces in NT largely through eleeō — as in the blind man's cry "ἐλέησόν με" (Mark 10:47), which is the direct Greek echo of the Psalter's channênî.
