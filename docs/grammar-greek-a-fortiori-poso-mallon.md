# A Fortiori Argument — πόσῳ μᾶλλον / πολλῷ μᾶλλον (Greek)

## Definition

The a fortiori ("from the stronger") argument is a rhetorical-logical structure in which an accepted lesser premise is used to establish a greater conclusion. In NT Greek, this pattern is signaled by the fixed phrase **πόσῳ μᾶλλον** ("how much more," interrogative) or **πολλῷ μᾶλλον** ("much more," declarative). Both use μᾶλλον (G3123, comparative adverb "more") modified by a dative of degree of difference — either πόσῳ (G4214, interrogative "how much?") or πολλῷ (G4183, adjective "much").

1. **Interrogative form (πόσῳ μᾶλλον)** — poses the greater conclusion as a rhetorical question expecting obvious assent: "how much more will X?"
2. **Declarative form (πολλῷ μᾶλλον)** — states the greater conclusion as a direct assertion: "much more will X"
3. **Variant forms** — πῶς οὐχὶ μᾶλλον ("how shall not rather...?"), οὐ πολλῷ μᾶλλον / οὐ πολὺ μᾶλλον (negated rhetorical question expecting "yes")

## Form Recognition

The a fortiori is a two-part structure:

**Protasis (lesser premise):** introduced by εἰ γάρ / εἰ δέ / εἰ οὖν (conditional particle + conjunction), sometimes implied from context; states what is conceded or already known.

**Apodosis (greater conclusion):** introduced by one of the following trigger phrases:

| Trigger Phrase | Morph Codes | Force | Translation |
|----------------|-------------|-------|-------------|
| πόσῳ μᾶλλον | Q-DSN + ADV | Interrogative | "how much more" |
| πόσῳ ... χείρονος | Q-DSN ... A-GSF | Interrogative-negative | "how much worse/sorer" |
| πολλῷ μᾶλλον | A-DSN + ADV | Declarative | "much more" |
| πολὺ μᾶλλον | A-ASN + ADV | Declarative | "much more/rather" |
| πῶς οὐχὶ μᾶλλον | ADV-I + PRT-I + ADV | Rhetorical question | "how shall not rather" |
| οὐ πολλῷ μᾶλλον | PRT-N + A-DSN + ADV | Negated question | "shall he not much more" |

**Grammatical note on the dative:** πόσῳ and πολλῷ are both dative singular neuter — the **dative of degree of difference** (Wallace §169), specifying the measure by which the conclusion exceeds the premise. The neuter form functions adverbially, modifying μᾶλλον.

## Contrast with Related Structures

| Structure | Marker | Function | Relationship to A Fortiori |
|-----------|--------|----------|---------------------------|
| Simple comparison | μᾶλλον alone | Comparative preference ("rather X than Y") | No conditional premise; e.g., Matt 10:28 "fear rather him who..." |
| μᾶλλον ἤ | μᾶλλον + ἤ | Explicit comparative alternative | Selects between two options, not escalating from one |
| Conditional (εἰ ... τότε) | εἰ ... τότε/οὖν | Simple logical inference | Inference without degree-escalation; no "how much more" |
| Qal wahomer (Hebrew) | None (structural) | Rabbinic a fortiori | Hebrew rhetorical equivalent; same logic, no fixed lexical marker |

The Greek a fortiori is distinguished from simple comparison by three features: (1) an explicit or implied conditional protasis stating the lesser case, (2) a degree-of-difference dative (πόσῳ or πολλῷ) establishing that the conclusion is not merely different but proportionally greater, and (3) the adverb μᾶλλον tying the two together.

## Structural Notes

**Hebrews concentration:** Five of the 22 NT instances occur in Hebrews (2:2–3; 9:13–14; 10:29; 12:9; 12:25). Four of these are negative a fortiori arguments for judgment (if lesser disobedience was punished, greater disobedience will be punished more). Only Heb 9:13–14 is a positive a fortiori (Christ's blood accomplishes more than animal blood). This pattern drives the book's paraenetic structure: the superiority of Christ's priesthood entails proportionally greater accountability.

**Pauline concentration in Romans 5:** Four instances in Rom 5:9–17 form a rhetorical cascade, each building on the previous: justified → saved from wrath (v.9), reconciled → saved by his life (v.10), offense → grace abounded (v.15), death reigned → reign in life (v.17). The repeated πολλῷ μᾶλλον creates cumulative rhetorical force.

**2 Corinthians 3 triplet:** Three consecutive a fortiori arguments (vv.8, 9, 11) establish the superiority of the new-covenant ministry over the old in three dimensions: Spirit vs. letter, righteousness vs. condemnation, permanent vs. transient.
