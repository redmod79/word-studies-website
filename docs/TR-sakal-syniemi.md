G4920 συνίημι draws most heavily from H995 bîyn ("discern, understand"), but also significantly
from H7919 śākal. Both Hebrew roots are about understanding — bîyn emphasizes *separating/
discerning*, śākal emphasizes *circumspect synthesis*. The LXX merges them into one Greek verb.

**Semantic continuity:** Both śākal and syníēmi carry the idea of mental synthesis — "putting
together" what one sees/hears into understanding. The LXX translation is accurate and natural.

**Psa 14:2 / Rom 3:11 link:** The Hebrew śākal ("did understand, seek God") becomes syníēmi in
the LXX, which Paul then quotes in Romans 3 in his indictment of universal sin. The Hiphil force
of śākal ("act with discernment") survives in the Greek — it's not just mental apprehension but
the active response of seeking God.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same:**
- The core meaning of synthetic, active understanding — grasping truth and responding to it
- The link between understanding and seeking/obeying God (Psa 14:2 → Rom 3:11)
- The divine dimension: understanding is given by God, not self-generated (Dan 9:22 → Eph 5:17)

**What shifts:**
- Hebrew śākal has a strong outcome-component ("prosper, succeed") that Greek syníēmi lacks; the
  NT never uses syníēmi with the "prosperity" semantic
- The "maskil" title dimension (skilled teacher, Dan 11:33) is not carried into NT syníēmi usage
- NT syníēmi is used in parable contexts (Mat 13) with a new emphasis on *concealment* — those
  who don't understand are judicially hardened, a theme absent from Hebrew śākal
