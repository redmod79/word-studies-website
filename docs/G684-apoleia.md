## Lexicon Definition

From Strong's: "Ruin or loss (physical, spiritual, or eternal)." Built on G622 ἀπόλλυμι, the verb of destruction/ruin/loss. The noun denotes (1) the **act or state of ruin** (destruction, wastage); (2) the **condition of loss** (perishing, being lost); and (3) the **destiny or outcome of ruin** (perdition, final destruction). In the NT it is overwhelmingly a negative-eschatological term, contrasted with σωτηρία (salvation) and ζωή (life).

### 2. "Son of Perdition" — Destiny-Genitive Construction (ὁ υἱὸς τῆς ἀπωλείας)
Only two NT uses of this exact construction:
- **John 17:12** — "none of them is lost, but the son of perdition (ho huios tēs apōleias); that the scripture might be fulfilled" (of Judas)
- **2 Thess 2:3** — "that man of sin be revealed, the son of perdition (ho huios tēs apōleias)" (of the eschatological opponent)

The genitive τῆς ἀπωλείας functions as a **Semitic "son of" idiom of destiny/character** (cf. Hebrew *ben-māwet* "son of death" 1 Sam 20:31; *ben-bᵉliyyaʿal* "son of Belial"): the person is characterized by and destined for apōleia. It does not say *what caused* the perdition — it labels the person as belonging to that end.

### 3. The Beast's End — "Goeth into Perdition"

## Destiny-Genitive Semantics (Grammar Note)

The construction **υἱὸς τῆς ἀπωλείας** ("son of apōleia") is a Semitic-influenced genitive of **destiny/characterization**, not origin. Compare:
- τέκνα ὀργῆς — "children of wrath" (Eph 2:3) — destined for wrath
- υἱοὶ τῆς ἀπειθείας — "sons of disobedience" (Eph 2:2, 5:6)
- υἱοὶ φωτός — "sons of light" (1 Thess 5:5)

In these constructions the governing noun's genitive names the **sphere, character, or destined outcome** of the person. ὁ υἱὸς τῆς ἀπωλείας therefore means "the one whose character and destiny are apōleia" — not "the one produced by destruction" but "the one belonging to / destined for destruction." The definite article (ὁ … τῆς) in both John 17:12 and 2 Thess 2:3 makes the designation a quasi-title rather than a generic description.

The same grammatical pattern with εἰς + accusative (εἰς ἀπώλειαν — Acts 8:20; Rom 9:22 "fitted to [destruction]"; Heb 10:39 "unto perdition") also carries a destiny-directional sense: heading toward / bound for apōleia.
