## Semantic Categories

### 1. Divine Radiance / Theophanic Brightness (Exo 24:10)

> "And they saw the God of Israel: and there was under his feet as it were a paved work of a sapphire stone, and as it were the body of heaven in his **clearness**." (Exo 24:10)

**Hebrew:** וּכְעֶצֶם הַשָּׁמַיִם לָטֹהַר — literally "and as the substance/bone of the heavens for brightness/purity"

**Context:** Moses, Aaron, Nadab, Abihu, and seventy elders of Israel ascend Sinai and see God. The text describes the pavement beneath God's feet using two similes: (1) like a work of sapphire stone, and (2) like the very substance of the sky in its brightness. The preposition לָ (la) + טֹהַר functions as "in/for its clearness/brightness" — describing the quality of radiant clarity.

**Parsing (BHSA):** טֹהַר — Noun.ms.Abs, lemma טהר, gloss "purity"

**Significance:** This is the premiere use of tohar and it deploys the word's literal sense: visual radiance, crystalline clarity. The sky at its most luminous serves as the comparison for the divine presence. The sapphire pavement + sky-brightness imagery communicates transcendent, unapproachable brilliance.

### 2. Royal Splendor / Glory (Psa 89:44 [MT 89:45])

> "Thou hast made his **glory** to cease, and cast his throne down to the ground." (Psa 89:44)

**Hebrew:** הִשְׁבַּתָּ מִטְּהָרוֹ — literally "Thou hast caused to cease from his brightness/splendor"

**Context:** Psalm 89 is a royal covenant psalm (Maschil of Ethan the Ezrahite). Verses 1-37 celebrate God's covenant with David; verses 38-51 lament its apparent failure. In v. 44, the psalmist accuses God of extinguishing the king's tohar — his brightness, his splendor, his radiating glory. The word is paired with the casting down of the throne, forming a parallelism: brightness ceases / throne falls.

**Parsing (BHSA):** מִטְּהָרוֹ — Preposition מִן + Adj.ms.Abs + 3ms suffix (BHSA tags it adjectival, but the form is the noun tohar with prefix and pronominal suffix: "from his brightness")

**Significance:** Here tohar describes royal splendor — the visible radiance associated with the anointed king's dignity and authority. The Hiphil of shabbat (cause to cease) treats tohar as something that was actively shining and has now been extinguished. This parallels the imagery of Exo 24:10 but transfers the concept of radiance from the divine theophany to the Davidic king, consistent with the king's role as God's representative.

### 3. Ceremonial Purification Period (Lev 12:4, 12:6 — if assigned to H2892)

## Key Verses with Full Analysis

### Exodus 24:10
> "And they saw the God of Israel: and there was under his feet as it were a paved work of a sapphire stone, and as it were the body of heaven in his clearness."

This is the defining passage for tohar. The word describes the most intense form of brightness available in human experience — the clear sky itself. The context is a theophany: the elders of Israel are permitted to see God, and the text describes the dazzling, crystalline quality of the divine pavement. The choice of tohar rather than zohar (fiery brilliance) or nogah (outward radiance) emphasizes purity of light rather than intensity — the brightness is clear, transparent, uncontaminated.

**Cross-reference:** Ezekiel 1:26 uses sapphire imagery similarly ("the likeness of a throne, as the appearance of a sapphire stone") but pairs it with zohar (H2096) for the surrounding radiance (Eze 1:27-28). This suggests a semantic distinction: tohar = the clarity of the medium (sky-like transparency), zohar = the intensity of the glow (fire-like brilliance).

### Psalm 89:44 (MT 89:45)
> "Thou hast made his glory to cease, and cast his throne down to the ground."

The psalmist laments that God has extinguished the Davidic king's tohar. The parallel structure (glory ceases / throne falls) treats tohar as the visible marker of royal authority — the brightness that signifies God's favor on the anointed king. When the king is defeated and humiliated, his tohar is quenched. The use of shabbat (Hiphil, "cause to cease") implies tohar was an active, ongoing radiance — not a static attribute but a living splendor that God sustains or withdraws.
