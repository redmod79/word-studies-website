## Lexicon Definition

A primitive root; properly, to cut off, i.e. (figuratively) to decree. KJV: "determine."

The physical root meaning is **cutting** — severing a portion from a whole. The figurative extension to "decree" or "determine" follows the same semantic path as several other Hebrew "cut" verbs (see Easily Confused Words below): an authoritative decision *cuts off* or *marks out* a defined portion from an undefined whole.

## Semantic Categories

### 1. Decreeing / Allotting a Fixed Period
- **Dan 9:24** — "Seventy weeks **are determined** upon thy people and upon thy holy city, to finish the transgression, and to make an end of sins, and to make reconciliation for iniquity, and to bring in everlasting righteousness, and to seal up the vision and prophecy, and to anoint the most Holy."

The cutting metaphor conveys precision: a specific quantity (70 weeks) is *cut off* from the larger flow of time and assigned a definite purpose. The Niphal stem indicates divine passive — God is the unstated agent who decrees.

Several Hebrew verbs share the semantic field of "cutting → decreeing/determining":

| גָּזַר (gâzar) | H1504 | "decree," "cut off" (13x) | Means "to cut, divide" — broader usage including physical cutting (1 Kgs 3:25) and being cut off from community (Isa 53:8). |
| כָּרַת (kârath) | H3772 | "cut off" (288x) | The dominant "cut off" verb — used for covenant-making ("cut a covenant"), physical cutting, and divine judgment. Also in Dan 9:26 ("Messiah shall be cut off"). |

**Note:** חָתַם (châtham, H2856) — "to seal" — is phonetically similar to châthak and appears in the same verse (Dan 9:24, "seal up the vision"). Despite the similar consonants (חתך vs. חתם), the roots are unrelated: châthak = cut off; châtham = close up, seal.
