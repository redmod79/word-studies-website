## Lexicon Definition

From σκάνδαλον (the trigger-stick of a trap); literally, to cause someone to stumble over a snare-stick. Figuratively: to entrap, to trip up, to set a stumbling-block in the way; in the passive, to be caught, to take offense, to be caused to fall (morally, from faith, or into sin). Unique to Hellenistic Jewish and Christian Greek — absent from classical Greek, coined in the LXX tradition.

## Semantic Categories

### 1. Causing a Member of One's Own Body to "Offend" — the Self-Amputation Sayings
Six uses govern one's own body parts (eye, hand, foot) as the subject that "offends" — the hyperbolic call to radical self-denial.

- **Matt 5:29** — "if thy right eye offend thee, pluck it out"
- **Matt 5:30** — "if thy right hand offend thee, cut it off"
- **Matt 18:8** — "if thy hand or thy foot offend thee, cut them off"
- **Mark 9:43, 45, 47** — parallel foot/eye/hand sayings

### 2. Causing "Little Ones" Who Believe to Stumble
The most severe warnings in the NT attach to this verb: a millstone-drowning is better than the alternative.

- **Matt 18:6** — "whoso shall offend one of these little ones... it were better for him that a millstone were hanged about his neck"
- **Mark 9:42** — parallel
- **Luke 17:2** — parallel

### 3. Taking Offense at Jesus' Person or Teaching
People "are offended" by Jesus — his origin, his claims, his hard sayings. This is the passive-voice usage: to be tripped up by him.

- **Matt 11:6 / Luke 7:23** — "blessed is he, whosoever shall not be offended in me"
- **Matt 13:57 / Mark 6:3** — "they were offended in him" (Nazareth)
- **Matt 15:12** — "the Pharisees were offended, after they heard this saying"
- **John 6:61** — "Doth this offend you?" (hard bread-of-life discourse)

### 4. Falling Away Under Pressure (Apostasy)
The passive takes on an eschatological edge: to be "offended" is to fall from faith under tribulation or persecution.

- **Matt 13:21 / Mark 4:17** — parable of the sower: "when tribulation or persecution ariseth... he is offended"
- **Matt 24:10** — "then shall many be offended, and shall betray one another"
- **Matt 26:31, 33 / Mark 14:27, 29** — "All ye shall be offended because of me this night"
- **John 16:1** — "These things have I spoken unto you, that ye should not be offended"

### 5. Giving Occasion of Scandal Through Use of Liberty (Paul)
Paul's two uses are distinct: not receiving offense, but *causing* a weaker brother to stumble through one's own eating/liberty.

- **Rom 14:21** — "[it is] good neither to eat flesh, nor to drink wine, nor [any thing] whereby thy brother stumbleth, or is offended, or is made weak"
- **1 Cor 8:13** — "if meat make my brother to offend, I will eat no flesh while the world standeth"

### 6. Empathetic Suffering (2 Corinthians)
One distinctive use: Paul identifies with those who are "offended."

- **2 Cor 11:29** — "who is offended, and I burn not?"

### 7. Avoiding Offense by Accommodation
One use concerns avoiding needless offense — paying the temple tax.

- **Matt 17:27** — "lest we should offend them, go thou to the sea, and cast an hook..."

## Pauline Context — Rom 14:13, 20, 21 and 1 Cor 8:9, 13

Though this study remains context-neutral, the target cluster deserves note: Paul uses the verb σκανδαλίζω *only* in Rom 14:21 and 1 Cor 8:13, and in both he pairs or neighbors it with the cognate noun πρόσκομμα (Rom 14:13, 20; 1 Cor 8:9). In these two chapters the σκανδαλ-/προσκομμ- field is used to frame the ethics of Christian liberty toward a "weaker brother." The verb in Paul's usage is causative and transitive ("make my brother to offend"), not the Synoptic passive "be offended at Christ." This is a distinct Pauline sub-usage within the overall semantic range.
