**Semantic shift in translation:** The LXX splits pânîym's dual function: it uses πρόσωπον for the *face/presence* senses, and Greek spatial prepositions (ἔναντι, ἔμπροσθεν) for the prepositional *before/in front of* senses. This narrows πρόσωπον to the concrete face/presence domain, while losing pânîym's prepositional breadth. The phrase *liphnê* (before the face of) is typically rendered with the preposition + genitive rather than πρόσωπον. The divine presence idiom is preserved: "my face/presence" → πρόσωπόν μου.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The fundamental meaning of *face/presence* as relational orientation — to be before God's face is to be in his presence
- The devotional pattern: seeking the face (Ps 27:8 → Rev 22:4)
- The judicial formula "respect of persons" (*nāśāʼ pânîym*) carried directly into NT Greek as προσωποληψία
- The OT theophany of Moses' face (Exo 34) is explicitly interpreted in 2 Cor 3 via the same word chain

**What shifts:**
- The LXX and NT *never* use πρόσωπον as a preposition — the prepositional function of pânîym (before/in front of) is absorbed by ἔναντι and ἔμπροσθεν; πρόσωπον retains only the noun senses
- In the NT, πρόσωπον can refer to the "outward appearance" (Gal 2:6; 2 Cor 5:12) in a mildly pejorative sense not present in Hebrew pânîym
- The full divine-presence theology of pânîym is concentrated in NT eschatology (Rev 22:4) and Christology (2 Cor 4:6 — "the face of Jesus Christ")
