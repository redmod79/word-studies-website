**Semantic shift in translation:** The LXX collapses Hebrew's distinction between the dispute brought (*riyb*) and the verdict rendered (*mishpat*) into a single Greek term (krisis). Where Hebrew uses *riyb* for the contention and *mishpat* for the judicial decision, krisis covers both the process and the outcome. This merger carries into NT usage where krisis encompasses both God's lawsuit against humanity and the sentence pronounced.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The forensic/judicial framework persists: both *riyb* and krisis operate within a courtroom metaphor where parties contend, evidence is weighed, and a verdict follows
- God as both plaintiff and judge: OT *riyb YHWH* (the LORD's controversy) corresponds to NT krisis as divine judgment
- The advocacy pattern: OT "plead my cause" (riyb) maps onto NT vindication at the krisis

**What shifts:**
- Hebrew distinguishes the dispute (*riyb*) from the verdict (*mishpat*); Greek krisis absorbs both
- OT *riyb* frequently describes this-worldly legal proceedings between humans; NT krisis shifts predominantly toward eschatological final judgment
- The prophetic covenant-lawsuit genre (*riyb* pattern in Hosea, Micah) has no direct NT structural equivalent, though the concept of divine prosecution continues in passages like Jude and Revelation
