## Lexicon Definition

From α- (as a negative particle) and a derivative of μετατίθημι: "unchangeable, immutable, not able to be moved/transferred." The word belongs to a cluster of alpha-privative verbal adjectives in Hebrews used to describe the fixed, final, irrevocable character of God's work and word (cf. ἀσάλευτος "immovable" Heb 12:28; ἀπαράβατος "unchangeable" Heb 7:24; ἀκατάλυτος "indissoluble" Heb 7:16). Literally: "not-transferrable," i.e., not subject to being set aside, withdrawn, or altered.

## Semantic Categories

With only two tokens, both in a single paragraph (Heb 6:13–20), the word functions in one tightly integrated argument. The two uses differ in grammatical role, not in meaning.

### 1. Abstract / Substantival — "immutability" as a quality of God's purpose
- **Heb 6:17** — "God, willing more abundantly to shew unto the heirs of promise the **immutability** (τὸ ἀμετάθετον) of his counsel (τῆς βουλῆς αὐτοῦ), confirmed [it] by an oath"
  - The neuter adjective with article functions as an abstract noun: "the unchangeableness." It predicates a property of God's βουλή (counsel, deliberate purpose).

### 2. Attributive / Concrete — "immutable things" that guarantee the promise
- **Heb 6:18** — "That by two **immutable** things (διὰ δύο πραγμάτων ἀμεταθέτων), in which [it was] impossible for God to lie, we might have a strong consolation"
  - Agreeing with πραγμάτων ("things, matters, realities"). The "two" are identified by context as (1) God's **promise** (v. 13, to Abraham) and (2) God's **oath** (v. 13, 17 — sworn by Himself, v. 13). Each is independently unalterable; together they form the unshakeable ground of the believer's hope.

## Hebrew Source

No direct Hebrew source via LXX: G276 does not occur in the canonical LXX. (The word is attested in classical and Hellenistic Greek — e.g., 3 Macc 5:1, 12 uses *ametathetos* of a king's fixed decree — and the Hebrews author draws on this juridical-decree background, not an OT lexeme.) The conceptual OT parallel is אֱמֶת (*ʾemet*, H571 — firmness, faithfulness) and the oath-formula "by myself have I sworn" (Gen 22:16, quoted in Heb 6:13–14), where the Hebrew root שׁבע (*shaba*) + the self-referential guarantee carries what Hebrews crystallizes in *ametathetos.*
