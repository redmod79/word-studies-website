# Grammar Analysis: Ezekiel 29:11-13 — Egypt's Forty-Year Desolation

## Summary

Ezekiel 29:11-13 predicts a forty-year desolation of Egypt using **plain šānāh** ("year") grammar — no day-for-year formula, no apocalyptic framing, no symbolic decoding. This is a standard prophetic oracle of judgment in ordinary calendrical time.

The exegetical weight: **Ezekiel is the very prophet who explicitly declared the yôm laššānāh hermeneutic at Ezek 4:6.** Yet twenty-five chapters later, describing a different kind of prophecy (geopolitical judgment on Egypt, not sign-act symbolism), he uses the same word (šānāh) in its plain literal sense. The same prophet operates both hermeneutics, and the text itself signals which one is in force by genre and explicit declaration — not by the mere presence of a time-number.

## Key Grammatical Features

### 1. אַרְבָּעִ֥ים שָׁנָֽה — The Plain Time-Expression (all three verses)

**Form:** `אַרְבָּעִים` (cardinal number, mp abs) + `שָׁנָה` (Noun.fs.Abs) — numeral + absolute noun = standard Biblical Hebrew cardinal time construction ("forty years"). Used identically in all three verses.

**Significance:** The noun שָׁנָה (šānāh, "year") appears in the **absolute** state, not in construct with any symbolic noun. There is no qualifier like "day for a year" (yôm laššānāh), no apocalyptic sign-context, no vision, no prophetic act being decoded. This is calendrical Hebrew: forty literal solar/lunar years of calendar time.

**Contrast with Ezek 4:5-6:** In the sign-act of bearing iniquity, Ezekiel is commanded to lie on his side for specific day-counts, and YHWH *explicitly declares*: "I have appointed thee each day for a year" (יוֹם לַשָּׁנָה, Ezek 4:6). Without that explicit declaration, days would not convert to years. The formula is **overt, not implicit.**

**Companion passage:** [ezk-4-4-6.md](ezk-4-4-6.md) — the explicit yôm laššānāh declaration by the same prophet.

### 2. מִקֵּ֞ץ אַרְבָּעִ֤ים שָׁנָה֙ — Construct Chain "At the End of Forty Years" (v.13)

**Form:** Preposition `מִן` + `קֵץ` (Noun.ms.Cst, "end of") + `אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה` ("forty years"). The construct-chain analysis confirms:
```
קֵץ (end)    — CONSTRUCT
אַרְבָּעִים    — ABSOLUTE
שָׁנָה        — ABSOLUTE
```
This is the classic "from the end of X years" temporal construction (cf. Gen 16:3; 2 Sam 15:7; Jer 34:14).

**Significance:** The phrase `miqqēṣ ʾarbāʿîm šānāh` is a **terminus** marker — "when forty years have elapsed." The preposition `min` + `qēṣ` fixes a point at the completion of the specified duration. This is the same quantitative construction used throughout prose narrative for real elapsed time (e.g., "at the end of forty days" in the Flood, Gen 8:6). It is not apocalyptic language; it is chronographic.

**Word study:** [H7093-qets.md](../../word-studies/H7093-qets.md) — qēṣ as temporal terminus.

### 3. Clause Typology: Prose-Oracle, Not Vision (Domain Q across all verses)

**Form:** The clause analyzer tags every clause in vv.11-13 with domain **Q** (direct speech / quotation — YHWH's oracle) and uses standard prose-clause types:
- v.11: `xYqX` / `WXYq` / `WxY0` — negated yiqtol predictions
- v.12: `WQt0` × 2 / `WXYq` — weqatal chains ("and I will give... and I will scatter...")
- v.13: `xQtX` (messenger formula) / `xYq0` (main oracle yiqtol) / `xQt0` (relative clause)

**Significance:** This is **oracular prose**, not visionary-symbolic discourse. The weqatal chains (`וְנָתַתִּ֣י` → `וַהֲפִצֹתִ֤י` → `וְֽזֵרִיתִ֖ים`) are the standard predictive-future backbone of prophetic speech — the same pattern Jeremiah uses for the seventy years (Jer 25:11). The predicted events are concrete geopolitical actions: desolation of land, scattering of population, eventual regathering.

Contrast Ezek 4: a **sign-act narrative** (Ezekiel physically lies on his side), framed by explicit symbolic decoding (`yôm laššānāh nĕtattîw lāk`). Genre markers are categorically different.

**Grammar reference:** [syntax-wx-qatal-circumstantial.md](../hebrew/syntax-wx-qatal-circumstantial.md) (contrast with the predictive weqatal chain here, which is mainline, not circumstantial).

---

### 4. Weqatal Predictive Chain (v.12)

**Form:** Three weqatal 1cs verbs in sequence:
- וְנָתַתִּ֣י (Qal perf 1s, weqatal) — "and I will make"
- וַהֲפִצֹתִ֤י (Hiphil perf 1s, weqatal) — "and I will disperse"
- וְֽזֵרִיתִ֖ים (Piel perf 1s + 3mp suff, weqatal) — "and I will scatter them"

**Significance:** This is the classic YHWH-first-person future-predictive weqatal chain used throughout the prophets for announcements of divine action. Same grammar as Jer 25:9-12 (Babylon / seventy years). The time-expression `אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה` is embedded as a **[Time]** phrase modifying the middle predicate `תִּֽהְיֶ֣ינָ שְׁמָמָ֔ה` ("they shall be desolate"), identified as such by the clause-analyzer. A time-adverbial to a concrete prose verb is a literal duration, not a cipher.

---

### 5. Pual/Hophal Participles: נְשַׁמּוֹת and מָחֳרָבוֹת (v.12)

**Form:**
- `נְשַׁמֹּ֗ות` — Niphal participle fp abs of שׁמם (be desolate): "being [already] desolate"
- `מָֽחֳרָבֹות֙` — Hophal participle fp abs of חרב (be laid waste): "being [already] laid waste"

**Significance:** These passive/stative participles describe **concurrent historical conditions** — Egypt will join the ranks of *presently*-desolate lands and *presently*-ruined cities (i.e., the other nations Nebuchadnezzar devastated). This is a grounded geopolitical comparison, not a cosmic or apocalyptic image. Further evidence the prophecy is operating in real-world historical time.

**Grammar reference:** [stem-niphal.md](../hebrew/stem-niphal.md), [stem-hiphil.md](../hebrew/stem-hiphil.md) (Hophal is the passive of Hiphil).

## Clause Structure Summary

**v.11 (3 clauses):**
1. `xYqX` — "No foot of man shall pass in it" (subject-last, negated yiqtol)
2. `WXYq` — "And foot of beast shall not pass in it" (parallel)
3. `WxY0` — "And it shall not be inhabited forty years" — **[Time]: אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה**

**v.12 (4 clauses):**
1. `WQt0` — "And I will make the land of Egypt a desolation in the midst of desolate lands"
2. `WXYq` — "And her cities among laid-waste cities shall be a desolation forty years" — **[Time]: אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה**
3. `WQt0` — "And I will disperse Egypt among the nations"
4. `WQt0` — "And I will scatter them in the lands"

**v.13 (3 clauses):**
1. `xQtX` — "For thus says Lord YHWH" (messenger formula)
2. `xYq0` — "**From the end of forty years** I will gather Egypt from the peoples" — **[Adju]: מִקֵּץ אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה**
3. `xQt0` — "whither they have been dispersed" (relative)

In all three verses, `אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה` functions as a bare temporal adverbial modifier on a concrete verb of historical action (inhabit / be desolate / gather). No symbolic frame, no sign-act, no vision.

## The Day-Year Question

**Ezekiel is the one prophet in the OT who explicitly declared yôm laššānāh** (Ezek 4:6). If the day-year hermeneutic were a general principle of prophetic time-language, we would expect Ezekiel above all others to deploy it freely. He does not.

Within Ezekiel himself:

| Passage | Time expression | Genre | Hermeneutic |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ezek 4:5-6 | 390 days / 40 days | Sign-act (symbolic lying on side) | Day-for-year **explicitly declared** |
| Ezek 29:11-13 | 40 years | Prose oracle (geopolitical judgment) | **Plain years** (no conversion formula) |

The same word (šānāh) occurs in both contexts. The same prophet wrote both. The difference is not lexical — it is **genre + declared hermeneutic**. Ezek 4 decodes days into years; Ezek 29 uses years straight.

### Implication for the broader "day-year" question

Ezek 29:11-13 is a **control case**: a non-Danielic, non-apocalyptic time-prophecy in literal years, authored by the very prophet who gave us yôm laššānāh. It demonstrates that:

1. The day-year hermeneutic is **not** triggered by the mere presence of a number + time-word.
2. It requires an **explicit textual marker** (sign-act + decoding formula, as in Num 14:34 and Ezek 4:6).
3. Prophets — including Ezekiel — freely use literal-year grammar in standard oracular prose.
4. Any systematic application of day-for-year to non-apocalyptic time-prophecies (outside Daniel / Revelation) must answer why Ezek 29 escapes the conversion but, say, a 1260/2300/70-weeks number would not.

### Historical fulfillment (briefly)

Traditionally correlated with Nebuchadnezzar's invasion of Egypt (ca. 568/567 BC — attested in fragmentary cuneiform BM 33041) and the 40-year span of political eclipse / Persian dominance that followed. Whether one accepts a precise literal fulfillment or reads the 40 as a standard biblical-round judgment-duration (cf. 40 years in the wilderness, 40 days of flood-rain), **the grammar itself is unambiguously literal-year grammar.** Fulfillment debates are about history; the parsing is clean.

## Bottom Line

**Ezek 29:11-13 uses plain calendrical years.** The grammar is שָׁנָה absolute, embedded as [Time]/[Adju] on concrete prose predicates within weqatal-driven oracle-prose, with no symbolic decoding formula present. Same word šānāh, same prophet — but a categorically different genre from Ezek 4:5-6. The presence of a number-plus-time-word does **not** by itself invoke day-for-year; only the explicit yôm laššānāh formula does. This is a robust control case for the broader day-year hermeneutic question.
