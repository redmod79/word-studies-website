## Lexicon Definition

*From Thayer's / Strong's (reconstructed):* From μετά (after, denoting change) and the middle voice of μέλω (to care, be concerned). Literally "to care afterwards." To feel regret, remorse, or sorrow for something already done (or not done); to change one's feeling or concern about a prior act. The word describes an emotional/affective response — the pang of having cared about the wrong thing, or not cared enough — rather than a settled change of mind or direction.

*Etymology and register:* Unlike μετανοέω (G3340), which is fundamentally cognitive/volitional ("to think differently afterward"), μεταμέλλομαι is fundamentally affective ("to care differently afterward"). It can describe repentance that leads to transformation (Matt 21:29, the son who changed his mind and went), remorse that produces no reformation (Matt 27:3, Judas), or a passing regret that is itself repented of (2 Cor 7:8, Paul's feeling about his letter). Of God it denotes the opposite: the absence of any such change of feeling concerning his oath (Heb 7:21).

## Semantic Categories

### 1. Genuine Regret That Leads to Right Action (Positive)
- **Matt 21:29** — "He answered and said, I will not: but afterward he repented (metamellomai), and went." (The obedient son in the parable — regret produced reversal of conduct.)

### 2. Refusal to Regret (Negative — Unbelief)
- **Matt 21:32** — "ye, when ye had seen it, repented not (ou metemelēthēte) afterward, that ye might believe him." (The religious leaders, unlike the publicans and harlots, did not even feel the pang of regret that would open them to John's message.)

### 3. Remorse Without Transformation (Judas)
- **Matt 27:3** — "Then Judas, which had betrayed him, when he saw that he was condemned, repented himself (metamelētheis), and brought again the thirty pieces of silver." (The archetypal case: deep affective remorse, restitution of the money, confession — yet followed by despair and suicide, not by restoration. Matthew deliberately chooses metamellomai, not metanoeō, for Judas.)

### 4. Pastoral Regret Concerning Sorrow Caused
- **2 Cor 7:8** — "For though I made you sorry with a letter, I do not repent (ou metamelomai), though I did repent (ei kai metemelomēn): for I perceive that the same epistle hath made you sorry, though it were but for a season." (Paul's emotional oscillation about his severe letter — he *had* felt regret, then ceased to regret once he saw the godly sorrow it produced. Here metamellomai describes the fluctuating pastor's heart, not repentance from sin.)

### 5. Divine Unchangeableness of Oath
- **Heb 7:21** — "the Lord sware and will not repent (ou metamelēthēsetai), Thou art a priest for ever after the order of Melchisedec" (quoting Ps 110:4 LXX). (Of God: he will not experience the "afterward-caring" — no subsequent change of concern, no shift of affection or purpose regarding the Son's priesthood. The point is not that God cannot change his mind in general but that this particular sworn commitment is immune to any future regret.)

Whereas metanoeō in LXX covers both nâcham (emotional) and, less often, shûwb (directional), metamellomai is almost exclusively tied to the affective/relenting nuance of nâcham. This is why Heb 7:21 uses metamellomai specifically: the question is whether God's *concern/stance* concerning his sworn oath will change — and the emphatic answer is no.
