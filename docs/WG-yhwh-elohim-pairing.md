# WG: YHWH–Elohim Pairing and Alternation

## Overview

The Hebrew Bible names its God by several distinct terms that are neither interchangeable nor mutually exclusive. The two dominant names — **YHWH** (H3068, the covenantal proper name) and **Elohim** (H430, the generic/majestic noun "God") — stand in a complementary relationship: every biblical writer knows they designate the *same* Deity, yet the choice between them, their pairing ("YHWH Elohim"), and their alternation across parallel texts is a **pervasive, documentable theological pattern** rather than random variation. Around these two names cluster the older term **El** (H410), the rare/poetic **Eloah** (H433), and the honorific title **Adonai** (H136) — the last of which the Masoretes made the oral substitute for the Tetragrammaton itself.

This entry synthesizes data already derived in the underlying Strong's entries (see Cross-References below) and maps the **pairing logic** — when the names appear together, when they alternate, and what that alternation means.

## Relationships

### Shared Morphological Root

- **H410 אֵל (El) → H433 אֱלוֹהַּ (Eloah) → H430 אֱלֹהִים (Elohim)**
  El is the archaic shortened form; Eloah is the prolonged (emphatic) singular; Elohim is the masculine plural of Eloah/El used as a "plural of majesty" with singular agreement when denoting the true God. These three form a single cognate family around the Semitic root for "strength / mighty one."

- **H3068 יְהֹוָה (YHWH)** stands apart morphologically — a proper noun derived from the verb **הָיָה** (H1961, "to be / become"), self-exegeted in Exo 3:14 as *ʾehyeh ʾăsher ʾehyeh* ("I AM THAT I AM").

- **H136 אֲדֹנָי (Adonai)** is lexically unrelated (< H113 אָדוֹן "lord/master") but functionally bound to YHWH by the Masoretic *qere* tradition.

### LXX Overlaps

| Hebrew | Primary Greek | Notes |
|--------|---------------|-------|
| YHWH (H3068) | κύριος (*kyrios*) | ~6,000× — LXX renders YHWH by the same word used for Adonai |
| Adonai (H136) | κύριος | The LXX conflates what Hebrew distinguishes — both become "Lord" |
| Elohim (H430) | θεός (*theos*) | Dominant mapping; singular or plural per context |
| El (H410) | θεός / ἰσχυρός | Usually θεός; occasionally ἰσχυρός ("mighty one") |
| Eloah (H433) | θεός / κύριος | Mostly θεός in Job |

**Consequence:** the Greek NT inherits a vocabulary in which κύριος and θεός are the routine divine designations — meaning that whenever NT writers apply κύριος to Jesus (e.g., Phi 2:11, Rom 10:9–13), they are using the LXX equivalent of YHWH. The pairing pattern of the Hebrew Bible is not visible in Greek translation; it must be read back from the Hebrew.

**Pattern:** the names enter scripture *in a deliberate narrative sequence*. Creation → covenantal pairing → invocation → patriarchal epiphany → Mosaic programmatic revelation. Exo 6:3 is the hinge: the name YHWH was *used* by the patriarchs in the Genesis narrative (the narrator's voice), but its covenantal self-disclosure to the nation Israel occurs with Moses.

## Documented Alternation Patterns

### 1. Genesis 1 vs Genesis 2–3 — The Classical Source-Critical Contrast

| Passage | Divine Name | Approximate Count |
|---------|-------------|-------------------|
| Gen 1:1 – 2:3 (first creation account) | **Elohim** alone | 32 occurrences of "God" in Gen 1; YHWH absent |
| Gen 2:4 – 3:24 (garden narrative) | **YHWH Elohim** (compound) | 20 occurrences of "LORD God" in Gen 2–3 |
| Gen 4+ | YHWH alone predominates | — |

- **Gen 1:1 – 2:3** uses *Elohim* exclusively — fitting a cosmic, universal, transcendent portrait: God speaks and it is so.
- **Gen 2:4 – 3:24** uses the hybrid *YHWH Elohim* — functioning as a **theological equals sign**: the creator Elohim of ch. 1 *is* the covenantal YHWH of what follows. The narrative then tightens toward human intimacy (forming from dust, breathing life, walking in the garden, calling to the man).
- **Gen 4+** drops *Elohim* from the compound and uses *YHWH* freely, now that the identification has been established.

The 228 total KJV occurrences of "LORD God" concentrate sharply in Gen 2–3 (20×) and Exo 9:30 onward as a literary/covenantal marker; the compound is *not* a routine phrase but a **signaling device**.

### 2. Elohistic Psalter (Psa 42–83) vs the Rest of the Psalter

Counting bare occurrences of "LORD" and "God" in the KJV text:

| Section | "LORD" (YHWH) | "God" (Elohim) | Ratio |
|---------|---------------|-----------------|-------|
| **Psa 42–83 (Elohistic)** | **40** | **272** | ~1 : 6.8 (Elohim dominates) |
| **Rest of Psalter** (1–41, 84–150) | **689** | **156** | ~4.4 : 1 (YHWH dominates) |

- The **Elohistic Psalter** (a term coined by modern scholarship for Pss 42–83, bounded by the doxologies of Pss 41:13, 72:18–19, 89:52) shows a striking and statistically undeniable preference for Elohim over YHWH.
- The clearest evidence is **Psa 14 // Psa 53**, two near-identical psalms where Psa 14 (outside the Elohistic block) uses YHWH four times and Psa 53 (inside the block) systematically substitutes Elohim. The editor/redactor of the Elohistic collection has **replaced the covenantal name with the generic name** while leaving the text otherwise intact.
- Parallel editorial replacements occur between Psa 40:13–17 and Psa 70 (same phenomenon).
- The theological effect: Elohim frames the God of Israel as the universal God of all nations — appropriate to exilic or diaspora liturgical use where the covenant name was avoided.

### 3. Chronicles vs Samuel–Kings — Parallel-Passage Name Swaps

Chronicles reworks material from Samuel–Kings and shows characteristic divine-name editorial adjustments:

| Samuel–Kings | Chronicles | Swap |
|--------------|------------|------|
| **2 Sam 24:1** — "the anger of *the LORD (YHWH)* was kindled against Israel, and he moved David against them" | **1 Chr 21:1** — "*Satan* stood up against Israel, and provoked David" | Attribution shifted from YHWH to Satan |
| **2 Sam 6:2** — "ark of God, whose name is called by the name of *the LORD of hosts (YHWH Tsevaot)*" | **1 Chr 13:6** — "ark of God *the LORD (YHWH)*" (shorter form) | Condensation of the compound |
| Frequent — Samuel/Kings YHWH | Chronicles often inserts "Elohim" / "our God" | Chronicler's elevated cultic vocabulary |

**Chronicler's pattern:** more frequent use of *Elohim* (and *YHWH Elohim* compounds), more use of "the God of Israel," avoidance of anthropomorphic attributions to YHWH, and increased cultic/priestly vocabulary. The parallel between Samuel–Kings and Chronicles is thus a **controlled experiment in divine-name usage** — same stories, different name choices, revealing the Chronicler's theological editing.

### 4. Job — The Eloah/El/Shaddai Preference

Job is the outlier of the OT books: **YHWH** appears almost entirely in the prose frame (chs. 1–2, 38–42); the poetic dialogues use **El** (H410), **Eloah** (H433, 41× of its 62 total), and **Shaddai**. The dialogue speakers are non-Israelites (Job, Eliphaz, Bildad, Zophar, Elihu), and the author has them speak in the older / more universal divine-name register. The frame narrator knows this is YHWH; the characters speak of Eloah/El.

### 5. Daniel — Aramaic Elah + Hebrew Elohim

Daniel is the second major YHWH-sparse book: YHWH appears only in ch. 9 (Daniel's Hebrew prayer, 7×). The Aramaic chapters (2–7) use **Elah** (H426, Aramaic cognate of Eloah), and the Hebrew chapters otherwise use Elohim. This is conditioned by Daniel's diaspora setting and bilingual composition.

## Pattern Observations

### Elohim — the Generic / Creative / Universal Pole

- Opens Genesis as the creator of heaven and earth — addressing cosmos, not covenant.
- Dominates Job (of Eloah/El) and the Elohistic Psalter — contexts that universalize God.
- Plural form with singular agreement = "plural of majesty" / "plural of fullness" (GKC §124).
- Used freely of pagan deities (e.g., Dagon, Chemosh), human judges (Exo 21:6), and angels — because it is a **generic class-term** for deity/divine-being that YHWH fills supremely.
- Shared across Semitic religion; not distinctive to Israel.

### YHWH — the Personal / Covenantal / Redemptive Pole

- Enters as the God-of-the-garden (Gen 2–3), the God-of-the-patriarchs (Gen 12+), the God-of-the-Exodus (Exo 3, 6).
- Saturates Deuteronomy (45.7 per 100 verses) and Jeremiah (45.0) — the two great covenant-name books.
- The name Israel is to "call upon" (Gen 4:26; Joel 2:32) and to which all other nations will ultimately submit (Isa 45:23).
- Self-defined in Exo 3:14 via *ehyeh* — "I AM" — as the self-existent one.
- Distinctive to Israel; unknown as a name of other Semitic deities (Moabite Mesha Stele references YHWH *of Israel*).

### YHWH Elohim — the Compound as Identity Claim

The pairing *YHWH Elohim* is **not** a mere stylistic variation. It is a theological equals-sign: **the covenantal YHWH of Israel is none other than the universal creator Elohim**. The compound is deployed at critical redactional hinges:

- **Gen 2:4** — opening the garden narrative, identifying the creator of Gen 1 with the YHWH of the patriarchs.
- **Exo 9:30** — Moses to Pharaoh, asserting YHWH's universal claim.
- **1–2 Chronicles** — the Chronicler's preferred cultic formula.
- **Prophetic formulas** — "Thus saith the Lord GOD" (Adonai YHWH) plays a similar identifying role in Ezekiel.

### Adonai — the Oral Bridge

Adonai (H136) is morphologically unrelated to either YHWH or Elohim, but becomes the **spoken substitute** for YHWH in post-exilic Judaism. When the written text has אֲדֹנָי יהוה (Adonai YHWH), the Masoretes pointed the second word with the vowels of Elohim to cue the reader to say "Adonai Elohim" — producing the KJV rendering **"Lord GOD"** (small-caps GOD) in 302 occurrences. This is the ultimate fusion: the reader utters the generic class-name (Elohim) to avoid pronouncing the proper name (YHWH), while the written text presents the honorific (Adonai) in tandem with both.

### The Three Names as Triangulation

A useful summary:

- **Elohim** answers *what* God is (deity, creator, majestic).
- **YHWH** answers *who* God is (I AM, covenantally named).
- **Adonai** answers *how* God is addressed (my Lord, reverentially).

The biblical authors triangulate these — sometimes distinguishing them for emphasis (Gen 1 Elohim / Gen 2–3 YHWH Elohim), sometimes pairing them to identify (YHWH Elohim), sometimes substituting one for another for editorial reasons (Elohistic Psalter) — always assuming the reader understands they name the same Deity through different lenses.

## Key Passages Where Multiple Group Words Appear Together

- **Gen 2:4** — YHWH + Elohim (first compound, creator-covenant identification)
- **Gen 15:2** — Adonai + YHWH (Abraham's first lament; Adonai YHWH compound inaugurated)
- **Exo 3:14–15** — Ehyeh (I AM) + YHWH + Elohim ("YHWH God of your fathers" — self-exegesis of the name)
- **Exo 6:2–3** — YHWH + Elohim + El Shaddai + YHWH (programmatic: "I am YHWH; I appeared as El Shaddai but was not known by my name YHWH")
- **Deut 6:4** — "YHWH our Elohim, YHWH is one" (the Shema — covenantal unity formula)
- **Psa 14 // Psa 53** — YHWH in one version, Elohim in the other (editorial swap demonstrated)
- **1 Kings 18:21, 39** — YHWH vs Ba'al as competing Elohim ("YHWH, he is the Elohim")
- **Amo 3:7** — Adonai YHWH ("the Lord GOD will do nothing but he revealeth...")
- **Isa 12:2; 26:4** — JEHOVAH preserved, paired with YHWH ("in the LORD JEHOVAH is everlasting strength")
- **Joel 2:32** — "whosoever shall call on the name of YHWH shall be delivered" (picks up Gen 4:26; cited of Jesus at Acts 2:21, Rom 10:13)
