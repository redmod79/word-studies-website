# G238 — ἀλληγορέω (allēgoréō)

## Core Data
- **Strong's:** G238
- **Original:** ἀλληγορέω
- **Transliteration:** allēgoréō
- **Pronunciation:** al-lay-gor-eh'-o
- **Part of Speech:** Verb
- **Root:** Compound of ἄλλος (állos, G243 — "other") + ἀγορεύω (agoreúō — "to speak in the assembly/marketplace"); literally "to speak otherwise" — i.e., to say one thing while meaning another.
- **Occurrences:** 1 in NT (hapax legomenon)
- **First Occurrence:** Galatians 4:24 — "Which things are an allegory"
- **Hapax Legomenon:** YES — this verb appears only once in the entire NT. It is also absent from the LXX.

## Lexicon Definition

From ἄλλος (another) and ἀγορεύω agoreúō (to harangue [compare ἀγορά, marketplace]); **to allegorize** — "be an allegory" (KJV rendering of the periphrastic participle ἀλληγορούμενα).

Strong's glosses it simply as "to allegorize." Louw-Nida defines it as "to speak allegorically, to employ an analogy, to use a likeness" — i.e., to present a narrative whose elements stand for something beyond their literal surface sense.

## Semantic Categories

Because the word is a hapax, "semantic range" reduces to the single attested usage plus its classical/Hellenistic-Jewish lexical background. Both are documented here to keep the entry context-neutral.

### 1. The Single Attested Biblical Use — Typological Correspondence (Paul)
Paul applies the verb to the Abraham/Sarah/Hagar narrative, declaring that the historical persons and events **are** something beyond themselves — they correspond to the two covenants. The passive participle ἀλληγορούμενα ("things being spoken allegorically") treats the OT narrative itself as the speaker; Paul is uncovering, not inventing, the correspondence.

- **Gal 4:24** — "Which things are an allegory (ἅτινά ἐστιν ἀλληγορούμενα): for these are the two covenants; the one from the mount Sinai, which gendereth to bondage, which is Agar."

### 2. Lexical Background — "To Speak Otherwise"
The verb is a transparent compound: ἄλλος ("other") + ἀγορεύω ("to speak publicly"). In classical and Hellenistic Greek it denotes speech in which the surface sense points to a deeper or different meaning. Philo of Alexandria uses this vocabulary extensively for his allegorical reading of the Pentateuch; Josephus uses it for figurative speech generally. This is the cultural milieu of Paul's single deployment.
