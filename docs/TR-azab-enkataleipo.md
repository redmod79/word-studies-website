## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The core meaning of relational abandonment carries through intact. In both testaments, enkataleipo/azab describes the severing of a personal bond — God forsaking his people, a disciple forsaking his mentor (2 Tim 4:10), or the sufferer's experience of being abandoned by God.
- The covenantal framework persists: forsaking is consistently tied to covenant violation in both OT prophetic usage and NT ethical exhortation (Heb 10:25 — not forsaking the assembly).

**What shifts:**
- The OT overwhelmingly uses azab for corporate/national covenant failure (Israel forsaking YHWH). The NT applies enkataleipo primarily to individual experience — Christ's cry, Paul's personal abandonment by Demas, individual believers not forsaking the assembly.
- The Psa 22:1 quotation at the crucifixion transforms the word from lament into christological event. What was a psalmist's complaint becomes the theological center of the atonement narrative.
- The OT "promise not to forsake" (Deut 31:6, Josh 1:5) reappears in Heb 13:5 but using a different Greek construction (οὐ μή σε ἀνῶ οὐδ' οὐ μή σε ἐγκαταλίπω), demonstrating the continuity of the azab → enkataleipo chain even in free quotation.
