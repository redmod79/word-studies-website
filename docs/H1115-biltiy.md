## Lexicon Definition

Constructive feminine of H1086 (equivalent to בַּל, *bal*); properly, *a failure of*, i.e. (used only as a negative particle, usually with a prepositional prefix) **not, except, without, unless, besides, because not, until**. KJV renders: because un(satiable), beside, but, continual, except, from, lest, neither, no more, none, not, nothing, save, that no, without.

Almost always attached to a preposition (typically לְ־ *l-*, forming *lᵉbiltî*) and followed by an infinitive construct. This is the standard Hebrew construction for **negative purpose/result clauses** ("so that…not," "to the end that…not") and for **exceptive/restrictive clauses** ("except," "save," "unless").

## Semantic Categories

Seven functional patterns emerge from the 107 verses. The particle has no lexical content of its own — it marks the *syntactic relation* (negation, purpose, exception, restriction) between clauses or constituents.

### 1. Negative Purpose Clause: "so that…not / to the end that…not" (*lᵉbiltî* + infinitive)
The dominant pattern (~40% of uses). God or a speaker acts with the intent of preventing an outcome.

- **Gen 4:15** — "lest any finding him should kill him" (*lᵉbiltî hakkôt ʼōtô*)
- **Gen 19:21** — "that I will **not** overthrow this city" — Yahweh's concessionary purpose toward Lot
- **Exo 8:22** — "that no swarms of flies shall be there"
- **Ezek 20:9, 14, 22** — "that it should not be polluted before the heathen" (triple repetition marking Yahweh's self-restraint in judgment)

### 2. Prohibition / Command Negation ("that…not")
Negating an action someone is obligated to avoid. Frequently appears in covenant stipulations and sworn undertakings.

- **Gen 3:11** — "whereof I commanded thee that thou shouldest **not** eat"
- **Deut 4:21** — "that I should **not** go over Jordan"
- **Jer 35:8–9, 14** — the Rechabites' vow "to drink no wine all our days"
- **Dan 9:11** — "by departing, that they might **not** obey thy voice"

### 3. Exceptive: "except / save / unless / only" (restrictive exception)
Introduces a single item excluded from a prior negation — the particle marks the exception.

- **Num 11:6** — "there is nothing at all, **beside** this manna"
- **Num 32:12** — "**Save** Caleb the son of Jephunneh… and Joshua"
- **Jos 11:19** — "There was not a city that made peace with the children of Israel, **save** the Hivites"
- **Amos 3:3** — "Can two walk together, **except** they be agreed?"

### 4. Privative: "without" (absence / lack)
Marks the absence of an accompanying thing or condition.

- **Deut 8:11** — "in **not** keeping his commandments"
- **Job 42:8** — "**lest** I deal with you after your folly"
- **Hos 8:7** (contextual) — negation of outcome
- **Jer 2:15; Ezek 14:15** (contextual pattern)

### 5. Absolute Negation of Existence: "none / no more / nothing"
Standalone nominal negation, often with a following noun or in construct.

- **1 Sam 2:2** — "there is **none** holy as the LORD: for there is **none beside thee**"
- **Hos 13:4** — "thou shalt know no god but me: for there is **no** saviour beside me"
- **Isa 14:6** — "he who smote the people… with a **continual** stroke" (lit. "without ceasing")
- **Job 14:12** — "till the heavens be **no more**"

### 6. Consecutive / Result Clause ("so that…not / that they shall not")
Marks result rather than intent. Common in prophetic judgment language.

- **Jer 23:14** — "that none doth return from his wickedness"
- **Ezek 13:3** — prophets who follow their own spirit "and have seen **nothing**"
- **Jer 51:62** — "to cut it off, **that none** shall remain in it"

### 7. Accommodation / Concession Language (key Torah instances)
A minority use where *lᵉbiltî*-clauses register divine forbearance, conceded exception, or restrained judgment rather than absolute prohibition.

- **Gen 19:21** — "I have accepted thee… that I will **not** overthrow" (concession to Lot's request)
- **Gen 38:9** — Onan's contrivance "**that** he should **not** give seed" (narrated action, not command)
- **Exo 20:20** — "that his fear may be before you, **that** ye sin **not**" (purpose-of-theophany, disciplinary intent)
- **Deut 17:20** — "that he turn **not** aside from the commandment" (permissive-king statute framed by negative purpose)

This is the nuance behind the "accommodation-language particle" characterization: *lᵉbiltî* marks the *direction* of a concession or accommodation (the thing being prevented or excluded), without asserting that the prevented thing would be inherently good or bad. In Torah legal material the particle appears in both apodictic prohibition (Gen 3:11; Lev 18:30) and concessionary statute (Deut 17:20), so the particle by itself does not determine whether the governing verb concedes, commands, or forbids — context does.

## Notes on Accommodation-Language Assessment

When evaluating whether a Torah clause expresses divine *accommodation*, *concession*, or *permission* versus absolute *command* or *prohibition*, the presence of *biltî/lᵉbiltî* is **not a sufficient marker**. The particle appears across the full range:

- **Absolute prohibition:** Gen 3:11; Lev 18:30; Num 14:16; Deut 17:12
- **Sworn/covenantal undertaking:** Deut 4:21; Jer 35:8–9
- **Concessionary limitation of judgment:** Gen 19:21; Ezek 20:9, 14, 22
- **Purpose-of-theophany (disciplinary):** Exo 20:20
- **Permissive statute with guardrail:** Deut 17:20 (king must "turn not aside")

What *biltî* consistently does is mark the **negative pole** of a purposive or exceptive relation. It tells the reader "this is what is being prevented / excluded / failed-of." Whether the governing clause is command, concession, or accommodation must be determined from the speech-act verbs, modal framing, and narrative voice — not from the particle itself.
