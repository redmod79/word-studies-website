# Grammar Analysis: Psalm 82:1–6

## Overview

Psalm 82 is the single most exegetically load-bearing passage for the grammatical question of *whether the Hebrew plural אֱלֹהִים (elohim, H430) necessarily denotes the one God of Israel, or whether it is a flexible, applicable-by-context title*. Within six verses the psalm applies אֱלֹהִים three distinct ways:

1. **v.1a** — אֱלֹהִים as **singular subject** of a singular participle (נִצָּב *niṣṣāḇ*, ms.) = "God [one] stands";
2. **v.1b** — אֲדַת־**אֵל** ("the congregation of **El**," H410) with a different divine noun as the setting;
3. **v.1c** — בְּקֶרֶב אֱלֹהִים *yišpōṭ* ("in the midst of **elohim** he judges") with אֱלֹהִים now denoting the plural of the ones being judged;
4. **v.6** — אֱלֹהִים אַתֶּם ("**elohim** [are] you [plural]") — a verbless predicate-nominal clause in which אֱלֹהִים is predicated of the 2mp addressees ("you are elohim / gods"), paired with בְּנֵי עֶלְיוֹן כֻּלְּכֶם ("sons of the Most High, all of you").

This is the passage Jesus cites in John 10:34–36 (quoting LXX Ps 81:6, ἐγὼ εἶπα θεοί ἐστε) as his Scriptural ground for the proposition that calling someone "God" (θεός) is not inherently blasphemy *when Scripture itself uses the term of another referent*. The grammar therefore bears directly on:

- Whether the *morphological* plural of אֱלֹהִים carries numerical/ontological plurality.
- Whether אֱלֹהִים is flexible enough in Hebrew to denote non-YHWH beings (members of a divine council, human judges, or ideal-type magistrates).
- What it means that the very same form is used (a) of the judging God, (b) of the judged assembly, and (c) of the 2mp addressees within one pericope — with singular verb agreement in v.1 and plural predicative use in v.6.

This file documents the grammar only. The *interpretation* of who the אֱלֹהִים addressed in v.6 are (angelic divine council members; Israelite judges; Israel-at-Sinai; typological rulers) is downstream of the grammar and is left to theological/exegetical work.

## Clause Structure (BHSA)

### Psalm 82:1

- **Clause 1** — `NmCl` (nominal clause, the superscription):
  `[PreC] מִזְמוֹר לְאָסָף` — "A psalm of / belonging to Asaph."
- **Clause 2** — `Ptcp` (participial clause, domain Q = quotation/poetic):
  - `[Subj]` **אֱלֹהִים** (noun.mp.Abs)
  - `[PreC]` **נִצָּב** (Niphal.Ptcp.ms.Abs — *masculine singular*)
  - `[Cmpl]` בַּעֲדַת־אֵל
  - Literal: "Elohim [is] standing in the congregation of El."
  - The agreement between an *mp.Abs* subject and a *ms* participle is the classic "plural-of-majesty / plural-of-excellence" pattern (cf. Gen 1:1 אֱלֹהִים בָּרָא 3ms Qal.Perf.). A **singular verb / participle** agrees with *aleh-im* when the referent is the one God of Israel.
- **Clause 3** — `xYq0` (non-initial yiqtol clause):
  - `[Cmpl]` בְּקֶרֶב אֱלֹהִים
  - `[Pred]` **יִשְׁפֹּט** (Qal.Impf.3ms — again, *masculine singular*)
  - Literal: "In the midst of elohim he judges."
  - The *ms* predicate here is governed by the same subject as Clause 2 (אֱלֹהִים = the God-judge). The second אֱלֹהִים in the *Cmpl* phrase is the **plural subject of judgment** (the ones being judged), not a second instance of the divine subject.

### Psalm 82:6

- **Clause 1** — `XQtl` (fronted X + qatal):
  - `[Subj]` אֲנִי
  - `[Pred]` אָמַרְתִּי
  - Literal: "*I* — [it is I who] said."
- **Clause 2** — `NmCl` (verbless predicate-nominal clause — THE KEY CLAUSE):
  - `[PreC]` **אֱלֹהִים**
  - `[Subj]` אַתֶּם (2mp pronoun)
  - Literal: "elohim [are] you [pl.]."
- **Clause 3** — `NmCl` (parallel verbless NmCl):
  - `[Conj]` וּ
  - `[PreC]` בְּנֵי עֶלְיוֹן
  - `[Subj]` כֻּלְּכֶם
  - Literal: "and sons of Elyon [are] all of you."

The **fronted predicate** (אֱלֹהִים before אַתֶּם) is the normal word order for predicate-nominal identification in Hebrew (cf. Exo 3:14 type, Ezek 28:2 אֵל אָנִי "God [am] I," Isa 43:10 ff.). The predicate position carries emphasis: "*elohim* is what you are."

## Construct Chains

### Psalm 82:1

- **עֲדַת־אֵל** = "congregation / assembly of El"
  - `Cst` עֲדַת (Noun.fs.Cst from עֵדָה, "congregation/assembly")
  - `Abs` אֵל (Noun.ms.Abs, H410)
  - The *nomen rectum* (אֵל) definitizes the whole chain by its proper-name-like semantic force. In OT Hebrew, עֲדַת־X ("the assembly of X") typically takes a named or proper reference: עֲדַת יִשְׂרָאֵל, עֲדַת קֹרַח, עֲדַת יְהוָה. "Congregation of El" is thus an *assembly belonging to / presided over by El* — the divine council venue (cf. Psa 89:7 *sôd qedôšîm*; 1 Kgs 22:19 *ṣeḇāʾ haššāmayim*; Job 1:6; 2:1 *benê hāʾelōhîm*).
- **קֶרֶב אֱלֹהִים** = "midst of elohim"
  - `Cst` קֶרֶב (Noun.ms.Cst, "midst/interior")
  - `Abs` אֱלֹהִים (Noun.mp.Abs, H430)
  - Here the *nomen rectum* is the *plural absolute* אֱלֹהִים. Grammatically this is the plural-referent usage: "in the midst of [the] elohim" = in the midst of a plural group.

### Psalm 82:6

- **בְּנֵי עֶלְיוֹן** = "sons of Elyon"
  - `Cst` בְּנֵי (Noun.mp.Cst from בֵּן, H1121)
  - `Abs` עֶלְיוֹן (Adj.ms.Abs substantivized, H5945 — "Most High" as divine title)
  - Structural parallel: Psa 29:1 בְּנֵי אֵלִים; Psa 89:7 בִּבְנֵי אֵלִים; Deu 32:8 (DSS/LXX: בְּנֵי אֱלֹהִים / υἱῶν θεοῦ); Job 1:6; 2:1; 38:7.
  - The "sons of X" idiom in Hebrew is a *qualitative/member-of-class* genitive — members of the divine/heavenly class presided over by Elyon.

## Key Grammatical Features

### 1. Singular verb-agreement with plural-morphology אֱלֹהִים (v.1a, 1c)

- **Form:** אֱלֹהִים (Noun.mp.Abs) + נִצָּב (Niphal.Ptcp.**ms**.Abs) + יִשְׁפֹּט (Qal.Impf.**3ms**).
- **Significance:** Hebrew standardly uses *singular* verbs/adjectives/participles with אֱלֹהִים *when the referent is the one God of Israel* and *plural* agreement when the referent is actually plural beings (cf. Gen 20:13 הִתְעוּ אֹתִי אֱלֹהִים "elohim caused me to wander" — 3mp hiphil of תעה in Abraham's speech to a foreigner; Gen 35:7 נִגְלוּ אֵלָיו הָאֱלֹהִים "elohim revealed themselves to him" — 3mp niphal). The singular agreement at v.1 *grammatically identifies* the first and third אֱלֹהִים in v.1 as the divine subject-judge. No Trinitarian argument runs on plurality-of-form here; the grammar forces singular-referent reading when singular agreement obtains.
- **Grammar reference:** See [ehyeh-1cs-imperfect](../hebrew/ehyeh-1cs-imperfect.md) for divine-speech 1cs; pattern of singular agreement with אֱלֹהִים is a lexicographic standard (BDB s.v. אֱלֹהִים 1; HALOT; Joüon–Muraoka §136d; GKC §145h — "pluralis majestatis / intensivus").

### 2. Niphal participle נִצָּב (v.1a)

- **Form:** *niṣṣāḇ*, Niphal masculine-singular participle of נצב ("stand, take one's stand, station oneself").
- **Significance:** The Niphal of נצב is tolerative/stative-reflexive — "he has taken his stand / stations himself." The participle presents the act as ongoing/present — God *is standing* in the divine council. The scene is judicial-forensic: the judge takes his stand. Compare Isa 3:13 נִצָּב לָרִיב יְהוָה ("YHWH is standing to contend") — same Niphal participle, same forensic register.
- **Grammar reference:** [stem-niphal](../hebrew/stem-niphal.md) for the full range of Niphal functions (passive / middle / reflexive / tolerative / stative).

### 3. Verbless predicate-nominal clause אֱלֹהִים אַתֶּם (v.6)

- **Form:** Zero-copula NmCl — `[PreC] אֱלֹהִים` + `[Subj] אַתֶּם` (2mp).
- **Significance:** This is the key grammatical datum for John 10:34–36. The clause is:
  - **predicate-first** (marked word order — predicate emphasis),
  - **verbless** (zero-copula, present-state equation),
  - **2mp addressee** — addressed to a plural "you."
  - With אֱלֹהִים *predicated of* 2mp pronominal subject, the form cannot function as "plural of majesty" for the one God, since the subject (אַתֶּם) is itself plural. Grammatically, אֱלֹהִים here *must* denote a class-noun predicate — "elohim [= gods / divine-class-members]" applied to the plural addressees.
  - This directly parallels Ezek 28:9 אֱלֹהִים אָנִי ("elohim [am] I") attributed to the king of Tyre as a claim, and Ezek 28:2 אֵל אָנִי ("El [am] I") — also predicate-first NmCl. The grammar makes clear that in Hebrew, *being identified as elohim* is a predicate role that can be asserted of non-YHWH referents, whether truly (v.6 by divine fiat) or falsely (Ezek 28 as blasphemous claim).
- **Grammar reference:** [symbol-decoding-predicate-nominal](../hebrew/symbol-decoding-predicate-nominal.md) for verbless NmCl identification constructions; [isa-14-ezek-28-self-deification](isa-14-ezek-28-self-deification.md) for the predicate-first אֱלֹהִים/אֵל NmCl pattern applied to non-YHWH claimants.

### 4. 1cs qatal with emphatic pronoun: אֲנִי־אָמַרְתִּי (v.6)

- **Form:** `[Subj]` אֲנִי (1cs independent pronoun) + `[Pred]` אָמַרְתִּי (Qal.Perf.1s of אמר). XQtl construction (fronted subject pronoun + qatal).
- **Significance:** The independent pronoun is grammatically unnecessary (the 1cs perfect already encodes the subject); its presence marks **emphasis or contrast** — "*I myself* said…" or "*I* [as opposed to you] said." This highlights that the status of being called *elohim* is a **divine speech-act** — something YHWH himself declared, not something the addressees claimed for themselves. The verse thus encodes: the *designation* is YHWH's; the *responsibility* is theirs. The perfective aspect אָמַרְתִּי ("I said / I have said") references a prior speech-event (constitutive declaration) whose content is now being recalled.
- **Grammar reference:** See [syntax-wx-qatal-circumstantial](../hebrew/syntax-wx-qatal-circumstantial.md) for fronted-X + qatal pragmatics; GKC §142a on emphatic-pronoun + finite verb.

### 5. Construct chain עֲדַת־אֵל with a *different* divine noun (v.1b)

- **Form:** `Cst` עֲדַת + `Abs` אֵל (H410). Note that אֵל is a distinct noun from אֱלֹהִים — it is singular, non-plural-of-majesty, and carries an ancient West-Semitic / Ugaritic resonance as the name of the patriarchal high-god who presides over the divine council (*ʾilu / ʾil*).
- **Significance:** The psalm deliberately varies its divine vocabulary within one verse: אֱלֹהִים (Subj) → עֲדַת־**אֵל** (Cmpl) → קֶרֶב **אֱלֹהִים** (Cmpl). This is not random lexical variation. אֵל here evokes the divine-council setting language of OT theophany/enthronement poetry (cf. Psa 29:1 *benê ʾēlîm*; Psa 89:7 *ʾēl*). The identity of the אֵל whose assembly this is, versus the אֱלֹהִים who stands judge, versus the אֱלֹהִים being judged, is a **referential** (not grammatical) question — each use is grammatically well-formed. The grammar supplies three distinct *syntactic roles* for three distinct *referents* sharing divine vocabulary.
- **Word study:** [H410-el](../../word-studies/H410-el.md).

### 6. Imperatives of judicial-administrative responsibility (vv. 3–4)

- **Forms:** שִׁפְטוּ (Qal.Impv.2mp of שפט); הַצְדִּיקוּ (Hiphil.Impv.2mp of צדק — "cause-to-be-righteous / vindicate"); פַּלְּטוּ (Piel.Impv.2mp of פלט — "rescue"); הַצִּילוּ (Hiphil.Impv.2mp of נצל — "deliver").
- **Significance:** Four consecutive 2mp imperatives explicitly address the אֱלֹהִים-addressees as having **judicial duties toward human social classes** — דַל (poor), יָתוֹם (orphan), עָנִי (afflicted), רָשׁ (impoverished), אֶבְיוֹן (needy). These are the standard categories of Israelite case-law for protected parties (Exo 22:21–27; Deu 24:17–21; Isa 1:17). The stem-choices carry force:
  - **Hiphil הַצְדִּיקוּ** = *declare-righteous* (causative/declarative) — judicial verdict.
  - **Piel פַּלְּטוּ** = *intensively rescue* / set free — active intervention.
  - **Hiphil הַצִּילוּ** = *cause-to-be-delivered* — extraction from hand-of-wicked.
- This imperative sequence grammatically presupposes that the addressees **exercise judicial function** — which constrains but does not decide the identification question. *Whoever* the אֱלֹהִים of v.6 are, the psalm is addressing them as having a task that includes deciding cases in court. This makes some readings (e.g., "angels addressed as elohim") harder and others ("judges / magistrates") more natural — but that is an interpretive move, not a grammatical demand.
- **Grammar reference:** [hiphil-imperfect](../hebrew/hiphil-imperfect.md) for the declarative-estimative function of Hiphil (הַצְדִּיקוּ = "declare just"); [qal-imperfect](../hebrew/qal-imperfect.md) for imperative paradigm; word studies for [H8199-shaphat](../../word-studies/H8199-shaphat.md).

### 7. "Sons of Elyon" construct chain (v.6b)

- **Form:** בְּנֵי עֶלְיוֹן — Noun.mp.Cst + Adj.ms.Abs (substantivized divine title).
- **Significance:** The "sons of X" construct is Hebrew's standard member-of-class idiom. בְּנֵי עֶלְיוֹן is structurally parallel to:
  - בְּנֵי אֵלִים (Psa 29:1; 89:7) — "sons of the Els / heavenly beings"
  - בְּנֵי אֱלֹהִים (Deu 32:8 in LXX/4QDeutj; Job 1:6; 2:1; 38:7) — "sons of God" / members of the divine council
  - בְּנֵי הָאֱלֹהִים (Gen 6:2, 4) — "sons of God"
- The genitive עֶלְיוֹן ("Most High," H5945) is itself a divine title used sometimes independently (Num 24:16; Deu 32:8; Psa 9:3; 18:14; Isa 14:14). The construct בְּנֵי עֶלְיוֹן therefore places the v.6 addressees *in the same class as* the *bĕnê ʾēlōhîm* of Genesis 6, Deuteronomy 32, and Job 1–2 — **grammatically**. Whether they are **identical** with those beings or merely share the class designation is an interpretive/referential question.
- **Word study:** [H1121-ben](../../word-studies/H1121-ben.md) on the qualitative genitive idiom.

## Inventory: The Three Syntactic Roles of אֱלֹהִים in Psalm 82:1–6

| Occurrence | Verse | Syntactic Role | Agreement | Referent (grammatically constrained) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 82:1a | Subject of participial NmCl | singular (נִצָּב ms) | singular being — the judging God |
| 2 | 82:1c | Abs in construct chain קֶרֶב אֱלֹהִים | n/a (governed by Cst) | plural group — the ones in whose midst judgment occurs |
| 3 | 82:6 | Predicate of verbless NmCl | (predicate of 2mp Subj) | plural addressees — the ones being called elohim |

Additionally: **אֵל (H410)** appears once (v.1b) as the Abs of construct עֲדַת־אֵל, denoting the presiding deity whose assembly this is. **עֶלְיוֹן (H5945)** appears once (v.6b) as Abs of construct בְּנֵי עֶלְיוֹן, denoting the parent of the addressees.

The psalm thus uses **three distinct divine nouns** (אֱלֹהִים, אֵל, עֶלְיוֹן) to mark **three distinct roles** in a single judicial-council scene:

- **אֵל** = host/presider of the council venue;
- **אֱלֹהִים (sg-referent)** = the standing-judge who enters and speaks;
- **אֱלֹהִים (pl-referent)** = the addressed members being evaluated;
- **עֶלְיוֹן** = the parent/source of the class identity of those members.

Depending on interpretation, אֵל, אֱלֹהִים (sg), and עֶלְיוֹן may refer to the **same** being (YHWH), or to **distinguishable** roles within a single divine identity, or to **different** beings. The grammar does not force any of the three readings.

## The Grammar / NT Interface: John 10:34–36

John 10:34–36 (NA28 / TR):

> ἀπεκρίθη αὐτοῖς ὁ Ἰησοῦς· οὐκ ἔστιν γεγραμμένον ἐν τῷ νόμῳ ὑμῶν ὅτι ἐγὼ εἶπα· θεοί ἐστε; εἰ ἐκείνους εἶπεν θεοὺς πρὸς οὓς ὁ λόγος τοῦ θεοῦ ἐγένετο, καὶ οὐ δύναται λυθῆναι ἡ γραφή, ὃν ὁ πατὴρ ἡγίασεν καὶ ἀπέστειλεν εἰς τὸν κόσμον, ὑμεῖς λέγετε ὅτι βλασφημεῖς, ὅτι εἶπον· υἱὸς τοῦ θεοῦ εἰμι;

Jesus' citation "ἐγὼ εἶπα θεοί ἐστε" is a **word-for-word LXX quotation** of Ps 81:6 LXX (= MT 82:6):

- MT: אֲנִי־אָמַרְתִּי אֱלֹהִים אַתֶּם
- LXX: Ἐγὼ εἶπα· Θεοί ἐστε
- Jesus: ἐγὼ εἶπα· θεοί ἐστε

The LXX has rendered the **predicate-first verbless NmCl** of Hebrew (`[PreC] אֱלֹהִים [Subj] אַתֶּם`) with the **predicate-first nominal clause** of Greek (`[PreC] θεοί [Subj/Cop] ἐστε`), using the present-indicative 2pl of εἰμί as the copula. Greek requires an explicit copula where Hebrew does not; otherwise the syntactic structure is preserved.

Jesus' argument-form (*a fortiori* / *qal-wa-ḥōmer*):

- Premise 1: Scripture (τὸ γεγραμμένον, "what stands written") *itself* applies θεοί to "those to whom the word of God came" (πρὸς οὓς ὁ λόγος τοῦ θεοῦ ἐγένετο) — a description invoking either prophetic reception, Sinai-revelation recipients (Israel), or the divine-council recipients;
- Premise 2: Scripture cannot be broken (οὐ δύναται λυθῆναι ἡ γραφή) — the grammatical fact cannot be gainsaid;
- Conclusion: Therefore, calling the one whom the Father sanctified and sent "Son of God" (υἱὸς τοῦ θεοῦ) is not *per se* blasphemy.

The grammar of Psa 82:6 is the load-bearing evidence. If אֱלֹהִים אַתֶּם (applied to a 2mp human/creaturely addressee) *is legitimate Scripture* (and grammatically it plainly is — verbless NmCl, predicate-first, 2mp subject), then the term θεός / אֱלֹהִים is not a locked single-referent term in Hebrew or Greek. The plural-form morphology is *not* what makes אֱלֹהִים a "God"-word; **context** and **agreement** do.

## Summary: What the Grammar Does and Does Not Decide

**What the grammar does decide:**

1. אֱלֹהִים in v.1a and v.1c (as subject of ms verb/participle) refers to a *single* being.
2. אֱלֹהִים in v.6 (predicate of 2mp NmCl) refers to *plural* addressees being predicatively called "elohim / gods."
3. The morphological plural form of אֱלֹהִים is **not** a reliable indicator of numerical/ontological plurality — agreement controls reference.
4. אֱלֹהִים **can** be predicated of non-YHWH beings within inspired Hebrew Scripture without grammatical irregularity; the NmCl at 82:6 is morphologically unremarkable.
5. The psalm uses **three distinct divine nouns** (אֱלֹהִים, אֵל, עֶלְיוֹן) in distinct syntactic roles within a single pericope.
6. The addressees of v.6 are grammatically commanded (vv.3–4) to perform **judicial duties** toward human protected-parties.
7. Jesus' LXX-faithful citation of v.6 in John 10:34 preserves the Hebrew predicate-first NmCl and presupposes the unbreakability of this grammatical fact.

**What the grammar does not decide:**

1. The *identity* of the אֱלֹהִים-addressees of v.6 — divine-council members / angels / human judges / Israel-at-Sinai / typological rulers are all consistent with the grammar.
2. Whether the אֵל of v.1b and the אֱלֹהִים-subject of v.1a and the עֶלְיוֹן of v.6b are the **same** being (common Christian / Jewish reading) or **distinct** members of a hierarchy (ancient Near Eastern council background reading).
3. Whether the plural form of אֱלֹהִים ever carries meaningful Trinitarian signal — in *this* passage the plural form is clearly **not** functioning that way (it is applied, with plural agreement, to creaturely addressees in v.6 and to the being(s) judged in v.1c); whether it functions that way elsewhere (Gen 1:26 נַעֲשֶׂה אָדָם; Isa 6:8 מִי יֵלֶךְ־לָנוּ) is a separate grammatical investigation.
4. The theological weight of v.7 (not treated here — beyond the stated scope of vv.1–6) — כְּאָדָם תְּמוּתוּן "you will die like men" — which is where the mortality-contrast lands and sharpens the referential question.

The grammatical upshot for the Trinitarian / plural-of-majesty debate: **Psa 82 is strong evidence that Hebrew אֱלֹהִים is a flexible class-noun whose referent is fixed by syntactic agreement and context, not by morphology alone.** Any argument that infers intra-divine plurality from the *-im* ending of אֱלֹהִים must reckon with the fact that the identical form is grammatically predicated of plural creaturely addressees in Ps 82:6, and is governed by singular agreement (hence singular-referent) in Ps 82:1a. The *-im* ending by itself does no referential work.
