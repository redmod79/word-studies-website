## Lexicon Definition

From κοινός (G2839); to make (or consider) profane (ceremonially): — call common, defile, pollute, make unclean.

The verb form of κοινός: to render something common, i.e. to remove it from the sphere of the holy or clean into the sphere of the ordinary or defiled. In the NT, used both of ritual/ceremonial defilement and of moral contamination proceeding from the heart.

## Semantic Categories

### 1. Moral Defilement from the Heart (Jesus' teaching on inner vs. outer purity)
The dominant use. Jesus redefines defilement as originating internally — from the heart — rather than from external food or unwashed hands. The things that "come out" of a person are what truly defile.

- **Matt 15:11** — "Not that which goeth into the mouth defileth a man; but that which cometh out of the mouth, this defileth a man."
- **Matt 15:18** — "But those things which proceed out of the mouth come forth from the heart; and they defile the man."
- **Matt 15:20** — "These are the things which defile a man: but to eat with unwashen hands defileth not a man."
- **Mark 7:15** — "There is nothing from without a man, that entering into him can defile him: but the things which come out of him, those are they that defile the man."
- **Mark 7:20** — "That which cometh out of the man, that defileth the man."
- **Mark 7:23** — "All these evil things come from within, and defile the man."

### 2. Recategorization of Clean/Unclean (Cornelius narrative)
God's voice commands Peter not to "make common" (i.e. consider profane) what God has cleansed. Here κοινόω is used in the prohibitive imperative — a human judgment of defilement being overruled by divine declaration.

- **Acts 10:15** — "What God hath cleansed, that call not thou common."
- **Acts 11:9** — "What God hath cleansed, that call not thou common."

### 3. Desecration of Sacred Space
Applied to the physical pollution of the Jerusalem temple — bringing Gentiles into forbidden precincts. The perfect tense (κεκοίνωκεν) marks a completed act of profanation.

- **Acts 21:28** — "This is the man, that teacheth all men every where against the people, and the law, and this place: and further brought Greeks also into the temple, and hath polluted this holy place."

### 4. Ritual Defilement Requiring Purification (Levitical frame)
The perfect passive participle describes persons in a state of having been defiled — the condition that the Levitical system of animal blood and ashes of a heifer was designed to address.

- **Heb 9:13** — "For if the blood of bulls and of goats, and the ashes of an heifer sprinkling the unclean, sanctifieth to the purifying of the flesh:"

### 5. Eschatological Exclusion
The present active participle used substantivally: "anything that defiles" is permanently excluded from the New Jerusalem, paired with abomination and falsehood.

- **Rev 21:27** — "And there shall in no wise enter into it any thing that defileth, neither whatsoever worketh abomination, or maketh a lie: but they which are written in the Lamb's book of life."

## Hebrew Source

No strong LXX bridge. κοινόω is a distinctly Hellenistic-Jewish term that arose in the Second Temple period to describe the rendering of something ceremonially common/profane. The closest OT Hebrew equivalent by semantic correspondence (not LXX mapping) is H2490 חָלַל (chalal) in its Piel sense "to profane, make common" and H2930 טָמֵא (tame) "to be/make unclean."
