| chezev (H2376) | ὅρασις (G3706) | 6x | Aramaic vision word also mapped here |
| chazah (H2372) | ὅρασις (G3706) | 8x | Root verb's abstract usage |

**Semantic scope in translation:** The LXX uses horasis for multiple Hebrew vision words, broadening its semantic range beyond chazon alone. In the Hebrew OT, chazon specifically denotes divinely granted prophetic revelation. The LXX Greek horasis can include both the revelatory content (= chazon) and the visual appearance/form (= mar'eh). This flattening means the Greek reader loses the Hebrew distinction between the content of revelation (chazon) and the visual form seen (mar'eh).

The NT largely shifts to ὅραμα (horama, G3705) for visionary experiences (Acts 9:10, 10:3, 16:10) and ἀποκάλυψις (apokalypsis, G602) for divine revelation. Horasis retains limited but significant usage, especially in Revelation.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- Vision as divinely granted perception remains central in both testaments
- The Joel → Acts quotation explicitly extends OT chazon into the NT era
- Revelation uses horasis in the same apocalyptic register as Daniel's chazon

**What shifts:**
- The NT predominantly uses horama (G3705) rather than horasis (G3706) for visionary experiences — a vocabulary shift from the LXX's preferred term
- The NT adds apokalypsis (G602) as the primary term for divine disclosure, absorbing much of chazon's theological space
- Chazon as a book-title superscription (Isa 1:1, Nah 1:1) has no NT parallel — NT books use different framing conventions
