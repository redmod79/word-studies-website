**Semantic shift in translation:** The Hebrew batach is straightforwardly about trust and security — leaning on something, feeling safe. The LXX translators chose peitho (a persuasion verb) as the primary rendering, revealing a Greek understanding that trust is the result of being persuaded. In Hebrew, batach is fundamentally relational and volitional (you choose to lean on someone); in Greek, peitho frames the same reality as the outcome of a cognitive process (you were convinced, therefore you trust). This is not a distortion but a lens shift — the perfect tense of peitho (pepoitha = "I have been persuaded" → "I trust") bridges the two concepts seamlessly.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The core concept of reliance on God as the foundation of right living
- The distinction between proper trust (in God) and misplaced trust (in self, flesh, wealth, armies)
- The connection between trust and security/confidence as its outcome

**What shifts:**
- Hebrew batach is purely about trust/reliance; Greek peitho adds the active sense of "persuade" (absent in batach), giving the NT an extra dimension: one who trusts is one who has been persuaded
- The NT expands the obedience dimension: peitho in the passive/middle means "obey" (Heb 13:17; Gal 5:7), connecting trust and obedience as two sides of the same coin — a connection less explicit in Hebrew, where batach (trust) and shama' (obey/hear) are separate roots
- The Greek cognate family (pistis, pisteuo) enormously expands the trust/faith vocabulary in ways that trace back through peitho to the OT batach concept
