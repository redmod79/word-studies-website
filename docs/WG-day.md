# WG: Day and Night (Hebrew/Greek Time Units)

## Overview

The biblical vocabulary for "day" spans Hebrew, Aramaic, and Greek — from the 24-hour cycle to the
eschatological Day of the LORD. Hebrew *yôwm* (H3117) is one of the most frequent words in the OT
(1926 verses, all 39 books). Its derivative *yôwmâm* (H3119) specifies the daytime period. Its
antonym *layil* (H3915) means night. The related word *ʻêth* (H6256) marks appointed moments within
a day. The NT Greek word ἡμέρα (G2250) is the direct LXX and NT equivalent of *yôwm*, bridged with
1485 co-occurrences and a PMI of 25.24 — the strongest single-pair lexical bridge in the entire Bible.

## Semantic Relationships

### Core Pair: yôwm / layil (Day / Night)
The "day and night" (*yomam walaylah*) merism signals totality — continuous, unbroken action. The
pairing appears in contexts of continuous watching, worship, study, or judgment (Josh 1:8; Ps 1:2;
1 Kgs 8:59; Rev parallel: hēméras kai nuktos, Rev 4:8; 7:15; 12:10; 20:10).

### Narrowing: yôwm / yôwmâm
*Yôwmâm* is always a narrowing of *yôwm* — it specifies the daylight portion. It never appears
in the "Day of the LORD" eschatological sense. It functions grammatically as an adverb where *yôwm*
is a noun.

### Appointed Moment: yôwm / ʻêth
*Yôwm* marks the day as a unit; *ʻêth* marks the moment within it. Together in "at the time of
that day" or "not the time for the cattle to be gathered" (Gen 29:7) they layer the when (yom =
which day) with the what moment (ʻêth = the right time within it).
