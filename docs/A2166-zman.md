## Lexicon Definition

(Aramaic) From H2163 (zâman); the same as H2165: season, time. An appointed occasion — a fixed, designated, or scheduled point in time. The word carries the inherent sense of pre-arrangement or divine/royal scheduling, distinguishing it from general time-duration words.

## Semantic Categories

### 1. Divine Sovereignty over Time-Periods
God's authority to change, appoint, and govern the epochs of history.
- **Dan 2:21** — "And he changeth the times (zimnin) and the seasons: he removeth kings, and setteth up kings"
- **Dan 7:25** — "and think to change times (zimnin) and laws: and they shall be given into his hand until a time and times and the dividing of time"

### 2. Specific Calendar Occasion / Appointed Moment
A fixed point on the calendar when something is scheduled to occur.
- **Dan 3:7** — "Therefore at that time (zimna), when all the people heard the sound of the cornet, flute, harp..."
- **Dan 3:8** — "Wherefore at that time (zimna) certain Chaldeans came near, and accused the Jews"
- **Ezr 5:3** — "At the same time (zimna) came to them Tatnai, governor on this side the river"
- **Dan 4:36** — "At the same time (zimna) my reason returned unto me"

### 3. Fixed Repetition / Scheduled Regularity
Recurring appointed times in a pattern of observance.
- **Dan 6:10** — "he kneeled upon his knees three times (zimnin) a day, and prayed"
- **Dan 6:13** — "but maketh his petition three times (zimnin) a day"

### 4. Duration or Period Granted
A span of time allotted by decree or providence.
- **Dan 2:16** — "Daniel went in, and desired of the king that he would give him time (zᵉmân)"
- **Dan 7:12** — "yet their lives were prolonged for a season (zᵉmân) and time"
- **Dan 7:22** — "and the time (zᵉmân) came that the saints possessed the kingdom"

### Note on Dan 7:25 Pairing with ʻiddân
In Dan 7:25, both zᵉmân and ʻiddân appear: the horn "thinks to change times (zimnin) and laws" while the saints are given into his hand "until a time (ʻiddân) and times (ʻiddânîn) and the dividing of time (ʻiddân)." The two words serve distinct roles — zᵉmân is the object of the horn's attempted alteration (appointed festivals/seasons), while ʻiddân measures the duration of the horn's power.
