## Lexicon Definition

From βδελύσσομαι (bdelyssomai, G948): detestable, abominable. Specifically, idolatrous or morally loathsome. The verbal adjective form (-τος suffix) indicates a passive quality: "worthy of being abhorred," "deserving detestation." The word carries the visceral revulsion of its root (βδέω, "to stink") into a moral-religious judgment: these persons are not merely wrong but repulsive in the sight of God.

Thayer notes its occurrence in the LXX at Proverbs 17:15 (describing the one who justifies the wicked and condemns the just — "both are abomination to the LORD") and in the Apocrypha (Sirach, 2 Maccabees).

## Semantic Categories

### 1. Moral-Religious Detestation — Character Exposed by Works
The sole NT occurrence applies βδελυκτός to persons whose professed knowledge of God is contradicted by their conduct. The word functions as a predicate adjective in a participial clause (βδελυκτοὶ ὄντες), characterizing the subjects as detestable in ongoing state.
- **Tit 1:16** — "They profess that they know God; but in works they deny him, being abominable (βδελυκτοί), and disobedient (ἀπειθεῖς), and unto every good work reprobate (ἀδόκιμοι)."

The three adjectives form a descending triad: (1) βδελυκτοί — detestable before God, (2) ἀπειθεῖς (G545) — disobedient/unpersuadable toward God's authority, (3) ἀδόκιμοι (G96) — failing the test, disqualified for any good work.

### 2. LXX Background — What God Finds Detestable
Though not a NT semantic category per se, the LXX usage at Proverbs 17:15 illuminates the word's moral force: "He that justifieth the wicked, and he that condemneth the just, even they both are abomination (βδελυκτός) to the LORD." Here the adjective characterizes judicial perversion — reversing moral categories — as personally detestable to God.

## Grammatical Note (Titus 1:16)

In its sole NT occurrence, βδελυκτοί is parsed as:
- **Form:** βδελυκτοί — Nominative Plural Masculine
- **Construction:** Predicate adjective in a causal participial phrase (βδελυκτοὶ ὄντες καὶ ἀπειθεῖς) modifying the implied subject of ὁμολογοῦσιν/ἀρνοῦνται
- **Function:** Explains *why* their profession is hollow — they are, in their character, detestable

The full clause structure:
- Θεὸν ὁμολογοῦσιν εἰδέναι — "They profess to know God" (Present Active Indicative)
- τοῖς δὲ ἔργοις ἀρνοῦνται — "but by works they deny [him]" (Present Middle/Passive Indicative)
- βδελυκτοὶ ὄντες καὶ ἀπειθεῖς — "being detestable and disobedient" (Present Active Participle of εἰμί)
- καὶ πρὸς πᾶν ἔργον ἀγαθὸν ἀδόκιμοι — "and unto every good work reprobate"
