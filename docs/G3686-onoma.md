**Shem background:** In Hebrew thought, שֵׁם (shem) is not a mere label but encompasses the bearer's revealed character, reputation, and effective presence. When God "puts his name" in a place (Deut 12:5, 11), he grants his covenantal presence there. When one "calls on the name of the LORD" (Gen 4:26; 12:8; Joel 2:32), one invokes his self-revealed identity. ὄνομα inherits this full-bodied Semitic sense in the NT: to act "in the name of Jesus" is to act with his delegated authority and in union with his revealed character, not merely to utter a syllable.

## Grammatical Note — Mat 28:19 (singular "name")

Parsing (from greek_parser.py): **τὸ ὄνομα** [N-ASN] — Accusative Singular Neuter, governed by preposition εἰς (into/unto).

> πορευθέντες οὖν μαθητεύσατε πάντα τὰ ἔθνη, βαπτίζοντες αὐτοὺς **εἰς τὸ ὄνομα** τοῦ Πατρὸς καὶ τοῦ Υἱοῦ καὶ τοῦ Ἁγίου Πνεύματος
>
> "baptizing them **into the name** (singular) of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Ghost"

Grammatically the noun ὄνομα is **singular** (τὸ ὄνομα, not τὰ ὀνόματα), yet it is followed by three coordinated genitives (τοῦ Πατρὸς καὶ τοῦ Υἱοῦ καὶ τοῦ Ἁγίου Πνεύματος), each with its own definite article. The articles set off three distinct referents, while the single noun "name" holds them together. Greek readily uses the plural ὀνόματα when multiple distinct names are in view (e.g., Luk 10:20; Act 1:15; Rev 3:4; 11:13; 17:8), so the singular here is a deliberate grammatical choice, not a stylistic default.

In Hebraic idiom, "the name" of a party denotes that party's revealed identity and authority (see Hebrew Source above). A single ὄνομα shared by Father, Son, and Holy Spirit parallels the Hebrew formula in which baptism "into the name" marks incorporation into one owning authority — cf. 1Co 10:2 (baptized "unto Moses"), 1Co 1:13-15 (the question "in whose name were ye baptized?"), Act 8:16 / 19:5 ("baptized in the name of the Lord Jesus"). The triadic genitive with singular ὄνομα is a grammatical pattern unique in the NT and has been a major data point in Trinitarian formulations from the earliest creeds forward.
