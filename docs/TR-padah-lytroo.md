## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The core mechanism: redemption requires a costly payment. The OT padah always implies a price (lamb, silver, God's mighty hand), and the NT lytroo retains this — explicitly rejecting silver/gold (1 Pet 1:18) in favor of blood.
- The subject of redemption: in both testaments, God is the ultimate redeemer. Human agents may perform padah in ritual contexts (Exod 13:13), but the Deuteronomic-prophetic tradition and all NT uses attribute redemption to God/Christ.

**What shifts:**
- From ritual to Christological: OT padah governs firstborn-redemption law and Exodus-liberation theology. The NT drops the ritual and concentrates entirely on Christ's atoning death as the ransom event.
- From national to moral scope: The Emmaus disciples' hope (Luke 24:21) is still national ("redeem Israel"), but Titus 2:14 and 1 Peter 1:18 redefine redemption as liberation from sin and empty tradition — an inward-moral rather than outward-political act.
- Voice emphasis: All NT occurrences use middle or passive voice, emphasizing that believers are recipients of redemption rather than active agents.
