**Semantic continuity:** The Hebrew root אחר (ʼachar, "behind/after") produces both ʼachărôwn (adj: hindmost/last) and ʼachărîyth (noun: the aftermath/latter end). Greek éschatos absorbs both. The LXX translation is remarkably consistent — wherever H314/H319 signal final position in time, sequence, or rank, the translator reaches for G2078.

**Notable shift:** Hebrew ʼachărôwn is primarily spatial and positional (the one at the rear, the hindmost). Greek éschatos intensifies toward temporal finality — "last days," "last time," "the last trump." The NT usage becomes explicitly eschatological in ways the OT usage does not always demand.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The basic meaning: "the one at the rear/end" — positional finality whether spatial or temporal
- The first/last pairing: already present in OT (Gen 33:2; Isa 44:6) and carried directly into NT
- ʼachărôwn applied to God in Isa 44:6 / 48:12 — Jesus claims this same title in Revelation

**What shifts:**
- Hebrew usage is often spatial/sequential (hindmost in a march, last sign in a series); NT usage tilts heavily eschatological — "last days," "last time," "last day" (resurrection)
- The noun ʼachărîyth (latter end/future, H319) merges with the adjective ʼachărôwn into a single Greek word — Greek éschatos becomes broader than either Hebrew word alone
- NT gives éschatos a uniquely Christological dimension absent from the Hebrew: Christ as the eschatos (last Adam, the First and Last) who defines the endpoint of all things
