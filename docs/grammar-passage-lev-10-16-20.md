## Key Grammatical Features

### 1. Cognate Infinitive Absolute: דָּרֹ֥שׁ דָּרַ֛שׁ (v.16) and אָכֹ֨ול תֹּאכְל֥וּ (v.18)

Two separate cognate-intensification constructions appear in this passage, each with a different finite verb form and a different rhetorical function.

**V.16: דָּרֹשׁ דָּרַשׁ** — Qal.InfAbs + Qal.Perf.3ms of דרשׁ (inquire/seek). The infinitive absolute precedes the cognate perfect, producing the KJV rendering "diligently sought." The construction intensifies the thoroughness of Moses' investigation — he did not casually notice the goat's absence but conducted an emphatic, purposeful inquiry. The clause parser tags the infinitive absolute as `[Modi]` (modifier) to the `[Pred]` finite verb, confirming its adverbial-intensifying function. The fronted object `אֵת שְׂעִיר הַחַטָּאת` before the verb complex is a topicalization move: the goat of the sin-offering is what the narrative draws attention to before describing the search.

**V.18: אָכוֹל תֹּאכְלוּ** — Qal.InfAbs + Qal.Impf.2mp of אכל (eat). Here the infinitive absolute intensifies an imperfect with obligatory/modal force: "you should certainly have eaten it." The same construction appears in Gen 2:16 (אָכֹל תֹּאכֵל "thou mayest freely eat") and Gen 2:17 (מוֹת תָּמוּת "thou shalt surely die"). In v.18 the modal direction is reproach: Moses declares what ought to have been done. The clause tags the infinitive as `[Modi]` to the imperfect `[Pred]`.

- **Grammar reference:** [infinitive-absolute-intensification](../hebrew/infinitive-absolute-intensification.md)

### 2. Dual Purpose Infinitive Construct Chain: לָשֵׂאת ... לְכַפֵּר (v.17)

This is the grammatical centerpiece of Moses' accusation. Two *ל*-prefixed infinitive constructs appear in sequence, each heading its own clause (Clauses 4 and 5 of v.17), both dependent on the finite verb נָתַן ("he gave"):

- **לָשֵׂאת** (Qal.InfCon of נשׂא, H5375) — "to bear." Object: אֶת עֲוֹן הָעֵדָה "the iniquity of the congregation."
- **לְכַפֵּר** (Piel.InfCon of כפר, H3722) — "to make atonement." Complement: עֲלֵיהֶם "upon/for them." Adjunct: לִפְנֵי יְהוָה "before YHWH."

The two infinitives are syntactically parallel but semantically sequential. Bearing (נשׂא) the iniquity is the function of the priestly eating — the priest absorbs the guilt-load of the offering by consuming the sacrificial flesh. Making atonement (כפר, Piel factitive = "effect the covering") is the result that the bearing produces. The clause parser treats each infinitive as the predicate of its own clause, confirming that these are two distinct purpose statements chained from a single governing verb, not a single compound purpose.

The Piel stem of כפר is significant: it is the factitive function ("bring about the state of being covered/atoned"), not the Qal stative. The priest's eating accomplishes the atonement — it does not merely describe a state.

- **Word studies:** [H5375-nasa](../../word-studies/H5375-nasa.md), [H3722-kaphar](../../word-studies/H3722-kaphar.md), [H5771-avon](../../word-studies/H5771-avon.md), [TR-nasa-airo](../../word-studies/TR-nasa-airo.md)

### 3. Hophal Perfect הוּבָא and the Blood-Destination Rule (v.18)

**הוּבָ֣א** (Hophal.Perf.3ms of בוא, H935) — "was brought." The Hophal is the causative passive of the Hiphil הֵבִיא "to bring in." Moses uses the agent-suppressed passive: the blood "was not brought in" — the focus is on the state of the blood's non-arrival, not on who failed to bring it. The subject is אֶת דָּמָהּ "its blood" (the blood of the sin-offering).

The directional adverb פְּנִ֑ימָה ("inward," with directional heh suffix -âh) combined with the prepositional phrase אֶל הַקֹּדֶשׁ ("to the holy place") doubles the spatial reference: the blood was not brought *to the holy place* *inward*. This double locative emphasizes the specific inner sanctuary destination.

This clause articulates the blood-destination rule that governs the two-path system for sin-offering disposal:

**Path A — Blood enters the sanctuary (אֶל הַקֹּדֶשׁ פְּנִימָה):**
When blood is brought inside the holy place (as for the priest's own sin-offering, Lev 4:5-7, or the congregation's sin-offering, Lev 4:16-18), the flesh must be **burned** outside the camp (Lev 4:12, 21). It is not eaten. The governing text is Lev 6:23 [Heb; 6:30 Eng]: וְכָל חַטָּאת אֲשֶׁר יוּבָא מִדָּמָהּ אֶל אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד לְכַפֵּר בַּקֹּדֶשׁ לֹא תֵאָכֵל בָּאֵשׁ תִּשָּׂרֵף ("And every sin-offering whose blood is brought [Hophal.Impf.3ms of בוא] into the tent of meeting to make atonement in the holy place shall not be eaten; in fire it shall be burned"). The same Hophal stem (יוּבָא) appears in Lev 6:23 as in 10:18 (הוּבָא), creating a verbal link between the rule and its application.

**Path B — Blood stays outside (not brought in):**
When the blood is applied only to the outer altar (as for the ruler's sin-offering, Lev 4:25, or the individual's, Lev 4:30, 34), the flesh is **eaten** by the priests in the holy place. This is what Moses expected in Lev 10:18: since the blood of this particular sin-offering was NOT brought inside (לֹא הוּבָא ... אֶל הַקֹּדֶשׁ פְּנִימָה), the priests were obligated to eat it.

The Hophal form grammatically encodes the institutional/procedural character of the blood manipulation — the blood is "caused to come" by priestly action, and the passive form focuses on the blood's resulting location rather than the agent.

- **Grammar reference:** [stem-hophal](../hebrew/stem-hophal.md), [directional-locative-heh](../hebrew/directional-locative-heh.md)
- **Word studies:** [H935-bo](../../word-studies/H935-bo.md), [H6944-qodesh](../../word-studies/H6944-qodesh.md), [H2403-chattaah](../../word-studies/H2403-chattaah.md), [TR-chattaah-hamartia](../../word-studies/TR-chattaah-hamartia.md)
- **Related passage:** [lev-4-5-7](lev-4-5-7.md) (priestly sin-offering blood path)

### 4. Pual Perfect שֹׂרָ֑ף (v.16) — Discovery of Wrongful Burning

**שֹׂרָ֑ף** (Pual.Perf.3ms of שׂרף, H8313) — "it had been burned." The Pual is the passive of the Piel (D-stem passive), indicating that the goat was in a state of having been thoroughly burned. The Pual form (as opposed to the more common Niphal passive תִּשָּׂרֵף, which appears in Lev 6:23) emphasizes the completed, resultative state of destruction — Moses arrives and discovers the goat already fully consumed by fire.

This is the finding that triggers the confrontation. The discovery sequence is narratively compressed: (1) דָּרֹשׁ דָּרַשׁ — emphatically sought; (2) וְהִנֵּה — dramatic presentative "behold!"; (3) שֹׂרָף — the result-state of burning. The הִנֵּה + Pual perfect combination presents the burned state as a surprise discovery from Moses' point of view.

The wrongness of the burning is established by the blood-destination rule: since the blood had NOT been brought inside (v.18), the goat should have been eaten, not burned.

### 5. Construct Chain קֹ֥דֶשׁ קָֽדָשִׁ֖ים (v.17) — "Holy of Holies" Classification

**קֹדֶשׁ** (Noun.ms.Cst) + **קָדָשִׁים** (Noun.mp.Abs) — a superlative construct chain: "holiness of holinesses" = "most holy." The clause parser identifies this as `[PreC]` (predicate complement) in a nominal clause with הִוא as subject: "it [is] most holy."

This classification is the grounds for Moses' accusation. Because the sin-offering is classified as קֹדֶשׁ קָדָשִׁים, the Levitical code assigns specific handling requirements. The construct chain functions as a technical cultic classification, not merely a descriptive adjective. The same phrase appears in Lev 6:22 [Heb; 6:29 Eng]: קֹדֶשׁ קָדָשִׁים הִוא, immediately before the blood-destination rule in Lev 6:23, linking the "most holy" classification to the two-path disposal system.

- **Grammar reference:** [construct-state](../hebrew/construct-state.md)
- **Word study:** [H6944-qodesh](../../word-studies/H6944-qodesh.md)

### 6. Niphal Participle הַנֹּותָרִ֖ם (v.16) — "The Remaining Ones"

**הַנֹּותָרִם** (Art + Niphal.Ptcp.mp.Abs of יתר, "remain") — "the remaining/surviving ones." The Niphal participle carries a stative/resultative force: Eleazar and Ithamar are in the state of having remained alive. The article הַ substantivizes the participle as a restrictive modifier of בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן — these are specifically "the sons of Aaron who were the survivors."

The participial form carries a narrative shadow: Nadab and Abihu have just been consumed by divine fire (Lev 10:1-2). The Niphal "remaining ones" implicitly acknowledges the death of the other two sons and frames the confrontation in that context — Moses is addressing the survivors of a catastrophe, which becomes relevant to Aaron's defense in v.19.

- **Grammar reference:** [stem-niphal](../hebrew/stem-niphal.md)

### 7. Interrogative הֲ + Imperfect: הַיִּיטַ֖ב בְּעֵינֵ֥י יְהוָֽה (v.19) — Aaron's Rhetorical Counter

**הַיִּיטַב** (Introg.הַ + Qal.Impf.3ms of יטב, H3190) — "Would it be good?" Aaron's response constructs a rhetorical question expecting a negative answer. The interrogative heh prefix (הֲ-) on the imperfect creates a counterfactual: "If I had eaten the sin-offering today, would it have been good in the eyes of YHWH?" The imperfect יִּיטַב carries modal force (potential/hypothetical), not simple future.

The idiom **יטב בְּעֵינֵי X** ("be good in the eyes of X") is a standard Hebrew formula for approval/acceptance (Gen 12:13; 34:18; 41:37). Aaron deploys it to reframe the question: Moses asked why they did not eat; Aaron asks whether eating would have been acceptable given the day's events.

The resolution in v.20 uses the same root: וַיִּיטַ֖ב בְּעֵינָֽיו (Qal.Wayq.3ms of יטב + "in his eyes") — "and it was good in his eyes." The verbal echo creates an inclusion: Aaron's hypothetical "would it be good in the eyes of YHWH?" is answered by the narrative's "and it was good in his [Moses'] eyes." Moses accepts Aaron's reasoning.
