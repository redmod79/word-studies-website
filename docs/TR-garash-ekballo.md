The Hebrew verb for forcible expulsion — of Adam from Eden, Canaanites from the land, wives by divorce, widows from their homes — is rendered in the LXX predominantly by ἐκβάλλω. That same Greek verb becomes the standard NT term for Jesus casting out demons, expelling merchants from the temple, and — in at least one direct OT citation — Paul's argument from Sarah casting out Hagar.

**Semantic observation:** The LXX funnels several distinct Hebrew expulsion verbs (גרשׁ, שׁלך, טול, נתשׁ) into a single Greek verb ἐκβάλλω. The Hebrew distinguishes *how* someone is removed (driven, hurled, plucked); the Greek collapses these into a general "eject" that then diversifies in NT usage through prepositions and objects.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same:**
- **Forceful expulsion** is the core sense across both testaments — never gentle removal
- **Agency by the holy** — whether YHWH drives out Canaanites, Sarah expels Hagar, or Jesus casts out demons, the expeller is the one with covenantal right; the expelled is what does not belong
- **"From before the face of"** (מִפְּנֵי → ἀπὸ προσώπου) — the spatial idiom of removing someone from the presence of the rightful occupant survives the translation
- **Legal/household expulsion** carries through: the Hebrew gerushah (divorced woman) and the NT's commands about ekballō of a bondwoman share the same register

**What shifts:**
- **From land to persons/demons** — OT gârash is overwhelmingly about expelling *peoples from territory*; NT ekballō is overwhelmingly about expelling *unclean spirits from persons*. The arena miniaturizes: the land becomes the human body, the Canaanite becomes the demon. Conquest language is internalized as exorcism.
- **From YHWH's agency to Messiah's authority** — in the OT YHWH Himself drives out the nations; in the NT Jesus casts out demons "by the finger of God" (Luk 11:20), claiming that same divine prerogative. The verb carries the messianic claim.
- **Divorce sense narrows** — NT ekballō does not carry the technical divorce idiom the way the Qal passive of גרשׁ does; divorce language in the NT shifts primarily to ἀπολύω.
- **Collapse of distinctions** — Hebrew distinguishes גרשׁ (expel), ירשׁ (dispossess), שׁלך (hurl), שׁלח (send away), נדח (drive off); all route through ἐκβάλλω (and relatives) in the LXX, so NT readers trained on Greek inherit a flatter vocabulary for a richer Hebrew field.
