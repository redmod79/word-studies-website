The LXX consistently routes the Hebrew/Aramaic dream-word family to ἐνύπνιον (enypnion). The LXX also brings along pshar (H6591, "interpretation") and Belteshazzar / Joseph (the interpreters) as part of the cluster — showing that for the LXX translators, dream-discourse is a package of dream + interpretation + interpreter.

**Semantic shift in translation:**
- enypnion literally "in-sleep (thing)" — makes explicit the nocturnal/sleep setting that chalom implies contextually
- The polemical nuance of Jer/Deut ("false dreams") is preserved; LXX keeps enypnion even in critique passages

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same:**
- Dream remains a recognized mode of divine self-disclosure (Num 12:6 → Acts 2:17)
- Twin stream of legitimate (Joseph, Solomon, Joel/Peter) vs illegitimate (Deut 13, Jer 23 → Jude 8) dreams persists into the NT
- Dream paired with "vision" in both testaments (chalom + chazon → enypnion + horasis, preserved in Joel/Acts 2:17)

**What shifts:**
- **Vocabulary split in NT:** Matthew prefers ὄναρ (onar, 6x) for narrative dreams; Luke/Acts and Jude use the ἐνύπνιον / ἐνυπνιάζομαι word-family, which is the LXX standard. The Matthean onar is not a LXX term — suggesting a Koine / common-Greek register in the infancy narrative while Luke's Acts 2 quotation follows LXX phrasing.
- **Frequency collapse:** chalom is frequent in the OT (54x) and dominant in Genesis; in the NT the dream-word is rare (total ~9x across all Greek terms). The NT shifts revelation primarily to the incarnate Word, the Spirit, and vision (ὅραμα, ὀπτασία), with dream surviving as a minor channel.
- **Interpretive apparatus drops out:** LXX carried pshar (interpretation) and the interpreter-figures as part of the dream-cluster; NT dream passages do not require interpretation — the dreams are directly intelligible commands (Matthean dreams) or quoted eschatological promise (Acts 2).
