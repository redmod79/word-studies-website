# Grammar Analysis: Daniel 8:8-9

The fall of the great horn and the emergence of the little horn. Two verses dense with verb alternation (perfect/wayyiqtol), a rare construct-numeral idiom (ḥāzûṯ ʾarbaʿ), a hapax diminutive (miṣ-ṣəʿîrāh), an intensifying noun-adverbial (yeṯer), and the notorious antecedent ambiguity of min-hā-ʾaḥaṯ mēhem — "from one of them" — where "them" can grammatically refer to either the four horns or the four winds of v.8.

## Text

**Hebrew (Dan 8:8):** וּצְפִ֥יר הָעִזִּ֖ים הִגְדִּ֣יל עַד־מְאֹ֑ד וּכְעָצְמֹ֗ו נִשְׁבְּרָה֙ הַקֶּ֣רֶן הַגְּדֹולָ֔ה וַֽתַּעֲלֶ֜נָה חָז֤וּת אַרְבַּע֙ תַּחְתֶּ֔יהָ לְאַרְבַּ֖ע רוּחֹ֥ות הַשָּׁמָֽיִם׃

**Hebrew (Dan 8:9):** וּמִן־הָאַחַ֣ת מֵהֶ֔ם יָצָ֥א קֶֽרֶן־אַחַ֖ת מִצְּעִירָ֑ה וַתִּגְדַּל־יֶ֛תֶר אֶל־הַנֶּ֥גֶב וְאֶל־הַמִּזְרָ֖ח וְאֶל־הַצֶּֽבִי׃

**KJV (Dan 8:8):** Therefore the he goat waxed very great: and when he was strong, the great horn was broken; and for it came up four notable ones toward the four winds of heaven.

## Clause Structure

### Daniel 8:8 — Four-clause sequence (WXQt → InfC → WxQX → WayX)

| Clause | Type | Text | Function |
|--------|------|------|----------|
| 1 | **WXQt** (waw-X-qatal) | וּצְפִיר הָעִזִּים הִגְדִּיל עַד־מְאֹד | Topical/circumstantial: "Now the he-goat — it magnified exceedingly" (subject-fronted) |
| 2 | **InfC** (כ + infinitive construct + suffix) | כְעָצְמֹו | Temporal subordinator: "when/as he grew mighty" |
| 3 | **WxQX** (waw-qatal-X with non-consecutive nuance) | וּנִשְׁבְּרָה הַקֶּרֶן הַגְּדֹולָה | Fronted predicate, patient as subject: "was-broken the great horn" — the perfect with fronted verb signals the key rupture event |
| 4 | **WayX** (wayyiqtol) | וַתַּעֲלֶנָה חָזוּת אַרְבַּע תַּחְתֶּיהָ לְאַרְבַּע רוּחֹות הַשָּׁמָיִם | Sequential narrative: "and four conspicuous [ones] came up in its place, toward the four winds of heaven" |

The verse is thus **X-perfect → temporal infinitive → perfect(waw) → wayyiqtol**, a marked pattern: the big event (breaking) is narrated in the perfect rather than the expected wayyiqtol, backgrounding it relative to the wayyiqtol that follows. The narrative "foreground" becomes the ascent of the four — not the rupture.

### Daniel 8:9 — Two-clause sequence (WxQX → Way0)

| Clause | Type | Text | Function |
|--------|------|------|----------|
| 1 | **WxQX** (waw-complement-qatal-X) | וּמִן־הָאַחַת מֵהֶם יָצָא קֶרֶן־אַחַת מִצְּעִירָה | Fronted prepositional complement "from the one of them" precedes verb — focus on **source**: "and from one of them emerged a horn — one [small thing] out of a little-one" |
| 2 | **Way0** (zero-subject wayyiqtol) | וַתִּגְדַּל־יֶתֶר אֶל־הַנֶּגֶב וְאֶל־הַמִּזְרָח וְאֶל־הַצֶּבִי | Narrative progression, 3fs verb agreeing with qeren: "and it grew-great excessively toward south and toward east and toward the glorious" |

The **fronted מִן־הָאַחַת מֵהֶם** is syntactically emphatic — this is **where the horn came from** is the point of contention, exactly the feature that triggers the antecedent ambiguity.

## Construct Chains

### Dan 8:8
- **צְפִיר הָעִזִּים** — "he-goat of the goats" (apposition-like construct; idiom for the buck-goat)
- **אַרְבַּע רוּחֹות הַשָּׁמָיִם** — three-link construct chain: "four-of winds-of the-heavens" = "the four winds of heaven"

### Dan 8:9
- No formal construct chain, but **קֶרֶן־אַחַת** ("horn-one") functions like a maqqef-bound unit ("one-horn / a single horn"), and **מִן־הָאַחַת מֵהֶם** is a partitive phrase "from the one-of them."

## Key Grammatical Features

### 1. נִשְׁבְּרָה (niš-bərāh) — Niphal Perfect 3fs of שׁבר "break"

- **Form:** Verb.Niphal.Perf.3fs. Root שׁבר "break, shatter."
- **Function:** The Niphal is the default medio-passive stem. Here it is classically **passive of Qal**: "the great horn **was broken**." The agent is suppressed — classic Hebrew usage when the author wants the event foregrounded without naming the breaker. The OT reader hears a thematic echo of Jer 48:25 (*nišbərāh qeren Môʾāḇ* — "the horn of Moab is broken") and Jer 49:35, where national power is ruptured by YHWH's decree. Compare also the impersonal/fated tone of Job 38:15.
- **Significance:** By using the Niphal rather than a Qal active ("X broke it"), the text refuses to specify a human breaker. The breaking is presented as **happening to** the horn at its peak of strength (כְעָצְמֹו, "as he grew mighty"). This is the grammatical substrate for later exegetical claims that the breaker is providential/divine, not Ptolemy, Seleucus, or any earthly power. The verb's placement in a **WxQX clause** (pred-fronted perfect with waw) — not a wayyiqtol — also slows the narrative down at this moment: it is **marked**, circumstantial, backgrounded to what follows.
- **Grammar reference:** [hebrew/stem-niphal.md](../hebrew/stem-niphal.md) • [hebrew/syntax-wx-qatal-circumstantial.md](../hebrew/syntax-wx-qatal-circumstantial.md)
- **Word study:** see [H7665 relevant breaking-verbs] (no canonical entry yet in word-studies/)

### 2. חָזוּת אַרְבַּע (ḥāzûṯ ʾarbaʿ) — "four conspicuousness"

- **Form:** Noun.fs.Abs (חָזוּת "visibility, conspicuousness, prominent one") + numeral. The literal force is "a conspicuousness of four" or "four-of-conspicuousness" — a construct-like idiom with the numeral following the abstract noun.
- **Significance:** חָזוּת normally means "vision, appearance" (Isa 21:2; Dan 8:5, 8:27). In the collocation *ḥāzûṯ ʾarbaʿ* it functions substantivally for **"conspicuous ones, prominent [horns]"** — what the KJV renders "four notable ones." The abstract noun for "conspicuousness" stands in for "conspicuous things." The 3fp verb **וַתַּעֲלֶנָה** (wayyiqtol Qal 3fp of עלה) confirms the subject is plural and feminine, matching **horns** (קרן is fs, but plural feminine collectively). So: "four prominent [horns] came up in its place."
- The word **תַּחְתֶּיהָ** ("in its place" — noun תחת with 3fs suffix) refers back to הַקֶּרֶן הַגְּדֹולָה. This replacement-in-place is the grammatical core of the succession: the four rise **where the one fell**.
- **Grammar reference:** no dedicated entry yet. Analogous idiomatic abstract-for-concrete constructions appear under [hebrew/plural-noun-singular-verb-agreement.md](../hebrew/plural-noun-singular-verb-agreement.md).

### 3. וַתַּעֲלֶנָה (wat-taʿăleynāh) / וַתִּגְדַּל (wat-tigdal) — wayyiqtol 3fp / 3fs

- **Form:** Verb.Qal.Wayq.3fp of עלה "ascend" (Dan 8:8) and Verb.Qal.Wayq.3fs of גדל "grow great" (Dan 8:9). Classic narrative *wayyiqtol* (prefix conjugation with waw-consecutive).
- **Function:** The *wayyiqtol* is Hebrew's default **sequential narrative tense**, conveying successive foregrounded events. In v.8 it marks the **ascent of the four** as the key event (after the backgrounded perfect נִשְׁבְּרָה). In v.9 וַתִּגְדַּל — with 3fs agreement pointing to קֶרֶן (feminine) — marks the little horn's subsequent growth as the next narrative foreground.
- **Significance:** The two wayyiqtols **bracket** the ambiguous antecedent in v.9. The flow is: breaking (perf) → ascent (wayq 3fp, 4 horns) → emergence (perf ייצא, of little horn from "one of them") → growth (wayq 3fs). The wayyiqtol-in-the-tail of v.9 reveals what the little horn *does*; the perfect יָצָא in v.9 narrates its origin with deliberate syntactic fronting of the source-PP.
- **Grammar reference:** no dedicated wayyiqtol entry yet. Related: [hebrew/qal-imperfect.md](../hebrew/qal-imperfect.md), [hebrew/syntax-waw-conjunction.md](../hebrew/syntax-waw-conjunction.md).

### 4. מִן־הָאַחַת מֵהֶם (min-hā-ʾaḥaṯ mēhem) — "from the one of them" (THE ANTECEDENT PROBLEM)

- **Form:** Prep מִן + definite article הָ + Noun.fs.Abs אַחַת "one" + Prep מֵ + 3mp suffix הֶם. Two antecedent candidates:
  1. **קַרְנַיִם / קרנות** — the four horns (implicit plural of קֶרֶן, which is feminine singular in the lexeme but here clearly referring to four of them).
  2. **רוּחֹות הַשָּׁמָיִם** — the four winds of heaven (noun רוח is feminine; רוּחֹות is feminine plural).
- **The grammatical tension:**
  - **אַחַת is feminine singular** (agreeing with either horns-f.pl or winds-f.pl equally).
  - **מֵהֶם is masculine plural ("from them").** This is the **telling feature**: the 3mp suffix does *not* agree cleanly with either a feminine-plural "horns" or a feminine-plural "winds." In BH, however, **3mp is the default/unmarked plural pronominal suffix**, often used even when referents are feminine (cf. Joüon-Muraoka §149). So the gender-mismatch is **not decisive**: mēhem could grammatically point to either antecedent.
  - **Proximity (attraction) argument:** "winds of heaven" is the nearer plural antecedent linearly. By simple **syntactic proximity**, *mēhem* would normally attach to the nearest matching antecedent = **winds**.
  - **Semantic coherence argument:** "horns" is the dominant topic-chain (the great horn broke → four horns rose → from one of them a horn came). Horns produce horns; winds do not. By **discourse topicality / thematic continuity**, the antecedent = **horns**.
- **Significance:** This ambiguity is real and ancient. The LXX (Theodotion) and Vulgate preserve it without disambiguating. Most modern commentators (Collins, Goldingay, Lucas) read the antecedent as **winds** on proximity/strict grammar; others (Young, Shea, Doukhan) argue **horns** on thematic grounds, pointing out that קרן-from-קרן is the natural biological image and that a horn arising "out of a wind" is odd unless "wind" = a compass-region (which is precisely the SDA-style reading: Rome arose "out of" one of the four directional quadrants of Alexander's divided empire rather than out of one of the four diadochi).
- **Neither reading is grammatically impossible.** The ambiguity is intentional or at least tolerated by the author, and the choice is made on wider exegetical grounds, not on morphology alone.
- **Grammar reference:** no dedicated antecedent-ambiguity entry yet — candidate for creation as `hebrew/pronominal-antecedent-ambiguity.md`.

### 5. מִצְּעִירָה (miṣ-ṣəʿîrāh) — "from a little-one" (hapax noun, H4704)

- **Form:** Prep מִן + Noun.fs.Abs צְעִירָה. The lemma is given by the parser as **צעירה** (a nominalized feminine form from the adjective צעיר "small, young"). Strong's H4704 classifies it as a distinct noun — "a little one" — and it is functionally a **hapax** in this exact nominal form at Dan 8:9. The adjective cognate (H6810 צעיר) is common (e.g., Gen 19:31, "the younger," Jer 49:20 "little ones of the flock").
- **Function:** **Diminutive, likely pejorative** — "out of something little." Two parsings are possible:
  1. **Adjectival** qualifying קֶרֶן: "a horn — [a] little one." Some translations take it so ("a little horn," KJV, ASV).
  2. **Prepositional phrase with מִן**: "from a little/small [beginning/source]" — describing the horn's modest origin, parallel to the מִן in the first clause. The parser segments the מִ as a preposition, which supports reading 2.
- The consonantal מצעירה can be read either as the preposition מן assimilated + noun צעירה ("from a little-one"), or as a stative participial form ("[while] small"). The parser's segmentation (מִ + צְּעִירָה) favors the prepositional reading, which then contrasts semantically with the following wat-tigdal-yeṯer "but it grew excessively."
- **Significance:** The form is structurally and theologically loaded. The horn **begins small** — this is the interpretive key. Pejorative diminutives in apocalyptic describe unassuming beginnings that outpace expectation (cf. Dan 7:8 *qeren ʾoḥrî zəʿêrāh*, "another little horn," Aramaic cognate). The pairing of **miṣ-ṣəʿîrāh** (start-small) with **tigdal-yeṯer** (grew-excessive) is a rhetorical "from tiny to outsized" arc.
- **Word study:** [word-studies/H4704-mitstsiyrah.md](../../word-studies/H4704-mitstsiyrah.md)

### 6. וַתִּגְדַּל־יֶתֶר (wat-tigdal-yeṯer) — "and it grew to excess"

- **Form:** Verb.Qal.Wayq.3fs of גדל (H1431) "be/grow great" + Noun.ms.Abs יֶתֶר (H3499) "surplus, remainder, excess" functioning **adverbially**.
- **Function:** יֶתֶר is a substantive — literally "surplus, what-remains-over, excess." In this construction it is attached to the verb as a **cognate-like modifier** expressing intensity/excess: "she grew — excess." The parser flags it as [Modi] (modifier) in the clause analysis, not as object or complement. It intensifies: "grew **exceedingly**, grew **beyond measure**, grew **more than**."
- **Significance:** The v.8 goat *higdîl ʿaḏ-məʾōḏ* ("magnified unto exceedingly") is the **superlative** for the goat. The little horn of v.9 then *tigdal-yeṯer* — "grew with surplus/excess" — the verb root is identical (גדל), but the intensifier is different (and arguably *stronger*, suggesting growth beyond the goat's already-superlative scope). In v.10-11 this growth continues (*wat-tigdal ʿaḏ ṣəḇāʾ haš-šāmayim*, "grew up to the host of heaven"). The rhetorical escalation is built on this root: great horn → four conspicuous ones → little-horn-that-grew-excess → up to the host of heaven.
- **Word studies:** [word-studies/H1431-gadal.md](../../word-studies/H1431-gadal.md), [word-studies/H3499-yether.md](../../word-studies/H3499-yether.md)

### 7. The Triad of Expansion: אֶל־הַנֶּגֶב וְאֶל־הַמִּזְרָח וְאֶל־הַצֶּבִי

- **Form:** Three prepositional phrases with אֶל, each with definite article + noun:
  1. הַנֶּגֶב (han-negeḇ) — "the south" (negev, the southern desert; here compass direction)
  2. הַמִּזְרָח (ham-mizrāḥ) — "the east" (literally "the sunrise place")
  3. הַצֶּבִי (haṣ-ṣəḇî) — "the beauty / the glorious [land]"
- **Significance — what is missing?** The compass expectation is four points (N/S/E/W). Here only **south and east** appear; **north and west are not named**. Instead, the third direction is **הַצֶּבִי** — "the glorious," a by-word for the Land of Israel (cf. Jer 3:19, *ʾereṣ ṣəḇî* "a land of beauty"; Ezek 20:6, 15; Dan 11:16, 41, 45 explicitly *ʾereṣ-haṣ-ṣəḇî*, "the glorious land"). The triad thus is **not** three compass points: it is **two directions (south, east) + one destination (the glorious land / Palestine)**.
- This is syntactically and semantically significant:
  1. The third ʾel-phrase **breaks the compass pattern**. If the author intended three compass points (south, east, + a third direction such as north or west), the natural choice would have been one of those. Instead, the third item is a **charged toponym-substitute** — the covenant land itself.
  2. The omission of **north and west** is probably not a geographic oversight. It is a focalization: the horn's expansion is oriented toward **Israel** from the diaspora perspective (south toward Egypt, east toward Mesopotamia/Persia, and the target: the glorious land between). Some commentators (Goldingay, Lucas) treat ṣəḇî as directional (pointing toward the west or southwest); others (SDA tradition, Shea, Doukhan) treat ṣəḇî unambiguously as "the glorious land = Palestine/the sanctuary," reading the triad as "expanded toward south, toward east, and [culminatingly] toward the glorious land."
  3. The article on all three (han-negeḇ, ham-mizrāḥ, haṣ-ṣəḇî) marks them as **known, referenced** places — not just cardinal directions in the abstract but specific regions, reinforcing the reading of ṣəḇî as a concrete toponym.
- **Word study:** [word-studies/H6643-tsebi.md](../../word-studies/H6643-tsebi.md)

### 8. יָצָא — Qal Perfect 3ms "went out" (of a fs subject קֶרֶן)

- **Form:** Verb.Qal.Perf.3ms of יצא "go out, emerge, come forth."
- **Apparent gender mismatch:** The verb is 3**ms**, but the subject קֶרֶן־אַחַת is **feminine singular**. Two explanations:
  1. **Word order / impersonal:** with the verb preceding its subject in a fronted-complement clause (WxQX — the complement מִן־הָאַחַת מֵהֶם is fronted, then verb, then delayed subject), BH frequently uses the default 3ms form as an impersonal/early placeholder ("there went out"), which is then clarified by the delayed postposed subject. Cf. the impersonal "there was" pattern (יְהִי) before fs subjects.
  2. **Genitive attraction:** the gender is drawn to masculine through attraction to a contextually dominant masculine entity (e.g., a wind, or the he-goat as general subject of the scene).
- The parser simply tags יָצָא as Qal.Perf.3ms. The gender mismatch with קֶרֶן is **syntactically allowed** under verb-before-subject rules.
- **Word study:** [word-studies/H3318-yatsa.md](../../word-studies/H3318-yatsa.md)

## The Grammatical Logic of the Two Verses

Both verses share a **perfect → wayyiqtol** alternation that carries the theological weight:

| v.8 | v.9 |
|-----|-----|
| *higdîl* (perf) — goat grew great | *yāṣāʾ* (perf) — horn went out |
| *niš-bərāh* (perf) — great horn broken | *tigdal-yeṯer* (wayq) — grew excessively |
| *wat-taʿăleynāh* (wayq) — four came up | |

The **perfects** mark setup/circumstance; the **wayyiqtols** mark forward-motion. In each verse the wayyiqtol names the outcome that matters for the vision's argument: (v.8) the succession of four, (v.9) the unchecked growth of the little horn.

The **fronted complements** (*ʿaḏ məʾōḏ* → *ke-ʿāṣmô* → *taḥtêhā* in v.8; *min-hā-ʾaḥaṯ mēhem* in v.9) function as **marked information** — they tell the reader *where to look*: to the extremity of greatness, the timing-at-peak-strength, the place-under, and the source-from-one. Each fronted PP forces a reading where those referents carry focus.

The critical exegetical question — what is "one of them" — is **not a mere translation puzzle**. It is a deliberate syntactic feature of v.9's fronted *min*-phrase. The author chose to place ambiguous reference in the slot of focus. The pronominal suffix *hem* is doing interpretive work precisely because it is underdetermined.
