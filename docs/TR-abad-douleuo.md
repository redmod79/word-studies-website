**Semantic differentiation in translation:** The LXX translators systematically split ʻâbad's unified semantic field into distinct Greek verbs:
- **douleúō** — chosen when the relational subjection is dominant (serving a master, political servitude, serving God as covenant Lord)
- **latreúō** — chosen when cultic/religious service is in view (temple worship, sacrifice, priestly duties)
- **ergázomai** — chosen when physical labor is primary (tilling, manual work)
- **proskynéō** — chosen when the physical act of worship/bowing is emphasized

This split is itself theologically significant: Hebrew holds together the concept that working, serving, and worshipping are facets of a single relationship, while Greek separates these into conceptually distinct activities.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The exclusive-allegiance demand: "serve the LORD" (OT) becomes "serve the Lord Christ" (NT) — Matt 6:24, Col 3:24
- The idolatry accusation: "served other gods" (Deut/Judg/Jer) echoes in "ye did service unto them which by nature are no gods" (Gal 4:8)
- The slavery-to-freedom narrative: Egyptian bondage (Exo 1:13) parallels bondage to sin (Rom 6:6) and to the law (Gal 4:25)

**What shifts:**
- The agricultural sense of ʻâbad (tilling, plowing) does not carry into douleúō; that domain was captured by ergázomai instead
- douleúō in the NT narrows to the master-slave relationship more consistently than ʻâbad, which had a broader range
- Paul develops a paradox absent from the OT: the Christian is simultaneously a doûlos of Christ (willing slavery) and freed from douleía to sin — a dialectic foreign to the Hebrew ʻâbad, which simply posed the choice of which master to serve
