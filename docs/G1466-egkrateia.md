## Lexicon Definition

Strong's: "self-control (especially continence) — temperance." Formed from ἐν + κράτος: the internal mastery or dominion a person exercises over his own passions, appetites, and impulses. In classical Greek ἐγκράτεια is a cardinal virtue (cf. Plato, Aristotle's *Nicomachean Ethics*) denoting the settled disposition by which reason holds the appetites in check. In the NT it names a fruit of the Spirit (Gal 5:23) and a rung in Peter's ladder of Christian virtues (2 Pet 1:6), and it is an ethical topic Paul presses on a Roman governor (Acts 24:25).

## Semantic Categories

With only 3 verses, the categories are really three discrete *contexts* rather than broad usages. The word's meaning is stable across all three; what varies is the setting.

### 1. Prophetic Ethical Confrontation — Self-Control Before a Magistrate
- **Acts 24:25** — "And as he reasoned of righteousness, temperance (ἐγκράτεια), and judgment to come, Felix trembled." Paul preaches to the procurator Felix (living with another man's wife, Drusilla) and picks three themes that indict his hearer: righteousness, self-control, and coming judgment. Here ἐγκράτεια is paired with δικαιοσύνη and κρίμα — not as one virtue among many, but as a direct moral claim on a man in public power.

### 2. Fruit of the Spirit — Self-Control as Spirit-Produced Virtue
- **Galatians 5:23** — "Meekness, temperance (ἐγκράτεια): against such there is no law." Final item in the ninefold fruit of the Spirit (Gal 5:22–23). It stands opposite the "works of the flesh" catalogued in 5:19–21 (fornication, drunkenness, revelings, etc.), several of which are precisely failures of self-mastery. Paul's closing clause ("against such there is no law") flags self-control as the kind of Spirit-wrought disposition that law could never legislate into being.

### 3. Ladder of Christian Growth — Self-Control as an Added Virtue
- **2 Peter 1:6** — "And to knowledge temperance (ἐγκράτεια); and to temperance (ἐγκράτεια) patience; and to patience godliness." The one verse with two tokens. Peter's chain runs: faith → virtue → knowledge → **self-control** → patience → godliness → brotherly kindness → love (1:5–7). Self-control sits between knowledge (the informed mind) and patience (endurance under trial), suggesting that what knowledge yields must be lived out through self-mastery before it can mature into perseverance.

## Hebrew Source

ἐγκράτεια does not correspond to any single Hebrew word in the LXX. The LXX uses it only in Sirach and the Wisdom books (e.g., Sir 18:30, the chapter heading Περὶ ἐγκρατείας ψυχῆς). Classical Hebrew expressed the concept through verbal phrases rather than an abstract noun — e.g., מָשַׁל בְּרוּחוֹ (māshal b'rucho, "rule over his own spirit," Prov 16:32; 25:28) and חָשַׂךְ (chasak, "restrain, hold back"). The abstract-noun virtue ἐγκράτεια is a hellenistic philosophical contribution; Paul and Peter adopt the term but reorient it around the Spirit and Christ rather than Stoic self-sufficiency.

## Cultural Background Note

ἐγκράτεια was a standard theme in Greek moral philosophy — Aristotle devotes much of *Nic. Ethics* VII to ἐγκράτεια vs. ἀκρασία, and it appears prominently in Stoic and Cynic diatribes on the passions. Luke's placement of the term on Paul's lips before Felix (Acts 24:25) is striking: Paul uses a concept the Roman elite would recognize as a virtue, and turns it against a governor whose personal life failed it.
