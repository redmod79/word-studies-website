## Roles in the Concept

- **Quality:** pikros (G4089) — the adjective describing what is bitter (taste or disposition)
- **Action:** pikraino (G4087) — the verb for making or becoming bitter
- **State:** pikria (G4088) — the noun for the condition of bitterness
- **Manner:** pikros (G4090) — the adverb for doing something bitterly (weeping)
- **Substance:** apsinthos (G894) — a specific bitter plant; wormwood
- **Visceral:** chole (G5521) — gall/bile; paired with pikria in "gall of bitterness"
- **Antonym:** glykys (G1099) — sweet; the fundamental taste opposition

## Key Passages Where Multiple Group Words Appear Together

- **Rev 10:9-10** — pikraino (G4087) + glykys (G1099): "it shall make thy belly bitter, but it shall be in thy mouth sweet as honey... it was in my mouth sweet as honey: and as soon as I had eaten it, my belly was bitter." The sweet-bitter reversal of the prophetic scroll.
- **Rev 8:11** — pikraino (G4087) + apsinthos (G894): "the name of the star is called Wormwood... they were made bitter." Wormwood as agent of embittering waters.
- **Acts 8:23** — pikria (G4088) + chole (G5521): "in the gall of bitterness, and in the bond of iniquity." Doubled bitterness metaphor for Simon's spiritual state.
- **James 3:11-14** — pikros (G4089) + glykys (G1099): sweet/bitter water from one fountain (v.11), then "bitter envying" (v.14). Bridges literal taste and figurative moral quality within one discourse.
