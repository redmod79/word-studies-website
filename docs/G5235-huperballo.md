## Lexicon Definition

From ὑπέρ (G5228) and βάλλω (G906); to throw beyond the usual mark, i.e. (figuratively) to surpass (only active participle ὑπερβάλλων).

## Morphological Note

All 5 NT occurrences are **present active participles** — the word never appears as a finite verb in the NT. The participial form functions adjectivally ("the surpassing [X]") or adverbially ("surpassingly"), describing a quality that continuously exceeds all measure.

## Semantic Categories

### 1. Surpassing Divine Glory (New Covenant Superiority)
- **2 Cor 3:10** — "For even that which was made glorious had no glory in this respect, by reason of the glory that excelleth [huperballousa]." The glory of the new covenant so far surpasses the old that the old has "no glory" by comparison.

### 2. Surpassing Divine Grace
- **2 Cor 9:14** — "And by their prayer for you, which long after you for the exceeding [huperballousan] grace of God in you." God's grace in the Corinthians provokes intense longing and prayer from other believers.
- **Eph 2:7** — "That in the ages to come he might shew the exceeding [huperballon] riches of his grace in his kindness toward us through Christ Jesus." The purpose of salvation is an eternal display of grace that surpasses comprehension.

### 3. Surpassing Divine Power
- **Eph 1:19** — "And what is the exceeding [huperballon] greatness of his power to us-ward who believe, according to the working of his mighty power." The power available to believers surpasses normal categories — Paul piles four power-words (dunamis, energeia, kratos, ischus) to approximate its magnitude.

### 4. Surpassing Divine Love (Beyond Knowledge)
- **Eph 3:19** — "And to know the love of Christ, which passeth [huperballousan] knowledge, that ye might be filled with all the fulness of God." Christ's love is paradoxical: knowable yet exceeding the capacity of knowledge. The participle marks a love that continuously overflows cognitive limits.
