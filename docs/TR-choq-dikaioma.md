**Semantic shift in LXX translation:** The translators chose δικαίωμα — literally "a righteous deed" or "that which has been decreed as right" — rather than νόμος (law) or ἐντολή (commandment). This choice emphasizes the *equitable/just* character of the enactment, not merely its legal force. The word carried built-in moral weight: a chôq rendered as δικαίωμα was not just a rule but a *just ruling*.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same:**
- The core idea of a formally enacted righteous standard — whether divine statute in OT law or the "righteous requirement" of the law in Paul
- The cultic/ritual dimension: Heb 9 uses dikaíōmata to describe the OT ceremonial ordinances, in direct continuity with LXX usage

**What shifts:**
- In Paul (Rom 5:16,18), dikaíōma undergoes a singular-use shift: from "standing statute" to "one righteous act" (Christ's obedience), and to "the verdict of justification" — exploiting the root dikaio- family's forensic range
- The cosmic decree sense (Psa 148:6; Prov 8:29) does not carry into NT usage of this word
- The allotment/portion sense (Gen 47:22; Prov 30:8) similarly does not carry forward
