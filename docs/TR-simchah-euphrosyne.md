**Semantic continuity:** The LXX translators found a near-perfect match. Greek εὐφροσύνη already carried the connotation of banquet/festive gladness (classical Attic: symposium cheer, Dionysian mirth); applied to simchâh, it is redirected to the worship-context of Israel's festivals without losing its meal/celebration register. The result is a Greek noun that, in biblical usage, means "the gladness that belongs to the presence of God and his gifts."

### The Deuteronomy Samach-Imperatives

Where the Hebrew shifts from noun (simchâh) to verb (śāmaḥ, H8055), the LXX shifts from εὐφροσύνη to the cognate verb εὐφραίνω. The Deuteronomic imperatives to "rejoice before the LORD" use εὐφραίνω, while the curse-text that names the failure uses the noun εὐφροσύνη:

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- **Gladness belongs to God's presence.** Simchâh in Psa 16:11 and euphrosynē in Act 2:28 share the same formula — joy in God's countenance / at God's face.
- **Gladness is table-shaped.** LXX εὐφροσύνη regularly names the mood of the covenant meal (Deu 14:26; 16:11–15; Neh 8:12); Act 14:17 names God as the one who fills hearts "with food and gladness." The banquet register persists.
- **Gladness is commanded, not merely felt.** Deuteronomy issues samach imperatives; Deu 28:47 turns the failure to rejoice into a capital covenant offense. Luke/Acts receives a word whose native habitat is this commanded gladness.

**What shifts:**
- **From festival-located to resurrection-located.** In the OT simchâh is located at the central sanctuary, tied to the appointed feasts and agricultural cycles. In Act 2:28 euphrosynē is located "with thy countenance" — in Christ's resurrection presence with the Father — effectively relocating the central-sanctuary joy to the risen Christ.
- **From Israel-specific to universal.** Act 14:17 extends euphrosynē to all nations as the proper response to creation's witness. The Deuteronomic meal-gladness becomes a Gentile apologetic category.
- **Frequency inversion.** In the LXX εὐφροσύνη is the primary noun for religious joy; in the NT χαρά eclipses it (59 vs. 2). The NT shifts its vocabulary of joy toward the inward/Spirit-given register (χαρά in the Spirit, Rom 14:17), while retaining εὐφροσύνη for the two moments where Luke wants to activate the specifically Deuteronomic/Psalmic resonance.
