**Semantic shift in translation:** The LXX consolidates a cluster of Hebrew "destruction" verbs (ʼâbad, shâmad, kârath, shâdad, yârash) into a single Greek verb apóllymi. Where Hebrew distinguished *perishing* (ʼâbad, intransitive) from *exterminating* (shâmad, transitive causative), Greek apóllymi covers both voices (active "destroy" / middle-passive "perish"). This consolidation is why NT apóllymi can carry either nuance depending on voice — continuing a distinction Hebrew marked lexically but Greek marks morphologically.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- **Total-destruction sense preserved.** Both shâmad and apollymi denote final, irreversible destruction — not damage or reform.
- **God as ultimate agent.** In both corpora, the destruction is most often sourced in divine action or divine permission (Deut 28; Matt 10:28; Rev 11:18).
- **Covenant-conditioned.** OT: destruction for breaking covenant (Deut 28). NT: destruction for unbelief / rejection of Christ (John 3:18, 2 Thess 1:9). Same conditional logic on a new covenant basis.
- **Two-destinies framework.** Psa 145:20 ("the LORD preserveth all them that love him: but all the wicked will he destroy") anticipates the NT saved/perishing dichotomy (1 Cor 1:18, 2 Cor 2:15).

**What shifts:**
- **Voice consolidation.** Hebrew used *different verbs* for active destroy (shâmad Hiphil) vs passive perish (ʼâbad). Greek uses *one verb* (apollymi) with voice morphology. This lets NT apollymi carry both nuances fluidly — Jesus both "destroys" (Matt 10:28) and is the one through whom we do not "perish" (John 3:16).
- **Eschatological intensification.** OT shâmad is largely historical (Canaanite conquest, Assyrian/Babylonian destruction). NT apollymi is largely eschatological (final judgment, the age to come).
- **Redemptive inversion.** The OT threat ("thou shalt be destroyed") becomes the NT negated promise ("should not perish"). The same verb, now used to describe what believers are *rescued from*.
- **Interior dimension.** NT apollymi can describe the destruction of soul as well as body (Matt 10:28); OT shâmad is predominantly about bodies, houses, nations, cities — external and corporate.
