## Lexicon Definition

From H2490 (chalal); a flute (as perforated): — pipe.

The noun is derived from the root meaning "to bore/pierce," describing the instrument's construction: a hollow tube with finger-holes bored through it. The name thus identifies the instrument by its manufacturing process — a pipe made by perforation.

## Semantic Categories

### 1. Prophetic/Ecstatic Worship Instrument
- **1 Sam 10:5** — "thou shalt meet a company of prophets coming down from the high place with a psaltery, and a tabret, and a pipe, and a harp, before them; and they shall prophesy"

The chaliyl appears in a list of instruments accompanying prophetic ecstasy. It is one member of a four-instrument ensemble (nebel, toph, chaliyl, kinnor) used in processional worship.

### 2. Communal Celebration and Rejoicing
- **1 Ki 1:40** — "And all the people came up after him, and the people piped with pipes, and rejoiced with great joy, so that the earth rent with the sound of them"
- **Isa 30:29** — "Ye shall have a song, as in the night when a holy solemnity is kept; and gladness of heart, as when one goeth with a pipe to come into the mountain of the LORD, to the mighty One of Israel"

Used at Solomon's coronation procession and in Isaiah's vision of festival pilgrimage to the temple mount. Both contexts involve corporate joy in religious celebration.

### 3. Feasting and Revelry (Negative Context)
- **Isa 5:12** — "And the harp, and the viol, the tabret, and pipe, and wine, are in their feasts: but they regard not the work of the LORD, neither consider the operation of his hands"

Isaiah condemns the misuse of instruments (including the chaliyl) at feasts where revelers ignore God. The instrument itself is not condemned — the spiritual indifference of the feasters is.

### 4. Lamentation and Mourning
- **Jer 48:36** — "Therefore mine heart shall sound for Moab like pipes, and mine heart shall sound like pipes for the men of Kirheres: because the riches that he hath gotten are perished"

The doubled occurrence in this verse uses the chaliyl as a metaphor for mourning. The prophet's heart "sounds like pipes" — a wailing, mournful tone. This shows the instrument could produce plaintive sounds associated with lamentation, not only celebration.

## LXX Mapping

| Greek Equivalent | Strong's | Frequency | Notes |

| αὐλός (aulos) | G836 | 4× (primary) | Flute/pipe — direct semantic equivalent; G836 appears 1× independently in NT (1 Cor 14:7) |

The LXX consistently renders chaliyl as aulos, confirming the identification as a wind instrument (flute/pipe). The Greek aulos family includes:
- **G836 αὐλός (aulos)** — the flute/pipe instrument itself (1 Cor 14:7)
- **G832 αὐλέω (auleo)** — to play the flute (Matt 11:17, Luke 7:32, 1 Cor 14:7)
- **G834 αὐλητής (auletes)** — a flute-player/piper (Matt 9:23, Rev 18:22)

This LXX mapping is significant for distinguishing chaliyl from machol (H4234). The LXX translators — working centuries before modern debates — rendered chaliyl as aulos ("flute") but machol as choros ("dance/chorus"), treating them as entirely different semantic categories.
