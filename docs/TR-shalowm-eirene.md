**Semantic mapping in translation:** The LXX consistently renders shalom as eirene, preserving the primary "peace" sense. However, shalom's broader semantic range (welfare, health, wholeness, greeting) gets partially narrowed. When shalom means "health" or "welfare," LXX occasionally uses hygiaino ("to be healthy") or iasis ("healing") rather than eirene. The greeting function ("Is he well?") maps inconsistently. The bridge is strong for the core peace/wholeness meaning but the Hebrew word's range exceeds what eirene carries alone.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- Peace as a divine gift rather than human achievement (Num 6:26 → Rom 1:7, Phil 4:7)
- The formula "go in peace" as a blessing of wholeness (1 Sa 1:17 → Mk 5:34, Lk 7:50)
- False peace warned against (Jer 6:14 → 1 Th 5:3 "when they shall say, Peace and safety")
- Peace connected to righteousness (Isa 32:17, Psa 85:10 → Rom 14:17, Heb 12:11)
- Messianic/christological identification: "he shall be the peace" (Mic 5:5 → Eph 2:14 "he is our peace")

**What shifts:**
- NT epistolary formula "grace and peace" (charis kai eirene) has no exact OT equivalent; it fuses Greek greeting (chairein) with Hebrew greeting (shalom)
- NT narrows the greeting/welfare-inquiry usage that was so prominent in OT narrative; eirene functions more as theological concept than social formula
- NT applies shalom/eirene specifically to the reconciliation achieved through Christ's death (Col 1:20 "having made peace through the blood of his cross"), developing Isa 53:5's hint into full atonement theology
- The "peace offering" (shelem H8002) connection is not carried into NT eirene usage; the sacrificial dimension routes through different vocabulary (thusia, prosphora)
