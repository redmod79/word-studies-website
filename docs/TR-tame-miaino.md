**Semantic profile in translation:** The LXX translators reserved miaino specifically for the *verbal action* of defiling — the process of becoming or making unclean. They used akathartos (adjective) for the *resulting state* of being unclean. This verb/adjective distinction is consistent and deliberate.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- Defilement as *contact-transmitted contamination* — in both OT and NT, miaino describes pollution that spreads from a source to others (Lev 5:3 touch → Heb 12:15 root of bitterness affecting many)
- The *unfitness for the holy* — defilement renders one unable to approach God's presence (OT temple/Passover → John 18:28 judgment hall)

**What shifts:**
- **From ritual to moral/internal** — OT tame is overwhelmingly about bodily and ceremonial impurity (skin disease, corpse contact, bodily emissions); NT miaino is used 3 of 4 times for moral/conscience defilement (Titus 1:15 mind and conscience, Heb 12:15 bitterness, Jude 1:8 flesh via immorality). Only John 18:28 preserves the OT ritual sense.
- **From temporary to settled** — OT tame defilement is typically resolved by evening, washing, or sacrifice; the NT perfect tenses in Titus 1:15 (μεμιαμμένοις, μεμίανται) suggest a settled, ongoing state of defilement rather than a temporary ritual condition.
