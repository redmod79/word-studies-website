# Grammar Analysis: 1 Corinthians 11:23-30

**Theme:** Lord's Supper institution — cup of the new covenant, unworthy participation brings judgment.

## Text

## Key Grammatical Features

### 1. παρέλαβον ... παρέδωκα (v.23) — The technical pair for tradition transmission

- **Form:** Both aorist active indicative, 1st singular. παρέλαβον is a 2nd aorist of παραλαμβάνω (G3880, "receive alongside"); παρέδωκα is the 1st aorist of παραδίδωμι (G3860, "deliver over").
- **Significance:** This is rabbinic/technical vocabulary for passing on received tradition (compare 1 Cor 15:3). The aorists mark two completed, punctiliar acts: Paul received from the Lord and delivered to the Corinthians. The balanced pair establishes the chain of custody for the words that follow — Paul is not innovating but transmitting.
- **Grammar reference:** See [`grammar-reference/greek/aorist-infinitive-accusative-of-duration.md`](../greek/aorist-infinitive-accusative-of-duration.md) for aorist aspect theory more generally.
- **Word studies:** None yet for παραδίδωμι / παραλαμβάνω (gap).

### 2. παρεδίδετο (v.23) — Imperfect passive of betrayal

- **Form:** Imperfect passive indicative, 3rd singular, from παραδίδωμι (G3860).
- **Significance:** The imperfect aspect portrays the act as **in process** ("was being handed over") rather than as a completed event (which would take an aorist). This aligns with the temporal clause ἐν τῇ νυκτὶ ᾗ ("in the night in which") — the betrayal-process is unfolding while Jesus is celebrating the meal. The passive voice leaves the agent unexpressed; in Pauline usage this can shade toward the divine passive (God handing over the Son, cf. Rom 4:25; 8:32) as well as Judas's act.
- **Lexical irony:** The **same verb** (παραδίδωμι) used here for Christ being betrayed is used in v.23 for Paul "delivering" the tradition. Paul hands over what Christ handed himself over for.
- **Grammar reference:** See [`grammar-reference/greek/divine-passive.md`](../greek/divine-passive.md).

### 3. τοῦτο ποιεῖτε (vv.24, 25) — Present imperative of ongoing command

- **Form:** Present active imperative, 2nd plural, from ποιέω (G4160).
- **Significance:** The present imperative commands a **habitual, ongoing** practice, not a one-time action. Paired with ὁσάκις ἐὰν πίνητε ("as often as you drink") in v.25 and ὁσάκις ... ἐὰν ἐσθίητε ... πίνητε in v.26, the present stem confirms repeated, iterative observance. Contrast with an aorist imperative, which would command a single completed act (see [`grammar-reference/greek/mood-aorist-imperative.md`](../greek/mood-aorist-imperative.md)). The choice of present over aorist is intentional: communion is a repeated remembrance, not a one-off ceremony.

### 4. Predicate nominative identifications — Τοῦτό ... ἐστιν τὸ σῶμα (v.24) and Τοῦτο τὸ ποτήριον ἡ καινὴ διαθήκη ἐστὶν (v.25)

- **Form:** Demonstrative subject (Τοῦτο, D-NSN) + present indicative of εἰμί (ἐστιν) + articular predicate nominative (τὸ σῶμα / ἡ καινὴ διαθήκη).
- **Significance:** The Greek follows the normal equative sentence pattern: **subject is the articular nominative that agrees in gender/number with the pronoun's referent**, while the predicate is the other articular nominative. In v.25 the identification is chained: "this cup is the new covenant in my blood." Grammatically ἐν τῷ ἐμῷ αἵματι modifies either διαθήκη ("the new covenant [ratified] in my blood") or the whole predication ("this cup is the new covenant, in/by my blood"). Either way, the εἰμί clause is not physical-identity metaphysics but representational predication — the cup stands for / embodies / corresponds to the covenant-in-blood, the same idiomatic force as "the seven lamps are the seven Spirits" in Rev 4:5.

### 5. μετὰ τὸ δειπνῆσαι (v.25) — Articular infinitive of time

- **Form:** μετά + τὸ (accusative article) + δειπνῆσαι (aorist active infinitive of δειπνέω, G1172).
- **Significance:** μετά + articular infinitive in the accusative is a fixed temporal construction meaning **"after [verb]ing."** The aorist infinitive marks the completed action of supping; the temporal preposition places the cup event "after having supped." This locates the cup sequentially after the bread and the meal proper — the "cup after supper" — which is the standard Passover seder pattern for the third cup (the cup of blessing). The same construction family (articular infinitive with preposition) appears in different cases for cause, purpose, and time; see the related entry [`grammar-reference/greek/aorist-infinitive-accusative-of-duration.md`](../greek/aorist-infinitive-accusative-of-duration.md).

### 6. ὁσάκις ἐὰν ... πίνητε / ἐσθίητε (vv.25, 26) — Indefinite temporal/frequency clause with present subjunctive

- **Form:** ὁσάκις ("as often as") + ἐὰν (G1437, conditional particle) + present active subjunctive 2pl.
- **Significance:** ὁσάκις ἐάν + subjunctive constructs an **indefinite-frequency clause**: "as often as, whenever, you [happen to] eat/drink." The present subjunctive reinforces ongoing/iterative action. Paul is not prescribing a schedule; he is wrapping the memorial around every occurrence of the meal. v.26 makes this the basis for an indicative consequence: καταγγέλλετε ("you proclaim"), a present indicative that is continuous and declarative — every celebration = an act of proclamation τὸν θάνατον τοῦ Κυρίου ("the death of the Lord") ἄχρι οὗ ἔλθῃ ("until he comes," aorist subjunctive in an indefinite temporal clause with future reference).

### 7. ὃς ἂν ... ἐσθίῃ ἢ πίνῃ (v.27) — Indefinite relative with subjunctive

- **Form:** ὅς (R-NSM) + ἄν (G302) + present subjunctive 3sg twice, joined by ἤ ("or").
- **Significance:** ὃς ἄν + subjunctive is the standard generalizing relative: **"whoever [at any time] eats or drinks."** The ἤ ("or") is important: eating the bread OR drinking the cup unworthily — either one — makes the participant liable. That is, the two elements are treated as a single sacramental complex; you cannot opt out of one half. The following ἔνοχος ἔσται ("will be liable/guilty") uses the future middle of εἰμί (ἔσται), marking the consequence as certain and predictive.

### 8. ἀναξίως (v.27) — Adverb of manner

- **Form:** Adverb of ἀνάξιος, G371, formed with the -ως adverbial suffix.
- **Significance:** **This is an adverb, not an adjective.** It modifies the manner of eating/drinking, not the moral worthiness of the participant as a person. The text does not read ὁ ἀνάξιος ("the unworthy person") but rather ἀναξίως ("in an unworthy manner"). This nuance is critical for pastoral reading: Paul is not barring the unworthy-as-such from the table (everyone is that); he is warning against unworthy *conduct at* the table — the specific contextual sin being the divisive, class-based eating of 1 Cor 11:17-22. The adverbial construction points to behavior, not to a character verdict.

### 9. ἔνοχος ἔσται τοῦ σώματος καὶ τοῦ αἵματος (v.27) — Predicate adjective with objective genitive

- **Form:** ἔνοχος (A-NSM, predicate adjective, "liable/guilty of") + future middle of εἰμί + triple genitive σώματος ... αἵματος ... Κυρίου.
- **Significance:** ἔνοχος with a genitive typically takes the genitive of the **offense** (e.g., "guilty of death") or of the **party wronged/the thing violated**. Here the genitives are **the body and blood of the Lord** — the offended realities. The guilt is of a kind corresponding to treating Christ's body and blood contemptibly; the unworthy participant is charged as though they had wronged the body and blood itself. The double-articled τοῦ σώματος καὶ τοῦ αἵματος (each with its own article) treats each as a discrete entity of liability.

### 10. δοκιμαζέτω ... ἐσθιέτω ... πινέτω (v.28) — Three third-person present imperatives

- **Form:** Present active imperative, 3rd singular. δοκιμαζέτω (G1381, "let him test/examine"), ἐσθιέτω (G2068, "let him eat"), πινέτω (G4095, "let him drink").
- **Significance:** Third-person imperatives in Greek render something between "must" and "let him." They address an individual via the generic ἄνθρωπος ("a man/person"). The sequence is ordered: **examine first, then eat, then drink** — καὶ οὕτως ("and thus/in this way") ties the eating/drinking to the prior self-testing as both prerequisite and method. The present stem again indicates ongoing practice, not a one-time audit. Parallel to [`grammar-reference/greek/mood-aorist-imperative.md`](../greek/mood-aorist-imperative.md) — but here present, not aorist, because the examination is repeated each celebration.

### 11. ὁ ... ἐσθίων καὶ πίνων ... μὴ διακρίνων (v.29) — Articular and anarthrous participles

- **Form:** ὁ ἐσθίων καὶ πίνων = substantival articular present participles ("the one eating and drinking"). μὴ διακρίνων = adverbial anarthrous present participle with μή ("not discerning") modifying the main verbs ἐσθίει ... πίνει.
- **Significance:** Two different participial functions in one verse:
  1. **Substantival:** ὁ ἐσθίων καὶ πίνων names the agent — "the one who eats and drinks" — treating the participles as a noun phrase.
  2. **Adverbial circumstantial:** μὴ διακρίνων τὸ σῶμα modifies the manner/cause of the eating and drinking: he eats and drinks *while/because not discerning* the body. The negation with μή (not οὐ) is expected with participles expressing condition or manner.
- The adverbial participle gives the *reason* judgment falls: the eating is self-condemning because it is done without discerning the body. "The body" (τὸ σῶμα) here echoes the σῶμα of v.24 (Christ's body given) and is the semantic hinge between sacramental and ecclesial meaning.
- **Word studies:** [`word-studies/G2917-krima.md`](../../word-studies/G2917-krima.md), [`word-studies/G4983-soma.md`](../../word-studies/G4983-soma.md).

### 12. κοιμῶνται (v.30) — Present passive of "sleep" (euphemism for death)

- **Form:** Present passive indicative, 3rd plural, from κοιμάομαι (G2837, "fall asleep").
- **Significance:** κοιμάομαι used absolutely (without qualification) is the standard NT euphemism for Christian death (cf. 1 Cor 15:6, 18, 20, 51; 1 Thess 4:13-15). The present tense here is durative/iterative: "a considerable number are sleeping" — meaning the community is currently experiencing a wave of deaths that Paul attributes to the abuse of the Supper. The progression in v.30 is diagnostic, ascending in severity: ἀσθενεῖς ("weak") → ἄρρωστοι ("sick") → κοιμῶνται ("are falling asleep/dying). The passive voice of κοιμάομαι is deponent (middle/passive in form, intransitive in meaning), not an agent-backgrounding passive.

## Clause Structure Notes

- **vv.23-25** form a single transmission block introduced by γάρ — Paul reports what he received/delivered. The main verbs are the aorists παρέλαβον, παρέδωκα (Paul's action) and ἔλαβεν, εὐχαριστήσας/ἔκλασεν/εἶπεν, λέγων (Christ's action on the night he was being betrayed).
- **v.26** begins a Pauline commentary (marked by γάρ): the ὁσάκις ... ἐὰν ... πίνητε / ἐσθίητε clauses are temporal-conditional; καταγγέλλετε is the present indicative main verb of declared consequence; ἄχρι οὗ ἔλθῃ closes the period with an indefinite temporal clause pointing forward to the parousia.
- **v.27** is a full result/inference clause (ὥστε) with an indefinite subject (ὃς ἂν ... ἐσθίῃ ἢ πίνῃ ... ἀναξίως) and a future predicate-adjectival clause (ἔνοχος ἔσται).
- **v.28** uses δέ to pivot from warning to prescription; three 3rd-person present imperatives structure the positive command.
- **v.29** restates v.27 in indicative mode: the one who eats/drinks without discerning eats κρίμα to himself. γάρ ties it back as explanation.
- **v.30** (διὰ τοῦτο, "on account of this") gives the empirical fallout — present-tense observables in the Corinthian church.
