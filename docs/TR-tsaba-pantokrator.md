**Semantic shift in translation:** The LXX translators made a striking choice: instead of a literal "Lord of armies," they frequently used παντοκράτωρ ("all-ruler / all-powerful"). This *universalized* the military imagery — from "LORD commanding Israel's armies" to "LORD who rules all." Simultaneously they sometimes preserved σαβαώθ untranslated, keeping the Hebrew flavor for worship settings. And when ṣāḇāʾ meant the cosmic *host of heaven* (Isa 34:4; Isa 40:26) they used δύναμις. So one Hebrew word splits into three Greek paths depending on which aspect — sovereignty, liturgy, or cosmos — is in view.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The divine warrior-king title survives intact — as *pantokrátōr*, as *sabaṓth*, or as "Lord Almighty." Revelation alone uses it 9 times, making it arguably the most OT-flavored title in the NT.
- The phrase *ṣāḇāʾ ha-šāmayim* ("host of heaven") crosses over as both *stratiá toú ouranoú* (Luke 2:13; Acts 7:42) and *dynámeis toú ouranoú* (Matt 24:29 par.). The heavenly-host concept is preserved.
- The implicit warrior-courtroom imagery behind "LORD of hosts" (1 Ki 22:19; Isa 6) re-emerges in Revelation's throne-room (Rev 4–5).

**What shifts:**
- Military/earthly-army uses of ṣāḇāʾ (common in Numbers, Joshua, Samuel, Kings) largely *do not* carry across. NT armies (*strateuma*, *parembolē*) are described with different vocabulary; the OT military *ṣāḇāʾ* drops out except in rare echoes.
- The Numbers-style *cultic* use ("perform the service of the tabernacle," Num 4:23) has no direct NT analogue with this word — cultic service in the NT is handled by *leitourgía / diakonía*.
- The "appointed time of hardship" sense (Job 7:1; Isa 40:2) does not cross over via this chain; NT idioms of the appointed time use *kairós*.
- *Pantokrátōr* — originally a translator's interpretation for *ṣəḇāʾôṯ* — becomes a stand-alone divine title in its own right (especially Revelation), now functioning less as "commander of hosts" and more as "all-ruling sovereign."

**Why this matters for Daniel 8:** The LXX splits *ṣāḇāʾ* into *pantokrátōr* (for the divine title), *dýnamis* (for cosmic/star host), and *stratiá* (for military/angelic host). In Dan 8:10 the LXX uses *dýnamis*, which is the same word Jesus uses in Matt 24:29 for the "powers of the heavens shaken." In Dan 8:11 the Theodotion Greek has *ho archistratēgos* ("chief-captain"), echoing Josh 5:14 — a deliberate cross-reference built into the Greek translation of Daniel.
