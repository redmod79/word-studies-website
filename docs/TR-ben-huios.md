**Semantic shift in translation:** The LXX renders ben almost uniformly with huios — the mapping is remarkably consistent. Both words carry the same breadth: literal son, genealogical marker, membership in a class, and divine/covenantal sonship. The Greek huios preserves the legal/heir dimension of ben (a son who builds the family name). Teknon (birth/childhood emphasis) is chosen selectively by the LXX when the Hebrew context stresses infancy or birth rather than legal standing.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The word huios in Greek NT carries every register of ben: literal son, patronymic, class membership, and divine/covenantal sonship
- The messianic trajectory of Ps 2:7 and 2 Sam 7:14 flows directly through LXX huios into the NT christological title "Son of God"
- The "sons of God" language for the people of God (Exo 4:22; Deut 14:1) is continued in NT adoption theology (Rom 8:14–19; Gal 4:4–7)

**What shifts:**
- In the NT, huios as "Son of God" narrows to mean uniquely Jesus Christ in most occurrences (195 of 382 are "Son" with theological capital-S emphasis)
- The NT distinguishes huios (legal/covenantal son) from teknon (born child) more deliberately than Hebrew ben could — Paul exploits this distinction in Galatians 4: believers are both born (teknon) of the Spirit and legally adopted (huios) as heirs
- "Son of Man" (ben adam in Hebrew; huios tou anthropou in Greek) becomes a major NT Christological title — derived from Ezek 2:1 + Dan 7:13
