# Perfect Tense (Greek)

## Definition

The Greek perfect tense denotes a completed action whose resulting state persists into the present. It is the only Greek tense that intrinsically fuses two temporal frames: a past event and its abiding outcome. The perfect is fundamentally stative-resultative — not merely "what happened" but "what stands because it happened."

1. **Intensive/Resultative** — emphasis on the present state produced by the completed action
2. **Consummative** — emphasis on the completed action brought to its finish
3. **Stative (present-force)** — the past-event component has receded; functions as a present state (οἶδα, ἕστηκα, πέποιθα)

## Form Recognition

- **Reduplication** — initial consonant repeated with ε: γέ-γραπται (γράφω), τε-τέλεσται (τελέω), πε-πίστευκα (πιστεύω), κέ-κριται (κρίνω)
- **Active endings:** -κα, -κας, -κεν, -καμεν, -κατε, -κασι(ν) (first perfect); second perfects lack κ (γέγονεν, ἕστηκα)
- **Middle/Passive endings:** -μαι, -σαι, -ται, -μεθα, -σθε, -νται (no κ infix)
- **Infinitive endings:** -κέναι (active), -σθαι (middle/passive)
- **Participle:** reduplicated stem + -κώς/-ότες (active), -μένος (middle/passive)
- **Subjunctive:** reduplicated stem + mood vowel (only attested with οἶδα in NT)
- **Imperative:** -κε / -σθε (only 4 NT occurrences)
- **Πarser search:** `tense=perfect` (all moods), add `mood=indicative|participle|infinitive|subjunctive|imperative` to narrow
- **Morph code patterns:** `V-RAI-3S` (active indicative), `V-RPI-3S` (passive indicative), `V-RPP-NSM` (passive participle), `V-RAP-NSM` (active participle), `V-RAN` (active infinitive), `V-RPN` (passive infinitive), `V-RAS-2P` (active subjunctive), `V-RPM-2S` (passive imperative)

## Functions with Examples

### 1. Intensive/Resultative Perfect

The past action recedes; the focus is on the present state that endures. Most common with passive voice, emphasizing "what has been done and now stands."

| Reference | Greek | Lemma | Morph Code | KJV Translation |
|-----------|-------|-------|------------|-----------------|
| John 19:30 | Τετέλεσται | τελέω | V-RPI-3S | "It is finished" |
| Mark 1:15 | Πεπλήρωται | πληρόω | V-RPI-3S | "The time is fulfilled" |
| Matthew 4:4 | Γέγραπται | γράφω | V-RPI-3S | "It is written" |
| John 3:18 | κέκριται | κρίνω | V-RPI-3S | "is condemned already" |
| Galatians 2:19 | συνεσταύρωμαι | συσταυρόω | V-RPI-1S | "I am crucified with Christ" |

John 19:30 Τετέλεσται — the work of redemption is a completed past act whose finished state abides permanently. Matthew 4:4 Γέγραπται — what has been written stands authoritative; the perfect passive of γράφω is the standard formula for citing Scripture as binding. John 3:18 κέκριται — the verdict has been rendered and the condemned state continues.

### 2. Consummative Perfect

The emphasis falls on the completed action, often summarizing a life, mission, or event brought to its endpoint. Most common with active voice.

| Reference | Greek | Lemma | Morph Code | KJV Translation |
|-----------|-------|-------|------------|-----------------|
| 2 Timothy 4:7 | τετέλεκα | τελέω | V-RAI-1S | "I have finished [my] course" |
| 2 Timothy 4:7 | τετήρηκα | τηρέω | V-RAI-1S | "I have kept the faith" |
| Hebrews 10:14 | τετελείωκεν | τελειόω | V-RAI-3S | "he hath perfected for ever" |
| John 17:4 | τελειώσας | τελειόω | V-RAP-NSM | "I have finished" (PTCP) |
| John 12:18 | πεποιηκέναι | ποιέω | V-RAN | "he had done" (INF) |

2 Timothy 4:7 τετέλεκα and τετήρηκα — Paul views his ministry as a completed whole. Hebrews 10:14 τετελείωκεν — Christ's single offering has permanently perfected; the adverbial εἰς τὸ διηνεκές ("for ever") reinforces the perfect's abiding force.

### 3. Stative Perfect (Present-Force)

Certain verbs have lost the sense of a prior act entirely and function as simple present-state verbs. Greek grammarians call these "present perfects."

| Reference | Greek | Lemma | Morph Code | KJV Translation |
|-----------|-------|-------|------------|-----------------|
| Matthew 6:8 | οἶδεν | οἶδα | V-RAI-3S | "knoweth" |
| Romans 8:38 | πέπεισμαι | πείθω | V-RPI-1S | "I am persuaded" |
| Revelation 3:20 | ἕστηκα | ἵστημι | V-RAI-1S | "I stand" |
| Romans 5:2 | ἑστήκαμεν | ἵστημι | V-RAI-1P | "we stand" |
| 1 John 1:1 | ἑωράκαμεν | ὁράω | V-RAI-1P | "we have seen" |

οἶδα (from *εἴδω, "see") is the paradigm — a completed act of seeing has become a present state of knowing; always translated as present "I know." ἕστηκα (ἵστημι) = "I stand" (not "I have stood"). πέπεισμαι (πείθω) = "I am persuaded." πέποιθα (πείθω, second perfect) = "I trust / I am confident."

## Distribution Across Moods

The perfect appears predominantly in the indicative and participial moods. Other moods are rare or unattested in the NT.

| Mood | Occurrences | Status | Examples |
|------|-------------|--------|----------|
| Indicative | ~800+ | Common | τετέλεσται (JHN 19:30), γέγραπται (MAT 4:4) |
| Participle | ~500+ | Common | σεσῳσμένοι (EPH 2:8), δεδιωγμένοι (MAT 5:10) |
| Infinitive | ~15 | Rare | γεγονέναι (JHN 12:29), εἰδέναι (LUK 20:7) |
| Subjunctive | ~10 | Rare, only οἶδα | εἰδῆτε (1JN 5:13), εἰδῶμεν (1CO 2:12) |
| Imperative | 4 | Very rare, formulaic | πεφίμωσο (MRK 4:39), Ἔρρωσθε (ACT 15:29) |
| Optative | 0 | Unattested in NT | — |

**Perfect Participle.** The perfect participle stably attributes a state resulting from a completed action. It is frequently used in periphrastic constructions with εἰμί: ἐστε σεσῳσμένοι (EPH 2:8, V-RPP-NPM) "you are (in a state of) having been saved." The perfect passive participle is the standard form for stating a permanent condition or status.

**Perfect Infinitive.** Rare; typically appears after verbs of perception or as complement: γεγονέναι (JHN 12:29) "to have happened," πεποιηκέναι (JHN 12:18) "to have done."

**Perfect Subjunctive.** Attested only with the stative verb οἶδα in the NT. All 10 occurrences follow ἵνα: ἵνα εἰδῆτε (1JN 5:13) "that ye may know." The perfect subjunctive of other verbs is unattested in the NT corpus.

**Perfect Imperative.** Only 4 attestations in the NT — two active (ἴστε from οἶδα, EPH 5:5; JAS 1:19), two passive (πεφίμωσο "be still," MRK 4:39; Ἔρρωσθε "fare ye well," ACT 15:29). Both passives are formulaic — πεφίμωσο is a command in narrative context, while Ἔρρωσθε is an epistolary farewell formula ("be strengthened/be well").

## Contrast with Aorist

| Feature | Perfect | Aorist |
|---------|---------|--------|
| **Primary focus** | Present state from past action | Past action as a single whole event |
| **Time frame** | Past event + present result | Past event only |
| **English gloss** | "has been finished" / "is finished" | "finished" / "was finished" |
| **Voice distribution** | Heavy passive use (result state) | Active/passive/middle all common |
| **Example** | ἤγγικεν (MAT 3:2) "is at hand" (and remains near) | ἐβάπτισεν (MRK 1:8) "baptized" (the event) |

The aorist views a past action externally as a punctiliar event. The perfect views a past action through the lens of its continuing consequence. Compare κέκριται (JHN 3:18, perfect — "stands condemned") with the aorist of the same verb — the perfect asserts the state, not merely the verdict-event. When translated "has X'd" in English, the perfect points not to the recency of the action but to the persistence of its outcome.

## Voice in the Perfect

All three voices occur in the perfect, with passive dominating numerically.

**Active:** Subject performs the action and exists in the resulting state. τετέλεκα (2TI 4:7) "I have finished" — Paul the agent, Paul the finished one. πεπίστευκα (2TI 1:12) "I have believed" — the act of believing has produced a state of faith.

**Passive:** Subject has been acted upon and exists in the resulting condition. Τετέλεσται (JHN 19:30) "it has been finished / it stands finished." γέγραπται (MAT 4:4) "it stands written." The perfect passive is the NT's primary tense-form for expressing permanent divine acts and their abiding effects.

**Middle:** Rare in the perfect. Subject is self-involved in the action and its result. συνεσταύρωμαι (GAL 2:19) "I am crucified with" — Paul's self-involvement in Christ's crucifixion as a continuing personal state.
