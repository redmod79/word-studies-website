## OT Usage (Hebrew)

| Strong's | Word | Occurrences | Primary Meaning |
|----------|------|-------------|-----------------|
| H3772 | כָּרַת (kârath) | 280 | cut, cut off, make a covenant |
| H1285 | בְּרִית (beriyth) | 284 | covenant, compact |

The idiom **karat berit** (כָּרַת בְּרִית — lit. "cut a covenant") is the standard Hebrew formula for entering a solemn binding agreement. The "cutting" refers to the covenant ratification rite of splitting animals and passing between the pieces, binding the parties with the implicit oath: *may what happened to this animal happen to me if I break faith* (cf. Gen 15:9–17; Jer 34:18–19).

**Key OT passages:**
- **Gen 15:18** — "In the same day the LORD made (karat) a covenant with Abram"
- **Jer 31:31** — "I will make (karat) a new covenant with the house of Israel"
- **Jer 34:18** — "the men that have transgressed my covenant...when they cut the calf in twain, and passed between the parts"

## LXX Bridge

| Hebrew | Greek Translation | Count | Notes |
|--------|------------------|-------|-------|
| kârath (H3772) + berit | διατίθεμαι (G1303) | 71x | Primary verb for covenant-making contexts |
| kârath (H3772) + berit | διαθήκη (G1242) | 83x | LXX sometimes renders the phrase as the noun rather than the verb |
| kârath (H3772) cutting | ἐκκόπτω (G1581) | 13x | Used for excision/judgment senses |

**Semantic shift in translation:** The Greek **diatithemi** means "to arrange, dispose, appoint" — it lacks the "cutting" imagery entirely. The LXX translators abstracted the concept from the ritual act to the legal effect. More significantly, the related noun **diatheke** (G1242) carries a second sense absent in Hebrew: a unilateral *last will and testament*, which becomes theologically exploited in Hebrews 9:16–17. The Hebrew *karat* (cutting) implied the oath-bond between living parties; the Greek *diatheke* can imply a posthumous disposition — setting up a NT argument that the covenant takes effect through the death of the covenant-maker.

**All NT occurrences of G1303:**

- **Hebrews 8:10** — "For this is the covenant that I will make (diatithemi) with the house of Israel after those days, saith the Lord" — direct quotation of Jer 31:33 (LXX)
- **Hebrews 9:16** — "For where a testament (diatheke) is, there must also of necessity be the death of the testator (diatithemi)" — playing on both senses of the word
- **Hebrews 9:17** — "For a testament is of force after men are dead: otherwise it is of no strength at all while the testator (diatithemi) liveth"
- **Hebrews 10:16** — "This is the covenant that I will make (diatithemi) with them after those days, saith the Lord" — second quotation of Jer 31:33

*Note: Heb 9:16–17 uses diatithemi as a participial noun ("testator"), exploiting the will-and-testament sense to argue that Christ's death was necessary to bring the new covenant into force.*

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same:**
- The fundamental act: God unilaterally initiating a covenant (as in Gen 15 where only God passes through)
- The parties: God and his people (Israel → church)
- The content: law written on hearts (Jer 31 → Heb 8–10)

**What shifts:**
- The "cutting" metaphor is lost in Greek; *diatithemi* abstracts to "disposition/appointment"
- The noun *diatheke* acquires the additional legal sense of a will/testament, which Hebrews 9 exploits: the covenant requires the death of the covenant-maker (Christ)
- The blood rite from OT covenant-cutting is now the blood of Christ (Luke 22:20 "the new testament in my blood") — the physical "cutting" finds its ultimate referent
