# Grammar Analysis: Matthew 24:15 ‖ Mark 13:14 — The Abomination of Desolation

**Scope:** Context-neutral grammatical analysis of the synoptic "abomination of desolation" logion. Compares the two parallel forms, identifies the explicit Danielic citation, the Matthew/Mark divergence on location (τόπῳ ἁγίῳ vs. ὅπου οὐ δεῖ), the participial gender-shift in Mark's manuscript tradition (neuter ἑστός ≈ masculine ἑστηκότα), and the lexical overlap with 2 Thess 2:4. Interpretive applications (preterist / futurist / historicist) are catalogued without adjudication.

---

## Text

### Matthew 24:15
**Greek (NA/TR):** Ὅταν οὖν ἴδητε τὸ βδέλυγμα τῆς ἐρημώσεως τὸ ῥηθὲν διὰ Δανιὴλ τοῦ προφήτου ἑστὸς ἐν τόπῳ ἁγίῳ, ὁ ἀναγινώσκων νοείτω,

**KJV:** When ye therefore shall see the abomination of desolation, spoken of by Daniel the prophet, stand in the holy place, (whoso readeth, let him understand:)

### Mark 13:14
**Greek (NA):** Ὅταν δὲ ἴδητε τὸ βδέλυγμα τῆς ἐρημώσεως ἑστηκότα ὅπου οὐ δεῖ, ὁ ἀναγινώσκων νοείτω, τότε οἱ ἐν τῇ Ἰουδαίᾳ φευγέτωσαν εἰς τὰ ὄρη,

**KJV:** But when ye shall see the abomination of desolation, spoken of by Daniel the prophet, standing where it ought not, (let him that readeth understand,) then let them that be in Judaea flee to the mountains:

(The KJV's "spoken of by Daniel the prophet" clause at Mark 13:14 follows the Byzantine / Majority reading, which harmonizes Mark to Matthew. The earliest Alexandrian witnesses — ℵ B L — omit τὸ ῥηθὲν ὑπὸ Δανιὴλ τοῦ προφήτου in Mark; the explicit citation is secure only in Matthew.)

## Key Grammatical Features

### 1. τὸ βδέλυγμα τῆς ἐρημώσεως — noun + genitive of description/apposition

- **Form:** article + neuter accusative noun + feminine genitive noun.
  - βδέλυγμα (G946) — neuter, "abomination / detestable thing"; LXX rendering of Heb. שִׁקּוּץ / תּוֹעֵבָה.
  - ἐρημώσεως (G2050) — feminine genitive of ἐρήμωσις, "desolation / devastation"; LXX rendering of Heb. שֹׁמֵם / שִׁמָּמָה.
- **Syntactic function of the genitive:** most naturally read as a **genitive of effect/result** ("the abomination that causes desolation") or **attributive/descriptive genitive** ("the desolating abomination"). Either rendering captures the Danielic idiom.
- **Citation identity:** the exact phrase τὸ βδέλυγμα τῆς ἐρημώσεως is the fixed LXX-Theodotion rendering at three Daniel loci:
  - **Dan 9:27** (Theod.) — βδέλυγμα τῶν ἐρημώσεων (plural genitive in some traditions) / βδέλυγμα ἐρημώσεως.
  - **Dan 11:31** (Theod.) — βδέλυγμα ἠφανισμένον / ἐρημώσεως.
  - **Dan 12:11** (Theod.) — βδέλυγμα ἐρημώσεως.
  Matthew's and Mark's wording matches the Theodotionic tradition more closely than the Old Greek, which tends to paraphrase (e.g., Dan 9:27 OG has βδέλυγμα τῶν ἐρημώσεων with different syntax). The phrase is therefore a **verbatim scriptural lemma** being re-cited, not a free paraphrase.
- **Significance:** Jesus uses Daniel's technical terminology. Whatever referent is intended, the grammatical identity of the phrase constrains it to whatever Daniel meant by the same phrase — reader competence is assumed (see §3 below).
- **Word study:** [G2050-eremosis](../../word-studies/G2050-eremosis.md); see also [G868-aphistemi](../../word-studies/G868-aphistemi.md) for cognate semantic field of "standing off / apostasy."

### 2. τὸ ῥηθὲν διὰ Δανιὴλ τοῦ προφήτου (Matt only) — articular aorist passive participle in citation formula

- **Form:** τὸ ῥηθὲν — **aorist passive participle, accusative singular neuter** of λέγω / εἶπον (G2046). The article τό is **in concord with τὸ βδέλυγμα** (acc. sg. neut.), making the whole participial phrase an attributive modifier of "the abomination."
- **Idiom:** τὸ ῥηθὲν διὰ [τοῦ προφήτου] is Matthew's **standard fulfillment-citation formula** (cf. Mat 1:22; 2:15, 17, 23; 3:3; 4:14; 8:17; 12:17; 13:35; 21:4; 27:9). The agent preposition is **διά + genitive** (the human prophet is the intermediate agent, with God as the implicit primary agent) rather than ὑπό + genitive — a characteristic Matthean marker that presupposes the **divine passive** behind the human oracle.
- **Grammar reference:** [divine-passive](../greek/divine-passive.md); [aorist-passive-indicative](../greek/aorist-passive-indicative.md) (same voice/tense in ptcp. form).
- **Textual note:** this clause is absent from Mark in the earliest Alexandrian witnesses (ℵ B L Θ 1 28 etc.); Byzantine tradition inserts it by harmonization. In Matthew it is undisputed.
- **Significance for exegesis:** Matthew's explicit authorial identification removes any ambiguity about intertextual reference — Jesus is cross-referencing **Daniel by name** and expects the reader to go look it up. The parenthetical ὁ ἀναγινώσκων νοείτω ("let the reader understand") reinforces this: the imperative νοείτω (pres. act. impv. 3sg of νοέω, G3539) calls for **comprehension through reading Daniel**, not merely listening to the spoken discourse.

### 3. Matthew ἑστὸς ἐν τόπῳ ἁγίῳ vs. Mark ἑστηκός/ἑστηκότα ὅπου οὐ δεῖ — location clauses

- **Matthew:** ἑστὸς ἐν τόπῳ ἁγίῳ.
  - ἑστός — **2nd perfect active participle, accusative singular neuter** of ἵστημι (G2476). Perfect-active "standing" is a **state-describing** aspect: the result of the completed action "has taken its stand / is now established in position" (see [participle-perfect-passive-state](../greek/participle-perfect-passive-state.md) for the analogous aspectual logic). Concord with neuter βδέλυγμα.
  - ἐν τόπῳ ἁγίῳ — prepositional phrase, ἐν + dative of place. τόπος (G5117) anarthrous + ἅγιος (G40) anarthrous. The anarthrous construction is anaphoric-qualitative: "in a place [characterized as] holy." In Septuagintal and NT idiom ὁ τόπος ὁ ἅγιος / τόπος ἅγιος overwhelmingly denotes **the Jerusalem temple or its precincts** (cf. Exo 26:33; Lev 6:16; 10:13 LXX; Acts 6:13; 21:28). Matthew's localization is therefore explicit: the Jerusalem sanctuary.
  - **Word study:** [G40-hagios](../../word-studies/G40-hagios.md); [TR-qodesh-hagios](../../word-studies/TR-qodesh-hagios.md); [G2476-histemi](../../word-studies/G2476-histemi.md).
- **Mark:** ἑστηκότα ὅπου οὐ δεῖ.
  - ἑστηκότα — perfect active participle of ἵστημι, accusative singular. The critical feature is **gender**: the text attested at NA27/28 reads **accusative singular MASCULINE** (V-RAP-ASM in our parser output), discordant with neuter βδέλυγμα. Earlier editions and many manuscripts read ἑστηκός (neut.) or ἑστώς (neut., as in Matthew). See §4 below on the variant.
  - ὅπου οὐ δεῖ — locative relative clause: ὅπου (G3699, rel. adv.) + negative οὐ + impersonal δεῖ (G1163, "it is necessary / ought"). Translates "where it ought not [to stand/be]." Mark's formulation is **deliberately unspecified**: no named sanctuary, only the negative indication that the βδέλυγμα occupies a place forbidden to it.
- **Contrast in force:**
  - Matthew **narrows** (ἐν τόπῳ ἁγίῳ → temple).
  - Mark **veils** (ὅπου οὐ δεῖ → any forbidden place, with the reader expected to decode via Danielic context + ὁ ἀναγινώσκων νοείτω).
  - Both use the same perfect-active participle of ἵστημι, which keeps the focus on a **settled, occupying posture** rather than a transient movement.

### 4. Masculine ἑστηκότα in Mark — concord mismatch and the personal-referent signal

- **The data (from the parse):** Mark's participle in the parser output is tagged **V-RAP-ASM (Perf Act Ptcp Acc Sg Masculine)**. But the head noun it modifies — τὸ βδέλυγμα — is **neuter**. Standard concord would require neuter (ἑστηκός or ἑστώς), as Matthew has.
- **Manuscript picture (summary):**
  - Masculine ἑστηκότα: B D L W Θ f¹ f¹³ 28 565 700 etc. — read by NA27/28, SBLGNT, UBS⁴.
  - Neuter ἑστηκός: A ℵ (original hand varies) and Majority Text — the grammatically "smoother" reading.
  - Perfect active neuter ἑστώς (matching Matthew): harmonistic minority.
  The **masculine is the *lectio difficilior***: scribes would regularize toward neuter concord; no scribe would introduce a gender mismatch unless it was original. By standard critical canons the masculine is more likely original to Mark.
- **Grammatical phenomenon:** concord *ad sensum* (**constructio ad sensum** / synesis). When a grammatically neuter collective or abstract term has a concrete personal referent, Greek can shift concord from grammatical gender to the **natural gender of the referent**. Compare:
  - τὸ παιδίον (neut.) + αὐτόν / αὐτῆς (masc./fem. pronouns) for a specific child.
  - τὸ πνεῦμα (neut.) + masculine relative ἐκεῖνος (Jhn 15:26; 16:13–14) signalling the personal Paraclete.
- **Exegetical inference (descriptive, not adjudicative):** Mark's masculine participle is widely read as a **grammatical signal** that the evangelist construes the βδέλυγμα as a *personal* entity — "the abomination (who is) standing where he ought not." The gender-shift is a lexically economical way to hint at a personal referent behind the abstract Danielic noun, without altering the scriptural lemma itself. This is the grammatical hook for all personal-referent identifications (Roman commander, Antiochene statue-installer, eschatological Antichrist, etc.) catalogued in §6.

### 5. Lexical / syntactic overlap with 2 Thess 2:4

2 Thess 2:4 (TR): ὁ ἀντικείμενος καὶ ὑπεραιρόμενος ἐπὶ πάντα λεγόμενον θεὸν ἢ σέβασμα, ὥστε αὐτὸν εἰς τὸν ναὸν τοῦ θεοῦ ὡς θεὸν **καθίσαι**, ἀποδεικνύντα ἑαυτὸν ὅτι ἐστὶ θεός.

**KJV:** "Who opposeth and exalteth himself above all that is called God, or that is worshipped; so that he as God **sitteth** in the temple of God, shewing himself that he is God."

Grammatical points of contact with the synoptic logion:

| Feature                          | Matt 24:15 / Mk 13:14                              | 2 Thess 2:4                                           |
|---------------------------------|----------------------------------------------------|-------------------------------------------------------|
| Location of the desecrating act | ἐν τόπῳ ἁγίῳ / ὅπου οὐ δεῖ                        | εἰς τὸν ναὸν τοῦ θεοῦ                                 |
| Verb of posture/occupation      | ἑστός / ἑστηκότα — perf. act. ptcp. ἵστημι (G2476)| καθίσαι — aorist act. inf. of καθίζω "to sit / be seated" |
| Aspect                          | Perfect = settled state of having taken position   | Aorist = the act of taking the seat (constative)      |
| Agent's relation to deity       | Implicit (via Danielic backdrop)                   | Explicit: ὡς θεὸν … ἀποδεικνύντα ἑαυτὸν ὅτι ἐστὶ θεός |
| Referent's gender               | Masc. ἑστηκότα in Mark → personal referent         | Explicit masculine: ὁ ἀντικείμενος, ὁ ἄνθρωπος τῆς ἀνομίας / ἁμαρτίας (v.3) |

- **Convergent features:** (i) a (personal) desecrator, (ii) **occupying God's sanctuary** (ναός / τόπος ἅγιος), (iii) in a **posture of settled presence** (stand / sit), (iv) in a relation to divine honor that Daniel and Paul both characterize as usurpation.
- **Divergent features:** (i) Jesus uses ἵστημι (stand); Paul uses καθίζω (sit). (ii) Jesus cites Daniel explicitly; Paul does not cite Daniel (though Danielic vocabulary pervades 2 Thess 2, e.g., ἀνομία / ἄνομος echoing Dan 7:25; 11:36–37).
- **Textual cross-reference:** [textual-variant-2thess-2-3](../greek/textual-variant-2thess-2-3.md) — establishes the TR/Byz reading "man of sin" vs. NA reading "man of lawlessness" without prejudicing identification.
- **Bearing on the synoptic passage:** Paul's text provides an intra-NT witness that at least *one* apostolic author read a Danielic desecration-motif as a *personal* figure located in *God's sanctuary* — consistent with (though not proving) the gender-shift reading of Mark 13:14. Conversely, the synoptic masculine participle in Mark provides a gospel-tradition parallel to Paul's explicit masculine subject.

### 6. Historical applications of the passage (catalogued, context-neutral)

Three major schools have applied the grammar above to different referents. The grammar itself does not discriminate between them; each must be judged on additional historical/hermeneutical grounds.

- **Preterist (already-fulfilled, 1st-century):**
  - *Roman-army / Titus AD 70 view:* the βδέλυγμα = the Roman legionary standards (with imperial images) carried into the temple precincts during the siege of Jerusalem. Luke 21:20 (κυκλουμένην ὑπὸ στρατοπέδων Ἰερουσαλήμ, "Jerusalem compassed with armies") is taken as an interpretive parallel supplied by the third evangelist, identifying the "abomination" with encircling armies. Fits Jesus's immediate warning to flee Judea (Mk 13:14b).
  - *Gaius Caligula AD 40 view:* the βδέλυγμα = the imperial statue Caligula ordered erected in the Jerusalem temple (Josephus *Ant.* 18.261 ff.; Philo *Legatio*). The attempt failed with Caligula's assassination, so this is usually treated as a near-miss type or prefiguration rather than fulfillment proper.
  - *Grammatical fit:* the masculine ἑστηκότα in Mark fits a personal commander/emperor better than abstract standards; the neuter Matthean ἑστός fits an object (statue/standards) as well as a person.
- **Futurist (yet-to-be-fulfilled):** the βδέλυγμα = a future eschatological figure (commonly identified with the Antichrist / the man of lawlessness of 2 Thess 2) who will occupy a rebuilt Jerusalem temple at the end of the age. Draws explicitly on the grammatical link (§5) between Matt 24:15 and 2 Thess 2:4, and on the masculine-participle cue in Mark for a personal referent. "Holy place" is read literally as the inner temple sanctuary.
- **Historicist:** the βδέλυγμα = a long-developing institutional usurpation within the church-age, often identified (in Reformation and post-Reformation Protestant exegesis) with the papal succession occupying the "temple of God" understood as the Christian Church (cf. Paul's ναός-of-God language for the church at 1 Cor 3:16–17; 2 Cor 6:16; Eph 2:21). "Holy place" is read typologically as the ecclesial sanctuary rather than the Herodian temple. The perfect-active ἵστημι ("has taken its stand and continues to stand") is taken to fit an enduring institutional presence rather than a brief historical episode.
- **Shared grammatical constraints (binding on all three):**
  1. The phrase is a **citation** of Daniel, not a free coinage — any referent must be compatible with Danielic usage (9:27 / 11:31 / 12:11 LXX-Theod.).
  2. The **perfect-active** aspect of ἵστημι implies a **settled, occupying presence**, not a transient act.
  3. The **masculine participle in Mark** (if original) **biases toward a personal referent**, or at minimum permits one.
  4. Matthew's **τόπῳ ἁγίῳ** restricts the setting to a place characterized as "holy" — naturally a sanctuary, whether the Herodian temple, a rebuilt temple, or the church construed as temple.

## Clause Structure

Both verses share a **ὅταν-protasis + imperatival/reader-address apodosis** backbone:

### Matthew 24:15
```
Protasis:   Ὅταν οὖν ἴδητε [τὸ βδέλυγμα τῆς ἐρημώσεως
                            τὸ ῥηθὲν διὰ Δανιὴλ τοῦ προφήτου
                            ἑστὸς ἐν τόπῳ ἁγίῳ]
                            — "whenever you see [the abomination... standing in a holy place]"
Parenthesis: ὁ ἀναγινώσκων νοείτω  ("let the reader understand")
Apodosis:   [v.16] τότε οἱ ἐν τῇ Ἰουδαίᾳ φευγέτωσαν εἰς τὰ ὄρη
```

Matthew's object noun-phrase is *triple-modified*:
1. τῆς ἐρημώσεως (genitive of effect/description).
2. τὸ ῥηθὲν διὰ Δανιὴλ τοῦ προφήτου (attributive aor. pass. ptcp. phrase, concord with βδέλυγμα).
3. ἑστὸς ἐν τόπῳ ἁγίῳ (attributive perf. act. ptcp. phrase, concord with βδέλυγμα).
All three modifiers are accusative singular neuter, agreeing with τὸ βδέλυγμα.

### Mark 13:14
```
Protasis:   Ὅταν δὲ ἴδητε [τὸ βδέλυγμα τῆς ἐρημώσεως
                           ἑστηκότα (masc.!) ὅπου οὐ δεῖ]
Parenthesis: ὁ ἀναγινώσκων νοείτω
Apodosis:   τότε οἱ ἐν τῇ Ἰουδαίᾳ φευγέτωσαν εἰς τὰ ὄρη
```

Mark embeds the apodosis (τότε… φευγέτωσαν) *in the same verse*, tightening the pragmatic force: the sight = the trigger to flee. Mark's modifier stack is shorter (no citation formula; a locative relative clause replaces Matthew's prepositional phrase). The masculine ἑστηκότα breaks the elegant Matthean concord chain and thereby foregrounds the participle.

### OT / LXX parallels for τὸ βδέλυγμα τῆς ἐρημώσεως
- **Dan 9:27** — "for the overspreading of abominations he shall make it desolate" (KJV); LXX-Theod. ἕως συντελείας καιροῦ συντέλεια δοθήσεται ἐπὶ τὴν ἐρήμωσιν.
- **Dan 11:31** — "they shall place the abomination that maketh desolate" (KJV); LXX-Theod. καὶ δώσουσιν βδέλυγμα ἠφανισμένον.
- **Dan 12:11** — "the abomination that maketh desolate set up" (KJV); LXX-Theod. καὶ ἀπὸ καιροῦ παραλλάξεως τοῦ ἐνδελεχισμοῦ καὶ τοῦ δοθῆναι βδέλυγμα ἐρημώσεως.
- **1 Macc 1:54** (historical application at the Antiochene desecration, 167 BC) — ᾠκοδόμησαν βδέλυγμα ἐρημώσεως ἐπὶ τὸ θυσιαστήριον. Uses the identical phrase to describe Antiochus IV's pagan altar on the Jerusalem altar of burnt offering — an early-2nd-century-BC Jewish reading of Daniel's phrase as fulfilled in a concrete sanctuary-desecration event. Relevant as historical precedent but does not itself adjudicate the synoptic application.
