**Semantic shift in translation:** In Hebrew, H3027 (yad) and H3709 (kaph) are distinct words for distinct aspects of the hand (extended vs. cupped). The LXX collapses both into χείρ, losing this distinction. NT χείρ therefore covers the semantic range of both Hebrew words without differentiating them. Additionally, many Hebrew idioms built on yad (e.g., "deliver into the hand of," "stretch out the hand") are sometimes rendered idiomatically in Greek (as verbs like παραδίδωμι or ἐκτείνω) rather than preserving the literal χείρ.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- χείρ preserves the full OT semantic breadth of yad: anatomical hand, divine power/agency, healing/sacramental touch, custody/transfer of authority, and judgment
- "The hand of the LORD" theology from the OT (Exo 9:3; 1Sa 5:6; Isa 40:2) carries directly into NT language about the Father's hand (Jhn 10:28–29; 1Pe 5:6; Heb 10:31)
- The "laying on of hands" ritual (OT ordination/sacrifice: Lev 1:4; Num 8:10) continues as a NT sacramental practice (Act 8:17–19; 1Ti 4:14; Heb 6:2)

**What shifts:**
- OT emphasizes the divine hand in salvation history (Exodus, conquest); NT applies this to Jesus' miracles and apostolic signs
- The Pauline epistles introduce the autograph motif — χείρ as personal signature authenticating letters (Gal 6:11; Col 4:18; 2Th 3:17), with no OT parallel
- NT adds the apocalyptic "mark in the hand" motif (Rev 14:9; 20:4) — a symbolic counter-image to the divine seal
