## Lexicon Definition

> *"From an unused root in the sense of expulsion; must or fresh grape-juice (as just squeezed out); by implication (rarely) fermented wine: — (new, sweet) wine."*

The primary lexical sense is the freshly expressed juice of the grape (Latin *mustum*), the product as it comes from the press. Strong's notes that the fermented sense is only *rare and by implication*, not the core meaning. It is typically distinguished from יַיִן (*yayin*, H3196), which is the general word for wine (fermented) and derived from a root meaning "to effervesce."

## Semantic Categories

### 1. The Triad: Corn (dagan) + Tirosh + Oil (yitshar) — Agricultural Produce
The dominant usage (appearing in over half of all occurrences). Tirosh stands in the second position of a fixed produce-triad representing the three staple yields of the land: grain, grape-product, olive-product. In every case the three are listed as agricultural commodities — not drinks served at tables.

- **Deut 7:13** — "thy corn, and thy wine (tirosh), and thine oil"
- **Deut 11:14** — "thou mayest gather in thy corn, and thy wine (tirosh), and thine oil"
- **Hos 2:8** — "I gave her corn, and wine (tirosh), and oil"
- **Joel 2:19** — "I will send you corn, and wine (tirosh), and oil"
- **Hag 1:11** — "drought... upon the corn, and upon the new wine (tirosh), and upon the oil"

### 2. Firstfruits and Tithe Offerings
Tirosh is repeatedly the object of offerings brought to the LORD — part of the produce tithed, not the cup poured. The parallel with raw grain and raw oil (both pre-processed) strongly suggests the product is *in its unprocessed state*.

- **Num 18:12** — "the best of the wine (tirosh)... the firstfruits of them which they shall offer"
- **Deut 18:4** — "The firstfruit also of thy corn, of thy wine (tirosh), and of thine oil"
- **Neh 10:37, 39** — tirosh brought to the storehouse chambers of the sanctuary
- **Neh 13:12** — "the tithe of the corn and the new wine (tirosh) and the oil unto the treasuries"
- **2 Chr 31:5** — "firstfruits of corn, wine (tirosh), and oil, and honey"

### 3. Harvest / Vintage-Press Product
Tirosh is what fills presses and vats, what is dried up by drought, what "fails" when the harvest fails — i.e. the product-in-production, grape juice as it comes from the treading.

- **Pro 3:10** — "thy presses shall burst out with new wine (tirosh)"
- **Joel 2:24** — "the fats shall overflow with wine (tirosh) and oil"
- **Isa 65:8** — "As the new wine (tirosh) is found in the cluster" (still inside the grape!)
- **Joel 1:10** — "the corn is wasted: the new wine (tirosh) is dried up"
- **Hos 9:2** — "the floor and the winepress shall not feed them, and the new wine (tirosh) shall fail"

### 4. Covenant Blessing (abundance of the land)
As part of the rhetoric of covenantal prosperity — what the land yields under God's favor.

- **Gen 27:28** — "plenty of corn and wine (tirosh)" (Isaac's blessing)
- **Deut 33:28** — "a land of corn and wine (tirosh)"
- **2 Ki 18:32 / Isa 36:17** — "a land of corn and wine (tirosh)" (Rabshakeh's parody of the covenant formula)
- **Zech 9:17** — "corn shall make the young men cheerful, and new wine (tirosh) the maids"
- **Jer 31:12** — "for wheat, and for wine (tirosh), and for oil"

### 5. Drinkable / Heart-Affecting Uses (rare)
A small set of verses use tirosh where the product is clearly drunk or has an effect on the drinker. These are the texts appealed to for the "fermented" sense.

- **Judg 9:13** — "Should I leave my wine (tirosh), which cheereth God and man" (Jotham's parable; in parallel with Judg 9:9-11 oil/fig/vine personifications — the vine's *product*, not necessarily the vine's *fermented juice*)
- **Hos 4:11** — "Whoredom and wine (yayin) and new wine (tirosh) take away the heart" — paired here with yayin; the juxtaposition suggests either (a) intensifying synonymy in a condemnation of drunkenness, or (b) that *the harvest itself* (agricultural abundance → complacency) takes away the heart, paralleling the broader Hosea theme (Hos 2:8) that Israel confused God's agricultural gifts with Baal's blessing
- **Mic 6:15** — "thou shalt tread the olives... and sweet wine (tirosh), but shalt not drink wine (yayin)" — here tirosh is what is *trodden/produced*, and yayin is what is *drunk*; the verse distinguishes them
- **Hos 7:14** — "they assemble themselves for corn and wine (tirosh)" (context: agricultural provision, not carousing)

Within the Hebrew-only corpus, only Hos 4:11 unambiguously implies an intoxicating effect, and even there the parallelism with yayin is what carries the fermentation sense. No verse clearly requires tirosh alone, apart from yayin, to mean intoxicating drink.

**The yayin / tirosh contrast** is the central question for the "alcoholic wine?" debate. The two words are not synonyms in classical Hebrew usage:
- *yayin* is what Noah was drunk on (Gen 9:21), what Lot's daughters used (Gen 19), what Nazirites forbid (Num 6:3 — where they abstain from *both*, showing they are distinct), what Nabal drank (1 Sam 25:37), what priests must not drink on duty (Lev 10:9).
- *tirosh* is what the presses burst with (Pro 3:10), what is "in the cluster" (Isa 65:8), what fills the vats at harvest (Joel 2:24), what is offered as firstfruits (Deut 18:4). It is never the object of a drinking verb without qualification; it is never forbidden to priests or Nazirites.

**Numbers 6:3** is decisive: the Nazirite is to "separate himself from *yayin* and *shekar* (strong drink)" — tirosh is not mentioned, because tirosh as such is not the category of intoxicating drink the vow addresses.

The LXX uniformly flattens the yayin/tirosh distinction by using οἶνος for both — one reason the nuance is lost in NT Greek readers unfamiliar with the Hebrew distribution. A handful of occurrences render tirosh with specific juice-terms (e.g. γένημα τῆς ἀμπέλου, μέθυσμα in varying contexts), but οἶνος is dominant. This LXX levelling underlies much of the interpretive difficulty: NT readers who approach "wine" through Greek do not see the Hebrew produce/drink split.
