**Semantic shift in translation:** Virtually none. Patassō preserves nakah's full range: battlefield smiting, divine plague, striking of objects (rock, water, dust), prophetic sign-acts, and the Messianic smitten-shepherd motif. The LXX's choice of patassō is one of the cleanest Hebrew-to-Greek verb bridges in the OT.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The **full semantic breadth** of nakah survives: physical blow, divine plague, prophetic sign, judicial strike. Patassō carries all four into the NT.
- The **divine-judgment blow** idiom ("the LORD smote / the angel of the Lord smote") passes through unchanged — Acts 12:23 (Herod) reads as a pure OT-style nakah scene in Greek.
- The **Messianic smitten-shepherd** in Zech 13:7 is quoted *verbatim* in Matt 26:31 / Mark 14:27, making this one of the most important single-verb OT→NT bridges in the Passion narrative.

**What shifts:**
- NT usage is **narrower in frequency** (10 occurrences vs. 460) — the NT simply has less battlefield and covenant-curse narrative to describe.
- The NT leans toward **supernatural / eschatological** striking: angelic blows (Acts 12:7, 12:23), the two witnesses (Rev 11:6), the Messianic warrior (Rev 19:15). Only Matt 26:51 / Luke 22:49–50 use patassō for ordinary human striking (Peter's sword in Gethsemane).
- The **Exodus-plague vocabulary** resurfaces eschatologically in Revelation — patassō + plēgē echoes how nakah + makkah functioned in Exodus 7–12.
