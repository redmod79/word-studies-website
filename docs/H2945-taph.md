# H2945 — טַף (ṭaph)

## Core Data
- **Strong's:** H2945
- **Original:** טַף
- **Transliteration:** ṭaph
- **Pronunciation:** taf
- **Part of Speech:** Masculine noun (collective)
- **Root:** Traditionally derived from טָפַף (ṭāphaph, H2952) — "to trip, take short steps" (referring to the tripping gait of small children); used collectively
- **Occurrences:** 42 unique verses (43 tokens) across 12 OT books
- **First Occurrence:** Gen 34:29 — "And all their wealth, and all their little ones (ṭaph), and their wives took they captive"
- **Hapax Legomenon:** No

## Lexicon Definition

From H2952 (perhaps referring to the tripping gait of children); a family (mostly used collectively in the singular): — (little) children (ones), families.

The term is almost always collective — a group of non-combatant dependents attached to a household, traveling group, city, or army's rear. It names a social category rather than a specific age cohort: the **dependents who cannot march, fight, or fend for themselves**. In most contexts this means young children, but by extension the word can sweep in the weaker household members generally (very young, sometimes women and the infirm) who must be transported, fed, protected, or disposed of alongside the children proper.

**Concentration pattern:** The word is overwhelmingly a **Torah-and-conquest** term. Genesis–Joshua account for 30 of 42 occurrences (~71%). Its two densest books (Numbers, Deuteronomy) are precisely the books of wilderness travel, tribal mustering, and conquest legislation — contexts where "who counts as a dependent vs. a combatant" is a pressing administrative question. After Joshua the word becomes rare and clusters around scenes of deportation, flight, or pogrom (Jeremiah 40–43, Ezekiel 9, Esther 3/8). Wisdom literature and the prophets (apart from Jer/Ezek) never use it.

## Semantic Categories

### 1. Non-combatants left in safety while men go to war
The classic "stay-behind" use: fighting men depart for battle while the ṭaph remain in the camp, cities, or protected territory.
- **Num 32:16–17, 24, 26** — Reubenites/Gadites: "We will build sheepfolds here for our cattle, and cities for our little ones (ṭaph)... but we ourselves will go ready armed before the children of Israel."
- **Deut 3:19** — "But your wives, and your little ones (ṭaph), and your cattle... shall abide in your cities which I have given you."
- **Josh 1:14** — "Your wives, your little ones (ṭaph), and your cattle, shall remain in the land which Moses gave you on this side Jordan."
- **Deut 20:14** — the exception to herem, sparing civilians of distant cities: "But the women, and the little ones (ṭaph), and the cattle... shalt thou take unto thyself."

### 2. Dependents taken as spoil or captives in war
When a city falls and the women/children are seized rather than killed, ṭaph is the word for the child-category of the plunder.
- **Gen 34:29** — Simeon and Levi's sack of Shechem: "all their wealth, and all their little ones (ṭaph), and their wives took they captive."
- **Num 31:9** — Midianite war: "the children of Israel took all the women of Midian captives, and their little ones (ṭaph)."
- **Deut 20:14** — the Deuteronomic law of siege: ṭaph explicitly named among permitted spoil.
- **Judg 21:10** — Jabesh-gilead: "smite the inhabitants... with the edge of the sword, with the women and the children (ṭaph)."

### 3. Dependents killed under ḥerem / total-destruction warfare
In the Transjordan conquests and certain later judgment scenes, the ṭaph are not spared but put to the sword with everyone else.
- **Num 31:17** — "Now therefore kill every male among the little ones (ṭaph), and kill every woman that hath known man by lying with him."
- **Deut 2:34** — Sihon: "we took all his cities... and utterly destroyed the men, and the women, and the little ones (ṭaph), of every city."
- **Deut 3:6** — Og of Bashan: "utterly destroying the men, women, and children (ṭaph), of every city."
- **Ezek 9:6** — Jerusalem judgment vision: "Slay utterly old and young, both maids, and little children (ṭaph), and women."

### 4. Dependents carried along on a journey / migration
The ṭaph travel with the adults — transported, nourished, counted among those "going up" or "going down."
- **Gen 43:8** — Judah to Israel: "we may live, and not die, both we, and thou, and also our little ones (ṭaph)."
- **Gen 45:19** — Pharaoh: "take you wagons out of the land of Egypt for your little ones (ṭaph), and for your wives, and bring your father, and come."
- **Gen 46:5** — "the sons of Israel carried Jacob their father, and their little ones (ṭaph), and their wives, in the wagons."
- **Exod 10:10, 24** — Pharaoh vs. Moses over who leaves Egypt: the ṭaph are the sticking point.
- **Ezra 8:21** — Ezra fasts at the Ahava "to seek of him a right way for us, and for our little ones (ṭaph), and for all our substance."

### 5. Dependents protected / provided for within the covenant community
The ṭaph are included in covenant gatherings, hearings of Torah, and provision — part of "the people" before God.
- **Deut 29:11** — covenant renewal: "Your little ones (ṭaph), your wives, and thy stranger that is in thy camp."
- **Deut 31:12** — Torah reading: "Gather the people together, men, and women, and children (ṭaph), and thy stranger."
- **Josh 8:35** — at Mount Ebal: Joshua read the law "before all the congregation of Israel, with the women, and the little ones (ṭaph), and the strangers."
- **Gen 47:12, 50:21** — Joseph "nourished" his father's house / his brothers "and your little ones (ṭaph)."
- **2 Chr 20:13** — "all Judah stood before the LORD, with their little ones (ṭaph), their wives, and their children" before Jehoshaphat's battle.

### 6. Dependents threatened with annihilation in a pogrom / mass-execution decree
Late-OT scenes where an enemy decree targets entire civilian populations, ṭaph included.
- **Esth 3:13** — Haman's decree: "to destroy, to kill, and to cause to perish, all Jews, both young and old, little children (ṭaph) and women, in one day."
- **Esth 8:11** — counter-decree permits Jews "to destroy... all the power of the people and province that would assault them, little ones (ṭaph) and women."
- **Jer 41:16, 43:6** — remnant flight and deportation: the ṭaph are dragged along with men, women, king's daughters.
