## Lexicon Definition

From ἀπό (G575) + δείκνυμι (G1166); to show off, exhibit; figuratively, to demonstrate, prove, proclaim, nominate, or appoint by public display. The compound carries the sense of setting something forth publicly so that its nature or identity is made evident — "to show to be such" / "to publicly designate as." Used in classical and Koine Greek for the public appointment of officials, the demonstration of a proposition, and the public exhibition of persons or things.

## Semantic Categories

### 1. Publicly Designated / Accredited by God
The perfect-passive participle in Acts 2:22 means "having been demonstrated / accredited" — Jesus is one whom God publicly certified by mighty works. The state is settled and publicly visible. Translators: "approved" (KJV), "attested" (RSV/ESV), "accredited" (NIV).
- **Acts 2:22** — "Jesus of Nazareth, a man approved (ἀποδεδειγμένον) of God among you by miracles and wonders and signs, which God did by him in the midst of you"

### 2. To Prove (Forensic / Legal Demonstration)
In court language, to establish a charge by producing evidence. Acts 25:7 uses the aorist infinitive of what the accusers could not do.
- **Acts 25:7** — "many and grievous complaints against Paul, which they could not prove (ἀποδεῖξαι)"

### 3. To Set Forth Publicly (Exhibit as a Spectacle)
Aorist active: God "set forth" the apostles in a public display. The background image is the Roman arena or triumphal procession where prisoners/gladiators are displayed last (ἐσχάτους), destined for death. The word carries the sense of theatrical exhibition.
- **1 Cor 4:9** — "God hath set forth (ἀπέδειξεν) us the apostles last, as it were appointed to death: for we are made a spectacle (θέατρον) unto the world, and to angels, and to men"

### 4. To Proclaim/Display Oneself As (Reflexive + ὅτι Clause)
Present active participle with reflexive pronoun ἑαυτὸν and a ὅτι-clause: a construction meaning "displaying himself to be / proclaiming himself that he is." The ὅτι introduces the content of the self-declaration. This is the man-of-sin's public self-designation as God.
- **2 Thess 2:4** — "so that he as God sitteth in the temple of God, shewing himself (ἀποδεικνύντα ἑαυτὸν) that he is God (ὅτι ἐστὶν Θεός)"

**Grammatical note on the reflexive + ὅτι construction:** The combination ἀποδείκνυμι + reflexive pronoun + ὅτι-clause functions as a public claim or self-proclamation to a particular identity. The verb's sense is "to cause to be recognized as" — so ἀποδεικνύντα ἑαυτὸν ὅτι ἐστὶν Θεός = "proclaiming himself (to all) that he is God," "setting himself forth as being God," "putting on public display his claim to be God." Compare the opposite case in Acts 2:22 where it is *God* who ἀποδείκνυσιν (accredits) another (Jesus); here the figure ἀποδείκνυσιν himself — a usurpation pattern.

## Syntactic Pattern: Reflexive + ὅτι-Clause (2 Thess 2:4)

The construction ἀποδείκνυμι + ἑαυτόν + ὅτι + indicative is distinctive enough to note as a canonical feature:

- **Form:** verb of public display + reflexive pronoun (self as object) + ὅτι-clause (content of the display)
- **Function:** self-proclamation — publicly setting oneself forth as something
- **Force:** not merely claiming in speech (λέγω), but staging a public demonstration designed to establish the identity
- **Parallel pattern:** The passive in Acts 2:22 (God accredits Jesus) is the theological inverse — legitimate accreditation comes *from* God *upon* another; the 2 Thess 2:4 figure performs the accreditation *upon himself*.

## Classical / Extra-Biblical Usage Notes

In classical Greek (Herodotus, Thucydides, Plato, Demosthenes), ἀποδείκνυμι commonly means:
1. To appoint, nominate to office (e.g., magistrates "appointed")
2. To demonstrate, prove (in argumentation/rhetoric — hence ἀπόδειξις = logical proof)
3. To display publicly, produce for inspection
4. To declare or proclaim someone as something

This same semantic spread is visible across the four NT tokens: accredit/approve (Acts 2:22), prove in court (Acts 25:7), exhibit publicly (1 Cor 4:9), proclaim-oneself-as (2 Thess 2:4).
