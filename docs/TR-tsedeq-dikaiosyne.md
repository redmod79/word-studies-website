**Semantic continuity:** The LXX translators chose dikaiosyne as the primary rendering for both tsedeq and tsedaqah. This is a stable noun-to-noun mapping with minimal semantic drift — the Greek preserves both the abstract standard (tsedeq) and the concrete acts (tsedaqah) under a single term. The dual coverage of tsedeq and tsedaqah by one Greek word means the NT use of dikaiosyne has inherited both the normative (what is right) and the active (right-doing) aspects of the Hebrew concept.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The association of dikaiosyne/tsedeq with God's character ("habitation of his throne," Ps 89:14 → "the righteousness of God," Rom 1:17)
- The pairing with judgment/krima (mishpat–tsedeq → krisis–dikaiosyne)
- The eschatological dimension: OT pouring-down of righteousness (Isa 45:8) → NT kingdom righteousness (Matt 6:33)
- Righteousness as both God's attribute and the standard for human conduct

**What shifts:**
- In the OT, tsedeq primarily describes the normative standard (what is right) in legal, social, and cosmic contexts. NT dikaiosyne gains a forensic justification dimension (especially in Paul) — a righteousness *imputed* or credited to the believer (Rom 4:3–6), building on but extending the OT usage.
- The tsedaqah/charity sense (almsgiving, concrete beneficence) common in post-biblical Hebrew does not dominate NT dikaiosyne, which skews more toward the Pauline forensic/ethical sense.
