## Lexicon Definition

From ἀθετέω (G114); cancellation (literally or figuratively): disannulling, put away.

The word is an action-noun (suffix -σις) from the verb ἀθετέω, which means "to regard as not placed" (α + τίθημι) — i.e., to treat as invalid, null, or void. The noun denotes the act or result of setting aside, abrogating, or annulling something previously established.

## Semantic Categories

### 1. Legal Abrogation — Setting Aside a Prior Ordinance
- **Heb 7:18** — "For there is verily a disannulling (athetēsis) of the commandment going before for the weakness and unprofitableness thereof."

The context is the replacement of the Levitical priesthood by the Melchizedek order. The "commandment going before" (proagousēs entolēs) refers to the Mosaic regulation establishing the Aaronic priesthood (cf. Heb 7:11-16). Athetēsis here is a juridical act: formal annulment of a legal provision because it proved "weak and unprofitable" (asthenēs kai anōphelēs, v. 18b).

### 2. Elimination / Removal — Putting Away Sin
- **Heb 9:26** — "but now once in the end of the world hath he appeared to put away (eis athetēsin) sin by the sacrifice of himself."

Here eis athetēsin + genitive (hamartias) expresses purpose: "for the cancellation of sin." The construction parallels the legal usage in 7:18 — sin is treated as something with standing that must be formally set aside or annulled. The means specified is "by the sacrifice of himself" (dia tēs thysias autou). The word "once" (hapax) and the temporal phrase "in the end of the world" (epi synteleia tōn aiōnōn) underscore the unrepeatable, eschatological finality of this cancellation.
