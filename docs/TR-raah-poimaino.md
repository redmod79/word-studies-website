**Semantic distribution in LXX:** The LXX translators used the poimen/poimaino word family as the primary rendering of ra'ah. The verb poimaino captures ra'ah's active sense (the act of shepherding), while the noun poimen captures the participial sense (the shepherd as person). Bosko is used for the more purely physical "feed/graze" aspect.

The Ps 2:9 quotation is significant: the MT has תְּרֹעֵם (tero'em, from ra'a = "break"), but the LXX read it as תִּרְעֵם (tir'em, from ra'ah = "shepherd"), producing ποιμανεῖς. The NT consistently follows the LXX reading, yielding "shepherd/rule" rather than "break."

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The shepherd metaphor for leadership and governance continues from OT to NT
- The dual sense of caring provision and authoritative rule persists in both ra'ah and poimaino
- God/Messiah as ultimate shepherd is consistent (Ps 23 → Rev 7:17)

**What shifts:**
- In the OT, ra'ah is predominantly literal (actual flocks) with significant metaphorical use for leaders; in the NT, poimaino is overwhelmingly figurative (only 2 of 11 uses are literal)
- The "rod of iron" formula (Rev 2:27, 12:5, 19:15) pushes poimaino toward firm authoritative rule, a meaning less prominent in typical OT ra'ah usage
- NT ecclesiology applies the shepherd role to church elders (Acts 20:28, 1Pet 5:2), a direct extension of OT prophetic critiques of unfaithful shepherds (Ezek 34)
