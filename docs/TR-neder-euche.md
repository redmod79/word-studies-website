**Semantic shift in translation:** Continuity is strong — εὐχή inherits the full Hebrew semantic field of vow-as-promise and vow-as-offering. The one expansion: classical Greek εὐχή already carried the sense "prayer / wish," so LXX translators adopted a word that naturally blurs "vow" and "prayer." This blur surfaces in Jas 5:15 (see below) where the same noun is rendered "prayer."

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The vow is directed toward God, never toward humans.
- Fulfillment ("pay the vow") remains the indispensable complement of making one.
- The Nazirite category of Numbers 6 is preserved intact into Acts 18 and 21.
- Voluntariness combined with binding obligation — the same paradox in both Testaments.

**What shifts:**
- Frequency collapses: 57 OT verses → only 3 NT uses of εὐχή. Vowing recedes as a central piety.
- εὐχή broadens to absorb "prayer" more fully (Jas 5:15), reflecting a general NT drift from cultic vow to inward petition.
- In Paul's Acts usage the vow is personal and narrative, not legislated — the Torah's elaborate women's-vows framework (Num 30) has no NT counterpart.
- ὁμολογία (confession / pledge) partly takes over the "public commitment to God" territory formerly held by neder (cf. Heb 3:1; 10:23).
