## Lexicon Definition

From Strong's: "from ἐκ and ὀλοθρεύω; to extirpate — destroy." The compound intensifies the base verb: not merely to destroy, but to destroy utterly, to root out completely, to exterminate from a group. The prefix ἐκ- adds the sense of "out from among" — the destruction removes the object entirely from a body or community.

## Semantic Categories

The NT occurrence is singular. Categories below are derived from the LXX usage pattern that informs Peter's citation, which the word study library's H3772-karath study documents in depth.

### 1. Judicial "Cut Off" from the Covenant Community
The LXX routinely uses ἐξολοθρεύω to render Hebrew כָּרַת (karath, H3772) and שָׁמַד (shamad, H8045) in the Pentateuchal formula "that soul shall be cut off from his people" — penalties for covenant violations (Lev 17:4, 17:9, 17:14; 18:29; 19:8; 20:3–6; 23:29; Num 9:13; 15:30–31).
- **Acts 3:23** — "every soul, which will not hear that prophet, shall be destroyed (ἐξολεθρευθήσεται) from among the people (ἐκ τοῦ λαοῦ)" — Peter quotes Deut 18:19 in the LXX-inflected form of Lev 23:29 ("cut off from his people"), applying the mosaic excision penalty to rejection of the prophet-like-Moses, i.e. the Messiah.

### 2. Total Extermination / Extirpation (LXX background)
In LXX historical and prophetic material the verb denotes complete destruction of nations, cities, or persons (e.g., Deut 4:3; 28:63; Josh 7:12; 1 Sam 15:18; Ps 36:9 LXX).

### 3. Divine Judgment in Prophetic Warnings (LXX background)
Used of Yahweh's threatened judgments on idolaters and covenant-breakers, often paired with καταρά or ὀργή.

*These categories are inferred from the word's LXX ancestry; the lone NT use (Acts 3:23) compresses all three senses into one verdict.*

## OT Quotation Background

Acts 3:22–23 is a composite citation:
- Acts 3:22 ≈ Deut 18:15–18 (prophet-like-Moses promise)
- Acts 3:23a ≈ Deut 18:19 (accountability for not hearing)
- Acts 3:23b ≈ Lev 23:29 LXX verbatim ("ἐξολεθρευθήσεται ἐκ τοῦ λαοῦ")

Lev 23:29 LXX originally threatened excision for anyone who would not "afflict himself" on the Day of Atonement. Peter re-applies this Yom Kippur penalty to the rejection of the risen Messiah, implicitly identifying response to Jesus with the central atonement-day posture.
