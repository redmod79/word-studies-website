Greek soteria absorbs three distinct Hebrew nouns (yeshuah, teshuah, yesha) plus the root verb yasha into a single Greek term. The Hebrew distinctions between abstract state (yeshuah), concrete rescue (teshuah), and safe condition (yesha) collapse in the LXX.

**Semantic shift in translation:** The LXX preserves the primary meaning well. Both Hebrew and Greek terms center on deliverance/rescue by God. The occasional LXX rendering of yeshuah as ἀγαλλίασις (exultation, 10x) and δικαιοσύνη (righteousness, 8x) shows the translators sometimes rendered the associated effect (joy, righteous standing) rather than the concept itself.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- Salvation belongs to and comes from God — the theocentric framing persists from Gen 49:18 through Rev 19:1
- The word retains its comprehensive scope: physical deliverance, spiritual rescue, eschatological hope
- The armor metaphor (Isa 59:17 → 1Th 5:8) transfers intact from YHWH's own armor to the believer's

**What shifts:**
- OT yeshuah is overwhelmingly national/corporate (Israel's deliverance); NT soteria tilts toward individual faith response ("to every one that believeth," Rom 1:16)
- OT usage is concentrated in poetry/worship (83% in Psalms+Isaiah); NT usage spreads across epistolary theology
- The three Hebrew nouns (yeshuah, teshuah, yesha) collapse into one Greek term, losing the nuanced distinctions between abstract state, concrete rescue, and safe condition
