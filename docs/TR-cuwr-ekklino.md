**Note:** The LXX also uses ἀφίστημι (G868, 57×) as a near-equal primary bridge. The two Greek words divide sur's semantic range: ekklino captures "turn aside from a path" and "deviate from right conduct," while aphistemi captures "depart from" and "stand away from" (see [G868-aphistemi.md](G868-aphistemi.md)).

**Semantic mapping in translation:** The LXX translators chose ekklino when sur described directional deviation (turning aside from a path or from moral conduct). They chose aphistemi when sur described departure from a person, place, or relationship. The Hiphil causative forms ("remove, take away") were rendered with peri/aphaireo. This three-way split shows the LXX recognized three distinct semantic cores within the single Hebrew verb.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The core meaning of moral deviation — turning aside from what is right — passes through intact from Hebrew to Greek in both testaments
- The "depart from evil" formula (sur meraʻ → ekklino apo kakou) is preserved verbatim through LXX into 1 Pet 3:11
- Rom 3:12 quotes the Hebrew diagnostic of universal moral failure (Ps 14:3 "they have all turned aside") using the same ekklino

**What shifts:**
- The Hebrew sur has a vast semantic range (physical turning, departure, causative removal, ritual removal), but only the moral-deviation slice survives into the NT through ekklino
- The Hiphil causative "remove/take away" sense does not transfer through ekklino at all — it goes through different Greek words (periaireo, aphaireo)
- The NT ekklino is exclusively moral/relational (deviate from God's way, avoid false teachers, shun evil) — the literal spatial "turn into a house" sense of Hebrew sur is absent
