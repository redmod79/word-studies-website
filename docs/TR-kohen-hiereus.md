Lexicon: Active participle of an unused root meaning "to officiate"; literally "one officiating." Used for Aaronic/Levitical priests, for the pre-Mosaic priest Melchizedek (Gen 14:18), and occasionally for foreign/pagan priests (Gen 47:22; Exo 2:16).

**Key OT passages:**
- **Gen 14:18** — "And Melchizedek king of Salem...he was the priest of the most high God" — pre-Mosaic priesthood; the Hebrews text will anchor the NT Melchizedek argument
- **Lev 1:5** — "the priests, Aaron's sons, shall bring the blood" — inauguration of Aaronic order
- **Num 3:3** — "the sons of Aaron, the priests which were anointed, whom he consecrated" — ordination language
- **Deu 17:9** — "thou shalt come unto the priests the Levites, and unto the judge" — priestly authority in civil matters

**Semantic continuity:** The LXX translators rendered כֹּהֵן as ἱερεύς with exceptional consistency (PMI 32.55 is very high). The Greek ἱερεύς — from ἱερός ("sacred, holy") — carries the same functional sense: one set apart to handle sacred things and mediate between God and people. The institutional and sacrificial dimensions of the Hebrew term carry through intact.

**Notable secondary mapping:** The occasional rendering of H3881 (Levite) as ἱερεύς, and H3548 as Λευΐτης, reflects the LXX translators' handling of the Levite/priest distinction — sometimes collapsing, sometimes differentiating.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- Core function: one set apart to mediate between God and the people, handling sacred things
- Connection to sacrifice and offering (προσφέρω appears 5x with ἱερεύς in NT)
- Concept of divine appointment and consecration to the office

**What shifts:**
- In the OT, kôhên exclusively refers to human officiant; in Hebrews, Christ holds the title permanently ("for ever," Ps 110:4)
- Melchizedek functions as a minor OT figure (Gen 14:18) but becomes the hermeneutical key to Christ's priesthood in Hebrews 7
- The Levitical priesthood (perpetuated in NT-era temple service) is reframed in Hebrews as anticipatory and temporary — "a shadow of good things to come" (Heb 10:1)
- Revelation applies ἱερεύς to all redeemed believers (1 Pet 2:9 uses ἱεράτευμα), extending the office beyond any hereditary line
