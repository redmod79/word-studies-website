**Key OT passages:**

The Hebrew qārab has a broader semantic range than its Greek equivalent. It covers physical approach to a place or person, judicial approach for case resolution, military advance, and the priestly act of presenting offerings. The Hiphil (causative) specifically means "to bring near" — to cause something to approach the altar.

**Semantic narrowing in translation:** Hebrew qārab covers both "approach" (intransitive) and "bring near/present" (transitive/causative). The LXX splits this into multiple Greek verbs: prosphérō for the transitive sacrificial sense, prosérchomai for the intransitive "come to," and engízō for spatial nearness. In the NT, prosphérō retains the transitive offering sense almost exclusively.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The core meaning of directional presenting — bearing something toward God as an offering — persists from OT qārab through LXX to NT prosphérō
- The word retains its priestly/cultic context: both qārab and prosphérō describe what priests do at the altar
- The co-occurrence with sacrifice-type nouns (offering, gift, burnt offering) is consistent in both testaments

**What shifts:**
- The broad approach/nearness sense of qārab is dropped in NT prosphérō, which is almost always transitive (bearing something toward someone)
- Hebrews narrows the application: prosphérō becomes the key verb for comparing repetitive Levitical offerings with Christ's once-for-all self-offering
- The NT adds a non-sacrificial extension: bringing sick persons to Jesus (Matthew), bringing someone before magistrates (Luke), treating/dealing with someone (Heb 12:7)
