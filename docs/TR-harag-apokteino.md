**Semantic shift in translation:** Greek *apokteinō* collapses three Hebrew verbs (*harag*, *muth*-Hiphil, *nakah*) into one — the LXX flattens the Hebrew distinction between *active slaying* (*harag*), *causing to die* (*muth*-Hiphil), and *lethal striking* (*nakah*). The narrower Hebrew distinctions — *ratsach* (judicial murder), *shachat* (ritual slaughter), *tabach* (butchery) — remain separable in Greek via *phoneuō*, *sphazō*, and *thyō* respectively. This is why NT writers can still invoke finer moral distinctions (e.g., Jas 2:11 uses *phoneuō* for the Decalogue's "do not murder" echoing *ratsach*) while using *apokteinō* for generic killing.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- **Fratricide frame** — Cain's *harag* of Abel (Gen 4:8) survives as a theological touchstone (Matt 23:35; Heb 11:4; 1 John 3:12; Jude 11). The kill-of-a-righteous-brother motif is unbroken.
- **Killing the prophets** — the OT trope (1 Kgs 19:10; Neh 9:26; Jer 26:20-23) becomes a NT indictment (Matt 23:37; Luke 11:47-51; Acts 7:52; Rom 11:3). Same lexical chain, same accusation.
- **Divine-judgment slaying** — God as subject of *harag* (Exod 13:15; Ps 78:31; Hos 6:5) carries forward to *apokteinō* passages in Revelation where death instruments are sent by divine decree (e.g., Rev 6:8 — *apokteinai*).
- **Figurative killing by abstractions** — Job 5:2, Prov 1:32, 7:26 (wrath/envy/folly/harlot slays) previews Rom 7:11 (sin slew me), 2 Cor 3:6 (letter kills), Eph 2:16 (Christ slew the enmity).

**What shifts:**
- **Moral vocabulary narrows in NT** — where OT *harag* was the broad narrative verb, the NT uses *apokteinō* for generic killing and reserves *phoneuō* for the Decalogue's "do not murder" (Matt 5:21; Jas 2:11). The Hebrew *harag*/*ratsach* distinction is preserved Greek-side via *apokteinō*/*phoneuō*.
- **Cross re-centers the verb** — in the NT, the most theologically loaded uses of *apokteinō* cluster around the death of Jesus (Matt 16:21; Mark 8:31; Luke 9:22; John 5:18; Acts 3:15) and believers who follow him (Matt 10:28; Rev 6:11; 11:7; 13:15). The OT's narrative-violence word becomes a passion-narrative word.
- **Redemptive inversion** — Eph 2:16 uses *apokteinō* of Christ "slaying the enmity" on the cross — a unique reversal where the kill-verb does saving work. No OT *harag* carries this redemptive sense.
- **"Killing the soul"** — Matt 10:28 introduces a dimension absent in OT *harag* usage: that physical killing is limited in reach, and a worse killing (of the soul, by God) is possible. OT *harag* never thematizes this distinction; the body/soul duality is a NT addition to the kill-verb's semantic range.
