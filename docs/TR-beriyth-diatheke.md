| beriyth (H1285) | διατίθεμαι (G1303) | 67x | Verbal form used in covenant-making contexts |

**Semantic shift in translation:** The Greek word διαθήκη carries a dual meaning absent in Hebrew: (1) covenant/compact (matching beriyth), and (2) a unilateral disposition or "last will and testament." This ambiguity becomes theologically significant in Heb 9:16–17, where the author exploits both senses. The Hebrew beriyth is strictly bilateral/relational — a solemn compact between parties — while diatheke can also emphasize the sovereign disposition of one party.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The concept of a solemn, binding agreement initiated by God
- "Blood of the covenant" language (Exo 24:8 → Matt 26:28, Heb 9:20)
- The "new covenant" promise of Jer 31:31 carried directly into NT via identical Greek
- God as the primary covenant-maker; human obligation to faithfulness

**What shifts:**
- KJV translates diatheke as "testament" 11x in the NT (never in OT), introducing a "last will" nuance absent from beriyth
- Heb 9:16–17 exploits the "will/testament" meaning: the covenant requires the death of the testator
- The NT concentrates covenant language in Hebrews (52% of NT uses), shifting from narrative/prophetic contexts to theological exposition
- NT emphasizes mediation: Christ as "mediator of the new covenant" (Heb 8:6, 9:15, 12:24) — a role without a direct OT parallel for beriyth
