**Semantic alignment in translation:** The LXX translators recognized ʼalah's dual nature. When the context emphasized the oath dimension, they used ὅρκος (horkos); when it emphasized the curse dimension, κατάρα (katara). But their primary choice ἀρά (ara) — 21 out of ~32 instances — preserves both senses, since Greek ἀρά means "prayer-as-imprecation," a prayer that invokes harm. The oath-curse unity is largely maintained.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The core sense of imprecation — words that invoke harm — carries through from Hebrew ʼalah to Greek ara to Paul's quotation in Romans
- Both testaments associate ʼalah/ara with deceit and moral corruption (Psa 10:7 → Rom 3:14)

**What shifts:**
- The OT covenant-oath dimension (ʼalah as the binding element in beriyth) has no direct NT continuation through ara — that function transfers to ὅρκος (horkos) and the covenant vocabulary around διαθήκη (diatheke)
- ara appears only once in the NT, making it a near-hapax; the broader curse concept shifts to κατάρα (katara, 6×), which inherits more from qelalah/arar than from ʼalah
- The dual oath-curse unity of Hebrew ʼalah fragments in the NT: oath language and curse language diverge into separate word groups
