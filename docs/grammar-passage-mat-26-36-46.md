# Grammar Analysis: Matthew 26:36-46 — Gethsemane, The Cup Accepted

## Overview

The pivot passage of redemption: at Gethsemane Christ asks three times whether the cup of wrath may pass, and in verse 42 submits to drink it. The Greek morphology carries the weight of this pivot — a shift from **παρελθάτω** (let it pass, v.39) to **γενηθήτω** (let it be done, v.42), from a conditional "if possible" to a divine-passive resolution. The narrative frame uses historical presents (**ἔρχεται, λέγει**) that pull the reader into real-time tension, while the closing clauses use the perfect (**ἤγγικεν**) to announce that the decisive hour has arrived and abides.

## Key Grammatical Features

### 1. παρελθάτω → γενηθήτω — The Pivot of the Two Prayers

The grammatical hinge of the passage lies between **v.39** and **v.42**. Both verbs are **third-person aorist imperatives** — a rare form in the NT that expresses a direct command issued toward a third-party subject (here, the "cup" and the "will").

- **v.39 παρελθάτω** (G3928, παρέρχομαι) — **V-2AAM-3S** — "let it pass by." The cup is the grammatical subject; Christ addresses the cup itself and commands it to pass. The conditional frame **εἰ δυνατόν ἐστιν** ("if it is possible") makes this a petition bounded by the Father's possibility.
- **v.42 γενηθήτω** (G1096, γίνομαι) — **V-AOM-3S** — "let it come to be." Form is **aorist passive** in morphology but middle/stative in sense ("become, happen"). The subject is **τὸ θέλημά σου** ("your will"). This is the same form used in the Lord's Prayer (Mat 6:10).

The structural pivot: in v.39 Christ commands the *cup* ("let it pass from me"); in v.42 he commands the *Father's will* ("let yours be done"). The subject of the imperative shifts from the object of his dread to the sovereign plan. The form is the same; the submission is total.

The conditional frames reinforce this. V.39 uses **εἰ δυνατόν ἐστιν** ("if it is possible") — open-ended first-class condition. V.42 flips it: **εἰ οὐ δύναται τοῦτο παρελθεῖν ἐὰν μὴ αὐτὸ πίω** ("if this cannot pass unless I drink it") — Christ now states the negated alternative that answers his own question. By v.42 he has received the answer: the cup cannot pass; therefore **γενηθήτω**.

- **Grammar reference:** [mood-aorist-imperative.md](../greek/mood-aorist-imperative.md)

### 2. πίω — The Aorist Subjunctive of Drinking the Cup

**v.42 πίω** (G4095, πίνω) — **V-2AAS-1S — Aorist Active Subjunctive, 1st person singular**. Embedded in **ἐὰν μὴ αὐτὸ πίω** ("unless I drink it"), this is the grammatical heart of the atonement decision. The aorist aspect frames the drinking as a single completed act — not a repeated sipping, but one decisive draught. The subjunctive mood registers the act as contingent in the condition-clause but, paired with γενηθήτω, becomes the accepted path forward.

Note: the pronoun **αὐτὸ** (it, neuter) matches **τὸ ποτήριον** (the cup, neuter) — the same cup of v.39 is now the thing he will drink.

### 3. ἤγγικεν — The Perfect That Closes the Scene (vv.45, 46)

**ἤγγικεν** (G1448, ἐγγίζω) appears twice — **V-RAI-3S — Perfect Active Indicative 3Sg**. The Greek perfect describes a completed past action whose result stands in the present: "has drawn near and is now here." Twice Christ marks the arrival:

- v.45 **ἤγγικεν ἡ ὥρα** — "the hour has arrived and stands."
- v.46 **ἤγγικεν ὁ παραδιδούς με** — "the betrayer has arrived and stands."

The perfect tense is dramatically different from an aorist here. An aorist would merely report "the hour drew near." The perfect asserts that the event has crossed the threshold and its result abides — the tension is no longer anticipatory but present. Pairing both perfects with **ἰδοὺ** ("behold!") turns v.45-46 into a dual arrival announcement: the hour is here; the betrayer is here.

### 4. παραδίδοται and παραδιδούς — The Handing-Over of Two Tenses (v.45-46)

Two forms of **παραδίδωμι** (G3860) frame the conclusion:

- **v.45 παραδίδοται** — **V-PPI-3S — Present Passive Indicative 3Sg** *(is being handed over)*. This is a **futuristic present** — an imminent event so certain that it is described as already in motion. The **passive voice** without stated agent can be read as a **divine passive**: the handing-over is under God's appointment. The agents named next — "into the hands of sinners" — are the instrument, not the ultimate cause.
- **v.46 ὁ παραδιδούς με** — **V-PAP-NSM — Present Active Participle, articular substantive** *(the one betraying me)*. The article + participle construction creates a title: "the betrayer." The present aspect is timeless-identifying — this is *who Judas now is*. The participle names a role he cannot step out of at this moment.

The symmetry is striking: the divine passive **παραδίδοται** (what is being done to the Son) is answered by the active participle **ὁ παραδιδούς** (the one doing it). Both are present — the action is unfolding in real time.

- **Grammar reference:** [divine-passive.md](../greek/divine-passive.md)

### 5. Historical Presents — ἔρχεται and λέγει (vv.36, 40, 45)

Matthew's narrative frame uses the **present indicative** for past events at the scene's major turns:

- v.36 **ἔρχεται... λέγει** ("comes... says")
- v.40 **ἔρχεται... εὑρίσκει... λέγει** ("comes... finds... says")
- v.45 **ἔρχεται... λέγει** ("comes... says")

These are **historical presents** (V-PNI-3S / V-PAI-3S) — past-time narration in present-tense morphology. The effect is immediacy: the reader is pulled into the scene as it happens. Three cycles of prayer-and-return are each opened with **ἔρχεται**, producing a three-beat rhythm that matches the three prayers of v.39, v.42, v.44.

### 6. γρηγορεῖτε — Present Imperative vs. Aorist Imperative (vv.38, 41)

The disciples receive imperatives in two aspects:

- v.38 **μείνατε** (Aor Impv) **καὶ γρηγορεῖτε** (Pres Impv) — "remain" (a single act: stay there) + "keep watching" (ongoing).
- v.41 **γρηγορεῖτε καὶ προσεύχεσθε** — both **Present Imperatives** *(keep watching, keep praying)*.

The **present imperative** commands continuous, habitual action. In v.41, after the first failure, Christ intensifies: not a single watchful moment but a sustained posture. The contrast with v.38's single **μείνατε** (aorist — "stay here in this place, one act") marks Christ's growing demand as the hour closes in.

- **Grammar reference:** [mood-aorist-imperative.md](../greek/mood-aorist-imperative.md) — covers the aorist/present imperative contrast

### 7. βεβαρημένοι — Perfect Passive Participle of Heavy Eyes (v.43)

**βεβαρημένοι** (G916, βαρέω) — **V-RPP-NPM — Perfect Passive Participle Nom Pl Masc**. Periphrastic construction: **ἦσαν... βεβαρημένοι** = "were [having been] weighed down." The perfect participle plus imperfect **ἦσαν** forms a periphrastic pluperfect — "their eyes stood weighed down." The passive voice leaves the weight's source unnamed (physical exhaustion, spiritual inability — Matthew does not say). The stative force of the perfect puts the disciples in a fixed condition: their eyes *are* heavy and *remain* heavy.

- **Grammar reference:** [participle-perfect-passive-state.md](../greek/participle-perfect-passive-state.md)

### 8. ἤρξατο λυπεῖσθαι καὶ ἀδημονεῖν — The Onset of Anguish (v.37)

**ἤρξατο** (G756) — **V-ADI-3S — Aorist Middle Indicative** *(he began)* + two **present active/passive infinitives** **λυπεῖσθαι** ("to be grieved") and **ἀδημονεῖν** ("to be in anguish"). The construction **ἄρχομαι + infinitive** is a formal incipient idiom: "he began to [do X] — and continued doing it." The **present infinitives** mark the grief and anguish as **ongoing states**, not momentary pangs. What begins in Gethsemane will not let go until Golgotha.

**ἀδημονέω** is the strongest Greek word for psychic distress — used only three times in the NT (Mat 26:37; Mar 14:33; Phi 2:26). It denotes distress so sharp it borders on collapse.

### 9. ἐγείρεσθε... ἄγωμεν — The Hortatory Close (v.46)

Verse 46 closes with two commands in different forms:

- **ἐγείρεσθε** — **V-PEM-2P** — Present Middle/Passive Imperative 2Pl *("rise up!")* — addressed to the disciples.
- **ἄγωμεν** — **V-PAS-1P** — Present Active **Subjunctive** 1Pl. This is a **hortatory subjunctive** — "let us go." The 1st-person-plural subjunctive replaces the imperative when the speaker includes himself in the command.

Christ does not command them to go somewhere; he joins them: "let *us* go." The hortatory subjunctive refuses to leave them behind — even as he walks into the betrayal. This single verb form carries the whole pastoral posture of the scene's closing.

### 10. Πάτερ μου — Vocative of Intimate Address (vv.39, 42)

**Πάτερ** (G3962, πατήρ) — **N-VSM — Vocative Singular Masculine**. Both prayers open with the **vocative** plus **μου** ("my"): "My Father!" The vocative is the direct-address form; paired with the first-person pronoun **μου**, it is intimate and possessive — the address of a son, not a petitioner.

Matthew preserves this Greek form of what Mark transliterates as **Αββα** (Mar 14:36). The vocative carries the whole Trinitarian weight of the scene: Christ addresses the Father as *his* Father even as he asks for the cup to pass, and addresses the same Father as *his* Father when he accepts the cup. The vocative form does not change between the two prayers — the relationship is constant; only the petition resolves.

## Clause Structure

The pericope divides into three prayer-and-return cycles, each opened with a historical-present **ἔρχεται**, followed by a finding of sleep, followed by a rebuke or declaration:

**Cycle 1 (vv.36-40):**
- Setting — v.36 (arrival at Gethsemane)
- Inner circle — v.37 (Peter + sons of Zebedee, onset of anguish)
- First address — v.38 (ψυχή exceedingly sorrowful; watch-command with aor + pres impv)
- **First prayer — v.39** (παρελθάτω — "let it pass")
- Return + finding asleep — v.40 (historical presents; rebuke to Peter)

**Cycle 2 (vv.41-43):**
- Exhortation — v.41 (present imperatives; spirit/flesh maxim)
- **Second prayer — v.42** (γενηθήτω — "let it be done")
- Return + finding asleep — v.43 (perfect passive ptcp — eyes "weighed down")

**Cycle 3 (vv.44-46):**
- Third prayer — v.44 (same λόγος as v.42; redundancy signals closure)
- Return + declaration — v.45 (perfect ἤγγικεν — "the hour has arrived"; futuristic present παραδίδοται)
- Final summons — v.46 (hortatory ἄγωμεν; perfect ἤγγικεν repeated; articular present participle ὁ παραδιδούς)

The **three prayers** echo the **three Lord-have-mercy pattern** of Jewish piety and match the **three returns**. The second prayer is the pivot; the third seals it. The scene closes not with prayer but with motion — **ἐγείρεσθε, ἄγωμεν** — the submission of v.42 now enacted in the body.

## The Pivot: Summary of Morphological Movement

| Position | Form | Parsing | Function |
|----------|------|---------|----------|
| v.39 Prayer 1 | **παρελθάτω** | Aor Act Impv 3Sg | "let the cup pass" (petition) |
| v.39 frame | εἰ δυνατόν ἐστιν | 1st-class cond | "if possible" (question open) |
| v.39 close | οὐχ ὡς ἐγὼ θέλω ἀλλ᾽ ὡς σύ | Pres Act Ind 1Sg + ellipsis | will-submission stated |
| v.42 frame | εἰ οὐ δύναται... ἐὰν μὴ πίω | 1st-cl neg + 3rd-cl cond | "if not... unless I drink" (question answered) |
| v.42 Prayer 2 | **γενηθήτω** | Aor Pass Impv 3Sg | "let it be done" (submission) |
| v.42 Prayer 2 | πίω | Aor Act Subj 1Sg | "I drink [it]" (the cup accepted) |
| v.45 | παραδίδοται | Pres Pass Ind 3Sg | divine passive — handing-over begins |
| v.45, 46 | ἤγγικεν | Perf Act Ind 3Sg | "the hour / betrayer has arrived and is here" |
| v.46 | ἄγωμεν | Pres Act Subj 1Pl — hortatory | "let us go" — motion into the hour |

The grammatical arc: **conditional petition** → **contrary condition answered** → **submissive imperative** → **accepted drinking** → **present-passive arrival** → **perfect standing-near** → **hortatory motion**. The cup is drunk grammatically before it is drunk physically.
