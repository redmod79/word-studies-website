## Semantic Categories

### 1. Feast of Tabernacles (Sukkot) — Israel's Annual Festival
The commanded 7-day festival in which Israelites dwell in booths to remember the wilderness journey. This is the dominant usage context.

- **Lev 23:34** — "The fifteenth day of this seventh month shall be the feast of tabernacles for seven days unto the LORD"
- **Lev 23:42–43** — "Ye shall dwell in booths seven days...that your generations may know that I made the children of Israel to dwell in booths, when I brought them out of Egypt"
- **Neh 8:16–17** — "The people...made themselves booths...all the congregation made booths and sat under the booths: for since the days of Joshua the son of Nun had not the children of Israel done so"
- **Zech 14:16** — "Every one that is left of all the nations...shall even go up from year to year to worship the King, the LORD of hosts, and to keep the feast of tabernacles"

### 2. Temporary Shelter — Booth or Hut
A simple, temporary constructed shelter for humans or animals; emphasizes the provisional, impermanent nature of the structure.

- **Gen 33:17** — "Jacob...built him an house, and made booths for his cattle: therefore the name of the place is called Succoth" (First occurrence; origin of the place name)
- **Jonah 4:5** — "Jonah...made him a booth, and sat under it in the shadow, till he might see what would become of the city"
- **Job 27:18** — "He buildeth his house as a moth, and as a booth that the keeper maketh" (Metaphor for the wicked man's fragile, temporary dwelling)

### 3. Royal / Military Pavilion
A tent encampment for kings or armies during battle or campaign.

- **1 Kgs 20:12** — "Benhadad heard this message...as he was drinking, he and the kings in the pavilions"
- **1 Kgs 20:16** — "Benhadad was drinking himself drunk in the pavilions, he and the kings"
- **2 Sam 11:11** — "Uriah said unto David, The ark, and Israel, and Judah, abide in tents; and my lord Joab, and the servants of my lord, are encamped in the open fields"

### 4. Divine Protective Shelter — God's Pavilion
Used metaphorically for God's sheltering, concealing presence — the place where God "hides" his people from danger.

- **Ps 18:11** — "He made darkness his secret place; his pavilion round about him were dark waters and thick clouds of the skies"
- **Ps 31:20** — "Thou shalt hide them in the secret of thy presence...thou shalt keep them secretly in a pavilion from the strife of tongues"
- **Isa 4:6** — "And there shall be a tabernacle for a shadow in the daytime from the heat, and for a place of refuge, and for a covert from storm and from rain"

### 5. Symbol of Frailty or Desolation
A sukkah as a figure for something abandoned, collapsing, or fallen — the opposite of a secure dwelling.

- **Isa 1:8** — "The daughter of Zion is left as a cottage in a vineyard, as a lodge in a garden of cucumbers, as a besieged city" (Jerusalem like an abandoned harvest hut)
- **Amos 9:11** — "In that day will I raise up the tabernacle of David that is fallen" (The Davidic kingdom as a collapsed booth — awaiting restoration)
- **Job 38:40** — "When they couch in their dens, and abide in the covert to lie in wait" (Animal lair/ambush hide)

## Related Words

### Word Family / Cognates
- **H5520 סֹךְ (sok)** — root noun (masculine): a thicket, lair, covert; the parent word from which sukkah is derived. 4x in OT (Ps 10:9; 27:5; 31:21; 76:3). More often refers to a natural concealment (animal den, undergrowth) while sukkah refers to a constructed shelter.
- **H5523 סֻכּוֹת (Sukkoth)** — proper name (plural of sukkah): "Booths"; the place Jacob named after building sukkot (Gen 33:17); also a wilderness camp site (Num 33:5–6). Named directly from this word.
