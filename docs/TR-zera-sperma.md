**Semantic continuity:** The LXX translation is remarkably consistent — σπέρμα captures both the agricultural and the genealogical/covenantal senses of זֶרַע without splitting them across different Greek words. This allows NT authors to appeal directly to the Hebrew promise without translation ambiguity.

**No semantic shift:** Paul's argument in Gal 3:16 that "seed" (σπέρμα) is singular and refers to Christ only works because the LXX preserved the singular form matching the Hebrew. The Greek σπέρμα is grammatically singular even when referring to a collective group (just as זֶרַע can be collective), enabling Paul's exegesis.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The word σπέρμα faithfully preserves all three registers of זֶרַע: botanical (agricultural parables), biological (offspring, levirate law), and covenantal (the Abrahamic promise chain)
- The singular/collective ambiguity of זֶרַע carries into σπέρμα, enabling NT theological use
- The seed-of-David royal trajectory (2 Sam 7:12) flows directly into the NT's Christological claims (John 7:42; Rom 1:3; 2 Tim 2:8)

**What shifts:**
- The NT narrows the ultimate referent: where OT זֶרַע could mean ethnic Israel collectively, the NT focuses the promise on Christ singularly (Gal 3:16) and then extends it to all in Christ (Gal 3:29)
- The "seed of the woman" promise (Gen 3:15) remains latent in OT but surfaces explicitly in Rev 12:17 as an end-time conflict between the dragon and the covenant community
- The biological/seminal sense (H7902 שְׁכָבָה connection) largely fades in NT; σπέρμα in NT is almost entirely covenantal, messianic, or parabolic
