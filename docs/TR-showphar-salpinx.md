The shôwphâr is the natural ram's horn used as a signal instrument across military, royal, liturgical, prophetic, and eschatological contexts. It is distinct from the priestly silver trumpet (H2689 חֲצֹצְרָה).

**Key OT passages:**
- **Exo 19:16** — "the voice of the trumpet exceeding loud; so that all the people that was in the camp trembled" — divine theophany at Sinai
- **Ezek 33:3–6** — the watchman who blows the trumpet warns the people; failure to blow it brings bloodguilt
- **Isa 27:13** — "the great trumpet shall be blown, and they shall come which were ready to perish" — eschatological ingathering

G4536 σάλπιγξ also renders H2689 (chătsôtsᵉrâh, 27x) and collocates with H8628 (tâqaʻ, 23x). The Greek word loses the distinction between ram's horn and silver trumpet — both become σάλπιγξ in the LXX.

**Semantic shift:** The Hebrew shôwphâr was specifically a ram's horn. In Greek the instrument type is unspecified — σάλπιγξ denotes any trumpet-like instrument. The *function* (signal, alarm, proclamation, divine summons) transfers completely; the specific material/manufacture distinction is lost.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same:**
- The trumpet as *divine signal* — announces God's presence, action, or coming judgment
- The association with *gathering* — military, liturgical, eschatological
- The *response imperative* — hearers are expected to act (Ezek 33; Amos 3:6; Matt 24:31)
- The *eschatological register* — both OT (Isa 27:13; Zech 9:14) and NT place the final trumpet at the climax of history

**What shifts:**
- The Hebrew distinction between shôwphâr (ram's horn) and chatsotsrah (silver priestly trumpet) collapses in Greek; NT uses σάλπιγξ for both senses
- The NT develops the *resurrection* function more explicitly (1 Cor 15:52; 1 Thess 4:16) — latent in OT's eschatological gathering but now foregrounded
- Revelation's *seven trumpets* multiplies and sequences what was a single eschatological blast in OT prophecy
