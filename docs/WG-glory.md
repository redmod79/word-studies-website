# WG: Glory and Honor

## Overview
The Greek concept of glory in the NT involves a cluster of words describing radiant majesty, honor, verbal praise, and the act of rendering glory. δόξα (doxa) is the central noun; δοξάζω (doxazō) is its verb; τιμή (timē) is the relational honor dimension; ἔνδοξος (endoxos) is the adjectival form; ἔπαινος (epainos) covers verbal commendation and praise.

## Key Passages Where Multiple Group Words Appear

- **Rev 4:9–11** — δόξα + τιμή + δύναμις in the throne-room doxology: "Worthy art thou to receive glory and honour and power"
- **Rev 7:12** — δόξα + seven other terms in angelic ascription: "Blessing, and glory, and wisdom, and thanksgiving, and honour, and power, and might"
- **Heb 2:7, 9** — δόξα + τιμή: Christ "crowned with glory and honour"
- **Rom 2:7, 10** — δόξα + τιμή + ἀφθαρσία: eschatological reward for the righteous
- **1 Pet 1:7** — τιμή + ἔπαινος + δόξα: faith proved by trial results in "praise and honour and glory"
- **John 5:41, 44** — δόξα used twice for human honor, contrasted with the honor that comes from God alone

## Key Distinction

δόξα and τιμή both translate as "honour" in some contexts, but they represent different facets:
- **δόξα** is intrinsic, visible radiance — what you *are*, manifest outwardly
- **τιμή** is relational, assigned value — what you are *worth* to others, like a price

When paired ("glory and honour"), δόξα leads because it grounds the other: true honor follows from genuine radiance.
