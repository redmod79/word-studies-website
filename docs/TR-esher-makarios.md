H835 is overwhelmingly the source word for makarios in the LXX. The other Hebrew words captured are formula elements ("man," "person") from the beatitude construction, not independent translations. This is one of the cleanest one-to-one bridges in the Hebrew-Greek lexical correspondence.

**Semantic shift in translation:** Minimal. Both esher and makarios function as exclamatory beatitude markers. The Hebrew form is a plural-construct noun used predicatively ("happinesses-of the-man-who..."), while the Greek is a simple adjective ("blessed/fortunate is..."). The grammatical shift from construct-noun to adjective does not alter the semantic function. Both introduce a declaration of the fortunate state of the described person.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The beatitude formula structure is preserved: "Blessed is the one who..." functions identically in Psalms, Proverbs, the Sermon on the Mount, and Revelation
- The connection between blessedness and right relationship with God persists throughout
- Both testaments apply the formula to forgiveness (Psa 32:1 → Rom 4:7), trust (Psa 2:12 → "she that believed" Lk 1:45), and obedience (Psa 119:1 → "happy are ye if ye do them" Jn 13:17)
- Eschatological beatitudes appear in both testaments (Isa 30:18, Dan 12:12 → Rev 14:13, 22:7)

**What shifts:**
- The NT dramatically expands the paradoxical beatitude: blessedness of the poor, mourning, persecuted (Matt 5:3-11) — categories absent from OT esher usage, which focuses on the righteous, wise, and obedient
- The NT applies makarios to God himself (1 Tim 1:11, 6:15 — "the blessed God") — a usage not found with esher in the OT
- OT esher is concentrated in Wisdom literature (79% Psalms+Proverbs); NT makarios is distributed across Gospels (55%), Epistles (31%), and Revelation (14%)
