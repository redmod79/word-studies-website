**Semantic shift in translation:**
The LXX collapses a richly differentiated Hebrew mercy-vocabulary (chanan = discretionary favor from superior to inferior; racham = womb-compassion; chesed = covenant loyalty; chus/chamal = sparing mercy) into a narrower Greek pair — eleeō (act of mercy) and oiktirō (feeling of pity). The Hebrew distinctiveness of chanan — its condescension imagery and its reflexive supplication form (Hithpael) — flattens into "have mercy." The Hebrew noun ḥēn (the gift received), by contrast, flows into χάρις (charis) rather than eleos (see TR-hen-charis).

## Petitioner Cry Pattern

The Hebrew petitionary form channênî ("be gracious to me") finds a precise Greek counterpart in ἐλέησόν με ("have mercy on me"), spoken by:
- Two blind men (Matt 9:27; 20:30-31; Mark 10:47-48)
- The Canaanite woman (Matt 15:22)
- The father of the demonized boy (Matt 17:15)
- The ten lepers (Luke 17:13)
- The blind beggar (Luke 18:38-39)
- The rich man in Hades (Luke 16:24)

Each case reenacts the Psalter's cry-to-YHWH pattern, with Jesus in the position of the gracious God who may chanan/eleéō.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The superior-to-inferior direction of mercy (one who has power bends down)
- Mercy as uncompelled / sovereign (Exod 33:19 → Rom 9:15)
- Mercy as the proper divine response to cry of need
- Showing mercy to the poor as evidence of reverence for God (Prov 14:31 → Matt 25:40; Jas 2:13)
- Mercy as reciprocal: the merciful receive mercy (Ps 18:25; Prov 19:17 → Matt 5:7)

**What shifts:**
- Hebrew distinguished the active grant (chanan) from the gift received (ḥēn); the LXX/NT route these through two different Greek families — eleos/eleeō for the act of mercy, charis for the gift/attribute of grace — so that NT "grace" (charis) sits adjacent to, not identical with, OT chanan
- The reflexive Hithpael (make supplication) has no single Greek counterpart; it distributes to deomai, parakaleō, proseuchomai
- The priestly blessing's "be gracious to you" (Num 6:25) becomes in NT usage a refrain on Jesus' lips ("go in peace") and in Paul's benedictions ("grace be with you") — formal continuity, theological intensification (grace now comes through the Son)
- In the NT, eleos is increasingly linked with covenant faithfulness toward Israel (Luke 1:54, 72; Rom 15:9), merging the chanan and chesed streams that Hebrew kept distinct
