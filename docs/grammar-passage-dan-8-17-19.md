## Key Grammatical Features

### 1. לְעֶת־קֵץ / לְמוֹעֵד קֵץ — the *inclusio* that frames the angel's response

Two parallel verbless clauses bracket the interpretation:

- **v17c:** `כִּי לְעֶת־קֵץ הֶחָזוֹן` — "for to a time of end [belongs] the vision"
- **v19b:** `כִּי לְמוֹעֵד קֵץ` — "for [it belongs] to an appointed-time of end"

Both clauses share the same skeleton: `כִּי לְ + [construct chain with קֵץ as the absolute]`. The particle `כִּי` gives the grounds; the preposition `לְ` (lamedh of reference / pertinence) assigns the vision *to* a specific timeframe. Neither clause has a finite verb — in Hebrew a verbless nominal clause predicates identity, possession, or classification. The structure is equational: **vision = (at-end-time-phenomenon)**.

The shift in vocabulary from `עֶת` (common, any point in time) to `מוֹעֵד` (an *appointed* time, a time fixed by decree) is deliberate and significant. `מוֹעֵד` comes from the root יעד "to appoint, fix by agreement"; the noun designates a time whose arrival is not contingent but decreed. Where v17 locates the vision at "end-time" generally, v19 sharpens it: it is *appointed*.

### 2. Verbless nominal clauses in prophetic interpretation

Clauses 8:17c and 8:19b are both **NmCl** (nominal/verbless clauses) in the quotation domain Q — the angel's speech. Hebrew uses a verbless clause to predicate a **static, timeless relationship** rather than an event. By choosing NmCl rather than a finite verb ("the vision *is* for / *will come at* the end-time"), the text asserts the vision's temporal reference as a matter of its *essence*, not an event it undergoes. This is why neither clause needs a copula in Hebrew — the assignment of the vision to its timeframe *is the predicate*.

The word order matters in the v17 clause:
- `לְעֶת־קֵץ` (PreC — predicate complement) precedes `הֶחָזוֹן` (Subj — the subject)
- Fronting the prepositional phrase places **focus on the time reference**, not on the vision itself. Reading: "it is *to the time of the end* that the vision pertains" — answering the question "*when*?"

### 3. בְּאַחֲרִית הַזָּעַם — "in the latter end of the indignation"

The construct chain `אַחֲרִית הַזָּעַם` makes two claims at once:

- `אַחֲרִית` (latter end, after-part, issue) is the **terminus** of something — not just "end" but the "hinder part," the *outcome* at the back of a process.
- `הַזָּעַם` (the indignation) has the definite article — a specific, already-identified indignation. In Dan 8's context, this is God's indignation against the profaning power of the little horn (8:9-13), and by extension the period during which that indignation runs its course.

Placed inside a relative clause with the Qal imperfect `יִהְיֶה` ("will be"), the phrase says the angel will reveal "*what shall be at the hinder-part of the indignation*" — i.e., what happens when God's indignation reaches its terminus. This frames the `קֵץ` / `מוֹעֵד קֵץ` not as the end of the *world* per se, but as the end of the *indignation period* tied to the vision's timeframe.

### 4. מוֹדִיעֲךָ — Hiphil active participle as futurum instans

The angel's answer begins `הִנְנִי מוֹדִיעֲךָ` — "behold me [who am] causing-you-to-know." `מוֹדִיעֲךָ` is a **Hiphil active participle**, masculine singular, from ידע ("know"). The Hiphil stem makes the verb causative: not "know" but "cause to know / make known / inform."

The pairing `הִנֵּה + participle` is a standard Hebrew idiom called *futurum instans* — an imminent future. It does not describe an action in progress so much as an action *on the verge of happening*. English "I am *about to* make you know" captures it better than a simple present. The angel is not saying "here is the knowledge I am now pouring out," but "here I am, ready to inform you" — the disclosure that follows (vv. 20-26) is the content he is about to deliver.

### 5. הָבֵן בֶּן־אָדָם — Hiphil imperative + vocative

The angel's opening command `הָבֵן` is a Hiphil imperative 2ms from בין. The Qal of בין means "discern, perceive"; the Hiphil intensifies toward "bring to understanding, make [something] make sense" — but in the imperative addressed to a human subject it collapses into the reflexive-causative "cause yourself to understand" = "pay attention and grasp this." It is stronger than the Qal would have been: not "notice" but "apply your mind and get this."

The vocative `בֶּן־אָדָם` (son of man / mortal) is a construct chain functioning as address. In Daniel this title for Daniel himself (cf. Ezek's repeated use of it for Ezekiel) emphasizes the hearer's creaturely frailty in the presence of heavenly revelation — reinforced here by Daniel's collapse in v17a and his stupor in v18a. The command to understand is paired with a body that literally cannot stand up without help.

### 6. The wayyiqtol ⇄ WxQ0 alternation in vv. 17-18 — discourse backgrounding

The WxQ0 clauses (`נִבְעַתִּי`, `נִרְדַּמְתִּי`) are not mainline events — they are **Daniel's internal state** set against the angel's actions. Niphal perfect forms ("I was terrified," "I was put into deep sleep") are passives of affected experience: things that happened *to* Daniel. Foreground Way0 verbs are all the angel's actions (came, said, touched, raised). The grammar itself paints the picture: Daniel is the passive recipient; the angel is the active agent.

### 7. Infinitive-construct temporal clauses — בְּבֹאוֹ / בְּדַבְּרוֹ

Both verses open their main narrative with a `בְּ + infinitive construct + pronominal suffix` construction:

- v17: `בְּבֹאוֹ` = בְּ (when) + בוא (come, Qal InfCon) + וֹ (3ms suffix as subject) = "in/when his coming" = "when he came"
- v18: `בְּדַבְּרוֹ` = בְּ (when) + דבר (speak, Piel InfCon) + וֹ (3ms suffix) = "in/when his speaking" = "while he was speaking"

The infinitive construct with `בְּ` produces a **temporal subordinate clause** whose logical subject is carried by the possessive suffix. These clauses are not independent events on the timeline; they frame the timing of the main verb. Together they establish a pattern: *while / as the angel does X, Daniel responds with Y*.

## Exegetical Significance of the Passage

Verses 17 and 19 together frame the vision of Daniel 8 — which culminates in the 2,300 evenings-mornings of 8:14 — within a deliberately *extended* time horizon:

- v14 alone ("unto 2,300 evenings-mornings, then shall the sanctuary be vindicated") gives a specific number.
- v17 adds: `לְעֶת־קֵץ הֶחָזוֹן` — "the vision [pertains] to a time of end."
- v19 tightens: `לְמוֹעֵד קֵץ` — "it is for an *appointed* end-time."

The grammatical moves are three:
1. **Construct chains with קֵץ** as absolute: the vision's temporal target is defined by its relationship to "end" — not an end in the vision, but the vision locked to an "end-time."
2. **Verbless predication (NmCl)**: the relationship is stated as a matter of being, not event — the vision *belongs to* the end-time as a defining property.
3. **Escalation עֶת → מוֹעֵד**: an ordinary temporal reference is sharpened to a *decreed* appointed time.

Together these grammatical choices push the timeframe of the vision forward — beyond the immediate historical horizon of the 2,300-day count — to an eschatological "appointed end-time" whose arrival is certain because it is appointed.
