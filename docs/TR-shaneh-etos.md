**Semantic shift in translation:** Minimal. Both Hebrew shaneh and Greek etos denote a discrete countable year. The LXX does make one meaningful distinction: when shaneh carries cyclical/ritual force (sabbatical year, feast year, jubilee), translators often prefer ἐνιαυτός (eniautos) over ἔτος (etos). Otherwise etos carries shaneh straightforwardly.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- **Date-formula syntax:** "in the Nth year of [ruler]" (Isa 6:1; Dan 1:1; Luke 3:1) survives intact from Hebrew narrative into Luke's Gospel
- **Age formulae:** lifespan and age-at-event reckoning (Gen 25:7 / Luke 2:36) carry over identically
- **Prophetic periods:** the "seventy years" of Jer 25 / Dan 9 and the "thousand years" of Rev 20 both use countable-year language on both sides of the bridge
- **Numerical pairings:** "forty years" (wilderness — Num 14; Heb 3:17; Acts 7:36) maps directly

**What shifts:**
- **LXX splits by register:** etos for plain counting, eniautos for cyclical/liturgical years — a distinction Hebrew shaneh does not formally mark. The NT inherits this split (cf. Heb 9 using eniautos for annual ritual, Rev 20 using etos for the prophetic millennium).
- **NT frequency:** shaneh is a high-density OT word (647 vv in 32 books); etos drops to 48 NT occurrences, reflecting the NT's shorter timeframe and lower density of regnal/genealogical material.
- **Theological loading:** eniautos gains concentrated jubilee/eschatological weight in Luke 4:19, activating Isa 61:2's shaneh as a programmatic frame for Jesus' ministry.
