# Grammar Analysis: Jeremiah 25:15-29

## Overview

Jeremiah receives a prophetic-symbolic commission: take the "wine-cup of this fury" from YHWH's hand and make all the nations drink. The passage moves from direct divine command (v. 15) through a catalog of recipient nations (vv. 18-26) to a closing warning aimed at those who might refuse the cup (vv. 27-29). Grammatically the unit is built on three hinges: the imperative + weqatal pair in v. 15 that commissions the act, the wayyiqtol narrative of v. 17 in which Jeremiah executes the commission, and the imperfect + infinitive-absolute emphatics of vv. 28-29 that seal the judgment.

## Key Grammatical Features

### 1. Qal imperative קַח ("take!") + weqatal וְהִשְׁקִיתָה ("and you shall make drink") — v. 15

- **Form:** קַח is a 2ms Qal imperative of לקח; וְהִשְׁקִיתָה is a Hiphil weqatal (perfect prefixed with `waw`) 2ms of שׁקה.
- **Significance:** This is the classic command-plus-weqatal chain in which the weqatal continues the force of the imperative. The effect is: "Take… and [having taken, then] cause to drink." The weqatal is not a simple coordinate past; it projects the imperative's volitional force onto the second action, making the whole a single compound commission. The Hiphil stem of שׁקה is causative: Jeremiah is not the one drinking, he is the agent who forces others to drink — a courier of divine wrath, not a participant.
- **Grammar references:**
  - [imperative-mood](../hebrew/imperative-mood.md)
  - [stem-hiphil](../hebrew/stem-hiphil.md)

### 2. Construct chain כּוֹס הַיַּיִן הַחֵמָה הַזֹּאת — v. 15

- **Form:** A construct chain where the head noun כּוֹס is in construct, followed by articular absolutes. The awkward-looking "the-wine the-fury" is best read as a complex nominal with חֵמָה in apposition or as a qualifying genitive — "the cup of the wine, (which is) this fury" or idiomatically "this wine-cup of fury."
- **Significance:** חֵמָה ("heat, wrath") recasts the wine: the liquid in the cup is not Bacchic revelry but divine burning anger (cf. word study on חֵמָה). The demonstrative הַזֹּאת closes the chain and points deictically to a concrete object YHWH is handing over. The construct binds "cup," "wine," and "fury" into a single referent: the cup *is* the wine *is* the fury.
- **Grammar reference:** [construct-state](../hebrew/construct-state.md)

### 3. Weqatal chain of drunkenness-verbs: שָׁתוּ / הִתְגֹּעֲשׁוּ / הִתְהֹלָלוּ — v. 16

- **Form:** Three 3cp weqatal perfects in sequence: Qal שׁתה "drink," Hithpolel געשׁ "stagger/reel," Hithpael הלל "act mad/deranged." The two reflexive-reciprocal stems (Hithpolel and Hithpael) are notable: the Hithpolel of געשׁ is rare, a pilpel-reflexive of "shake," conveying convulsive staggering; the Hithpael of הלל (normally "praise") means "behave as a madman."
- **Significance:** The triad is a deliberately escalating picture of intoxication-as-judgment: they drink, they reel, they go insane. The weqatal after an imperative + weqatal in v. 15 continues the projected-future force: this is what YHWH has declared *will* happen, not what Jeremiah *could* describe. The Hithpael/Hithpolel stems foreground what the drunk nations do to themselves — judgment is not only poured on them, it expresses itself through them.

### 4. Wayyiqtol narrative execution: וָאֶקַּח … וָאַשְׁקֶה — v. 17

- **Form:** Two 1cs wayyiqtols in sequence: Qal wayyiqtol of לקח ("and I took") and Hiphil wayyiqtol of שׁקה ("and I made drink"). Wayyiqtol is the standard Hebrew narrative past, chaining sequential actions.
- **Significance:** The grammatical shift from v. 15's commissioning imperatives/weqatals to v. 17's wayyiqtols marks the shift from divine speech to prophetic report. The parallelism between v. 15 (קַח… וְהִשְׁקִיתָה) and v. 17 (וָאֶקַּח… וָאַשְׁקֶה) is exact — command and obedient execution match verb for verb. The prophetic act is presented as already accomplished, which is characteristic of Jeremiah's symbolic-action oracles: the vision of the deed carries the same weight as the deed itself.

### 5. Imperative cascade of self-destruction: שְׁתוּ וְשִׁכְרוּ וּקְיוּ וְנִפְלוּ — v. 27

- **Form:** Four coordinated 2mp Qal imperatives: "drink, be drunk, vomit, fall" — followed by וְלֹא תָקוּמוּ, a negated imperfect ("and you shall not rise").
- **Significance:** This is an imperative of judgment, not permission. YHWH commands the nations to undergo the very process that will destroy them. The shift from the fourth imperative (נִפְלוּ "fall!") to the imperfect (לֹא תָקוּמוּ "you shall not rise") is grammatically deliberate: rising would be the natural next-step of a fallen drunk, and the imperfect with לֹא blocks it as a future state. The cascade mimics intoxication's own trajectory — drink → drunk → vomit → fall → stay down — compressed into a single rhetorical breath.
- **Grammar reference:** [imperative-mood](../hebrew/imperative-mood.md)
- **Word studies:**

  - [H7937-shakar (be drunk)](../../word-studies/H7937-shakar.md)

### 6. Infinitive absolute + finite verb for emphasis: שָׁתֹה תִשְׁתּוּ and הִנָּקֵה תִנָּקוּ — vv. 28-29

- **Form:** Two classic paronomastic infinitive-absolute constructions:
  - v. 28: שָׁתֹה (Qal InfAbs of שׁתה) + תִשְׁתּוּ (Qal Impf 2mp of שׁתה) = "drinking you shall drink" → "you shall surely drink."
  - v. 29: הִנָּקֵה (Niphal InfAbs of נקה) + תִנָּקוּ (Niphal Impf 2mp of נקה) = "being-acquitted shall you be acquitted?" — here in a rhetorical question followed by לֹא תִנָּקוּ, "you shall *not* go unpunished."
- **Significance:** The infinitive-absolute-plus-finite-verb construction intensifies the verb. In v. 28 the emphatic is flat certainty: there is no refusing the cup. In v. 29 the same construction is deployed ironically: would the guilty really be "thoroughly acquitted"? The rhetorical force is crushed by the immediate answer: לֹא תִנָּקוּ. The two emphatic constructions frame the section — v. 28 establishes that drinking is inevitable; v. 29 establishes that escape is impossible.
- **Grammar reference:** [infinitive-absolute-intensification](../hebrew/infinitive-absolute-intensification.md)

### 7. Niphal נִקְרָא שְׁמִי עָלֶיהָ — v. 29

- **Form:** Niphal Perfect 3ms of קרא, with שְׁמִי as grammatical subject and עָלֶיהָ as complement: "my name is called upon her." The Niphal is the mediopassive stem; here clearly passive.
- **Significance:** This is a fixed covenantal idiom — "the city upon which my name is called" = Jerusalem, the place bearing YHWH's name because of temple and election. The grammatical passivity of נִקְרָא is theologically loaded: Jerusalem did not name herself after YHWH; the name was placed *upon* her. If YHWH is now beginning (מֵחֵל, Hiphil Ptcp of חלל "begin") to bring calamity on the city that bears his name, then the a fortiori argument of the second half of v. 29 follows: the nations without that covenantal protection cannot escape.
- **Grammar references:**
  - [stem-niphal](../hebrew/stem-niphal.md)
  - [stem-hiphil](../hebrew/stem-hiphil.md) (for מֵחֵל)

### 8. Piel of מאן — v. 28

- **Form:** יְמָאֲנוּ is Piel Imperfect 3mp of מאן "refuse." The verb מאן exists only in the Piel stem in Biblical Hebrew — it is a "Piel-only" verb, so the Piel does not intensify a base-Qal meaning; it simply *is* the verb.
- **Significance:** The conditional frame "וְהָיָה כִּי יְמָאֲנוּ" ("and it shall be, if they refuse") is standard case-law protasis syntax. It sets up the apodosis in the weqatal וְאָמַרְתָּ: "and [then] you shall say." The point is that refusal is not an exit ramp — it triggers a further word of judgment, not a reprieve.
- **Grammar reference:** (piel as Piel-only verbs would be a helpful new entry)

## Clause Structure (selected verses)

### v. 15 — Four clauses
1. **xQtX** — messenger formula: "For thus said YHWH God of Israel to me"
2. **ZIm0** — bare imperative: "Take the cup of the wine of this fury from my hand"
3. **WQt0** — weqatal continuation: "and you shall make all the nations drink it"
4. **Ptcp** — relative participial clause: "whom I am sending you to"

The shape is: messenger-formula → command → projected continuation → participial relative defining the recipients. The participial אָנֹכִי שֹׁלֵחַ ("I am sending") is the present-tense commissioning frame that makes the command active.

### v. 28 — Seven clauses
1. **MSyn** — וְהָיָה as stand-alone macro-syntactic signal ("and it shall be")
2. **xYq0** — protasis "if they refuse"
3. **InfC** — purpose infinitive "to take the cup from your hand"
4. **InfC** — purpose infinitive "to drink"
5. **WQt0** — apodosis weqatal "and you shall say to them"
6. **xQtX** — messenger formula "thus said YHWH of hosts"
7. **xYq0** — the emphatic oracle "drinking you shall drink"

The nested infinitive clauses (3–4) depend on the verb "refuse" in clause 2; clauses 5–7 form the main apodosis and its quoted oracle. The structure is a classic conditional: *protasis* (they refuse) → *apodosis* (you speak YHWH's word) → *oracle* (they drink anyway).

### v. 29 — Seven clauses
1. **Ptcp** — "behold, in the city … I am beginning"
2. **xQtX** — relative "whom my name is called upon"
3. **InfC** — "to do evil"
4. **WXYq** — emphatic "and you — being-acquitted shall you be acquitted?"
5. **xYq0** — negated answer "you shall not be acquitted"
6. **Ptcp** — "for a sword I am calling upon all inhabitants of the earth"
7. **MSyn** — signature formula "declaration of YHWH of hosts"

The fronted וְאַתֶּם in clause 4 (subject before the emphatic construction) marks the contrastive pivot: "*as for you* — be cleared? no."

## Discourse Architecture

- **vv. 15-16** — Direct divine command (imperative + weqatals) with projected future effects.
- **v. 17** — Prophetic execution in wayyiqtol narrative, echoing v. 15 verb-for-verb.
- **vv. 18-26** — Catalog of recipient nations, chained by waw-conjunction without any finite verb: Judah first (v. 18, with a purpose-infinitive לָתֵת framing the result), then Egypt, Philistia, Edom/Moab/Ammon, Tyre/Sidon, Arabia, Elam/Media, "all the kings of the north," and finally Sheshach. The list is constructed as noun phrases dangling from v. 17's "all the nations," making the geographic scope function as a single extended direct object.
- **vv. 27-29** — Second oracle within the oracle: an imperative cascade (v. 27), a conditional for refusers (v. 28) with an infinitive-absolute emphatic, and an a fortiori warning (v. 29) with a second infinitive-absolute emphatic.

The grammatical center of gravity is the cup itself — הַכּוֹס appears six times in the passage, always either being taken (from YHWH's hand, from Jeremiah's hand) or being refused, or being the implicit object of drinking-verbs. The syntax treats the cup as a *moving* object that passes from divine to prophetic to international hands.
