## Lexicon Definition

Neuter substantive of G2413 (ἱερός, sacred); a sacred place, i.e. the entire precincts of the temple complex (whereas G3485 ναός denotes the central sanctuary/inner shrine itself). Used of the Jerusalem temple in all but one NT occurrence (Acts 19:27 — temple of Artemis at Ephesus).

## Semantic Categories

### 1. Jesus Teaching and Debating in the Temple Courts
The most common pattern — Jesus uses the ἱερόν (the accessible outer courts) as the arena for public teaching and confrontation with authorities.

- **John 7:14** — "Now about the midst of the feast Jesus went up into the temple, and taught."
- **John 8:20** — "These words spake Jesus in the treasury, as he taught in the temple."
- **Mark 14:49** — "I was daily with you in the temple teaching, and ye took me not."
- **Matt 26:55** — "Are ye come out as against a thief with swords and staves for to take me? I sat daily with you teaching in the temple."

### 2. Jesus Cleansing the Temple
All four gospels record Jesus driving out merchants — always ἱερόν, the outer commercial courts, never the inner ναός.

- **Matt 21:12** — "Jesus went into the temple of God, and cast out all them that sold and bought in the temple."
- **Mark 11:15–16** — "began to cast out them that sold and bought in the temple...would not suffer that any man should carry any vessel through the temple."
- **John 2:14–15** — "found in the temple those that sold oxen and sheep and doves...and he drove them all out of the temple."

### 3. The Temple as Place of Prayer, Worship, and Healing
People come to the temple precinct not only for sacrifice but for prayer and encounter with God.

- **Luke 18:10** — "Two men went up into the temple to pray; the one a Pharisee, and the other a publican."
- **Luke 2:37** — "she departed not from the temple, but served God with fastings and prayers night and day."
- **Matt 21:14** — "And the blind and the lame came to him in the temple; and he healed them."
- **Acts 3:1–2** — "Peter and John went up together into the temple at the hour of prayer...a certain man lame from his mother's womb was carried, whom they laid daily at the gate of the temple."

### 4. Early Church Continuing Temple Attendance
Luke's Acts presents the Jerusalem community maintaining daily presence in the ἱερόν for prayer, proclamation, and fellowship.

- **Acts 2:46** — "continuing daily with one accord in the temple, and breaking bread from house to house."
- **Acts 5:20–21** — "Go, stand and speak in the temple to the people all the words of this life...they entered into the temple early in the morning, and taught."
- **Acts 5:42** — "daily in the temple, and in every house, they ceased not to teach and preach Jesus Christ."
- **Luke 24:53** — "And were continually in the temple, praising and blessing God."

### 5. Confrontation and Arrest
The temple as a flashpoint for conflict — authorities challenging Jesus; Paul seized by a mob there.

- **Matt 21:23** — "the chief priests and the elders of the people came unto him as he was come into the temple."
- **Acts 21:27–28** — "the Jews which were of Asia...stirred up all the people, and laid hands on him."
- **Acts 26:21** — "For these causes the Jews caught me in the temple, and went about to kill me."

### 6. Temple as Institution / Sacred Precinct
Abstract or functional references — includes one non-Jerusalem pagan temple.

- **Matt 12:5–6** — "the priests in the temple profane the sabbath, and are blameless...in this place is one greater than the temple."
- **1 Cor 9:13** — "they which minister about holy things live of the things of the temple."
- **Acts 19:27** — "the temple of the great goddess Diana" — ἱερόν applied to a pagan sanctuary.

### Easily Confused Words

| Word | Strong's | Shared Translation | Key Difference |
|------|----------|--------------------|----------------|
| ναός (naos) | G3485 | both "temple" (KJV) | naos = inner sanctuary where God dwells; hierón = entire precinct including outer courts, colonnades, gates |
| οἶκος (oikos) | G3624 | "temple" (1x KJV) | oikos = general house/dwelling; only "temple" when context specifies "house of God" |

The ἱερόν/ναός distinction is exegetically significant: Jesus teaches, heals, and trades confront in the ἱερόν (open courts), but John 2:19–21 uses ναός when Jesus speaks of his body as the temple. Hebrews and Revelation use ναός exclusively for the heavenly sanctuary. ἱερόν never carries this theological weight.
