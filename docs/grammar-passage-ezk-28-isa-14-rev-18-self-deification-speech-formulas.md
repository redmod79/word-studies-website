## Key Grammatical Features

### 1. Heart-Located Speech

Isa 14:13 and Rev 18:7 explicitly locate the speech "in the heart" (`בִלְבָבְךָ`; `ἐν τῇ καρδίᾳ αὐτῆς`). Ezek 28:2 states the lifted heart before quoting the claim (`יַעַן גָּבַהּ לִבְּךָ וַתֹּאמֶר`). In all three, the grammar frames the claim as internal self-definition before it becomes public judgment material.

### 2. Enthronement/Sitting Language

Ezek 28:2 has Qal perfect 1cs `יָשַׁבְתִּי`; Isa 14:13 has Qal imperfect 1cs `אֵשֵׁב`; Rev 18:7 has present middle/passive indicative 1s `Κάθημαι`. The shared grammar is not identical language, but a similar self-enthronement predicate.

### 3. Divine or Royal Predicate Claim

Ezek 28:2 uses a predicate-first Hebrew nominal clause: `אֵל אָנִי`. Isa 14:14 uses Hithpael `אֶדַּמֶּה לְעֶלְיֹון`, a reflexive/self-likening verb. Rev 18:7 uses nominative predicate `βασίλισσα` with `Κάθημαι`: "I sit queen." These are three different grammatical strategies for self-elevating identity speech.

### 4. Formula Does Not Flatten Referents

The grammar establishes comparable speech patterns: heart-located self-claim, sitting/enthronement, self-elevation, and denial of creaturely vulnerability. It does not by itself decide whether the referents are individual rulers, cities, empires, spiritual powers, or typological layers.
