# Grammar Analysis: Revelation 13:11-18 (The Earth-Beast Pericope)

**Mode:** Passage unit analysis (context-neutral)
**Scope:** Rev 13:11-18 as a coherent discourse unit
**Aim:** Verse-by-verse parsing, key grammatical features (ἵνα purpose chains, dative-to-genitive case shift, πνεῦμα δίδωμι construction, divine passive ἐδόθη, καί + ἵνα + subjunctive enforcement chains), and structural relationship to the sea-beast pericope of vv.1-10. No interpretive commitments drawn beyond what the grammar supports.

**Verbal frame:** one finite aorist (εἶδον, vision-event) + two **imperfects** (εἶχεν, ἐλάλει — descriptive background). The imperfect pair is significant: v.11 does not use present participles (as v.1 did for the sea-beast) but shifts to imperfect indicatives — giving the earth-beast a more settled, habitual characterization. The two imperfects describe *ongoing* possession and speech, not punctiliar events.

**ἄλλο vs. ἕτερος:** ἄλλος (G243, "another of the same kind") rather than ἕτερος (G2087, "another of a different kind"). This is a grammatical marker of similarity in category — the second θηρίον is the same kind of entity as the first.

**Verbal frame:** two present actives (ποιεῖ ×2 — ongoing/habitual activity), a **future indicative after ἵνα** (see §4.1 below — grammatically irregular), and a resumptive aorist passive ἐθεραπεύθη in the relative clause (linking back to v.3).

**ποιεῖ + ἵνα + future indicative:** This is the first ἵνα in the pericope and it takes a **future indicative** (προσκυνήσουσιν) rather than the expected subjunctive. This is a well-attested Johannine/Revelation variant; see §4.1 below.

**ποιεῖ + accusative + ἵνα construction:** ποιέω here functions as a causative ("he causes … that …"), taking a double object: accusative of the people caused (τὴν γῆν καὶ τοὺς κατοικοῦντας) and the ἵνα-clause specifying the compelled action. This causative ποιέω + ἵνα is the structural backbone of vv.12-16.

**Verbal frame:** present πλανᾷ (ongoing deception) → aorist passive ἐδόθη (divine passive — the signs were *given* to perform) → discourse participle λέγων (introducing indirect command) → two aorist infinitives ποιῆσαι (first: complementary to ἐδόθη; second: indirect command after λέγων) → present ἔχει + aorist ἔζησεν (constative: "lived" = came back to life).

**ἐδόθη αὐτῷ ποιῆσαι:** This is the same divine-passive-plus-infinitive construction as vv.5, 7 of the sea-beast pericope. The earth-beast's miracle-working authority is framed with the identical granted-permission syntax. See `grammar-reference/greek/divine-passive.md`.

**λέγων + dative + infinitive:** λέγων τοῖς κατοικοῦσιν … ποιῆσαι εἰκόνα — the present participle of discourse (λέγων) introduces an **indirect command** via the aorist infinitive ποιῆσαι. The indirect-object dative (τοῖς κατοικοῦσιν) receives the command. This is standard discourse-participle syntax; see `grammar-reference/greek/participle-legon-discourse.md`.

**εἰκόνα τῷ θηρίῳ:** the image is made "for/to the beast" — the dative τῷ θηρίῳ is a **dative of advantage** (or possibly honor/dedication). This is the first mention of the εἰκών, and the beast here is marked in the **dative** case. This will shift to the **genitive** in v.15 — see §4.3 below.

**ὃς (masculine) for θηρίον (neuter):** the relative pronoun ὃς is **masculine nominative singular**, not the expected neuter ὅ to agree with τῷ θηρίῳ (neut.). This is **constructio ad sensum** — the relative follows the logical gender of the personal referent behind the neuter θηρίον. See `grammar-reference/greek/constructio-ad-sensum.md` and `grammar-reference/greek/concord-shift-neuter-to-masculine.md`.

**Verbal frame:** This verse is the most grammatically dense in the pericope. It contains: (1) the ἐδόθη + infinitive divine passive, (2) the unique πνεῦμα-δίδωμι construction, (3) a **nested double ἵνα** chain, and (4) a conditional-relative clause inside the second ἵνα. The full clause architecture is analyzed in §4.1 and §4.2 below.

**ἐδόθη αὐτῷ δοῦναι πνεῦμα:** A **double-δίδωμι** construction — the passive ἐδόθη ("it was given") takes the active infinitive δοῦναι ("to give") as its complement. The same root appears twice in different voices: "it was given [to him] to give." This is the **πνεῦμα δίδωμι construction** — see §4.2 below.

**τῇ εἰκόνι τοῦ θηρίου:** The image is now referred to with a **genitive** modifier (τοῦ θηρίου) rather than the **dative** of v.14 (εἰκόνα τῷ θηρίῳ). See §4.3 for the dative-to-genitive shift.

**Verbal frame:** present ποιεῖ (the fifth occurrence of this verb in the pericope) + ἵνα + aorist subjunctive δῶσιν (3p — indefinite "they" subject). This is the ποιεῖ + ἵνα + subjunctive pattern in its regular form (contrast v.12's future indicative after ἵνα).

**Triple merism:** The three pairs (μικροὺς/μεγάλους, πλουσίους/πτωχούς, ἐλευθέρους/δούλους) are all substantive adjectives in the accusative plural, each pair preceded by articular τούς. This is a **rhetorical exhaustion figure** (merism) — three opposite-pairs cover the totality of humanity. All six terms are accusative, functioning as appositional expansion of πάντας.

**δῶσιν (3p subject shift):** The subject of δῶσιν ("they give") is impersonal/indefinite third plural — an agent-shift from the earth-beast (3s ποιεῖ) to the agents who actually administer the mark. The ἵνα + subjunctive clause thus has a different grammatical subject than the main verb.

**Case alternation in ἐπί phrases:** ἐπὶ τῆς χειρός (genitive = location/surface) vs. ἐπὶ τὸ μέτωπον (accusative = direction/placement). The genitive with ἐπί implies "on/upon" as static location; the accusative implies motion toward or placement upon. This case alternation may be a stylistic variant rather than a semantic distinction, as Revelation's Greek frequently alternates preposition-case patterns.

**εἰ μὴ exception clause:** The standard Greek idiom for "except/unless" — εἰ μή introduces the sole condition under which buying/selling is permitted.

**Three-fold apposition:** τὸ χάραγμα, τὸ ὄνομα τοῦ θηρίου, ἢ τὸν ἀριθμὸν τοῦ ὀνόματος αὐτοῦ — the mark is defined as consisting in (or equivalent to) the name, or the number of the name. The three accusatives (χάραγμα, ὄνομα, ἀριθμόν) stand in an appositional or explanatory relationship, connected by ἤ (disjunctive "or"). The **genitive chain** τοῦ ὀνόματος αὐτοῦ ("of his name") creates a three-deep genitive dependency: ἀριθμόν → ὀνόματος → αὐτοῦ.

### Load-bearing observations

1. **Present indicative dominance.** Unlike the sea-beast pericope (dominated by aorists), the earth-beast pericope is driven by **present-tense main verbs** (7 of 14 finite indicatives). The earth-beast's activity is framed as *ongoing, habitual, present-tense* action rather than as narrative events viewed as wholes. This contrasts sharply with the aorist-backbone of vv.1-10.

2. **ποιέω saturation.** The verb ποιέω occurs **9 times** across vv.12-16 (4 present indicative, 2 present subjunctive/aorist subjunctive, 2 aorist infinitive, 1 aorist subjunctive). It is the structurally dominant verb of the pericope. The earth-beast is grammatically characterized as "the one who makes/causes/does."

3. **ἐδόθη count drops.** The sea-beast pericope has four ἐδόθη's (vv.5, 7). The earth-beast pericope has only two (v.14, v.15). The earth-beast's authority is derived (it acts ἐνώπιον αὐτοῦ, "in his presence/on his behalf"), so fewer direct divine-passive grants appear.

4. **ἵνα clauses carry the enforcement mechanism.** Verses 12-17 contain **six ἵνα clauses** (the highest density in Revelation). Each successive ἵνα specifies the *purpose or result* of the earth-beast's ποιεῖ-activity. This is the grammatical engine of the passage. See §4.1.

## 4. Key Grammatical Features

### 4.1 The ἵνα Purpose Chain Structure (vv.12-17)

The most distinctive syntactic feature of this pericope is the cascading chain of ἵνα-clauses. Six ἵνα-clauses appear in rapid succession, structurally layered:

```
v.12  ποιεῖ τὴν γῆν ... ἵνα προσκυνήσουσιν (FUT IND)    ← worship compulsion
v.13  ποιεῖ σημεῖα ... ἵνα πῦρ ποιῇ καταβαίνειν (PRES SUBJ) ← sign authentication
v.15a ἐδόθη ... δοῦναι πνεῦμα ... ἵνα λαλήσῃ (AOR SUBJ)  ← image speaks
v.15b     καὶ ποιήσῃ ἵνα ... ἀποκτανθῶσιν (AOR SUBJ)    ← NESTED: death decree
v.16  ποιεῖ πάντας ... ἵνα δῶσιν χάραγμα (AOR SUBJ)      ← mark imposed
v.17  καὶ ἵνα μή τις δύνηται ἀγοράσαι (PRES SUBJ)       ← economic enforcement
```

**Structural analysis:**

1. **v.12: ἵνα + future indicative.** The form προσκυνήσουσιν (V-FAI-3P) after ἵνα is morphologically **future indicative**, not aorist subjunctive. In classical Greek, ἵνα always takes the subjunctive. In Koine and especially in Revelation, ἵνα + future indicative appears as a variant construction. The phenomenon is well documented in Revelation (cf. Rev 3:9 ἵνα ἥξουσιν; 6:4 ἵνα … σφάξουσιν; 8:3 ἵνα δώσει; 9:4 ἵνα … βασανισθήσονται — textual variants show the copyist tradition struggled with these forms). BDF §369(2) treats this as a Semitizing tendency influenced by Hebrew/Aramaic purpose constructions where the imperfect can serve in purpose clauses. Wallace notes it as a rare but genuine Koine phenomenon (ExSyn 473–474). In this verse the construction may signal a stronger **predictive certainty** about the worship — less "in order that they might worship" (potential) and more "so that they shall worship" (assured outcome).

2. **v.13: ἵνα + present subjunctive.** Regular construction. The present subjunctive ποιῇ (rather than aorist) may emphasize the *ongoing/repeated* nature of the sign-making.

3. **v.15: Nested double ἵνα.** This is the most syntactically complex verse. The structure is:
   - **Level 1:** ἐδόθη αὐτῷ δοῦναι πνεῦμα τῇ εἰκόνι τοῦ θηρίου — main clause (divine passive + infinitive)
   - **Level 2:** ἵνα καὶ λαλήσῃ ἡ εἰκὼν τοῦ θηρίου — first purpose clause (image speaks)
   - **Level 2 continued:** καὶ ποιήσῃ — coordinated with λαλήσῃ (image also causes)
   - **Level 3:** ἵνα ὅσοι ἐὰν μὴ προσκυνήσωσιν … ἀποκτανθῶσιν — second ἵνα, nested inside the first, specifying what the image "causes"

   The nested ἵνα creates a **three-tier purpose chain**: *it was given → so that the image might speak and cause → so that whoever does not worship might be killed.* Each ἵνα adds a layer of purpose/result to the preceding clause.

4. **vv.16-17: καί + ἵνα continuation.** The ἵνα of v.17 (καὶ ἵνα μή τις δύνηται) is coordinated with the ἵνα of v.16 by καί. Both ἵνα-clauses depend on the same main verb ποιεῖ (v.16). The two purposes of the mark are: (a) the mark is given (v.16 ἵνα δῶσιν), and (b) no one can buy/sell without it (v.17 ἵνα μή τις δύνηται). The second ἵνα is **negative purpose** (ἵνα μή).

**Summary:** The six ἵνα-clauses form a progressive enforcement escalation: worship → signs → image animating → death decree → mark → economic exclusion. Each ἵνα grammatically subordinates a new compulsion to the earth-beast's main-verb ποιεῖ-activity. The chain is the syntactic embodiment of the coercive program.

### 4.2 The πνεῦμα δίδωμι Construction (v.15)

**Construction:** ἐδόθη αὐτῷ δοῦναι πνεῦμα τῇ εἰκόνι τοῦ θηρίου

This is a **double-δίδωμι** — the same verb appears in passive finite form (ἐδόθη) and active infinitive form (δοῦναι) in the same clause. "It was given to him to give breath/spirit to the image of the beast."

**Grammatical analysis:**

1. **ἐδόθη** (V-API-3S) — aorist passive indicative of δίδωμι. Divine passive: the unnamed agent who grants the earth-beast this capacity. See `grammar-reference/greek/divine-passive.md`.

2. **δοῦναι** (V-2AAN) — aorist active infinitive of δίδωμι. Complementary infinitive specifying what was "given" — the ability to give. The double-δίδωμι creates a **meta-giving** construction: the permission (ἐδόθη) and the action permitted (δοῦναι) use the same lexeme.

3. **πνεῦμα** (N-ASN) — accusative, direct object of δοῦναι. The term is semantically loaded: πνεῦμα can mean "spirit," "breath," or "wind" (see `word-studies/G4151-pneuma.md`). In this grammatical context (δοῦναι πνεῦμα + dative recipient), the construction echoes the life-giving idiom found in Gen 2:7 LXX (ἐνεφύσησεν … πνοὴν ζωῆς), Ezek 37:5-6 LXX (δίδωμι … πνεῦμα ζωῆς), and the broader OT pattern of God giving πνεῦμα/πνοή to creation.

4. **τῇ εἰκόνι** (N-DSF) — dative indirect object: the image *receives* the πνεῦμα.

**Theological-grammatical observation:** The construction is formally identical to the OT divine prerogative of giving breath/life. The grammar frames the earth-beast as a **delegated agent** (ἐδόθη = received permission) performing a **divine-type act** (δοῦναι πνεῦμα = giving spirit/breath). The double-δίδωμι underscores the derived nature of the capacity — the earth-beast cannot autonomously animate; it is *given to give*.

**Parallels in Revelation's ἐδόθη inventory:**

| Reference | Construction | Content |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| Rev 13:5 | ἐδόθη αὐτῷ … ποιῆσαι | authority to act (42 months) |
| Rev 13:7 | ἐδόθη αὐτῷ ποιῆσαι πόλεμον | to make war |
| Rev 13:14 | ἐδόθη αὐτῷ ποιῆσαι | to perform (signs) |
| **Rev 13:15** | **ἐδόθη αὐτῷ δοῦναι πνεῦμα** | **to give spirit/breath** |
| Rev 16:8 | ἐδόθη αὐτῷ καυματίσαι | to scorch |

The v.15 instance is unique in that the complementary infinitive is **δοῦναι** (same root as the main verb), creating the double-δίδωμι. All other instances use a different verb for the complement.

### 4.3 The Dative-to-Genitive Case Shift in εἰκών/θηρίον References

The relationship between the image (εἰκών) and the beast (θηρίον) is expressed in **two different case constructions** within this pericope:

| Reference | Construction | Case of θηρίον | Semantic Relationship |
|-----------|-------------|----------------|----------------------|
| v.14 | ποιῆσαι εἰκόνα **τῷ θηρίῳ** | **Dative** | image made *for/to* the beast |
| v.15a | τῇ εἰκόνι **τοῦ θηρίου** | **Genitive** | image *of* the beast |
| v.15b | ἡ εἰκὼν **τοῦ θηρίου** | **Genitive** | image *of* the beast |
| v.15c | τῇ εἰκόνι **τοῦ θηρίου** | **Genitive** | image *of* the beast |

**Analysis:**

1. **v.14 dative (τῷ θηρίῳ):** The beast is the **beneficiary** of the image-making. The dative is a **dative of advantage** — the image is constructed "for" or "in honor of" the beast. This is the natural case when the image is first introduced in the context of its *creation* (ποιῆσαι εἰκόνα τῷ θηρίῳ = "to make an image for/to the beast").

2. **v.15 genitive (τοῦ θηρίου ×3):** Once the image exists and has received πνεῦμα, the beast-referent shifts to the **genitive**. The genitive here functions as a **genitive of relationship/origin** — "the image of the beast" identifies the image as belonging to, representing, or deriving from the beast. The image is no longer being *made for* the beast (dative = goal/beneficiary) but is now *identified with* the beast (genitive = source/possession/representation).

3. **The shift marks a stage transition:** The dative in v.14 corresponds to the **construction phase** (the image is being made *for* someone). The genitive in v.15 corresponds to the **operation phase** (the image now *belongs to* and *represents* the beast). The case shift thus tracks a semantic transition from dedicatory relationship (dative) to possessive/representative relationship (genitive).

4. **Broader pattern:** This same dative-to-genitive transition is visible in idol-making language in the LXX and wider Hellenistic Greek: the beneficiary dative appears at the point of manufacture, and the possessive genitive appears when referring to the finished product. The grammar marks the difference between "to whom it is made" and "whose it is."

### 4.4 The Divine Passive ἐδόθη (vv.14, 15)

Two ἐδόθη instances appear in this pericope (vs. four in vv.1-10):

| v. | Construction | Complement | Content |
|----|-------------|------------|---------|
| 14 | ἐδόθη αὐτῷ ποιῆσαι | ἃ (= σημεῖα) | sign-performing authority |
| 15 | ἐδόθη αὐτῷ δοῦναι πνεῦμα | πνεῦμα + τῇ εἰκόνι | image-animating authority |

Both follow the standard Revelation ἐδόθη + dative + infinitive formula (see `grammar-reference/greek/divine-passive.md`). The unnamed agent of the passive is the same as in vv.5, 7 — God's sovereign permission, operating through the mediating agency chain.

**Key distinction from vv.1-10:** In the sea-beast pericope, the dragon is explicitly named as the active giver in v.2 (ἔδωκεν … ὁ δράκων), and the subsequent ἐδόθη's leave the agent unnamed. In the earth-beast pericope, no active giving by the dragon is stated — the earth-beast enters under ἐδόθη from the start (v.14). The entire earth-beast's authority-chain is framed in the passive voice.

### 4.5 The καί + ἵνα + Subjunctive Enforcement Chains (vv.15-17)

The enforcement mechanism of the death decree and the mark is grammatically structured as a series of **coordinated ἵνα-clauses** linked by καί:

```
v.15  ἵνα καὶ λαλήσῃ ἡ εἰκὼν τοῦ θηρίου         [purpose 1: speak]
      καὶ ποιήσῃ                                    [purpose 2: cause]
           ἵνα ὅσοι ἐὰν μὴ προσκυνήσωσιν …        [nested purpose: death]
                ἀποκτανθῶσιν

v.16  ποιεῖ πάντας …                               [main verb]
      ἵνα δῶσιν αὐτοῖς χάραγμα                    [purpose 3: mark]

v.17  καὶ ἵνα μή τις δύνηται ἀγοράσαι ἢ πωλῆσαι  [purpose 4: economic exclusion]
```

**The coordination pattern:** Each successive enforcement measure is added with καί + ἵνα (or καί within the ἵνα-clause). This is not loose parataxis but a **deliberate escalation chain** where each καί adds a new layer of enforcement:
- v.15: the image speaks **and** (καί) causes death for non-worshippers
- v.16: the beast causes all to receive the mark
- v.17: **and** (καί) no one can buy or sell without it

The καί connecting vv.16-17 is structurally significant: the ἵνα of v.17 depends on the same ποιεῖ of v.16, making the mark-imposition and the economic sanction **two purposes of a single causative action**, not two separate actions.

**Conditional-relative inside ἵνα (v.15):** The clause ὅσοι ἐὰν μὴ προσκυνήσωσιν is a **conditional relative clause** (ὅσοι = "whoever," ἐάν + subjunctive = indefinite condition). The negative μή negates the condition: "whoever does not worship." The ἐάν + subjunctive inside the ἵνα + subjunctive creates a double-subjunctive embedding — rare in the NT and characteristic of Revelation's complex subordination style.

## 5. ποιέω Saturation: The Earth-Beast's Signature Verb

The verb ποιέω (G4160) appears **9 times** in vv.12-16, making it by far the most frequent verb in the pericope:

| v. | Form | Parsing | Construction |
|----|------|---------|-------------|
| 12a | ποιεῖ | V-PAI-3S | τὴν ἐξουσίαν … πᾶσαν ποιεῖ ("exercises all authority") |
| 12b | ποιεῖ | V-PAI-3S | ποιεῖ τὴν γῆν … ἵνα ("causes the earth … that") |
| 13a | ποιεῖ | V-PAI-3S | ποιεῖ σημεῖα μεγάλα ("performs great signs") |
| 13b | ποιῇ | V-PAS-3S | ἵνα πῦρ ποιῇ καταβαίνειν ("so that he causes fire to descend") |
| 14a | ποιῆσαι | V-AAN | ἐδόθη αὐτῷ ποιῆσαι ("it was given to him to perform") |
| 14b | ποιῆσαι | V-AAN | λέγων … ποιῆσαι εἰκόνα ("saying … to make an image") |
| 15 | ποιήσῃ | V-AAS-3S | ἵνα … ποιήσῃ ("so that [the image] causes") |
| 16 | ποιεῖ | V-PAI-3S | ποιεῖ πάντας … ἵνα ("causes all … that") |

**Three distinct senses of ποιέω** in this pericope:
1. **"Exercise/perform"** (v.12a: exercises authority; v.13a: performs signs; v.14a: perform signs)
2. **"Cause/compel"** (v.12b, v.13b, v.15, v.16: causative — ποιεῖ + acc + ἵνα, or ποιεῖ + acc + inf)
3. **"Make/construct"** (v.14b: make an image)

The causative use is dominant (5 of 9 occurrences), reinforcing the earth-beast's grammatical identity as a **compelling agent** — one who *makes others do things*. This contrasts with the sea-beast, whose actions in vv.1-10 are described with diverse verbs (ἀναβαίνω, ἔχω, λαλέω, ἀνοίγω, βλασφημέω, πολεμέω, νικάω) rather than the monotonous ποιέω.

## 6. Structural Outline — Relation to vv.1-10

### 6.1 Parallel structure between sea-beast and earth-beast pericopes

| Feature | vv.1-10 (Sea-beast) | vv.11-18 (Earth-beast) |
|---------|---------------------|------------------------|
| Vision formula | εἶδον + ἐκ τῆς θαλάσσης (v.1) | εἶδον + ἐκ τῆς γῆς (v.11) |
| Description tense | pres ptcps ἀναβαῖνον, ἔχον (v.1) | impf indicatives εἶχεν, ἐλάλει (v.11) |
| Authority source | ἔδωκεν (active, dragon, v.2) | ἐνώπιον αὐτοῦ (before the 1st beast, v.12) |
| Divine passive count | ἐδόθη ×4 (vv.5, 7) | ἐδόθη ×2 (vv.14, 15) |
| Main verb | diverse (8+ different verbs) | ποιέω (9 occurrences) |
| ἵνα clauses | 0 | 6 |
| Closing formula | Ὧδέ ἐστιν ἡ ὑπομονὴ … (v.10) | Ὧδε ἡ σοφία ἐστίν (v.18) |
| Aorist imperative | ἀκουσάτω (v.9) | ψηφισάτω (v.18) |
| Worship verb | προσκυνέω (vv.4, 8) | προσκυνέω (vv.12, 15) |

### 6.2 The voice/tense contrast

The sea-beast pericope is driven by **aorist indicatives** (narrative-event backbone). The earth-beast pericope is driven by **present indicatives** (ongoing-habitual backbone). This aspect-shift maps a functional distinction: the sea-beast's career is narrated as a series of completed events; the earth-beast's activity is depicted as ongoing, present-tense enforcement.

### 6.3 The ἵνα contrast

Zero ἵνα clauses in vv.1-10 vs. six in vv.11-18. The enforcement/compulsion mechanism is grammatically encoded exclusively in the earth-beast's half of the chapter. The sea-beast *receives* authority; the earth-beast *deploys* it through purpose-clause chains.

### 6.4 Closing formula comparison

| v.10 | v.18 |
|------|------|
| Ὧδέ ἐστιν ἡ ὑπομονὴ καὶ ἡ πίστις τῶν ἁγίων | Ὧδε ἡ σοφία ἐστίν |
| "Here is the patience and faith of the saints" | "Here is wisdom" |
| Addressed to saints: *endurance* | Addressed to saints: *discernment* |
| εἴ τις + ἀκουσάτω (hearing formula) | ὁ ἔχων νοῦν + ψηφισάτω (calculating formula) |
| Aorist imperative of ἀκούω | Aorist imperative of ψηφίζω |

Both closings use the **Ὧδε + ἐστίν** deictic formula + a 3rd-person aorist imperative. The grammatical parallelism (same syntax, different vocabulary) marks these as **matched paraenetic conclusions** to the two halves of the chapter.

## 8. Executive Summary (for downstream studies)

- **Tense dominance:** Present indicative (7 instances) dominates the earth-beast pericope — ongoing/habitual characterization, not event-narrative. Contrast with the aorist-dominant sea-beast pericope.
- **ποιέω saturation:** 9 occurrences of ποιέω in 5 verses (vv.12-16), primarily in the causative sense ("cause/compel"). The earth-beast is grammatically characterized as "the compelling agent."
- **ἵνα chain architecture:** Six ἵνα-clauses (vv.12-17) form a progressive enforcement escalation: forced worship → miraculous signs → image animation → death decree → mark imposition → economic exclusion. Includes one nested double-ἵνα (v.15) and one irregular ἵνα + future indicative (v.12).
- **πνεῦμα δίδωμι construction (v.15):** Double-δίδωμι (ἐδόθη … δοῦναι πνεῦμα) — the earth-beast is *given the capacity to give breath*. Formally echoes the OT divine life-giving idiom (Ezek 37:5-6 LXX).
- **Dative-to-genitive shift (vv.14-15):** εἰκόνα τῷ θηρίῳ (dative of advantage, construction phase) → εἰκόνι τοῦ θηρίου (genitive of possession/representation, operation phase). The case shift marks the transition from image-making to image-functioning.
- **Divine passive ἐδόθη ×2** (vv.14, 15) — fewer than the sea-beast's four, reflecting the earth-beast's derivative authority. No active ἔδωκεν with named agent appears in this pericope.
- **Constructio ad sensum (v.14):** masculine relative ὅς for neuter antecedent θηρίον — the personal referent overrides grammatical gender.
- **Closing formula Ὧδε … ἐστίν + aorist imperative** (v.18) parallels v.10's formula exactly in syntax, switching from ἀκουσάτω (hear) to ψηφισάτω (calculate) — endurance vs. discernment as the paired audience-responses to the two beasts.
