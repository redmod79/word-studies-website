**Semantic mapping in translation:** The LXX translators preserved the hear-obey duality of shama through akouō, which in classical Greek already carried both "perceive with the ear" and "give heed to" senses. However, the LXX also disambiguated: when shama specifically means "obey," the compound ὑπακούω (hupakouō, literally "hear under") was sometimes used, making the subordination explicit. When God is the hearer of prayer, εἰσακούω (eisakouō, "hear into") was preferred, adding the nuance of favorable reception.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The fundamental hear-obey duality persists from Hebrew shama through Greek akouō. Both testaments treat genuine hearing as incomplete without responsive obedience.
- The covenant formula "hear/obey the voice of the LORD" (Deut 28) maps directly onto NT patterns: "he that heareth my words and doeth them" (Matt 7:24), "my sheep hear my voice" (John 10:27).
- Judicial deafness — hearing without understanding — carries across via the Isa 6:9 quotation chain (Matt 13:14; Acts 28:26).
- The Shema pattern (Deut 6:4 "Hear, O Israel") finds echoes in the Revelation refrain "He that hath an ear, let him hear" (Rev 2:7, etc.).

**What shifts:**
- Greek disambiguates what Hebrew holds together. NT writers have a richer vocabulary to separate the senses: akouō (basic hearing), hupakouō (obedient hearing), eisakouō (favorable hearing/answering prayer), parakouō (refusal to hear). Hebrew shama carries all these senses in a single word.
- The hearing-faith nexus becomes explicit in Paul: "faith cometh by hearing (akoē), and hearing by the word of God" (Rom 10:17). The OT implies this link (Abraham "obeyed my voice," Gen 26:5) but Paul states it systematically.
- In the NT, hearing becomes christologically focused — "hear him" (Matt 17:5, the Transfiguration voice from heaven) echoes the Deuteronomic prophet prophecy: "unto him ye shall hearken" (Deut 18:15).
