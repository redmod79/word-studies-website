## Lexicon Definition

A prolonged (transitive) form of μεθύω (G3184); **to intoxicate**, "to make drunk." KJV: "be drunk(-en)." The *-skō* suffix in Greek marks an inchoative/causative stem — denoting the *process* of becoming something or *causing* it. Where μεθύω (G3184) describes the state of being drunk, μεθύσκω describes **the act or process of getting drunk** (or of making someone drunk).

## Semantic Categories

### 1. Process of Becoming Drunk (Ingressive/Inchoative)
The *-skō* form highlights the *onset* of drunkenness — crossing the threshold into intoxication, not merely holding a cup.
- **Luke 12:45** — "and shall begin to beat the menservants and maidens, and to eat and drink, and to be drunken (methýskō)"
- **Eph 5:18** — "And be not drunk (methýskō) with wine, wherein is excess; but be filled with the Spirit"

### 2. Prohibitive / Vice Context
The verb appears under direct apostolic prohibition or in a vice that contrasts with righteous behavior.
- **Eph 5:18** — the imperative prohibition μὴ μεθύσκεσθε ("stop getting drunk / do not be getting drunk") — present imperative with μή, often read as "do not make a practice of getting drunk"
- **Luke 12:45** — listed alongside beating servants as behavior of the wicked servant whose lord surprises him in judgment

### 3. Eschatological Contrast with Sobriety
Drunkenness functions symbolically for spiritual unreadiness; its opposite is *nēphō* (to be sober, G3525) and *grēgoreō* (to watch, G1127).
- **1 Thess 5:7** — "For they that sleep sleep in the night; and they that be drunken (methýskō) are drunken (methýō) in the night" (Paul uses G3182 and G3184 together — the process and the state — both belonging to the darkness)

### G3182 vs. G3184 — The Critical Pair
Greek routinely derives inchoative/causative verbs from stative ones via the *-skō* infix (cf. γινώσκω from root *gnō-*; διδάσκω from *didak-*). The morphological contrast:

- **μεθύω (G3184)** — "to be in the state of drunkenness" (durative). Attested of people already drunk (Matt 24:49; Acts 2:15; 1 Cor 11:21), of wedding guests "when men have well drunk" (John 2:10), and of the whore of Babylon "drunken with the blood of the saints" (Rev 17:6).
- **μεθύσκω (G3182)** — "to get drunk / to make drunk" (ingressive or causative). The verb of *becoming* intoxicated.

In 1 Thess 5:7 Paul uses both in a single clause — "they that be drunken *(methyskomenoi* — G3182, getting drunk) are drunken *(methyousin* — G3184, are in a drunken state) in the night" — a deliberate pairing of process and state.

## Focus Note — Ephesians 5:18

μὴ μεθύσκεσθε οἴνῳ, ἐν ᾧ ἐστιν ἀσωτία, ἀλλὰ πληροῦσθε ἐν πνεύματι.

- **μὴ μεθύσκεσθε** — present middle-passive imperative with μή: "do not go on getting drunk" / "stop becoming drunk." The form is the *process* verb (G3182), not the state verb (G3184).
- **οἴνῳ** — dative of means: "with wine." Paul names wine (G3631, oînos) as the specific intoxicant under prohibition. The prohibition applies precisely because οἶνος in this construction is capable of producing μεθύσκω. A non-alcoholic beverage is not the referent of the warning.
- **ἐν ᾧ ἐστιν ἀσωτία** — "in which is dissipation/debauchery (G810)." The relative ᾧ is typically read as referring to the *action* (getting drunk) or to the wine-as-used-to-excess, not to wine as such.
- **πληροῦσθε ἐν πνεύματι** — antithetical imperative: "be filled with/in Spirit." The contrast is between two controlling influences, not between two beverages.
