**Semantic shift in translation:** Hebrew ʼemeth carries a dual sense: (1) truth/reality and (2) faithfulness/reliability/stability. The LXX splits this: ἀλήθεια captures the "truth/reality" sense (83x), while the "faithfulness" sense sometimes routes through πίστις (G4102, 5x) or ἔλεος (G1656, 25x — when paired with chesed). The notable LXX rendering as δικαιοσύνη (23x) shows that ʼemeth's "reliability of God" sense could be understood as his "righteousness." This semantic narrowing means NT ἀλήθεια leans more toward propositional/ontological truth than the Hebrew ʼemeth's broader "trustworthy stability."

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- Both ʼemeth and alētheia describe divine reality as opposed to falsehood
- The pairing of truth with righteousness persists (OT: emeth + tsedeq; NT: alētheia + dikaiosynē)
- "Walking in truth" / "doing truth" is present in both testaments (cf. 2Ki 20:3; Jhn 3:21; 3Jo 1:3-4)
- "The word of truth" spans both (Psa 119:43; Eph 1:13; 2Ti 2:15)

**What shifts:**
- Hebrew ʼemeth is fundamentally relational — God's truth is his faithfulness/reliability toward his covenant people. NT alētheia tilts more toward propositional/ontological truth — the content of revelation, correct doctrine
- The OT pair "mercy and truth" (chesed we'emeth, ~25x) becomes "grace and truth" (charis kai alētheia) in Jhn 1:14, 17 — a deliberate echo but with different Greek vocabulary
- NT develops "the truth" as a technical term for Christian teaching/gospel (absent in OT), especially in the Pastorals and Johannine letters
- Christ as personal embodiment of truth (Jhn 14:6) has no direct parallel in the Hebrew concept, which is an attribute of God rather than a person
