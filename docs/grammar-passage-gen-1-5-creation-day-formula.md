# Grammar Analysis: Genesis 1:5 — The Creation Day Formula

## Text
**Hebrew:** וַיִּקְרָ֨א אֱלֹהִ֤ים׀ לָאֹור֙ יֹ֔ום וְלַחֹ֖שֶׁךְ קָ֣רָא לָ֑יְלָה וַֽיְהִי־עֶ֥רֶב וַֽיְהִי־בֹ֖קֶר יֹ֥ום אֶחָֽד׃
**KJV:** And God called the light Day, and the darkness he called Night. And the evening and the morning were the first day.

## Word-by-Word Parsing (Formula Portion Only)

Clauses 3–5 constitute the creation day formula. BHSA tags two independent WayX (wayyiqtol-first) clauses for the erev and boqer statements, followed by a verbless nominal clause (NmCl) for the day designation. The formula thus consists of **three syntactic units**: two narrative event-clauses and one verbless predication.

## Key Grammatical Features

### 1. וַיְהִי (wayyhi) — Qal Wayyiqtol 3ms of היה (H1961)

- **Form:** Waw-consecutive + imperfect (jussive short form) of היה, 3rd masculine singular. The apocopated form יְהִי (rather than the full יִהְיֶה) is standard for wayyiqtol of היה.
- **Function in the formula:** Each occurrence of וַיְהִי introduces a new clause with עֶרֶב or בֹקֶר as its grammatical subject. The verb is parsed as [Pred] and the noun as [Subj] in all six occurrences of the formula (Gen 1:5, 8, 13, 19, 23, 31). This is a wayyiqtol narrative backbone structure — the same clause type (WayX) used for the main action verbs in the narrative (e.g., וַיִּקְרָא "and he called," וַיַּרְא "and he saw").
- **Significance:** The wayyiqtol form places the evening-then-morning sequence squarely within the narrative event chain. These are not circumstantial descriptions (which would use weqatal or a verbless clause with different syntax) but sequential narrative events: evening happened, then morning happened. The verb היה in the Qal wayyiqtol carries the sense "came into being" or "came to pass" — a real occurrence, not merely a state of affairs.
- **Word study:** [H1961-hayah](../../word-studies/H1961-hayah.md)

### 2. עֶרֶב / בֹקֶר — Bare Nouns Without Prepositions or Articles

- **Form:** Both עֶרֶב (H6153, Noun.ms.Abs "evening") and בֹקֶר (H1242, Noun.ms.Abs "morning") appear in absolute state, indefinite (no article), and without any preposition. They function as grammatical subjects of their respective וַיְהִי clauses.
- **Significance:** This is grammatically unusual. Elsewhere in the OT, temporal references to evening and morning typically appear with prepositions: בָּעֶרֶב ("in the evening"), בַּבֹּקֶר ("in the morning"), עַד־הָעֶרֶב ("until the evening"), or as prepositional complements. In the Genesis 1 formula, the nouns are bare subjects of existential verbs — "evening was" and "morning was" — rather than temporal modifiers telling *when* something happened.
- **Contrast with Dan 8:14:** In Daniel 8:14 the same two nouns appear as a bare pair עֶרֶב בֹּקֶר (Noun.ms.Abs + Noun.ms.Abs) — also without articles or prepositions. But in Daniel the two nouns stand in **asyndetic juxtaposition** (no waw between them) functioning as a compound time-unit modified by the numeral 2,300. In Genesis the nouns are **syndetically separated** by the repeated וַיְהִי, each heading its own clause. The asyndetic compression in Daniel collapses the two-clause Genesis formula into a single compound noun-phrase.
- **Word studies:** [H6153-ereb](../../word-studies/H6153-ereb.md), [H1242-boqer](../../word-studies/H1242-boqer.md)

### 3. יֹום אֶחָד (yom echad) — Cardinal "One" Instead of Ordinal "First"

- **Form:** יֹום (H3117, Noun.ms.Abs "day") + אֶחָד (H259, Noun.s.Abs "one"). BHSA parses אֶחָד as a noun (cardinal numeral), not an adjective.
- **The anomaly:** Days 2–5 use ordinal adjectives: שֵׁנִי "second" (Gen 1:8, Adj.ms.Abs), שְׁלִישִׁי "third" (1:13), רְבִיעִי "fourth" (1:19), חֲמִישִׁי "fifth" (1:23). Day 6 uses the ordinal הַשִּׁשִּׁי "the sixth" (1:31, Adj.ms.Abs with article — the only day with an article on the ordinal). But day 1 uses the **cardinal** אֶחָד "one" — not the ordinal רִאשׁוֹן "first." The first occurrence of רִאשׁוֹן in Genesis is Gen 8:13 (the first month of Noah's year), not in the creation account.
- **Significance:** The use of the cardinal rather than the ordinal for day 1 is a well-recognized grammatical feature. It creates an asymmetry: all other days are counted in sequence (second, third, fourth, fifth, sixth), but the first day is designated not as "first" but as "one." This may function as an absolute designation — "day one" rather than "day first-in-a-series" — implying a unique status for the inaugural day before any series exists.
- **Word studies:** [H3117-yowm](../../word-studies/H3117-yowm.md), [H259-echad](../../word-studies/H259-echad.md)

### 4. The Verbless Nominal Clause (NmCl): יֹום אֶחָד / יֹום שֵׁנִי / etc.

- **Form:** BHSA tags the day designation as a separate clause — a **nominal clause** (NmCl) with the day-phrase as [PreC] (predicate complement). There is no copula. There is no explicit subject.
- **Significance:** This verbless clause functions as an appositional summary or label for the preceding two wayyiqtol clauses. The pattern "and-was evening, and-was morning — day X" treats the day designation as a tagline that packages the two events (evening, morning) into a single named unit. The absence of a verb in the day-designation clause contrasts with the two explicit verbs in the erev-boqer clauses — the day label is a static predication, while the evening-morning events are narrated occurrences.

### 5. The Article on Day Six: יֹום הַשִּׁשִּׁי

- **Form:** In Gen 1:31 the day designation reads יֹום הַשִּׁשִּׁי — with the definite article הַ on the ordinal. Days 1–5 have no article on the numeral/ordinal: יֹום אֶחָד, יֹום שֵׁנִי, יֹום שְׁלִישִׁי, יֹום רְבִיעִי, יֹום חֲמִישִׁי.
- **Parser verification:** Gen 1:31 shows יֹום (Noun.ms.**Cst** — construct state, not absolute as in days 1–5) + הַ (Art) + שִּׁשִּׁי (Adj.ms.Abs "sixth"). The construct state of יום before the article is grammatically notable: "day of the sixth" — a construct-article combination that definitizes the whole phrase.
- **Significance:** The article on "the sixth" parallels the article on "the seventh" in Gen 2:2–3 (בַּיֹּום הַשְּׁבִיעִי, where יום also appears in construct). The article on days 6 and 7 but not on days 1–5 may mark a climactic progression: the final work-day and the rest-day are grammatically distinguished from the preceding days.

| Gen 1:19 | וַיְהִי עֶרֶב וַיְהִי בֹקֶר | יֹום רְבִיעִי | Ordinal ("fourth") | None |
| Gen 1:23 | וַיְהִי עֶרֶב וַיְהִי בֹקֶר | יֹום חֲמִישִׁי | Ordinal ("fifth") | None |

**Observations from parser output:**

1. **The wayyiqtol pair is verbatim identical across all six days.** Every occurrence has the same parsing: וַ (Conj) + יְהִי (Verb.Qal.Wayq.3ms) + עֶרֶב (Noun.ms.Abs), then the same for בֹקֶר. No variation in morphology or syntax.

2. **Gen 1:13, 19, 23 consist of the formula alone** — no other content in the verse. These are "pure formula" verses where the creation-day closing is the entire verse content. Gen 1:5, 8, 31 have naming or evaluation content preceding the formula.

3. **The BHSA clause structure is identical across all six occurrences:** two WayX clauses (erev, boqer) followed by one NmCl (day designation). This three-clause micro-structure is the invariant skeleton of the formula.

## The Formula's Absence from Genesis 2:1–3 (The Seventh Day)

**Parser-verified content of Gen 2:1–3:**

| Verse | Content | Formula Present? |
|-------|---------|-----------------|
| Gen 2:1 | וַיְכֻלּוּ הַשָּׁמַיִם וְהָאָרֶץ וְכָל־צְבָאָם | **No** |
| Gen 2:2 | וַיְכַל אֱלֹהִים בַּיֹּום הַשְּׁבִיעִי מְלַאכְתֹּו ... וַיִּשְׁבֹּת בַּיֹּום הַשְּׁבִיעִי | **No** |
| Gen 2:3 | וַיְבָרֶךְ אֱלֹהִים אֶת־יֹום הַשְּׁבִיעִי וַיְקַדֵּשׁ אֹתֹו | **No** |

The וַיְהִי עֶרֶב וַיְהִי בֹקֶר formula appears in **all six** creation work-days (days 1–6) and is **completely absent** from the seventh-day account. The words עֶרֶב and בֹקֶר do not appear anywhere in Gen 2:1–3.

**Grammatical observations on the absence:**

- The seventh day uses a different structural pattern: the phrase בַּיֹּום הַשְּׁבִיעִי ("on the seventh day") appears **three times** in 2:2–3 — twice in v.2 and once in v.3 — with the preposition ב + article on both יום and the ordinal. This contrasts with the bare יֹום + numeral/ordinal in the creation formula.
- The seventh day receives three unique verbs — כלה (Piel "finished"), שׁבת (Qal "ceased/rested"), ברך (Piel "blessed"), קדשׁ (Piel "sanctified") — none of which appear in the creation-day formula. The formula's verb is exclusively היה.
- The absence of the closing formula leaves the seventh day grammatically "open" — it has no endpoint marker equivalent to the evening-morning bracket that closes days 1–6.

## Connection to Daniel 8:14: עֶרֶב בֹּקֶר

**Dan 8:14 Hebrew:** עַ֚ד עֶ֣רֶב בֹּ֔קֶר אַלְפַּ֖יִם וּשְׁלֹ֣שׁ מֵאֹ֑ות

|---|------|-------|---------|-------|
| 1 | עַד | עד | Prep | unto |
| 2 | עֶרֶב | ערב (H6153) | Noun.ms.Abs | evening |
| 3 | בֹּקֶר | בקר (H1242) | Noun.ms.Abs | morning |

| 6 | שְׁלֹשׁ | שׁלשׁ | Noun.s.Cst | three |
| 7 | מֵאוֹת | מאה | Noun.fp.Abs | hundred |

**Grammatical comparison:**

| Feature | Genesis 1 Formula | Daniel 8:14 |
|---------|-------------------|-------------|
| עֶרֶב state | Noun.ms.**Abs** (subject of clause) | Noun.ms.**Abs** (first in compound) |
| בֹקֶר state | Noun.ms.**Abs** (subject of clause) | Noun.ms.**Abs** (second in compound) |
| Connection | **Syndetic:** וַיְהִי separates them into two clauses | **Asyndetic:** no conjunction, juxtaposed directly |
| Article | None on either | None on either |
| Preposition | None on either | עַד governs the whole compound |
| Verb | היה (Qal.Wayq.3ms) x2 | No verb in the time phrase |
| Function | Two narrative event-clauses | Compound time-unit designator |

**Shared features:** Both passages use the same two nouns in the same order (evening before morning), both in absolute state, both without articles. This is the only passage outside Genesis 1 that pairs עֶרֶב and בֹקֶר as bare absolute nouns in direct proximity without prepositions or articles intervening.

**Structural difference:** Genesis 1 deploys the nouns as subjects of two separate narrative clauses; Daniel 8:14 compresses them into a single asyndetic compound functioning as a unit-noun for a time measurement. The KJV translates the Daniel compound as "days" (plural), interpreting עֶרֶב בֹּקֶר as a meristic designation for a complete day-unit (evening + morning = one day).
