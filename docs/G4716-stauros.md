## Lexicon Definition

A stake or post (as set upright), i.e. (specially) the cross (as an instrument of capital punishment); figuratively, exposure to death, i.e. self-denial; by implication, the atonement of Christ. From the base of G2476 (histemi).

### Stauros vs. Xylon (G3586) — Curse-Invocation Contrast

The NT uses two distinct words for the instrument of Christ's death, and the distribution is not random:

**Stauros (G4716)** — 28 occurrences. Used exclusively by the Gospel narrators and Paul. Denotes the physical cross and the theological event of crucifixion. Never linked to Deut 21:23 or curse language.

**Xylon (G3586)** — 14 total occurrences; 4 refer to the cross as "tree" in explicit curse-invocation passages:
- **Acts 5:30** — "whom ye slew and hanged on a tree" (Peter before the Sanhedrin)
- **Acts 10:39** — "whom they slew and hanged on a tree" (Peter to Cornelius)
- **Acts 13:29** — "they took him down from the tree" (Paul at Pisidian Antioch)
- **Gal 3:13** — "Cursed is every one that hangeth on a tree" (quoting Deut 21:23)
- **1 Pet 2:24** — "Who his own self bare our sins in his own body on the tree"

In every case where a NT author wants to invoke the Deuteronomy 21:23 curse — "he that is hanged is accursed of God" — xylon ("tree") is chosen over stauros. This is a deliberate LXX echo: the Hebrew H6086 ets ("tree/wood") in Deut 21:22-23 was rendered ξύλον in the LXX, and the NT authors preserved this link. Paul himself uses both words: stauros for the cross as salvific event (1 Cor 1:17-18, Gal 6:14, Eph 2:16, Col 1:20) but xylon when quoting the curse text (Gal 3:13).
