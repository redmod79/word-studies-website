# WG: Power / Authority

## Overview

Five Greek words all rendered "power" or "authority" in KJV — but each marks a distinct mode of
power: vested right, inherent force, sovereign dominion, raw strength, or originating rank. Their
frequent pairing in cosmic-hierarchy lists (Eph 1:21; Col 1:16; 1 Pet 3:22) shows the NT authors
distinguishing graduated levels of authority, not treating them as synonyms.

## Relationships

### Shared Roots / Cognates
- ἀρχή (G746) and ἄρχων (G758) share the root ἄρχω (archō — to be first, to rule); ἄρχων is the participle ("the ruling one"), ἀρχή is the abstract ("the first position/authority")
- ἐξουσία (G1849), ἐξουσιάζω (G1850), and κατεξουσιάζω (G2715) form a single word family from G1832 ἔξεστι

## Roles in the Concept

- **Delegated right:** exousía (G1849) — the vested privilege or authorized capacity to act; emphasizes legitimacy and grant
- **Inherent force:** dýnamis (G1411) — power as energy, ability, or miraculous capacity; emphasizes what a being *can* do
- **Sovereign grip:** krátos (G2904) — dominion as overwhelming hold; used almost exclusively of God's almighty rule
- **Physical strength:** ischýs (G2479) — strength as bodily or measurable force; often paired with krátos in doxologies
- **Originating rank:** archē (G746) — the position of first place; both temporal ("beginning") and hierarchical ("principality")
- **Rank-holder:** archōn (G758) — the person occupying the archē position; ruler, prince, magistrate, demonic prince

## Key Distinguishing Contexts

- **Luke 9:1** — Jesus gave the disciples "power (δύναμις) and authority (ἐξουσία) over all devils": dynamis = the ability to cast out; exousia = the right/authorization to do so
- **Eph 1:19–21** — four terms stacked: δύναμις (ability), κράτος (dominion), ἰσχύς (strength), then above all ἀρχή + ἐξουσία (cosmic ranks)
- **Rom 13:1–3** — exousia alone used for governing civil powers; emphasizes legitimacy and ordained structure, not raw force
- **Rev 13:2, 4, 7** — the dragon gives the beast his exousia: delegated, derivative authority (not inherent power)
