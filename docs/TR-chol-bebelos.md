The βέβηλ- root etymologically means "accessible to all" (from the threshold [bēlós] that anyone may cross), capturing the Hebrew concept of what lies outside the sacred boundary.

## The κοινός (koinos) Parallel

The NT develops a parallel axis using κοινός (G2839, 12x) and κοινόω (G2840) to express the holy/common distinction, particularly in contexts involving Gentile inclusion:

- **Acts 10:14** — "Not so, Lord; for I have never eaten any thing that is common [κοινός] or unclean [ἀκάθαρτος]" — Peter pairs the two axes (common + unclean) exactly as Lev 10:10 and Eze 44:23 pair chôl + ṭâmêʼ
- **Heb 10:29** — "hath counted the blood of the covenant... an unholy thing [κοινός]" — covenant blood treated as "common" rather than sacred

The OT chôl → LXX bébēlos mapping is the direct predecessor of this NT koinos usage. Both words occupy the same conceptual slot: what lies outside the sacred boundary. The LXX used bébēlos for the formal priestly vocabulary (Leviticus, Ezekiel); the NT uses both bébēlos (for profane persons/speech) and koinós (for the ritual common/clean distinction, especially in the Gentile-inclusion debates).

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The exact βέβηλ- root carries intact from LXX into NT — same word family, same core meaning: "treating what is holy as though it were common"
- The Leviticus 10:10 framework (holy/common :: clean/unclean — two distinct axes) persists into the NT, with both bébēlos and koinós occupying the "common" slot and akáthartos occupying the "unclean" slot
- The objects that can be profaned remain the same: the temple (Acts 24:6, cf. Eze 22:26), the sabbath (Matt 12:5, cf. Eze 22:8), and sacred things generally

**What shifts:**
- In the OT, chôl/châlal is primarily cultic vocabulary confined to priestly discourse (Leviticus, Ezekiel); in the NT, bébēlos extends to describe persons (Esau, Heb 12:16; the lawless, 1 Tim 1:9) and speech (1 Tim 4:7; 6:20; 2 Tim 2:16) — a moralized, generalized application
- The NT develops κοινός as a second, less formal word for "common" — one that carries the Levitical concept into the Gentile-inclusion debate (Acts 10–11), while bébēlos retains its stronger connotation of active desecration or willful disregard for the sacred
- H2490 châlal's massive range (profane + begin + wound) collapses in the LXX: the βεβηλόω mapping captures only the "profane" stream, while ἄρχω captures only the "begin" stream — the polysemy does not survive translation
