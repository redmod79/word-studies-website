**Semantic shift in translation:** The Hebrew tsadaq covers a broader range including "be in the right" (relational/covenantal), "be vindicated" (passive), and "prove right by comparison" (Piel). The Greek δικαιόω narrows primarily to forensic declaration — "to declare/render righteous." The LXX translation choice shapes how NT authors use the concept, foregrounding the judicial dimension that Paul develops extensively.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The forensic/judicial dimension: both tsadaq (Hiphil) and dikaioo describe a declaration of righteous standing, not merely a moral description
- The contrast with condemnation: tsadaq vs. rasha' (OT) parallels dikaioo vs. katakrino (NT)
- The impossibility of human self-justification before God (Job 4:17, 9:2 → Rom 3:20, Gal 2:16)

**What shifts:**
- OT tsadaq is distributed across 5 stems with distinct meanings (Qal = be righteous, Hiphil = declare righteous, Piel = prove right, Niphal = be vindicated); NT dikaioo carries primarily the Hiphil sense of forensic declaration
- The OT Niphal (Dan 8:14 — vindication of the sanctuary) has no direct NT parallel via dikaioo; the concept of cosmic vindication takes different vocabulary in the NT
- Paul's usage extends the Hiphil concept: justification shifts from a judge's verdict on the already-righteous (Deut 25:1) to God's gracious declaration over the ungodly (Rom 4:5)
- The OT Piel sense (prove right by comparison, Jer 3:11, Ezek 16:51) largely drops out of NT usage
