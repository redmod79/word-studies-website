**Semantic shift in translation:** Minimal. The LXX coins ὁλοκαύτωμα ("whole-burnt [thing]," from ὅλος "whole" + καίω "burn") to capture precisely the distinctive feature of ʻôlâh — total consumption on the altar. The Greek gloss is a near-calque; the semantic field transfers almost intact. Where ʻôlâh's root nuance "ascent, going up" is faintly audible in Hebrew, the Greek foregrounds the "wholly burned" aspect instead.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The referent: a sacrifice entirely burned, retaining no portion for priest or offerer.
- The rhetorical function in prophetic/didactic critique: ʻôlâh / ὁλοκαύτωμα names the ritual that, though commanded, is repeatedly subordinated to obedience, mercy, knowledge of God, and love.
- The LXX→NT pipeline is tight: every NT use of G3646 either quotes an OT ʻôlâh text (Heb 10:6, 8 < Psa 40:6) or echoes the prophetic-critique trajectory (Mark 12:33 < 1 Sam 15:22; Hos 6:6; Mic 6:6–8).

**What shifts:**
- **Frequency collapse:** 262 OT occurrences compress to just 3 NT occurrences. The ritual institution itself recedes from view as the NT writers address a post-temple (or soon-post-temple) community.
- **Function:** In the OT, ʻôlâh is primarily *prescriptive* (most uses are in legal/cultic instruction). In the NT, all three occurrences are *evaluative* — cited to show the ritual's relative inadequacy and its fulfillment/transcendence (Hebrews) or its subordination to the love-command (Mark).
- **Semantic accent:** Hebrew ʻôlâh whispers "ascent/rising-up" (via root עלה); Greek ὁλοκαύτωμα accents "whole/entirely burned." The LXX shift centers attention on totality of consumption rather than directionality of ascent.
- **Christological re-reading:** Hebrews 10 deploys ὁλοκαύτωμα precisely to contrast with Christ's "body thou hast prepared me" — the whole-burnt victim is displaced by the whole-given person.
