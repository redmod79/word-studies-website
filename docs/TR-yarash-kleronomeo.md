**Direction of flow:** In the Hebrew Bible, *yârash* and *nâchal* are distinct (displacement vs. allotment). The LXX **collapses both into κληρονομέω**, shifting the NT conceptual default toward "inherit as received share" (*nâchal*-flavor) while preserving the "possess" sense through context. This is the single most important semantic consequence of the chain.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same:**
- The core "take as your own possession/inheritance" semantic holds from Qal *yârash* through κληρονομέω into NT usage.
- The promise-to-Abraham frame (land → heir → inheritance) is preserved: Gen 15 sits at the head of the chain; Rom 4 and Heb 11 sit at the tail.
- The righteous/meek inheriting the land (Ps 37) is carried into Matt 5:5 without semantic drift — Jesus quotes the LXX directly.

**What shifts:**
- **Loss of the dispossession edge.** Hebrew *yârash* is violent in the Hiphil — "drive out the nations." Greek κληρονομέω has no dispossession sense; it is strictly "inherit." The NT rarely deploys the displacement valence (Gal 4:30 is the clearest exception, and even there the verb κληρονομέω is paired with ἐκβάλλω "cast out" to recover the missing edge).
- **Object expansion.** OT object is almost always "the land" (H776 *ʼerets*). NT objects: "the earth" (Matt 5:5), "eternal life" (Mark 10:17; Luke 10:25), "the kingdom of God" (1 Cor 6:9–10; Gal 5:21), "the promises" (Heb 6:12), "a blessing" (1 Pet 3:9), "all things" (Rev 21:7), "salvation" (Heb 1:14). The concrete geography becomes eschatological.
- **Conflation with *nâchal*.** The LXX's single-verb rendering of both *yârash* (take-by-displacement) and *nâchal* (receive-as-allotted-share) means the NT inherits the softer *nâchal*-sense as default. "Inheritance" in Paul/Hebrews reads more like assigned share than conquered territory.
- **Qal-Hiphil distinction erased.** The Hebrew stem contrast (possess vs. cause-to-be-dispossessed) has no Greek counterpart; Greek relies on additional verbs (ἐκβάλλω, ἐξαίρω) to recover the Hiphil sense when needed.
