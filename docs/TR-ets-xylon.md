The LXX renders ets as xylon with near-total consistency. Critically, the execution sense in Deut 21:22-23 uses xylon, creating the linguistic bridge for NT authors to apply the Deuteronomic curse formula to crucifixion.

**Semantic continuity in translation:** The Greek xylon preserves the Hebrew ambiguity between "tree" and "wood" — both living plant and dead material. The LXX does not distinguish these senses with different words. This one-word rendering is what makes the NT tree-of-life and tree-of-the-curse connections possible in a single vocabulary item.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The material sense (wood, timber) carries through unchanged (1 Cor 3:12 "wood, hay, stubble")
- The "staves/clubs" sense (wooden weapons) appears in the Synoptic arrest scenes (Matt 26:47, Mark 14:43, Luke 22:52)
- The tree of life reappears in Revelation using the same word (Rev 2:7, 22:2,14) that the LXX used for Gen 2:9 and 3:22

**What shifts:**
- The execution-display sense undergoes the most significant theological development. In the OT, hanging on the ets marks the criminal as cursed by God (Deut 21:23). The NT takes this curse theology and applies it to the crucifixion — Christ bore the curse on behalf of others (Gal 3:13). The NT deliberately uses xylon rather than stauros in these theologically loaded passages to invoke the Deuteronomic curse via the LXX vocabulary.
- The tree of life, absent from the prophetic corpus, returns in Revelation as an eschatological restoration of Eden — the curse of Gen 3:24 (barring access to the tree of life) is reversed in Rev 22:2-3 where the xylon of life stands in the new Jerusalem and "there shall be no more curse."
