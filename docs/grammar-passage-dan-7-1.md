## Key Grammatical Features

### 1. חֵלֶם חֲזָה וְחֶזְוֵי -- The Dual Genre Designation (chelem + chezvey)

- **Form:** Noun.ms.Abs (חֵלֶם, chelem, "dream") as object of Peal.Perf.3ms (חֲזָה, chaza, "he saw"), immediately followed by conjunctive waw + Noun.mp.Cst (חֶזְוֵי, chezvey, "visions-of") in an elliptical clause.
- **Significance:** This is the single most architecturally significant feature of the verse. Daniel 7:1 is the only verse in Daniel that designates its revelatory content with *both* genre terms simultaneously in the same opening formula: חֵלֶם ("dream") AND חֶזְוֵי ("visions"). The two nouns are not synonymous -- they mark distinct revelatory registers:

  - **חֵלֶם** (chelem, A2493) -- the *dream* as experiential event, received during sleep. In Daniel, this word belongs to the Gentile-king-dream register: Nebuchadnezzar statue dream (Dan 2, 9 occurrences), Nebuchadnezzar tree dream (Dan 4, 6 occurrences). Dan 7:1 is the *only* place where Daniel himself is the dreamer.
  - **חֶזְוֵי** (chezvey, A2376) -- the *visions* as discrete visual scenes perceived. This is the Aramaic technical term for prophetic-visionary content, cognate with Hebrew chazon (H2377). Dan 7 uses it heavily (7:1, 2, 7, 13, 15, 20).

  The dual designation signals that Dan 7 participates in *both* genres -- it is a dream like Dan 2 and 4, but it is also a vision like Dan 8, 10-12. This is the grammatical marker of the chapter **bridge function** between the Aramaic court-tales (Dan 2-6, where revelation comes as chelem to kings) and the Hebrew apocalyptic visions (Dan 8-12, where revelation comes as chazon to Daniel).

### 2. בִּשְׁנַת חֲדָה לְבֵלְאַשַׁצַּר -- Dating Frame (bishnat + regnal-year dative)

- **Form:** Prep + Noun.fs.Cst (שְׁנַת) + Noun.fs.Abs (חֲדָה) + Prep (לְ) + PropN (Belshazzar) + Noun.ms.Cst (מֶלֶךְ) + PropN (Babylon). Aramaic construct-chain with lamed-dative of regnal year.
- **Significance:** This is the Daniel signature reduced dating formula -- *bishnat X le-Y melek Z* -- without the fronted narrative-launcher *wayhi* that Ezekiel uses (Ezek 1:1; 8:1). The identical frame opens Dan 8:1 (Hebrew), Dan 9:1, and Dan 10:1. The Aramaic uses חֲדָה (chad, "one") for the ordinal "first," whereas Dan 9:1 uses Hebrew אַחַת (achat). This parallel dating formula across the Aramaic-Hebrew boundary is itself a structural seam-marker: the same syntactic mold runs from 7:1 (Aramaic) through 8:1, 9:1, 10:1 (Hebrew).

### 3. Clause 2 (Ellp) -- Elliptical Clause as Additive Genre Tag

- **Form:** The ETCBC parser tags Clause 2 (וְחֶזְוֵי רֵאשֵׁהּ עַל־מִשְׁכְּבֵהּ) as type **Ellp** -- an elliptical clause. The conjunction וְ introduces the clause, but there is no predicate verb. The implied verb is supplied from Clause 1 (חֲזָה, "he saw").
- **Significance:** The ellipsis creates a grammatical asymmetry between the two genre terms. חֵלֶם is the direct object of an explicit Peal perfect verb (חֲזָה). But חֶזְוֵי appears in a verbless extension clause with only the locative formula רֵאשֵׁהּ עַל־מִשְׁכְּבֵהּ ("of his head upon his bed"). The elliptical structure sets the visions alongside the dream rather than subordinating them to it. Neither term governs the other; they are coordinate designations of the same revelatory event viewed from two angles -- the experiential (dream-on-the-bed) and the perceptual (visions-of-the-head).
- **Parallel:** Dan 4:2 (Aramaic 4:5 in some numbering) uses the same paired structure: חֵלֶם חֲזֵית ... וְחֶזְוֵי רֵאשִׁי יְבַהֲלֻנַּנִי ("a dream I saw ... and the visions of my head troubled me"). There too, chelem and chezvey appear in the same verse as coordinate but syntactically distinct terms.
### 4. בֵּאדַיִן חֶלְמָא כְתַב -- Narrative Connector + Response Verbs

- **Form:** Adverb (בֵּאדַיִן, be-edayin, "then/thereupon") + Noun.ms emphatic (חֶלְמָא, "the dream") + Verb.Peal.Perf.3ms (כְתַב, "he wrote"). Followed by a second clause: Noun.ms.Cst (רֵאשׁ, "head/sum") + Noun.fp.Abs (מִלִּין, "words") + Verb.Peal.Perf.3ms (אֲמַר, "he said").
- **Significance:** The temporal adverb בֵּאדַיִן ("then") marks a narrative transition from the dream-receipt to the response -- writing and speaking. Two response verbs follow: כְתַב ("he wrote") and אֲמַר ("he said"). Notably, the object of כְתַב is חֶלְמָא ("the dream" -- the emphatic/determined form with final aleph), while the object of אֲמַר is רֵאשׁ מִלִּין ("the sum of the words/matters"). The emphatic-state חֶלְמָא (vs. absolute חֵלֶם in Clause 1) marks the dream as now definite -- the previously introduced dream, now determined. The construct chain רֵאשׁ מִלִּין ("head-of-words") denotes the summary or essential substance of the revelation.

### 5. Third-Person Narrative Frame -- The Bilingual Hinge

- **Form:** The entire verse uses third-person reference for Daniel: דָּנִיֵּאל (PropN) as subject, חֲזָה (3ms), כְתַב (3ms), אֲמַר (3ms), plus 3ms pronominal suffixes on רֵאשֵׁהּ ("his head") and מִשְׁכְּבֵהּ ("his bed").
- **Significance:** Dan 7:1 is a third-person editorial superscription, not Daniel's own first-person report. The first-person voice begins at 7:2 (חָזֵה הֲוֵית "I was seeing"). This mirrors the structure of Dan 2:1, which also uses third-person narrative to introduce the dream before the dialogue shifts to first-person. The function is architectural: 7:1 as superscription *frames* the vision that follows, telling the reader that what they are about to encounter is simultaneously a dream (genre of Dan 2-4) and a sequence of visions (genre of Dan 8-12), and that Daniel *wrote it down* and *spoke its substance*. This superscription is the editorial seam that holds together the Aramaic and Hebrew halves of the book.

## Structural Comparison: Dan 7:1 as Bilingual Bridge

The dual genre designation in Dan 7:1 is architecturally unique. A comparison with the chapter-openers on either side of the language boundary reveals the bridge function:

| Reference | Language | Genre Term(s) | Dreamer | Verb |
|-----------|----------|---------------|---------|------|
| Dan 2:1 | Hebrew (but dream in Aramaic) | chalomot (Heb. "dreams") | Nebuchadnezzar | chalam (Qal.Perf.3ms) |
| Dan 4:2 | Aramaic | chelem + chezvey | Nebuchadnezzar | chazet (Peal.Perf.1s) |
| **Dan 7:1** | **Aramaic** | **chelem + chezvey** | **Daniel** | **chaza (Peal.Perf.3ms)** |
| Dan 8:1 | Hebrew | chazon ("vision") | Daniel | nir'ah (Niphal.Perf.3ms) |
| Dan 9:1-2 | Hebrew | (no vision term -- reading of Jeremiah) | Daniel | -- |
| Dan 10:1 | Hebrew | (davar, "word" revealed) | Daniel | -- |

Dan 7:1 is the *only* Daniel vision-opener that uses *both* chelem and chezvey as coordinate genre designations for Daniel's own experience. Dan 4:2 (Nebuchadnezzar's tree dream) pairs the same two words, but for a Gentile king-dreamer. When Daniel appears as seer in the Hebrew chapters (8:1 onward), the genre term is chazon alone -- dream terminology disappears. The grammatical dual-designation in 7:1 thus marks the chapter as belonging to both registers simultaneously.

Dan 8:1 explicitly acknowledges this bridge function by referencing Dan 7 as the prior vision: chazon nir'ah elay ... acharey hannir'ah elay battechillah ("a vision appeared to me ... after the one that appeared to me at the beginning"). The Hebrew chapter reaches back across the language boundary to the Aramaic vision.
