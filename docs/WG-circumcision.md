# WG: Circumcision

## Overview

The circumcision concept in the NT revolves around three words: the noun for the rite/condition/people (περιτομή), the verb for the act itself (περιτέμνω), and its direct antonym (ἀκροβυστία, uncircumcision). Paul uses these words as paired boundary markers for ethnic and covenant identity, then radically redefines them in terms of inner transformation and faith. The OT concept bridges through Hebrew מוּל (H4135, mûl = to circumcise) and עׇרְלָה (H6190, ʻorlāh = foreskin/uncircumcision), which are the primary LXX source words for the Greek pair.

## Roles in the Concept

- **The noun (G4061 περιτομή):** The rite, the state/condition of being circumcised, or collectively the Jewish people. Paul uses all three senses, often shifting between them within a single argument (e.g., Romans 2–4).
- **The verb (G4059 περιτέμνω):** The act of circumcising — commanded of Abraham, performed on the 8th day, disputed in the Jerusalem Council (Acts 15), and spiritually reapplied to the heart (Col 2:11).
- **The antonym (G203 ἀκροβυστία):** Uncircumcision — the Gentile condition and ethnic marker. In Paul, "the uncircumcision" (ἀκροβυστία) becomes a label for Gentile believers, paired with "the circumcision" for Jewish believers, before Paul relativizes both distinctions in Christ.
