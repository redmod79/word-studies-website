**Semantic shift in translation:** Minimal. The Hebrew châthân carries the double sense of "bridegroom" and "son-in-law" because affinity-by-marriage is the underlying idea. The LXX splits these: the bridegroom sense goes to νυμφίος (G3566); the son-in-law sense is typically rendered by γαμβρός (gambros) or periphrasis. So when the NT inherits νυμφίος, it inherits only the wedding-day / bridegroom nuance — the kinship sense is left behind in Greek.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The bridegroom is a figure of **joy** — Psa 19:5 (running his race), Isa 61:10 (decked with ornaments), carried into the synoptic "children of the bridechamber cannot fast" sayings.
- The fixed **pair "bridegroom and bride"** survives intact (H2860+H3618 → G3566+G3565) — Jer 7:34 directly reappears in Rev 18:23.
- The bridegroom figures **God / the Messiah in covenant delight** — Isa 62:5 ("as the bridegroom rejoiceth over the bride, so shall thy God rejoice over thee") grounds the NT's christological use in Mat 9:15, Jhn 3:29.

**What shifts:**
- The **son-in-law** sense (nearly half of H2860's OT occurrences) does not travel into νυμφίος; that kinship sense is lost at the LXX stage.
- The figurative "bloody husband" use of Exo 4:25–26 has no Greek equivalent and drops out of the chain.
- In the NT, νυμφίος becomes **christologically loaded** — nearly every NT use is of Jesus (Mat 9:15, Mat 25, Jhn 3:29), a narrowing not present in the OT noun.
- The Jeremiah **silencing oracle** ("voice of the bridegroom... shall cease") is reapplied in Rev 18:23 to Babylon, making the OT Judah-judgment a template for eschatological judgment on the world-city.
