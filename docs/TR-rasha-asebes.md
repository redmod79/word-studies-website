The tsaddiyq co-occurrence (63x) reflects asebes appearing in the same verse as the antithetical pairing, not as its translation.

**Semantic shift in translation:** Hebrew rasha covers the full range of moral wrongdoing, legal guilt, and active wickedness. Greek asebes narrows the focus to impiety — the alpha-privative a- + seb- ("reverence") literally means "without reverence [toward God]." The LXX translation thus frames OT wickedness primarily as a failure of proper orientation toward God, rather than as generic moral evil.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The rasha/asebes figure is consistently the opposite of the tsaddiyq/dikaios — both testaments maintain this fundamental moral binary
- The wicked face certain judgment and destruction — OT Psalms/Proverbs and NT Jude/2 Peter echo identical themes
- The link between wickedness and persecution of the righteous carries through (Psa 37:32 → 2 Pet 2:5-9)

**What shifts:**
- **Forensic reversal in Paul:** The OT insists God will not justify the rasha (Exo 23:7). Paul uses the same LXX vocabulary (dikaioo + asebes) to declare that God *does* justify the ungodly through faith (Rom 4:5) — the cross-testament chain makes this inversion maximally visible
- **Eschatological intensification:** NT usage concentrates asebes in judgment/eschatological contexts (2 Pet 2-3, Jude), while OT usage spans wisdom, legal, prophetic, and narrative contexts more broadly
- **Frequency collapse:** From 248 OT verses to just 8 NT verses — the concept narrows and concentrates in final-judgment passages
