## Lexicon Definition

An injunction or decree; by implication, authoritativeness. From ἐπί (G1909) + τάσσω (G5021). Denotes a command issued from a position of authority — a formal directive that carries binding force.

## Semantic Categories

### 1. Divine Authoritative Decree (commissioning the gospel)
- **Rom 16:26** — "according to the commandment (epitagē) of the everlasting God, made known to all nations for the obedience of faith"
- **1 Tim 1:1** — "Paul, an apostle of Jesus Christ by the commandment (epitagē) of God our Saviour, and Lord Jesus Christ"
- **Tit 1:3** — "manifested his word through preaching, which is committed unto me according to the commandment (epitagē) of God our Saviour"

In these three uses, epitagē designates a formal divine decree — God's authoritative order commissioning the apostolic mission. The word consistently appears with "God our Saviour" and in connection with Paul's apostleship or the manifestation of the gospel.

### 2. Apostolic Authority in Teaching
- **Tit 2:15** — "These things speak, and exhort, and rebuke with all authority (epitagē). Let no man despise thee."

The sole occurrence translated "authority." Here epitagē denotes the force and weight behind apostolic instruction — the right to command obedience in doctrinal and ethical matters.

### 3. Disclaimer of Apostolic Command (permission vs. command)
- **1 Cor 7:6** — "But I speak this by permission, and not of commandment (epitagē)."
- **1 Cor 7:25** — "Now concerning virgins I have no commandment (epitagē) of the Lord: yet I give my judgment"
- **2 Cor 8:8** — "I speak not by commandment (epitagē), but by occasion of the forwardness of others"

Paul explicitly distinguishes his personal counsel (gnōmē, permission) from a binding divine epitagē. The word's authority-weight is so strong that Paul must disclaim it when offering advice rather than issuing commands.
