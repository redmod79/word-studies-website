**Semantic shift in translation:** The LXX collapses the carefully maintained Hebrew distinction between bloodless grain offering (minchah) and animal slaughter-sacrifice (zebach) into the single Greek term thysia. In the Hebrew text, minchah is consistently bloodless and voluntary; zebach always involves animal slaughter. The LXX erases this boundary, rendering both with thysia — a term that in classical Greek already meant "sacrifice" broadly. This means any NT reader encountering thysia cannot tell from the word alone whether the OT referent was a grain offering or an animal sacrifice.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The concept of bringing something to God as an act of worship persists from OT minchah through NT thysia
- The prophetic critique that God values obedience/mercy over ritual offerings (1 Sam 15:22, Hos 6:6, Psa 40:6) carries through consistently into Hebrews and the Gospels

**What shifts:**
- Hebrew maintains a precise vocabulary: minchah (grain), olah (burnt), zebach (slaughter), shelem (peace). The LXX collapses these into thysia, losing the bloodless/blood distinction
- NT thysia expands metaphorically beyond ritual: "living sacrifice" (Rom 12:1), "sacrifice of praise" (Heb 13:15), "sacrifice of God are a broken spirit" (Psa 51:17 quoted conceptually)
- The specific ingredients of minchah (fine flour, oil, frankincense, salt) have no NT counterpart; the concept generalizes to any act of devotion
