**Semantic continuity:** The Hebrew middle/active voice split maps almost perfectly onto Greek. ʼâbad Qal → apóllymi middle/passive ("perish, be lost"); ʼâbad Piel/Hiphil → apóllymi active ("destroy"). The LXX translators did not need to disambiguate — the two verbs share the same voice logic.

**Minor shifts:** The Hebrew includes a "wandering" nuance (cognate with straying) that the Greek loses; in Greek, loss tends to focus on the end-state (ruin) rather than the process (straying). Parable contexts in the NT (lost sheep, lost coin) partially reconstruct the "wandering" sense by narrative framing.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- Voice-driven active/middle semantic split (destroy vs perish/be lost)
- Tight pairing with a "save" antonym (Hebrew yāshaʻ / Greek sōzō), often in the same clause
- Eschatological framing: perishing is the covenant sanction in Deut; the eternal-destiny sanction in NT
- Pastoral imagery (lost sheep) carries from Jer 50 / Ezek 34 into the Synoptic parables and Jesus' mission statements

**What shifts:**
- **From temporal to eternal:** In the OT, perishing typically names a this-life / this-generation event (exile, battlefield death, covenant curse). In the NT, it is increasingly framed as eschatological — eternal loss contrasted with eternal life (John, Paul, 2 Peter).
- **Universalizing scope:** OT "perish" targets individuals, tribes, or nations under covenant-specific curses. NT "perish" expands to "whosoever" / "all" / "the world" — anyone outside Christ.
- **Mission reversal:** In the OT, God destroys the wicked; in the NT, the Son of Man *comes to save that which was lost* — the same vocabulary is repurposed as a rescue verb.
- **"Wandering" nuance fades:** The Hebrew verb's root sense of straying/wandering survives mainly in parable imagery; the bare verb in Paul and John focuses on the end-state of ruin.
