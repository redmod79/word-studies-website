The Hebrew sense of "covenant marker" (Gen 9:12, Exo 31:13) maps cleanly into the Greek, where semeion retains this indicative function.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The core meaning: a visible indicator pointing to a reality beyond itself
- The "covenant sign" usage (Gen 17:11 → Rom 4:11)
- The "signs and wonders" formula as a fixed pair describing God's mighty acts
- Signs given to confirm or authenticate divine messages

**What shifts:**
- In the OT, ʼowth frequently denotes ongoing memorial signs (Sabbath, circumcision, phylacteries); in the NT, semeion tilts toward singular authenticating events (miracles of Jesus)
- John's Gospel uses semeion as a technical term for Jesus' miracles (17x), a concentration without OT parallel
- NT develops a negative theology of sign-seeking (Mat 12:39, 1Co 1:22) not present in the OT usage
