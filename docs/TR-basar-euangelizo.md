The bridge is nearly exclusive in both directions: basar maps to euangelizo, and euangelizo maps back almost entirely to basar. This is one of the tightest Hebrew-Greek semantic equivalences in the LXX for a theologically significant term.

**Semantic shift in translation:** Minimal shift. Both the Hebrew basar and the Greek euangelizo carry the core sense of "announce good news." The Greek word is more etymologically transparent (eu- "good" + angelizo "announce"), making the positive valence explicit, whereas the Hebrew can occasionally describe neutral or even unwelcome news (Jer 20:15). In the prophetic passages that define the theological usage (Isa 40:9; 52:7; 61:1), the Hebrew already carries the positive "good tidings" sense that euangelizo formalizes.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- The herald announces God's saving action — both OT mevasser and NT euangelizomenos carry news of divine deliverance
- The content includes "peace" (shalom / eirene) — Isa 52:7 "publisheth peace" → Eph 2:17 "preached peace"
- The good news is directed to the afflicted/humble — Isa 61:1 "meek" → Luke 4:18 "poor"
- The proclamation has a messenger who is sent — the herald model persists from battlefield runner to apostle

**What shifts:**
- **From event-report to person-centered message:** OT basar announces what God has done (reign, salvation, peace); NT euangelizo announces who Jesus is
- **From prophetic anticipation to historical fulfillment:** Isaiah's mevasser awaits a future herald; Luke 4:18-21 declares "This day is this scripture fulfilled"
- **From occasional to programmatic:** basar appears 21x in the entire OT; euangelizo appears 52x in the NT, and the noun euangelion 74x — the concept explodes in scope
- **From Israel-centered to universal:** OT basar brings news to Zion; NT euangelizo extends "to every creature" (Mark 16:15), "to every nation" (Rev 14:6)
