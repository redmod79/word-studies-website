# Constructio ad Sensum / Synesis (Greek)

**Type:** Agreement / syntax
**Alternate names:** κατὰ σύνεσιν, constructio ad sensum, sense-construction, agreement according to meaning
**Primary references:** BDF §§132–137 (Kongruenz, Inkongruenzen); Wallace 331–345 (ExSyn); Machen §145 (neuter plural subject)

---

## 1. Definition

**Constructio ad sensum** (Latin: "construction according to sense"; Greek: κατὰ σύνεσιν) is the phenomenon where grammatical agreement (gender, number, or both) follows the *meaning* or *real-world referent* rather than the strict morphological form of the governing word. It contrasts with **constructio ad formam** (agreement strictly matching the grammatical gender/number of the head noun).

The construction is not a grammatical error but a recognized feature of Greek at all periods. It occurs whenever the writer's mental image of the referent — persons rather than things, a group rather than a singular collective, a man rather than a neuter noun — overrides the mechanical concord rules.

BDF §134 states the governing principle: "the idea of persons supersedes that of things." Wallace (ExSyn 331–45) treats it under pronoun–antecedent agreement, subject–verb agreement, and relative clause agreement.

## 2. Categories with Examples

### 2a. Neuter Plural Subject with Singular Verb

Classical Greek permits (and often prefers) a **singular verb** with a neuter plural subject, treating the neuter plural as a collective mass. The NT retains this convention, though plural verbs also occur freely with neuter plural subjects.

| Reference | Greek Subject | Morph | Verb | Verb Morph | KJV |
|-----------|--------------|-------|------|------------|-----|
| Mark 4:4 | τὰ πετεινά | N-NPN | ἦλθεν … κατέφαγεν | V-2AAI-3S … V-2AAI-3S | "the fowls … came and devoured" |

Parser output confirms τὰ πετεινά (N-NPN, Nom Pl Neuter) governing singular verbs ἦλθεν and κατέφαγεν (both V-2AAI-3S, Aor Act Ind 3rd Sg). The birds are treated as a collective unit.

**Contrast — neuter plural with plural verb (synesis toward persons):**

| Reference | Greek Subject | Morph | Verb | Verb Morph | KJV |
|-----------|--------------|-------|------|------------|-----|
| John 10:27 | τὰ πρόβατα τὰ ἐμά | N-NPN | ἀκούουσιν … ἀκολουθοῦσίν | V-PAI-3P … V-PAI-3P | "My sheep hear … and they follow" |

Here τὰ πρόβατα (N-NPN, Nom Pl Neuter) governs **plural** verbs ἀκούουσιν and ἀκολουθοῦσίν (V-PAI-3P). The sheep represent *persons* (Christ's followers), and the plural verb follows sense. BDF §133 notes this shift occurs especially when neuter plurals denote persons or animate beings acting with personal agency.

### 2b. Collective Noun + Plural Verb/Participle (The πλῆθος Pattern)

A singular collective noun (τὸ πλῆθος, ὁ ὄχλος, ὁ λαός) takes a **plural** verb, participle, or pronoun because the writer thinks of the group as composed of individual persons.

**John 6:2** — ὄχλος (singular) + plural verb in following clause:

| Word | Lemma | Morph | Function |
|------|-------|-------|----------|
| ὄχλος πολύς | ὄχλος | N-NSM (Nom Sg M) | Subject: "a great multitude" |
| ἠκολούθει | ἀκολουθέω | V-IAI-3S | Singular verb: "followed" |
| ἑώρων | ὁράω | V-IAI-3P | **Plural verb**: "they saw" |

The singular ὄχλος governs singular ἠκολούθει in its own clause, but the ὅτι-clause shifts to plural ἑώρων — constructio ad sensum, since the crowd consists of individuals who each saw. BDF §134 cites this as a clean example: "when the plural which conforms to the sense does not appear until the following clause, we feel no offense."

**Luke 2:13** — πλῆθος (neuter singular) + masculine plural participles:

| Word | Lemma | Morph | Function |
|------|-------|-------|----------|
| πλῆθος | πλῆθος | N-NSN (Nom Sg N) | Subject: "a multitude" |
| στρατιᾶς οὐρανίου | στρατιά | N-GSF (Gen Sg F) | "of the heavenly host" |
| αἰνούντων | αἰνέω | V-PAP-GPM (Gen Pl M) | **Masc. plural ptcp**: "praising" |
| λεγόντων | λέγω | V-PAP-GPM (Gen Pl M) | **Masc. plural ptcp**: "saying" |

A neuter singular noun (πλῆθος) is modified by **genitive plural masculine** participles. The multitude = angels (masculine, personal agents), and the participles follow that sense. BDF §134 explicitly cites this verse under constructio ad sensum with collective nouns.

**Acts 21:36** — πλῆθος + masculine plural participle:

| Word | Lemma | Morph | Function |
|------|-------|-------|----------|
| τὸ πλῆθος | πλῆθος | N-NSN (Nom Sg N) | Subject: "the multitude" |
| τοῦ λαοῦ | λαός | N-GSM (Gen Sg M) | "of the people" |
| ἠκολούθει | ἀκολουθέω | V-IAI-3S | Singular verb: "followed" |
| κράζοντες | κράζω | V-PAP-NPM (Nom Pl M) | **Masc. plural ptcp**: "crying" |

The verb ἠκολούθει agrees grammatically with τὸ πλῆθος (singular), but the circumstantial participle κράζοντες shifts to **nominative plural masculine** — the crowd as crying persons. The same double-construction (grammatical verb + sense-driven participle) appears in Acts 5:16, where τὸ πλῆθος governs singular συνήρχετο but the attendant participle is φέροντες (V-PAP-NPM, Nom Pl Masc).

**Acts 4:32** — πλῆθος + masculine plural participle:

| Word | Lemma | Morph | Function |
|------|-------|-------|----------|
| τοῦ πλήθους | πλῆθος | N-GSN (Gen Sg N) | "of the multitude" |
| τῶν πιστευσάντων | πιστεύω | V-AAP-GPM (Gen Pl M) | **Masc. plural ptcp**: "of those who believed" |

The neuter singular πλῆθος is immediately qualified by a genitive plural masculine participle τῶν πιστευσάντων — the believers are persons, not things.

### 2c. Gender Shift from Grammatical to Natural Gender

When a grammatically neuter (or feminine) noun refers to a male (or female) person, subsequent pronouns, participles, or demonstratives may shift to the natural gender.

**Galatians 4:19** — neuter vocative + masculine relative pronoun:

| Word | Lemma | Morph | Function |
|------|-------|-------|----------|
| τέκνα μου | τέκνον | N-VPN (Voc Pl N) | "my children" — neuter |
| οὓς | ὅς | R-APM (Acc Pl M) | **Masculine** relative pronoun |

τέκνα is neuter plural, but the relative pronoun οὓς is **accusative plural masculine** — Paul thinks of the Galatian believers as *persons* (men and women), not as grammatically neuter "children." This is a textbook example of synesis in a relative clause.

**Philemon 10** — neuter noun + masculine relative pronoun:

| Word | Lemma | Morph | Function |
|------|-------|-------|----------|
| τέκνου | τέκνον | N-GSN (Gen Sg N) | "my child" — neuter |
| ὃν | ὅς | R-ASM (Acc Sg M) | **Masculine** relative pronoun |

Paul calls Onesimus "my child" (τέκνον, neuter), but the relative pronoun ὃν is masculine because Onesimus is a man. Wallace (ExSyn 337–38) cites this as a primary example of gender-synesis in relative pronouns.

**Revelation 3:4** — neuter noun + plural verb + masculine adjective:

| Word | Lemma | Morph | Function |
|------|-------|-------|----------|
| ὀνόματα | ὄνομα | N-APN (Acc Pl N) | "names" — neuter plural |
| ἃ | ὅς | R-NPN (Nom Pl N) | Neuter relative: "which" |
| ἐμόλυναν | μολύνω | V-AAI-3P | Plural verb: "defiled" |
| ἄξιοί | ἄξιος | A-NPM (Nom Pl M) | **Masculine** plural adj.: "worthy" |
| εἰσιν | εἰμί | V-PAI-3P | Plural verb: "are" |

"Names" (ὀνόματα, neuter) = persons in Sardis. The relative ἃ begins in neuter concord, but the sentence ends with masculine ἄξιοί εἰσιν ("they are worthy") — the persons behind the names emerge. The plural verbs (ἐμόλυναν, περιπατήσουσιν, εἰσιν) all follow sense-number as well: the neuter plural "names" denotes individuals who walk, defile, and are worthy.

### 2d. Neuter Plural Referent (Nations) + Masculine Pronoun

**Matthew 28:19** — neuter ἔθνη + masculine αὐτούς:

| Word | Lemma | Morph | Function |
|------|-------|-------|----------|
| τὰ ἔθνη | ἔθνος | N-APN (Acc Pl N) | "nations" — neuter plural |
| αὐτούς | αὐτός | P-APM (Acc Pl M) | **Masculine** pronoun: "them" |
| βαπτίζοντες | βαπτίζω | V-PAP-NPM (Nom Pl M) | "baptizing" — masculine (agrees with implied disciples) |

The object τὰ ἔθνη is neuter plural, but the pronoun referring back to them is αὐτούς (Acc Pl **Masculine**). Nations are composed of *persons* who are baptized — constructio ad sensum shifts to masculine. BDF §134 catalogs this pattern: neuter collective/ethnographic terms yielding to masculine pronouns when personal agency is in view.

**Acts 15:17** — neuter ἔθνη + masculine relative pronoun:

| Word | Lemma | Morph | Function |
|------|-------|-------|----------|
| πάντα τὰ ἔθνη | ἔθνος | N-NPN (Nom Pl N) | "all the nations" — neuter |
| ἐφ' οὓς | ὅς | R-APM (Acc Pl M) | **Masculine** relative: "upon whom" |
| αὐτούς | αὐτός | P-APM (Acc Pl M) | **Masculine** pronoun: "them" |

Same pattern: neuter ἔθνη → masculine οὓς and αὐτούς. The nations are *peoples* upon whom God's name is called.

### 2e. Compound/Neuter Subject + Masculine Demonstrative

**Revelation 17:16** — two neuter subjects + masculine demonstrative:

| Word | Lemma | Morph | Function |
|------|-------|-------|----------|
| τὰ δέκα κέρατα | κέρας | N-NPN (Nom Pl N) | "the ten horns" — neuter |
| τὸ θηρίον | θηρίον | N-NSN (Nom Sg N) | "the beast" — neuter |
| οὗτοι | οὗτος | D-NPM (Nom Pl M) | **Masculine** plural demonstrative |
| μισήσουσιν | μισέω | V-FAI-3P | Plural verb: "shall hate" |

Two neuter subjects (horns + beast) govern a **masculine plural** demonstrative οὗτοι. The symbolic entities represent personal agents (kings, Rev 17:12), and John shifts to masculine when predicating personal actions (hating, desolating, burning).

### 2f. Compound Subject with Singular Verb

Wallace (ExSyn 401–2; Basics 180) notes: "When two subjects, each in the singular, are joined by a conjunction, the verb is usually in the plural. However, when an author wants to highlight one of the subjects, the verb is put in the singular."

BDF §135 states the rules for compound subjects: (a) the verb agrees with the first subject if it stands before it; (b) with both subjects together if the verb follows the second; (c) with the first if the verb stands between them.

**1 Corinthians 10:7** — collective ὁ λαός + plural verb:

| Word | Lemma | Morph | Function |
|------|-------|-------|----------|
| ὁ λαός | λαός | N-NSM (Nom Sg M) | "the people" — singular |
| Ἐκάθισεν | καθίζω | V-AAI-3S | Singular verb: "sat down" |
| ἀνέστησαν | ἀνίστημι | V-AAI-3P | **Plural** verb: "rose up" |

The same collective subject (ὁ λαός) governs first a singular verb (Ἐκάθισεν, the group sitting as a unit) then a plural verb (ἀνέστησαν, individuals rising to play) — synesis within a single sentence.

## 3. How to Recognize the Pattern

Look for **any** of the following:

1. **Number mismatch** — singular collective subject (πλῆθος, ὄχλος, λαός, ἔθνος) with plural verb, participle, or pronoun.
2. **Gender mismatch** — neuter noun (τέκνον, ὄνομα, ἔθνος, πρόβατον, κέρας, θηρίον) with masculine/feminine pronoun, participle, adjective, or demonstrative.
3. **Shift within a clause chain** — grammatical concord in the first clause gives way to sense-concord in a following clause (John 6:2; 1 Cor 10:7).
4. **Relative pronoun gender ≠ antecedent gender** — especially neuter antecedent with masculine relative (Gal 4:19; Phlm 10; Acts 15:17).

**Diagnostic question:** Does the referent denote persons, while the grammatical form is neuter or collective singular? If so, any plural or masculine form in the vicinity is likely constructio ad sensum.

## 4. Contrast with Related Constructions

| Construction | Description | Key Difference |
|-------------|-------------|----------------|
| **Constructio ad formam** | Strict grammatical agreement with morphological form | The default; ad sensum is the deviation |
| **Neuter plural + singular verb** (classical rule) | Neuter plural subjects take singular verbs treating them as a mass | Opposite direction — *away* from persons toward things |
| **Attraction of relative** (BDF §294) | Relative pronoun attracted into case of antecedent | Case change, not gender/number change for semantic reasons |
| **Concord shift neuter → masculine** | Gender shift signaling abstract → personal referent | A specific subtype of ad sensum; see [concord-shift-neuter-to-masculine](concord-shift-neuter-to-masculine.md) |
| **Prolepsis** | Anticipatory reference to a noun not yet stated | Different mechanism (forward reference, not sense-agreement) |

## 5. Significance for Exegesis

Constructio ad sensum is context-neutral in its mechanics — it tells the interpreter that the writer was thinking of **persons** (or personal agents) rather than abstract entities, things, or undifferentiated masses. It does not by itself determine:

- The identity of the persons in question
- Whether a particular theological conclusion follows
- Whether the author intended a specific individual or a group

What it *does* reliably signal is that personhood, agency, or individuation was salient in the writer's mind at the point of the shift. This makes it an important grammatical datum for passages where the referent is debated.
