## Lexicon Definition

From G5228 (huper) and G4121 (pleonazo); to superabound: — be exceeding abundant.

The prefix ὑπέρ intensifies the already-augmentative verb πλεονάζω ("to have more, increase"), producing a double-intensified sense: to overflow beyond all measure.

## Semantic Categories

### 1. Superabundance of Divine Grace

- **1 Tim 1:14** — "And the grace of our Lord was exceeding abundant (huperepleonasen) with faith and love which is in Christ Jesus."

This is the sole occurrence. Paul's point is that grace did not merely appear or suffice — it overflowed beyond all calculation. The verb is paired with "with faith and love" (meta pisteos kai agapes), indicating that this superabounding grace brought faith and love in its wake. The context (vv. 12–16) contrasts Paul's former identity as "a blasphemer, and a persecutor, and injurious" (v. 13) with the overwhelming grace that transformed him — making him a paradigm case ("a pattern," v. 16) of grace exceeding sin.

**Romans 5:20 parallel:** "Moreover the law entered, that the offence might abound (pleonazo, G4121). But where sin abounded (pleonazo), grace did much more abound (huperperisseuo, G5248)."

This is the closest conceptual parallel. Paul uses the simpler root pleonazo for sin's increase, then the ὑπέρ-compound huperperisseuo for grace's overflow. In 1 Tim 1:14, Paul goes further — choosing the even rarer huperpleonazo (combining ὑπέρ with pleonazo itself rather than with perisseuo) for an intensification of the same theme applied to his personal experience.

### Easily Confused Words

| Word | Strong's | Shared Concept | Key Difference |
|------|----------|---------------|----------------|
| πλεονάζω (pleonazo) | G4121 | abound/increase | Standard verb for increasing or abounding; no ὑπέρ intensifier |
| περισσεύω (perisseuo) | G4052 | abound/overflow | Most common NT "abound" verb (39x); used of both surplus and spiritual growth |
| ὑπερπερισσεύω (huperperisseuo) | G5248 | super-abound | Parallel ὑπέρ-compound using perisseuo base instead of pleonazo; 2 NT uses |

The three "abound" verbs form a scale: perisseuo (standard overflow) → pleonazo (increase/be in excess) → huper-compounds (overflow beyond measure). Paul coined or selected huperpleonazo for 1 Tim 1:14 when even huperperisseuo (used in Rom 5:20) was insufficient to capture what grace did for him as "chief of sinners."
