| tôwrâh (H8451) | δικαίωμα (G1345) | 16x | When torah = righteous decree/statute |

**Semantic shift in translation:** Hebrew תּוֹרָה derives from יָרָה (to throw/direct/teach) — its fundamental sense is *instruction* or *divine direction*. Greek νόμος derives from νέμω (to distribute, apportion) — its sense is *regulation* or *legal distribution*. The LXX translation encoded torah's instructional character into a legislative frame. This shift became consequential in Paul's letters, where nomos carries legal weight that torah as "teaching" does not inherently require.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- Torah/nomos identifies the body of divine instruction given through Moses at Sinai
- Both OT and NT treat it as holy, righteous, and good (Psalm 19:7; Rom 7:12)
- It remains the covenant standard — faithfulness or violation is measured against it in both corpora
- Priestly/Levitical teaching function carries into NT (Luke 10:26; Matt 22:36)

**What shifts:**
- The LXX's choice of νόμος foregrounded the legal/regulatory aspect over the instructional/directional aspect of torah
- Paul's letters push heavily on nomos as law-code (not teaching), enabling the law-vs-faith contrast that has no exact Hebrew parallel
- NT expands nomos metaphorically: "law of faith", "law of the Spirit", "law of Christ", "law of sin" — usages with no direct OT torah parallel
- Nomos in NT can refer to OT generally (including Psalms and Prophets), broader than the Pentateuch alone
