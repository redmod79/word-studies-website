**Semantic shift in translation:** Checed's covenantal-relational dimension (obligation arising from relationship) narrows to compassion/pity in Greek. The LXX translators consistently chose eleos ("mercy/compassion") over possible alternatives like pistis ("faithfulness") or diatheke-adjacent terms, which collapsed checed's covenant-obligation pole into its emotional-compassion pole. This explains why NT "mercy" sometimes carries more weight than English "mercy" alone suggests — it inherits the OT covenantal freight.

## Continuity and Shifts

**What stays the same across testaments:**
- Eleos as a divine attribute — God is "rich in mercy" (Eph 2:4) as he was "abundant in checed" (Exo 34:6)
- Connection to covenant — Luke 1:72 explicitly ties eleos to God remembering "his holy covenant"
- Ethical demand — Jesus quotes Hos 6:6 to demand mercy over ritual, preserving checed's prophetic force
- Liturgical praise — the Magnificat (Luke 1:50-54) echoes the Psalms' celebration of God's checed
- Pairing with faithfulness — "mercy, and faith" (Matt 23:23) mirrors "checed and emunah/emeth"

**What shifts:**
- The covenant-obligation dimension weakens: NT eleos emphasizes compassion more than relational duty
- The eleemosyne derivative ("almsgiving") develops checed toward concrete charitable acts — a meaning absent in OT checed
- The pastoral epistles formulaic "grace, mercy, and peace" (1 Tim 1:2, 2 Tim 1:2, Tit 1:4) places eleos between charis and eirene, repositioning it within a distinctively Christian triad
- The eschatological dimension strengthens: "looking for the mercy of our Lord Jesus Christ unto eternal life" (Jude 1:21) applies checed to final salvation
