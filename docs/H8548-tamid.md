## Semantic Categories

### 1. The Continual Burnt Offering (Cultic Ritual)
The dominant use — tamid modifies the burnt offering (*olat tamid*), the twice-daily lamb sacrifice and associated offerings that formed the backbone of Israel's worship system.
- **Exo 29:42** — "[This shall be] a continual (tamid) burnt offering throughout your generations"
- **Num 28:3** — "two lambs of the first year without spot day by day, [for] a continual (tamid) burnt offering"
- **Num 28:6** — "[It is] a continual (tamid) burnt offering, which was ordained in mount Sinai"
- **Psa 50:8** — "thy burnt offerings, [to have been] continually (tamid) before me"

### 2. Other Continual Sanctuary Elements
Tamid also describes the perpetual showbread, the perpetual lamp, and the perpetual incense — items that were never to be interrupted in the tabernacle/temple.
- **Exo 25:30** — "thou shalt set upon the table shewbread before me alway (tamid)"
- **Exo 27:20** — "pure oil olive beaten for the light, to cause the lamp to burn always (tamid)"
- **Exo 30:8** — "a perpetual (tamid) incense before the LORD throughout your generations"
- **Lev 6:13** — "The fire shall ever (tamid) be burning upon the altar; it shall never go out"

### 3. Substantive "The Daily" (Daniel's Apocalyptic Usage)
In Daniel, tamid is used absolutely as a noun — "the tamid" (the continual [service]) — without any qualifying noun. KJV supplies [sacrifice] in italics.

### 5. Continual States and Conditions (Prophetic)
The prophets use tamid for uninterrupted states — both divine attention and persistent evil.

### Key Semantic Distinction
Tamid emphasizes *regular, uninterrupted recurrence* — a pattern that continues without cessation. Unlike olam (which looks at duration from beginning to end) or netsach (which emphasizes permanence/victory), tamid focuses on the *ongoing, repetitive nature* of the action. This is why it becomes the technical term for the twice-daily sacrifice: it is not eternal (olam) but regular and uninterrupted (tamid).
