## Semantic Categories

As a hapax legomenon, epilysis has only one attested NT usage, though its semantic range can be understood through its root verb G1956 and its LXX background.

### 1. Explanation / Interpretation of Scripture
- **2 Pet 1:20** — "Knowing this first, that no prophecy of the scripture is of any private interpretation (epilyseos)."

The genitive form ἐπιλύσεως (epilyseos) here is paired with ἰδίας (idias, "one's own / private" — G2398), forming the phrase "private interpretation." The context (v. 21) clarifies the contrast: prophecy did not originate from human will but from the Holy Spirit, and therefore its "unloosing" (interpretation) cannot be privately self-generated.

## Root Verb: G1956 ἐπιλύω (epilyo)

Since epilysis is a hapax, the root verb epilyo provides essential semantic context. It appears twice in the NT:

| Reference | Form | Parsing | Translation | Context |
|-----------|------|---------|-------------|---------|
| Mark 4:34 | ἐπέλυεν | Imperfect Active Indicative 3S | "he expounded" | Jesus privately explaining parables to disciples |
| Acts 19:39 | ἐπιλυθήσεται | Future Passive Indicative 3S | "it shall be determined" | Town clerk referring matters to a lawful assembly |

These two NT uses show both senses of the word: (a) explaining/interpreting meaning (Mark), and (b) resolving/deciding a matter (Acts). The Mark 4:34 usage is closest to the noun in 2 Pet 1:20 — Jesus "unloosing" the parables for his disciples is the same kind of activity Peter says no one should attempt privately with Scripture.

## LXX Background: The epilyo Word Group in Genesis

The lexicon entry for G1956 lists Gen 41:12 among its biblical usage references, and the G1955 entry lists Gen 40:8. Though the Hebrew-to-Greek mapping database does not register a formal LXX alignment (the LXX translators used different vocabulary), the connection is well-attested in the lexicographic tradition:

- **Genesis 40:8** (LXX context) — Joseph says to Pharaoh's butler and baker: "Do not interpretations belong to God? Tell me them, I pray you." The Hebrew word here is H6623 פִּתְרוֹן (pithrown, "interpretation of a dream"). The LXX renders the verb form in Gen 40:8 with διασαφέω (G1285), while epilyo appears in the LXX tradition for the broader Joseph interpretation narrative.
- **Genesis 41:12** — "And there was there with us a young man, an Hebrew... and he interpreted to us our dreams; to each man according to his dream he did interpret." The lexicon specifically cites this verse for epilyo.

The theological link is significant: Joseph insists that dream-interpretation belongs to God, not to human initiative — the same principle Peter applies to Scripture prophecy in 2 Pet 1:20.
