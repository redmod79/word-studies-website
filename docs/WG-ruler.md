## Key Distinctions

**nâgîyd vs. sar:** sar is the workhorse (368 verses), used for any ranked official. nâgîyd is marked — it always implies a special designation, usually divine. A man can be both: Saul is anointed as nâgîyd (1Sa 9:16) and then his subordinates are sarim. The sar holds his rank by organizational position; the nâgîyd holds his by direct divine commission.

**nâgîyd vs. nâsîyʼ:** nâsîyʼ emphasizes elevation (root nâsaʼ, "to lift up") — the person who has been raised above. This is commonly used for the twelve tribal leaders and Ezekiel's eschatological prince. nâgîyd emphasizes standing-in-front (root nâgad, "to be conspicuous") — the one publicly designated and placed before the people. Ezekiel's vision uses nâsîyʼ consistently for his eschatological prince; Daniel's prophecy uses nâgîyd for Messiah the Prince.

**mâshal vs. the noun forms:** mâshal is a verb — it describes the exercise of ruling power, not a title. A nâgîyd, sar, or nâsîyʼ mâshals (rules), but mâshal alone says nothing about how the ruling authority was obtained.

**qâtsîyn vs. the others:** The rarest of the group (12 verses), qâtsîyn comes from qâtsan ("to determine, decide"). It has a judicial flavor — the one who renders decisions. Used in wisdom literature and the ants-parable (Pro 6:7 — "no qâtsîyn").

## Key Passages Where Multiple Group Words Appear Together

- **1Ch 13:1** — "David consulted with the captains (sar) of thousands and hundreds, and with every leader (nâgîyd)" — both in same administrative context
