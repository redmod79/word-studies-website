## Lexicon Definition

From Strong's: "from another form of [μέθυ]; to drink to intoxication, i.e. get drunk." Classical Greek usage spans from literal intoxication to, by extension, drinking one's fill / drinking freely at a banquet (cf. LSJ). The lexical core is the effect of wine on the drinker — saturation, past the point of simple thirst-quenching.

## Semantic Categories

### 1. Literal Intoxication (Moral/Ethical Warning Context)
- **Mat 24:49** — "to eat and drink with the drunken (methyóntōn)" — the wicked servant's behavior signaling judgment.
- **1 Cor 11:21** — "one is hungry, and another is drunken (methýei)" — Paul rebukes the Corinthian abuse of the Lord's Supper; the contrast with "hungry" and the rebuke itself require genuine intoxication.
- **1 Thess 5:7** — "they that be drunken (methyskómenoi) are drunken (methýousin) in the night" — paired with sleep/darkness as the state believers of the day must reject.

In all three, methýō describes a negatively-valenced state: bodily intoxication treated as moral failure.

### 2. Accusation of Intoxication (Denied)
- **Acts 2:15** — "these are not drunken (methýousin), as ye suppose" — Peter rejects the charge that the Pentecost disciples are intoxicated. The mockers' accusation (Acts 2:13 uses γλεύκους, "new/sweet wine") presumes the normal sense of methýō is *drunk*, not merely *having drunk*.

### 3. Apocalyptic Figurative Intoxication
- **Rev 17:2** — "the inhabitants of the earth have been made drunk (emethýsthēsan) with the wine of her fornication" — aorist passive; Babylon's corrupting influence produces a drunkenness-like stupor.
- **Rev 17:6** — "the woman drunken (methýousan) with the blood of the saints" — figurative, but drawing on the full force of "staggering / saturated past reason." The image requires the strong sense, not mere "drinking freely."

### 4. Satiation at a Banquet (John 2:10)
- **John 2:10** — the architriklínos (master of the feast) says, "Every man at the beginning doth set forth good wine; and when men have well drunk (methysthōsin, aorist passive subjunctive), then that which is worse."

This is the *only* NT use where methýō is not explicitly negative or explicitly figurative intoxication. It appears in a proverbial saying about banquet practice — the master is stating a general customary pattern, not describing the Cana guests in particular. In classical and Hellenistic Greek, methýō at banquets can range from "drunk" to "have drunk freely / to the full," and commentators split on whether the architriklínos means "once guests are intoxicated [and can't tell the difference]" or "once guests have drunk their fill." Lexicons (BDAG, LSJ) register both the intoxication sense and the "drink freely" sense; context and semantic domain neighbors (all pointing to intoxication in the NT) weigh on the question.

### LXX Background

In the Septuagint, μεθύω consistently translates Hebrew verbs in the שָׁכַר (shakar) family, which denotes intoxication from שֵׁכָר (shēkar, strong drink). Representative LXX occurrences:
- **Gen 9:21** (LXX) — Noah "drank of the wine, and was drunken (emethýsthē)" — the archetypal intoxication scene, rendering Hebrew *wayyishkar*.
- **Gen 43:34** — Joseph's brothers "drank, and were merry (emethýsthēsan) with him" — ambiguous in the Hebrew (*wayyishkeru*); LXX chose methýō for what many English versions translate "drank freely."
- **1 Sam 1:14** — Eli accuses Hannah: "How long wilt thou be drunken (methysthēsē)?" — clearly intoxication.
- **Isa 29:9; 49:26; Jer 25:27** — figurative drunkenness of nations under judgment; methýō translates shikkor-forms.
- **Song 5:1** — "drink, yea, drink abundantly (methýsthēte), O beloved" — the clearest LXX case of methýō carrying the "drink freely/to satiety" sense rather than intoxication.

The strongest PMI bridges are with שָׁכַר/שִׁכּוֹר (the pure intoxication words), but the word also overlaps with רָוָה (to be saturated/watered) and the neutral שָׁתָה (to drink) in some contexts. Notably, methýō also maps to places where Hebrew רָוָה describes land being watered to saturation (Isa 55:10) — the semantic core is *saturation past the point of need*, with *intoxication* as the most common application.
