## Semantic Categories

### 1. Sacrificial Blood-Shedding as Prerequisite for Forgiveness
- **Heb 9:22** — "And almost all things are by the law purged with blood; and without shedding of blood (haimatekchysia) is no remission."

This is the only occurrence. The word functions as an abstract noun denoting the event of blood being shed in sacrificial context. The sentence frames it as a universal negative principle: no aphesis (remission/forgiveness) exists apart from haimatekchysia. The qualifier "almost" (schedon) acknowledges Levitical exceptions (flour offerings, Lev 5:11-13; water purification, Num 31:23), but the governing principle is that blood-shedding is the normative mechanism of ritual purification and forgiveness under the law.

The compound haimatekchysia fills a lexical gap: Greek had no single noun for "sacrificial blood-shedding." The author of Hebrews coined it by fusing the object (haima) with the action (ekchysis, from ekcheo) into one term, enabling the concise universal negative statement of Heb 9:22b.

## Hebrew Source

No direct LXX mapping exists — this word was coined for the NT and does not appear in the Septuagint.

The underlying OT concept draws on the Hebrew vocabulary of sacrificial blood manipulation:
- **H1818 דָּם (dam)** — blood; the OT substance term (360v)
- **H8210 שָׁפַךְ (shaphak)** — to pour out, shed (blood); the primary OT verb for blood-shedding (Gen 9:6, Lev 17:4, Deut 21:7)
- **H2236 זָרַק (zaraq)** — to sprinkle, toss (blood against the altar); ritual application verb

The phrase "shedding of blood" in Hebrew would be expressed periphrastically (shaphak dam, "pour out blood"), never as a single word. The Hebrews author's innovation was creating a Greek compound to express this entire concept as one term.
